# Editor Guide: Prose Trimming Patterns

## Summary: The Core Principle

Every sentence cut shared the same disease: **the narrator explaining the story to the reader instead of letting the story happen.** The trimmed version trusts the dialogue, the concrete details, and the reader's intelligence. When Pela speaks in clipped, decisive sentences, you don't need to tell the reader she "spoke with the clean, undecorated language of a woman reporting a strategic decision." Her language IS clean and undecorated. The reader is already there.

**Target ratio for future drafts**: If a descriptive passage is longer than the dialogue it surrounds, something is wrong. Dialogue IS characterization. Let it carry the weight.

---

## 1. Landscape / Architecture Over-Description

If the opening paragraph describes the setting.

**Pattern**: The opening paragraph describes the setting in 100+ words when 40-50 would anchor the reader.

**What I cut**: Geology lesson (mineral content, crystal veins "like frozen lightning"), the warmth metaphor chain ("not stifling, but insistent, the kind of warmth that pressed against the skin and settled into the lungs like a held breath"). Three separate sensory details for what only needed one.

**Rule**: A setting gets ONE specific visual, ONE sensory detail (smell/sound/temperature), and ONE establishing dimension (size/shape). The rest fills in through character interaction with the space. If a character later touches the stone, sits in the water, or comments on the acoustics, you don't need to pre-describe those surfaces.

**Before** (110 words):
> soaring caverns of rose quartz and veins of luminescent crystal threading the walls like frozen lightning... A natural grotto, perhaps sixty feet across, where the bedrock opened into a shallow dome of pale stone. Water seeped from fissures... each one a slightly different shade of blue-green depending on the mineral content...

**After** (55 words):
> a natural grotto sixty feet across, pale stone domed overhead, water seeping from ceiling fissures into tiered pools of blue-green.

## 2. Character Entrance Over-Specification

**Pattern**: When a character enters the scene, narration explains HOW they move plus WHY they move that way plus WHAT their movement reveals about their history.

**What I cut**: "the silent, flat-footed precision of someone who had spent several lifetimes navigating uncertain terrain" + the full tactical assessment list (exits, sight lines, structural vulnerabilities).

**Rule**: Give the movement ONE concrete detail. Let the character's dialogue and actions reveal their history. The reader doesn't need a biography delivered through a description of walking.

**Before** (60 words):
> Sylvanas descended the steps behind her with the silent, flat-footed precision of someone who had spent several lifetimes navigating uncertain terrain. Her crimson eyes swept the space in the automatic triangulation of a tactician assessing a room: exits, sight lines, structural vulnerabilities. The grotto had one entrance, no windows, and walls too smooth to climb. She catalogued this and moved on to the contents.

**After** (28 words):
> Sylvanas descended behind her, flat-footed and silent. Her crimson eyes swept the grotto once: one entrance, no windows, smooth walls. She catalogued this and moved on to the contents.

## 3. Attribution Over-Explanation (Hitlist #12)

**Pattern**: After stating what a character did/felt/looked like, adding a sentence that explains WHY it's significant. The narrator interprets the detail for the reader instead of trusting it to land.

**What I cut**:
- "Sylvanas's expression hadn't changed — it rarely did, that narrow, appraising calm she wore like plate armor —" (explaining that the raised eyebrow is notable BECAUSE her expression rarely changes)
- "a tell she couldn't control, one that Jaina had learned years ago correlated with elevated physical arousal" (explaining what glowing eyes mean)
- "with the grim satisfaction of someone who had been trying to describe this for twenty minutes and had finally been vindicated" (explaining Hasera's tone)
- "the voice of someone who had made hard calls in hard places and knew that deliberation had to end in decision" (explaining Pela's firmness)

**Rule**: State the observation. Stop. If "she sounded surprised" doesn't land alone, the observation isn't strong enough — rewrite the observation, don't add a footnote. The reader is smart. They know that raised eyebrows, glowing eyes, and flat tones carry weight. You don't need to annotate them.
