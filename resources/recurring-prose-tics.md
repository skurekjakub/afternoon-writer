# Recurring Prose Tics

Patterns that repeat across chapters. Unlike the items in `slop-hitlist.md`, these are not banned — they are legitimate prose moves that become tics through overuse. The detection test is **frequency within a chapter and across recent chapters**, not presence.

## Detection Procedure

For each tic below: count occurrences in the current chapter. Compare against the hard cap. If the count exceeds the cap, KILL the excess instances, starting with the weakest (most generic, least character-specific). The first N occurrences up to the cap survive as KEEPs.

---

## Tic 1: Corner-of-Mouth Micro-Expressions

**Pattern**: Any beat where a smile, reaction, or emotion is routed through "the corner(s) of [someone's] mouth" — twitching, tightening, lifting, curling, pulling, shifting.

**Why it's a tic**: It's a valid physical beat, but AI prose defaults to it instead of choosing from the full range of facial and body reactions. When every near-smile is a corner-twitch, the prose develops a monotone.

**Hard cap**: 1 per chapter, 2 per 3 consecutive chapters.

**What it looks like:**
- "One corner of her mouth twitched."
- "His mouth pulled at one corner."
- "The corners of his mouth tightened."
- "One corner of her mouth moved. Almost a smile."

**Alternatives**: Let the character actually smile. Use jaw, eyes, posture, hands, breath, or the line of dialogue itself to carry the reaction. A full smile with a qualifier ("smiled once, quickly") beats another corner-twitch.

---

## Tic 2: Overloaded "Marked"

**Pattern**: The verb "marked" used as a general-purpose word for noticed, observed, recorded, literally marked, branded, or left a sign. The word is versatile, which makes it a magnet for AI prose that reaches for the same tool repeatedly.

**Why it's a tic**: When "marked" appears 4+ times in a single chapter, it stops meaning anything specific. The reader loses track of whether someone is literally marking something (chalk, ink, a scar) or mentally noting it.

**Hard cap**: 2 per chapter. Prefer keeping literal/physical uses (ink stains, chalk marks, physical tracks) over metaphorical uses (she marked the route = she noticed the route).

**What it looks like:**
- "Ink marked two fingers of her left hand." (physical — keep)
- "She marked the feeder road with the point of her knife." (physical — keep)
- "She had marked this poorly." (mental — weaker, cut first)
- "Fresh hoofcuts marked the verge." (physical — keep)
- "The room was a marked point on someone else's map." (metaphorical — weakest)

**Alternatives**: For mental uses — noticed, caught, logged, read, clocked, filed. For physical uses — stained, scored, cut, scratched, tagged.

---

## Tic 3: Tightened/Eased as Emotional Shorthand

**Pattern**: Body parts "tightening" or "easing" as the default physical correlate for emotional tension or relief — jaw tightened, stomach tightened, grip tightened, face eased, knot eased, line eased.

**Why it's a tic**: Tightened and eased are legitimate body verbs, but AI prose uses them as the only two states on the emotional dial. Every tension beat is a tightening; every relief beat is an easing. The prose becomes a binary pump.

**Hard cap**: 3 "tightened" per chapter, 2 "eased" per chapter. At least half must be physical (grip, leather, rope, masonry) rather than emotional (jaw, stomach, chest, face).

**What it looks like:**
- "Calculation tightened his mouth." (emotional)
- "The corners of his mouth tightened." (emotional — also Tic 1)
- "Her jaw tightened, then eased." (emotional double tic — tightened AND eased in one beat)
- "Her stomach tightened." (emotional)
- "She tightened her grip on the ledger." (physical — stronger, keep)
- "The knot under her ribs eased." (emotional)

**Alternatives for tightened**: clenched, set, locked, went rigid, pulled taut, hardened, stiffened. For eased: loosened, softened, dropped, released, let go, unclenched.

## Tic 4: Mouth Shifted / Expression Shifted

**Pattern**: Using "shifted" as the default verb for any facial micro-expression change — "mouth shifted," "expression shifted," "something shifted in her face."

**Why it's a tic**: "Shifted" is maximally vague. It tells the reader something changed without specifying what changed or how. Combined with Tic 1, it creates a pattern where every reaction is a vague facial micro-movement.

**Hard cap**: 1 "shifted" for facial/expression use per chapter. Physical "shifted" (weight, position, grip, stance) does not count against this cap.

**What it looks like:**
- "Her mouth shifted."
- "The corner of his mouth shifted."
- "Something shifted behind her eyes."

**Alternatives**: Name the actual expression. "Her mouth thinned." "A smile broke through." "Her jaw set." "Amusement flickered and died." Or better: cut the beat and let the dialogue carry it.

---

## Tic 5: Eye/Gaze Repertoire Exhaustion

**Pattern**: Any beat where emotion, reaction, or connection is routed through eyes, gaze, or looking — widening, narrowing, darkening, softening, dropping, lingering, searching, meeting, holding.

**Why it's a tic**: Eyes are the default emotional channel in AI prose. Every surprise widens eyes, every suspicion narrows them, every arousal darkens them, every shame drops them. When 4+ eye/gaze beats appear on a single page, the characters' bodies vanish and every scene becomes a staring contest.

**Hard cap**: 4 eye/gaze beats per chapter. Prefer character-specific uses (a sniper's trained scanning habit, a spy who avoids eye contact) over generic emotional gaze beats.

**What it looks like:**
- "She held his gaze." (generic connection beat)
- "His eyes widened." (generic surprise)
- "Her gaze dropped to his hands." (generic discomfort)
- "Something shifted behind her eyes." (also Tic 4 — double tic)
- "Their eyes met across the room." (generic romance beat)
- "She searched his face." (generic assessment)
- "His gaze lingered on her mouth." (generic attraction)

**Alternatives**: Let the character's hands, posture, breath, stillness, or speech pattern carry the reaction. "She went still" beats "her eyes widened" for surprise. A character who turns away or picks up an object says more about discomfort than a dropped gaze. For connection moments, action together (walking in step, handing something over, matching rhythm) beats mutual staring.

## Tic 6: Weight/Heaviness as Emotional Metaphor

**Pattern**: Any metaphor where negative emotion is expressed through weight, heaviness, settling, pressing down, bearing down, or gravity.

**Why it's a tic**: "The weight of it settled over her" is the model's default vehicle for grief, guilt, dread, responsibility, and realization. Every heavy emotion gets the gravity treatment. The metaphor works once per chapter. At 3+, the prose develops a lead blanket.

**Hard cap**: 2 weight/heaviness metaphors per chapter. Keep the most specific or physically grounded instance; cut the abstract ones first.

**What it looks like:**
- "The weight of it settled over her." (abstract — cut first)
- "Heaviness in her chest." (body-located but generic)
- "Grief bore down on her." (abstract agent + gravity)
- "The realization pressed down on him." (also intent smear — double tic)
- "The burden of command." (abstraction as weight)
- "Something heavy sat behind her ribs." (body-located — keep if only instance)

**Alternatives**: Name the specific sensation. A tight throat. Nausea. Numbness spreading through the fingers. The inability to move. A sudden need to sit down. The physical reality of the emotion is more precise than the gravity metaphor.

## Tic 7: Dialogue Tag Avoidance of "Said"

**Pattern**: Dialogue tags other than "said" / "asked" used at high frequency — murmured, muttered, whispered, breathed, stated, offered, admitted, countered, conceded, mused, noted, observed, replied, responded, continued, added, began.

**Why it's a tic**: Modern fiction craft consensus is ~90% of dialogue tags should be "said" or no tag at all. "Said" is invisible to the reader. "Murmured," "muttered," and their cousins perform — they draw attention to the tag instead of the dialogue. AI prose inverts the ratio, cycling through fancy tags to avoid repeating "said," which creates a different kind of monotony: every line of dialogue comes with a tiny stage direction.

**Hard cap**: 3 non-"said" dialogue tags per chapter. Tags that convey genuinely different volume or mode — "shouted," "called," "yelled," "whispered" (when actual whispering matters to the scene) — do not count against this cap. The cap targets performance tags: murmured, muttered, breathed, mused, offered, admitted, countered, conceded, observed, noted, stated.

**What it looks like:**
- `"Come here," she murmured.` → `"Come here," she said.` Or cut the tag: `"Come here."`
- `"I don't think so," he muttered.` → `"I don't think so."` The dialogue carries the tone.
- `"Look," she breathed.` → `"Look." She was still catching her breath.`
- `"That's interesting," he mused.` → `"That's interesting."` Musing is shown by what follows.
- `"Fine," she conceded.` → `"Fine."` The word IS the concession.

**Alternatives**: Use "said." Use no tag at all — in two-person dialogue the reader tracks who's speaking. Use action beats instead of tags: `She set down the glass. "I don't think so."` The action carries the emotional color the fancy tag was trying to provide.

## Self-Check

After running detection:

1. Did you count ALL instances of each tic word/pattern, or did you stop after finding the first few?
2. For each KILL, is the instance genuinely the weakest (most generic) use, or are you killing a character-specific beat that happens to use the tic word?
3. Are you distinguishing physical from emotional uses of tightened/eased/marked?
4. Did a prior editor (slophunter) INTRODUCE any of these tics as replacements for other patterns? If so, those introduced instances are highest priority kills.
5. Are eye/gaze beats clustering in dialogue scenes? Dialogue-heavy scenes are the highest-risk zone for Tic 5.
6. Did you count weight/heaviness metaphors across both narration and interiority? They hide in inner monologue.
