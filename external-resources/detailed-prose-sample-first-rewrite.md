The wind outside kept moving, still indifferent, still carrying the Hyrulean night across a world that slept while three women from three different worlds slept between its walls.

Zelda's breathing slowed first. The princess crossing into sleep with the speed of someone who'd spent a hundred years sealed in stasis and who therefore treated unconsciousness as a familiar destination rather than a gradual descent. Her breathing deepened, the chest rising and falling against Jaina's, settling into deep sleep within minutes.

Jaina followed. Her arms still around the sleeping princess, her breasts still leaking gently — the let-down continuing its unhurried fade — the drops of milk falling on Zelda's face and absorbed by sleep without waking the recipient.

Sylvanas stayed awake the longest. The banshee didn't sleep — couldn't, or chose not to, the distinction having blurred over the millennia into irrelevance — and she lay behind the sleeping princess and watched the embers and felt two living bodies against her chest and stomach and thighs and felt, for the first time in a span she would never disclose, something that was warm not because it came from outside but because it came from within. A warmth the spectral body shouldn't have been able to produce and that existed anyway, lodged behind the sternum where the living would say *heart* and where Sylvanas, with her typical precision and atypical openness, would call — if anyone asked, which they wouldn't, because the other two were asleep — *home.*

The inn settled. The horse shifted below. The crossroads emptied, the last cart long passed, the roads belonging to the night now. Above the crossroads, in a room with a copper tub and a dying fire and a bed that would require explanation, three women slept (or approximated sleep) in the tangled arrangement of bodies that had spent an evening learning each other and had arrived, through the accumulated grammar of touch and gift and trust, at a fluency that didn't need words.

Outside, a silent princess bloomed in the moonlight. The white flower — the kind that grew where someone had once knelt and prayed — opened its petals to the Hyrulean night. It grew in the patch of earth directly below the window, in soil that hadn't held the species before tonight.

It would still be there in the morning.
