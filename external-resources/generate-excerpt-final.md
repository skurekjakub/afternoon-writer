# The Secret

The inn room smelled like old wood and someone else's soap. Two beds, pushed close, the space between them narrow enough to reach across without stretching. Tifa's split knuckles stung when she dropped her bag.

One door, one window. Rust had welded the latch to its track, and a spider had built something ambitious in the lower corner of the shutters. She cataloged the room in three seconds — habit. Both mattresses sagged toward the center, surrendered to years of gravity and indifference.

Aerith moved past her and claimed the wall-side bed. Didn't ask, didn't look at the other one. She set her bag between herself and the door and sat on the edge with her knees together.

"Cozy," Aerith said.

Smaller than the innkeeper had promised. Closer.

"That's one word for it." Tifa dropped onto the remaining bed and the springs shrieked. Both of them went still for half a second. Her mattress dipped under the weight, pulling toward the center.

"I think the last guest was bigger than both of us combined." Aerith tucked a strand of still-damp hair behind her ear. Dust and road-sweat had been replaced by the inn's cheap soap, something floral that tried too hard.

"At least they cleaned the sheets." Tifa tested the mattress with her thumb. It didn't bounce back. "Probably."

"That's reassuring."

"You asked for one room."

Something shifted behind Aerith's eyes — not quite a flinch, not quite a smile. "Two beds. That's practically luxury."

"Sure." Tifa unzipped her bag. "Luxury."

A Kalm Fang had caught Aerith across the ribs on the road. Jaws snapping in, Aerith twisting just fast enough that the bite became a rake instead of a puncture. Just barely.

"Let me see."

"It's fine, Tifa."

"You twisted out of a fang strike. That's not a scrape."

Aerith lifted the hem of her shirt without further argument. Up past the navel, past the lower ribs where mottled purple was already deepening into something ugly. Two ribs involved, maybe three, the bruise darkening at the center where the longest tooth had dragged hardest.

Tifa crouched and set her thumbs against the edges of the discoloration, mapping the spread. Aerith sucked a breath through her teeth when Tifa's thumb found the darkest edge.

"That one?"

"That one."

Two fingers walked the line of the lower rib, checking for give. Nothing shifted. Bone held firm.

Calloused thumbs traced where purple became pale, and the skin beneath them carried a heat that had nothing to do with the bruise. A pulse ticked against her thumb through the thin skin over the rib. Aerith's stomach tightened beneath her palm, muscles drawing taut.

Her hands stayed. One beat past useful. Two.

She pulled back. "You'll live."

Aerith let the shirt drop. Fabric settled against her hips. "You always this gentle?"

"No." Tifa flexed her hands once, and the split skin bit. "Usually I'm worse."

"I believe you." Aerith's voice was light. Her eyes were not.

---

Aerith changed facing the corner. A practiced turn — shoulder to the room, three-quarters away, too deliberate to be casual. She pulled her shirt overhead in one efficient motion, and Tifa's gaze landed on the ridge of her spine before discipline could intervene.

Each vertebra shifted under skin as Aerith reached for the sleeping shirt. Shoulder blades drawing together and apart, the back of a woman who had never been hit hard enough to compensate. A strip of paler skin above the waistband where the hem had ridden up, the hip curving into a line her eye traced to its end.

Sleeping shirt dropped over it, and the view closed like a curtain.

Tifa looked at her hands and realized she hadn't bandaged them, hadn't opened the kit. Pain arrived after the looking, breaking the gaze where discipline hadn't.

She pulled the first-aid kit from her bag. Tore tape with her teeth. Antiseptic wipe on the left hand first: the split ran along the second and third knuckles where the Kalm Fang's skull had met bone. She wound the wrapping tight, flexed, and it held.

"You always bite the tape like that?"

"Works."

"There are scissors in the kit."

"Slower."

Right hand, easier. Shallow damage, just the skin torn. Wrap, flex, bite the end free. She sat on the edge of the bed with both fists bandaged, opening and closing them. White strips catching the lamplight.

Adrenaline from the road fight hadn't settled. The fear-spike had burned off an hour ago, but the residue stayed — body still braced, muscles still expecting something with teeth. Usually it faded over dinner, over a drink. Something to convince the body the danger was done.

It wasn't fading. It was rerouting. Her hands still held the shape of Aerith's ribs, the heat mapped into her calluses.

She adjusted the wrapping. Looked at the ceiling. Safer than the corner where Aerith had just finished changing.

---

Aerith went to wash up. Bathroom door closed, and Tifa got up to retrieve her water bottle — the route passing the bathroom, unavoidable in a room this size.

Frosted glass. Honey-colored light behind it.

She should have kept walking.

Aerith's silhouette through the glass — in the shoulders, gathering her hair to one side. Tifa's stride didn't break. Eyes tracking the outline through obstruction, a body read through smoke or rain or frosted blur.

Something at hip level that the glass wouldn't resolve. Proportions her eye flagged before the rest of her caught up, the lower body carrying a form outside every template she kept filed.

She stopped walking. Three heartbeats of staring before she forced herself through those four steps to the bed, sat on the mattress, unzipped the bag, and looked into it at nothing.

Bag open. Hand on the zipper pull. She hadn't retrieved the bottle or anything else.

Behind her eyes the image replayed — shoulders, gathered hair, the form at the hip. She ran it the way she replayed fight footage, searching for the tell, the break.

The glass gave outlines, not answers. The form could have been — she stopped the thought there.

She pulled the water bottle out. Drank. Lukewarm, tasting of the road.

"Bathroom's free." Aerith emerged with damp ends darkening the collar of her sleeping shirt.

"Thanks." Tifa screwed the cap on.

"Hot water's almost gone. I'd hurry."

"In a minute."

Aerith tilted her head and shrugged. She crossed to the wall-side bed and pulled back the covers.

---

Common room, half-empty. Three travelers nursing drinks at the bar, an older couple by the fire, a man asleep in his stew. The innkeeper brought bread, a pot of something that qualified as stew on a technicality, and a carafe of red wine that Aerith poured before Tifa could object.

"To not dying." Aerith raised her glass.

"Low bar."

"Low bar's better than no bar. We fought actual wolves today, Tifa."

"Fangs."

"Wolves with worse teeth and no manners. My point stands." She drank and set the glass down with both hands wrapped around the stem, lamplight catching the damp ends of her hair. "You were incredible out there, by the way. The one you hit sideways — I didn't know things could fly that far."

"It was coming for you."

"I noticed." Aerith's mouth curved. "My knight in leather gloves."

"Don't."

"Leather. Gloves." She mimed pulling them on, grinning. "Very gallant."

Tifa tore bread and focused on chewing.

"Did you see that flower on the ridge? The blue one with the thorns."

"I was busy."

"Busy punching things." Aerith swirled the wine. "It was a late-blooming campion. Not supposed to flower past midsummer. Means the soil's still holding heat deeper down — winter won't come as early as everyone thinks."

"Good to know."

"You don't care about the flower."

"I care about winter."

Aerith studied her over the rim of her glass, something calculated in it, something fond. "Fair enough."

Stew held root vegetables, unidentifiable meat, and enough salt to preserve a body. Wine thin. Tifa listened while Aerith moved from the flower to the light on the river at the town's edge, to a grassland bird she couldn't identify. Attention split between Aerith's words and Aerith's hands, between the story and the face telling it — eyebrows climbing, mouth shaping exaggeration, chin tilting for emphasis.

Under the table, Aerith's knee bumped hers. The round cap of bone against the outside of Tifa's thigh, pressure held a beat past accidental. Voltage up her leg and settling behind her navel, amplified by everything since they'd walked through the door — skin under her thumbs and an outline she couldn't stop replaying.

Aerith recrossed her legs. Quick, practiced.

"You're staring," Aerith said.

"Thinking."

"About what?"

"Whether the innkeeper waters this down or if it just comes like that."

"Liar." Aerith smiled into her glass. "You're not thinking about the wine."

"You don't know that."

"I know that look."

Tifa set her glass down. "What look?"

Aerith opened her mouth. Closed it. Reached for the carafe instead. "Never mind."

The word landed heavier than it should have. *Never mind* — Aerith's version of a door closing. Tifa had heard it twice today and was already learning to count.

---

Smaller at night, the room. Shadows from the door-side lamp stretched long, and the distance between beds had either shrunk or the mattresses had sagged further toward each other.

Aerith sat on the wall-side bed in the oversized sleeping shirt, knees drawn to her chest, a book open across them. Eyes not tracking the page. Core protected, lap shielded, body arranged around itself like a guard stance held so long it had become resting position.

Tifa pulled off her boots. Lined them by the nightstand, toes at the door. She'd stopped apologizing for that years ago.

"What are you reading?"

Aerith turned a page she hadn't been reading. "Local folklore. Myths about the grasslands."

"Any good?"

"Terrible." A pause. "The Kalm Fangs are supposed to be extinct, according to this."

"Tell them that."

Aerith almost smiled. Closed the book with her thumb marking nothing.

Wall bed. Corner changing. The outline through the glass. Knee bump. Leg-cross. Drawn-up knees.

Separately: nothing. Wall bed meant she liked walls at her back. Corner changing was modesty. A knee was a knee. Drawn knees, a woman reading after a long day.

Together they formed something she could almost see — a constellation resolving from scattered points.

"Goodnight, Tifa." Aerith turned toward the wall.

"Night."

Tifa reached for the lamp. Darkness came as relief. It meant she couldn't watch anymore, which was the only thing that was going to stop her.

Sleep didn't come.

She lay flat on her back and listened to Aerith breathe. Even rhythm, measured, unhurried, the soft hush of air through an open mouth. The breathing of someone genuinely asleep or someone practiced at pretending. The mattress pulled at her spine, dragging toward the center.

She needed water. Finished the bottle at dinner. That was the reason she got up, the only reason, and she held onto it like rope in a current.

Silver light through thin curtains cut the room into bright and dark. Bars of it across both beds, across the floorboards, across Aerith's body.

Aerith had shifted in sleep. Drawn-up knees gone, legs unfolded. Sleeping shirt ridden above the hip, hem bunched at the waist. Every careful positioning — wall bed, corner turn, drawn knees — dissolved into the unguarded sprawl of sleep.

Tifa saw it.

Under the shirt hem, where fabric curled against skin: a shape. The silver light gave it dimension that frosted glass hadn't. A shadow with edges, the silhouette turned specific — something beneath the cotton that waking Aerith kept hidden behind every deliberate gesture since they'd walked through the door.

She couldn't confirm it. The light was silver, not bright, and fabric covered more than it revealed. Present enough to register, ambiguous enough that her mind couldn't close the case.

Three heartbeats standing over Aerith's bed, weight on the balls of her feet, breathing gone shallow. Water forgotten, the reason burned away like paper.

She went to the basin. Poured a glass from the pitcher. Drank it, poured another, drank that. Reflection in the dark window: a woman with bandaged fists and an expression she didn't want to identify.

Back in bed. Ceiling crack from the lamp hook to the far corner. She traced it with her eyes, completely and uselessly, a study in plaster substituting for the thing she actually wanted to examine.

She ran the day in sequence. The outline through the glass, the moonlit shape under the hem, the knee that lingered, the rehearsed leg-cross, the wall bed, the corner turn, the drawn knees. Searching for the tell, the break, the moment the pattern cracked open.

She'd been reading bodies her entire adult life. You watched how people carried weight, where they compensated. You built a model and updated it.

Aerith's model had a flaw. Something that didn't match the template, lodged in her skull like a bone out of joint.

She could ask. In the morning, over breakfast. She could find the words — and the thought collapsed, because there were no words that wouldn't sound insane. *I watched you through the glass. I stood over your bed. I've been cataloging your body since we walked through this door and something doesn't fit.*

Those sentences didn't exist in any language she wanted to speak.

She could wait. Let data accumulate until the pattern resolved on its own.

Hands aching under the bandages. Ribs sore where she'd twisted mid-swing to take a fang's weight on muscle instead of bone. The combat buzz long gone, replaced by something quieter: the itch of incomplete data and the restlessness of lying three feet from a mystery she'd already decided to study.

Across the distance, Aerith breathed. Moonlight striped her body in silver intervals, and Tifa watched the bars of light rise and fall, and she hated herself for the decision before she'd finished making it.

Sleep wasn't coming. She lay with bandaged hands on her stomach, eyes on the ceiling crack, and let the observations circle — wall bed, corner, knee, the shape under the hem — until the circling became a kind of rest.

This watching was different. It aimed inward. At her own hands holding the memory of ribs. At three heartbeats over a sleeping woman, searching for something she couldn't name.

Post-combat jitters. They'd settle by morning.

She flexed her hands. The wrapping held.

Outside, the road stretched east toward the grasslands, and somewhere in the dark the Kalm Fangs hunted in pairs, and the world was full of things that made sense. Tomorrow they'd walk back into that.

She closed her eyes. Behind them: the shape under the shirt hem, moonlit and half-resolved. Something she couldn't confirm and couldn't forget.

Tomorrow.
