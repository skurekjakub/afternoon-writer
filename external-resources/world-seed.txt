The world is encircled by a phenomenon called the Veil — a silver membrane stretched across the upper atmosphere, said to be the skin of a god who was flayed and pinned there after being defeated in the First Age. It dims starlight. It muffles magic. And it is tearing.

The Two Leads
Genreal — disgraced commander of the Ironmark Legion, stripped of rank after the Siege of Brennach where she made a battlefield call that saved eight hundred soldiers and killed three hundred civilians. She wasn't wrong. She was inconvenient. She now lives in internal exile in a border garrison town, drinking too much and fixing walls that don't need fixing. She is meticulous, ruthless in a crisis, and has not asked for help once in her entire life. Her magic is suppressed by a Loyalty Brand burned into her collarbone — standard punishment for disgraced officers. She doesn't know the brand is slowly killing her.
Fae protagonist — a fae spy of the Lách (the fae intelligence network whose name translates loosely as "the water that finds every crack"). She was sent to kill Sable specifically because Sable is the only living person who knows the precise location of the Godlock — the mechanism that keeps the imprisoned deity sealed. Iseir is brilliant, precise, and has successfully completed forty-three prior assignments. She is also terrified of the mission, though she would sooner step into the Veil than admit it. She has never killed someone famous before. She has never killed someone who haunts her dreams before arriving, either.

The Antagonist: The God Beneath
Known in texts as Vrath-Usal, the Unfinished One. Not a god of death or war — something older and stranger: the god of unmade things, of potential that was never permitted to exist. They were sealed away not because they were evil but because their nature was incompatible with a finished world — everything they touched became something else. Became possibility. Became chaos.
Their cultists believe this is liberation. Their enemies know it means unraveling.
Vrath-Usal is waking because the Veil is tearing. The Veil is tearing because someone has been feeding it a slow poison for forty years. That someone is powerful, patient, and currently holds a seat on the Imperial Council.

The Tension Engine
The story's central trap: Iseir arrives to kill Sable — and discovers that Sable is the only person who can navigate to the Godlock, because it's not on any map. It's tattooed on the inside of Sable's memory in the form of a route she walked once, blindfolded and half-drunk, being led there by a dying mentor. She doesn't know she knows it. She only knows it when she moves.
Iseir's kill order has a binding clause — fae contracts enforced by the Thornless Court. She cannot return home without completing it. But if Sable dies, the Godlock dies with her.
They are, in the most literal sense, each other's only option.

The Loyalty Brand (Ticking Clock)
The brand suppressing Sable's magic was designed to dissolve naturally within a year of her exile — the empire's way of saying you're not a threat anymore. But someone has been refreshing it. Every few months a courier passes through Greylock. Every few months the brand burns fresh. The magic suppression has been running six years instead of one. Without her abilities, Sable's body is slowly consuming itself to compensate — she's been getting headaches, losing feeling in her left hand. She thinks it's old battle injuries. It is not.
Iseir figures this out before Sable does. It is the first crack.

Tonal Notes
The world should feel twilight-era — not a dying empire in rubble, but one whose confidence is thinning, like paper held to the light. The fae are not whimsical; they are bureaucratic and cold, a civilization that has survived by being extremely organized about cruelty. The god beneath doesn't speak in prophecy — it bleeds through as wrongness: animals born without instincts, rivers that suddenly stop remembering their banks.
The romance is built on antagonistic competence — each woman is deeply good at what she does, and half the slow-burn is the grudging recognition of that. Sable doesn't trust easily. Iseir doesn't trust at all. Trust, when it comes, should feel like a structural failure — something giving way that they both pretended was load-bearing.
