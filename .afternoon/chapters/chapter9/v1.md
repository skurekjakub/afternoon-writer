# Chapter 9: The Trap

Eastweald had not gone wild.

That was the first wrong thing about it.

Wild country made its own decisions. It let hedges overtake walls, let tracks soften back into field, let carts rot where axles broke. The land north of Andorhal had gone the other way. Roads were kept clear. Ditches had been cut back. Dead orchards stood in disciplined rows with their lower limbs trimmed, sight lines opened through bark the color of old bone. Even the empty stretches looked tended.

Sylvanas rode at the head of the little formation and let her mare pick the firmer ground where wheel-ruts had dried hard. Horse sweat steamed faintly under the cold. Pale fungus had climbed the ditch stones in a crust that caught the morning light and made the roadside look sugared from a distance. Up close it smelled like cellars left shut too long.

Cyndia ranged left, where a broken wall ran beside the road and vanished into an orchard gone black at the crown. Lyriel held the right the way she always did, a little ahead when the ground opened, dropping back when the road narrowed. Jaina rode between them with her hood down despite the cold, fingers bare against the polished wood of her staff. The staff's crystal had been dark for an hour. That meant nothing. It never meant nothing for long.

Weeks on the widened hunt had burned the explanations out of them. Cyndia reported what she saw. Lyriel reported what had changed. Jaina said left, or down, or there, and Sylvanas shifted the line without wasting breath on agreement. They had become quick in the way only repetition bought.

At the next fork Cyndia lifted two fingers without looking back. Wagon scoring on the left branch, shallow and recent. Lyriel swung wide to cover the open field while Jaina leaned from the saddle, touched mud gone pale at the edge of the rut, and said, "Grain weight. Two days, perhaps three." Sylvanas sent them left with a turn of the wrist, and the formation folded through the bend so cleanly the horses scarcely had to break stride.

Lyriel glanced toward a chapel ruin half-swallowed by its own nettles and said, "No birds."

"None since dawn," Cyndia answered from the other side of the road.

Jaina did not look up from the field stone she had been studying with the edge of her attention. "Not here."

That was enough to keep Sylvanas from liking the ruin behind them. She touched two fingers to her bowstring, then let the hand fall back to the reins.

The road bent north-west around another spread of abandoned trees. Beyond them lay a farmhouse with one side caved in, two outbuildings, and a yard that had been cleaned too recently to belong to weather. The churn was old. The order wasn't.

Sylvanas saw Jaina go still before the human lifted her head.

The mare took three more steps. Jaina's hand tightened on the staff.

"Stop," she said.

Not loud. It did not need to be.

The whole line halted around it. Cyndia checked left. Lyriel turned in the saddle to take the rear. Sylvanas looked at the house.

From a distance it was only one more piece of country failing badly. Up close it had the tidy emptiness of a place abandoned by people who expected to come back and then chose not to.

"What kind?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina took a breath through her nose, short and sharp. "Recent. Not spill, not drift. Worked."

Sylvanas watched the broken roofline, the yard gate off one hinge, the dead apple trees beyond the well. Nothing moved.

"How recent?"

"Hours, maybe. Less if the cold hadn't slowed the bleed."

Lyriel spat into the weeds. "Warm site in open country. Convenient."

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

Jaina was already looking at the house with the particular stillness she got when the pattern underneath a place had teeth. "This isn't passive contamination. It's mass work. I can feel the shape of it even from here."

That earned Sylvanas's full attention.

Mass work. Not tainted bread. Not one more dead hamlet waiting to tip. Something closer to the hand on the lever.

She let her gaze move once around the property. The orchard to the north. The low wall to the east. Wagon tracks in the yard, blurred but not washed out. The half-collapsed lean-to on the west side where a patient man could hide three archers and a box of trouble.

"We take it," she said. "Cyndia, left. Lyriel, right and rear. No heroics with the buildings. Jaina with me."

Jaina's eyes were still on the house. "If this is what I think it is—"

"Then you get your look," Sylvanas said. "Fast."

Jaina nodded once. No argument. That, more than the words, told Sylvanas how valuable the read might be.

They dismounted in the yard. The horses hated the place at once. Lyriel caught two sets of reins before the panic could travel through the others. Cyndia led them back to the road and tied them under the lee of the wall where they could still see the line.

Sylvanas crossed the yard with her bow in hand and the cold air settling under the collar of her leathers. The well bucket had split down one seam. Amber grit lay in the cracks of the stone rim. Reagent. Cheaply handled. Recent enough that the rain had not yet found it.

Inside, the house smelled of wet plaster, old straw, lamp smoke, and the metallic sweetness of magic worked too often in too small a room.

The kitchen floor had been scored in circles and cross-cuts deep enough to hold powder. Jaina crouched at the threshold and put two fingers to the nearest line without touching it.

"Binding sequence," she said. "Part of one. No— the retaining shape. The part meant to survive handling."

Sylvanas let her speak and kept looking.

Shelves stripped. Crockery broken on purpose. A side table blackened where something hot had sat on it for too long. Three cloudy bottles. One intact. One shattered under a chair. One stoppered with wax impressed by a seal that had smeared before it cooled.

Jaina moved deeper, careful as a woman crossing river ice.

"Door," Sylvanas said.

Jaina stopped. Looked down at the doorway, then swore softly. "You were right."

"Usually."

The threshold was another line of the same work, faint under soot. Jaina knelt, peered, and made a face. "Alarm collar without the collar. Not for local use. For anyone trying to read in from outside."

Sylvanas leaned one shoulder to the doorframe and watched the yard through the broken wall where she still had part of the orchard in sight. "Can you kill it?"

"Already did."

"Good."

Jaina moved into the center of the room and went lower, one knee to the flagstone. The staff laid across her thighs. Her free hand hovered over the pattern's heart.

"This is better than Andorhal," she said, and there was the hunger she mistrusted more than fear. "Not larger. Cleaner. They stripped the site, but they left enough of the matrix that I can read the sequence underneath the carrier work."

Sylvanas took up a position where she could see the yard, the back room, and half the kitchen window. "Read."

Jaina closed her eyes.

The air changed around her without flash or drama. Heat shimmered low over the flagstones while the rest of the room stayed cold. Frost climbed the edge of the broken bottle and stopped there, indecisive. The small hairs at the back of Sylvanas's neck rose.

Minutes in a place like that were counted differently. Not by the clock. By how long a hostile country tolerated your stillness.

Sylvanas counted breaths instead.

At thirty, Cyndia clicked once from outside the left window: clear.

At forty-two, Lyriel answered from the yard wall: clear.

At fifty, Jaina said, "The body isn't only carrying it."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the orchard. "I know."

"No. I mean the body is part of the lock. They aren't only poisoning people. They're teaching the flesh something and waiting for death to finish the sentence."

The orchard stayed empty. Wind moved through the dead upper branches with a dry knocking sound.

"Can you use that?" Sylvanas asked.

"Part of it. Give me another—"

She broke off.

Not because the magic failed.

Because the first crossbow snapped from the orchard.

The bolt struck the inner wall hard enough to punch chalk and plaster across the room. Two more followed, lower. One took the doorframe where Sylvanas had been a heartbeat earlier. She was already moving.

"Out," she said.

Cyndia answered with an arrow from the yard. Lyriel shouted something sharp and ugly to the horses.

Jaina was still half inside the matrix. Sylvanas saw it in the delay—eyes open, body present, mind catching up from wherever she had gone to read the thing.

A second volley hit.

Sylvanas reached Jaina as a man came through the shattered side door with a short sword in one hand and a buckler in the other. Living. Hooded. Fast enough to be trained, not fast enough.

She put an arrow through his throat at six feet, dropped the bow, caught Jaina by the front of her coat, and hauled her clear as the man behind him tried to force the same doorway.

"Move," she said into Jaina's face.

Jaina blinked once, hard, and came back all at once. "I have part of it."

"Wonderful."

The second man hit the frame with his shield and came through low. Sylvanas kicked the chair into his knees and drew her knife on the same motion. He saw the knife, committed to the lunge anyway, and died for it. Behind him, more movement in the yard. The orchard had grown men.

Jaina's staff flashed blue-white. Frost sealed the broken kitchen window in jagged bloom just as a bolt tried to come through it. The wood smoked around the ice.

"Rear room," Sylvanas said.

"There isn't one."

"Then make one."

Lyriel was at the back threshold now, bow half raised, one side of her face powdered with plaster. "Three left, two on the well, one roof."

"Roof first," Sylvanas said.

Cyndia's arrow answered from outside. A body hit shingles, then the ground.

The line held for another three breaths. Then a bolt took the inside of Sylvanas's side just under the ribs, punching through leather, scoring hot across flesh, and knocking the air out of her.

Pain came white and immediate, then narrowed to a hard bright seam. She looked down once, saw blood starting under the torn leather, and ignored it.

Jaina saw it anyway.

"Sylvanas—"

"Later."

That one word cost more breath than she liked.

Jaina's face changed. The panic did not show. The speed did. "I can give you the binding run and the host answer. I lost the trigger."

"Carry the useful half."

"I am."

Good. She sounded angry. Anger moved people faster than fear if they knew what to do with it.

Another bolt hit the frost-blocked window and bounced away. Footsteps on the outer wall. Lyriel loosed through the door and swore satisfaction a second before someone screamed outside.

"Right side clear," Cyndia called.

"For now," Lyriel said.

Sylvanas snatched up the intact bottle from the table, smelled lamp oil through whatever else had been in it, and hurled it into the back corner where the floor was already sagging. Jaina understood at once. Frost bit the glass. It split with a crack that filled the room in reeking fire and smoke.

Not a rear room.

Enough confusion to leave by.

They went through the smoke, not the door. Cyndia met them in the yard with the horses already loose and Lyriel covering the orchard edge.

"North wall," Sylvanas said.

"Low enough to jump," Lyriel said.

"Then jump it."

Jaina caught Sylvanas's elbow before she could put weight on the wrong side. "You'll tear it wider."

"I know."

Her hand was cold and steady. Sylvanas let her have one heartbeat to shove folded cloth hard under the cut and wrap the bandage once around her waist. Not careful. Not gentle. Competent. Jaina's mouth had gone thin with concentration.

"That won't hold if you keep twisting," Jaina said.

"Then I won't."

They cleared the north wall on foot, caught the horses beyond it, and rode out through orchard rows where dead leaves had become black pulp underhoof.

No one spoke for half a mile.

When the trees thinned and the ground opened back to road and field, Sylvanas let the line slow enough to breathe without admitting that was what she was doing.

The bandage had gone wet already. The wound burned in time with the mare's stride. Jaina rode close enough to keep one eye on it and far enough not to make a show of the fact. Cyndia had mud up both sleeves and someone else's blood on one boot. Lyriel kept checking behind them with the alert, impatient tilt that meant she would rather be shooting than leaving.

Sylvanas fished the salvaged scraps out of her coat and passed them back to Jaina without turning. "Tell me we got something worth bleeding for."

Jaina took them, looked once, and breathed out through her nose.

Not relief. Never that easy.

"Yes," she said. "Not enough. Yes."

"Define it."

"The retaining sequence. The part that keeps the treatment alive through storage and baking. And..." She hesitated just long enough to annoy. "Proof the body is part of the answer even before death."

Sylvanas looked at the road ahead. "Still no full waking trigger."

"No."

"But enough to point higher."

"Yes."

There. Honest. Useful. No padding to soften the holes in it.

Jaina shifted in the saddle, unfolded one of the scraps fully, and added, "There was a route notation under the reagent shelf. Not Andorhal district code. Estate code. North-east approach, private stores, limited labor rotation. Big enough to matter."

Cyndia came in from the left, heard the tail end, and held out a hand without asking. Jaina passed the scrap across. Cyndia read faster than most people believed rangers could.

"Walled compound," she said. "Working road, but not a village road. Someone wants traffic in and witnesses out."

Lyriel snorted. "Sounds charming."

The larger compound sat three hours farther on, raised above the road on a long low rise with scrub oak at its shoulders and grain fields gone to stubble around it. Two outbuildings. Main house. Cart shed. No banners. No life visible from the road. Which meant nothing at all.

Sylvanas reined in below the rise and studied the place through a gap in the trees.

The perimeter was better than the farmhouse. No obvious watch. No men showing themselves where frightened men would have. Tracks kept to one lane. Yard clean, but not so clean it admitted fear. Professional ground. The kind meant to be taken at first glance for merely well-run.

Jaina drew up beside her and closed her eyes for half a breath.

Sylvanas didn't wait for the report. She could already see the seam in the patrol logic. A shallow dip along the north side where the rise broke the line of sight from the outbuildings. Too clean, almost. But not impossibly so. The sort of gap a disciplined man left because he trusted the land to do part of the work for him.

Her side hurt. The day had burned more time than she liked. Every delay now had Lyriel's next meal, Cyndia's next watch, Jaina's next spell, her own blood, all priced inside it.

"There," she said. "North side. We move the dip."

Jaina opened her eyes. "Wait."

One word. Not uncertain. Immediate.

Sylvanas disliked it on the spot.

"I need one sweep."

"You have half of one already."

"Not on this ground."

Sylvanas kept looking at the dip. No movement. No glint. No sound but wind in the dead stubble and the soft chink of tack from the horses behind them.

The seam still looked right.

Jaina touched her sleeve. "Sylvanas."

That decided it the wrong way.

She hated being touched like that in the field. It made caution feel like a hand on the reins.

"Cyndia left," she said. "Lyriel right. Jaina with me. Quick and low."

Jaina made a sound of frustration that should have pleased her. It didn't.

They moved on foot through the scrub oak. The ground dipped exactly where she had read it. Good cover. Easy angle into the yard wall. The sort of approach that made lesser scouts proud of themselves.

Halfway down, Jaina stopped so abruptly Sylvanas nearly walked into her.

"No," Jaina said.

Not to Sylvanas. To the ground.

"What's wrong?"

Jaina's gaze had fixed on nothing visible. "The corridor. It isn't empty."

Lyriel was already stepping into it from the right.

There was no flash.

Only a hard bloom of cold light waist-high across the dip and the sound the air made when magic chose violence over warning.

Lyriel vanished.

Not theatrically. Not in fire. One moment a body. The next a burst of white, a smell of ozone over sweet rot, and empty ground where the right flank had been standing.

The shock of absence hit harder than any scream would have.

Jaina's barrier went up a heartbeat later, catching the second pulse of the trap before it reached Sylvanas's chest. Blue-white light crawled over invisible lines in the air and showed the corridor for what it was, a throat lined with wards tuned to the intervals between moving bodies.

Farstrider spacing.

Built for them.

"Back," Jaina snapped.

This time Sylvanas obeyed before the sentence finished.

Cyndia had already dropped to one knee left of the dip, bow up, eyes fixed on the empty place to the right with a ferocity that did nothing at all.

There was nothing to recover. No body. No blood. Only scorched grass and the after-light burning itself out.

Sylvanas grabbed Jaina's shoulder and shoved her back behind the oak line. Another ward fired where Jaina's knee had been.

Then the whole slope woke.

Crossbowmen rose from slit trenches in the field. One on the shed roof. Two more behind the low stone wall. Living again. Always living when it cost them most.

Sylvanas fired on instinct. The man on the shed roof pitched backward without getting his shot away. Cyndia took the left trench. Jaina held the barrier a fraction too low, corrected, and caught the next volley on the second try.

No time for the missing.

That was the ugliest part. The field demanded the living answer first and did not care what it had already taken.

Sylvanas used the oak trunks as cover, moved three paces, loosed, moved again. The wound in her side bit every turn. She kept her face blank and her breathing shallow enough that no one would hear the difference.

Jaina spoke without looking at her. "Three layers. The outer one burns on spacing. The second waits for panic retreat. Don't go uphill blind."

Sylvanas adjusted the line at once. "Cyndia, on my arrow. Jaina, hold left choke. Two steps only when I move."

"Understood."

They broke contact by inches instead of speed. Jaina peeled the second layer open just long enough to let them out of its reach. Sylvanas covered the exit. Cyndia never looked at her when she answered. Her attention kept flicking right, then right again, then forward, body outrunning thought.

By the time they reached the tree line beyond the trap ground, the fight had become too expensive for the people uphill and too pointless for the people down it. No pursuit followed.

Sylvanas put a hand against a trunk and held there until the world stopped narrowing.

Jaina came close, saw the blood starting through the second bandage, and said nothing at all. Good.

Cyndia stood ten paces away scanning the right flank. Looked there again. Then the road ahead. Then right once more.

The second look was worse than any weeping.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

They moved.

After that, every choke point changed shape.

At the first narrow lane through the scrub, Sylvanas stopped without thinking and looked at Jaina.

Jaina saw it. Swept once. Nodded. "Clean."

Only then did Sylvanas take them through.

At the next broken fence line, Cyndia checked right, then right again before stepping over. She did it with such speed the motion might have passed for habit to anyone who had not spent years reading rangers.

Sylvanas read it.

Filed it nowhere. Carried it anyway.

They regrouped at dusk on a ridge overlooking fields silvered by frost and distance haze. The road split below them. One branch bent toward the Barov country and the deeper Eastweald. The other ran south-west in a long curve that would eventually meet the Hearthglen line. Good roads. Bad choices.

Cyndia unsaddled with the hard economy of a woman making sure the horses still existed because looking elsewhere cost too much. Jaina spread the salvaged route scraps and her copied notes across a flat stone. Sylvanas sat because standing made the wound pull and because pain wasted less time when admitted in small practical concessions.

The absence in the formation had weight now. Not grief exactly. Geometry. A shape ruined by subtraction.

Cyndia watered the horses, came back, and without seeming to know she was doing it, looked to the right edge of the ridge where Lyriel would have stood. Then there again. Then to the road below.

Jaina saw the motion too. Her mouth tightened. She bent back over the papers instead of speaking into it. Better.

"What do we have?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina laid one scrap over another. "The farmhouse and the compound use the same retaining family, but the compound's perimeter work is cleaner and much better funded. That means more than one site, more than one labor lane, and someone upstream standardizing the method."

"Barov?" Sylvanas asked.

"Barov roads." Jaina did not look up. "Barov access. Barov storage patterns, probably. Not confession."

"Good. Keep it there."

Cyndia tapped one of the route marks with a stripped fingernail. "Estate approaches. Three of them. Main road, north service lane, and something half-hidden through orchard breaks east of the wall. Old property. Built for people who trusted the land to keep strangers visible."

Useful. Already sorted into motion and ground.

"And the other line?" Sylvanas asked.

Cyndia took her hand away from the paper and stared out toward the dim southern road. "Retreating figure at the trap site."

Jaina looked up sharply. "You saw someone get out?"

"Maybe." Cyndia's voice stayed level by effort. "Tall. Hood up. Too far for a face. Moved before the crossbows did."

"You didn't say that earlier," Sylvanas said.

"We were leaving."

Fair.

"Anything else?"

Cyndia's gaze drifted once to the empty place on the right before she answered. "When he broke the tree line he said something to the men near the lower ditch. Couldn't hear all of it. Last words carried."

Sylvanas waited.

Cyndia said, "'May the stars keep your road.'"

The phrase landed where it always did: nowhere useful and too deep.

The old seller. The road man south of Strahnbrad. Forgettable faces, memorable farewell. Thin file. Weak file. One more page in it.

Not an answer.

Not nothing either.

Jaina had heard the shift in the room without understanding it fully. "You've heard that before."

"Yes."

"Same man?"

"I don't know."

Jaina did not press. Another good choice.

Hooves came up the ridge road from the north at a speed too deliberate for enemies and too tired for couriers trying to impress someone.

Cyndia was on her feet before the second stride. Checked right. Checked right again. Then drew on the approach.

Sylvanas knew the rider before the horse cleared the last rise.

Lor'themar reined in below them on a lathered bay with one broken buckle hanging loose at his shoulder and road grime dulling everything that usually looked clean on him by force of habit. He took in the camp in one sweep—the missing place, the blood on Sylvanas's side, Cyndia standing where two rangers should have stood—and did not ask the wrong question.

"You look terrible," he said.

"You've improved too."

He dismounted stiffly. That hurt to watch more than the words.

"Convocation?" Sylvanas asked.

"Among other delights."

Lor'themar reached inside his coat and produced a folded packet sealed in Silvermoon wax and another in the plain narrow hand of a Dalaran copyist. He handed the second to Jaina and the first to no one. Sylvanas took it off him anyway.

"That's meant to sound official," he said. "The official version is that the Convocation has grave concern about the widening scope of your authority, your absence from the northern defenses, and the number of reports arriving without the kind of proof a comfortable room can read."

Sylvanas broke the seal with her thumb.

"And the real version?"

Lor'themar looked at the blood on her bandage. "The real version is that I've spent three days trying to keep them from recalling you by force of precedent and insult. I bought you this much road by arguing that if they cut your line now, all they accomplish is ignorance with better posture."

Jaina opened her own packet. Her face did not change much. It did not need to.

"Antonidas?" Sylvanas asked.

"Methodology review," Jaina said. "The Council wants the evidence chain formalized before they commit resources at scale."

Lor'themar's laugh had no warmth in it. "Remarkable. We are being buried by two different institutions in exactly the same language."

He looked at the spread papers, the route marks, the split roads below the ridge.

"Tell me what you have before someone older and more decorative than I am decides we should stop having it."

They told him.

Not in speeches. In pieces.

Barov route pressure through Eastweald stores and estate approaches, but no final proof of willing guilt. The farmhouse read, incomplete but real: binding sequence, host response, no clean trigger. The compound trap built for disciplined scouts and tuned to the spaces between them. Hearthglen chatter rising from the south-west line, and with it Arthas—still the one outside authority likely to move before the rest of them had finished reviewing the danger. The repeated farewell again, still weak, still nothing anyone could ride to a commander and spend.

Lor'themar listened the way he always had, weighing each fragment before the next one landed.

When they finished, he said, "You don't have one road anymore."

"No," Sylvanas said.

"And you don't have four women either," he said, still looking at the papers.

That one landed where it belonged.

Jaina folded the Dalaran note once with great care. "If Barov roads are real and still moving, I can get through doors you can't."

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

It cost less to admit it now than it would have yesterday.

Jaina met her eyes over the map. "And if your two institutions are both collapsing into process, Arthas becomes a lever whether we like him or not."

"Also yes."

Lor'themar rubbed a hand over his mouth. "Then stop pretending there's a version of this where you stay one team and chase both before the paper walls close."

Cyndia checked the right flank again. Right. Right. Then the south-west road.

Sylvanas saw it. So did Jaina.

No one named it.

"You take the Barov thread," Sylvanas said to Jaina.

Jaina did not answer at once. Good. That meant she was measuring cost instead of pouncing on agreement.

"Alone," Lor'themar said, because that needed saying by someone.

"Not because she's the spare body," Sylvanas said. "Because she's the right one."

Jaina's chin came up a fraction. Not pride. Acceptance of an assignment correctly drawn.

"Route evidence, estate approaches, the partial read," Sylvanas went on. "You carry all of it. You do not promote Barov beyond what the papers earn. If a door opens because they still think you're only a Kirin Tor problem, use it."

"I know how to walk into hostile houses politely," Jaina said.

"That was never the concern."

Lor'themar almost smiled. "Touching."

Sylvanas ignored him and looked to Cyndia. "You ride with me."

Cyndia nodded once.

No protest. No visible relief. Only that hard stillness that had replaced whatever the day had taken out of her.

"Hearthglen first," Sylvanas said. "Then Arthas if he's still within reach. We need someone with rank, soldiers, and a habit of moving before committees finish introducing themselves."

Jaina's gaze flicked to the bandage at Sylvanas's side. "You need the wound looked at properly before you try to bully a prince."

"He's human. It won't take much."

That won the smallest shift at the corner of Jaina's mouth. There and gone.

Lor'themar set the Convocation packet down on the map and pinned it with two fingers. "You understand what this means if either of you fails?"

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

"I understand what it means if we do nothing," Jaina said.

Better answer.

The light went out of the fields by degrees. Frost thickened on the ridge grass. Cyndia checked the right flank again while she tightened one of the saddle straps. Right. Right. Then south-west where Sylvanas's road ran. The motion had already become its own language.

Jaina wrapped the farmhouse notes in oilcloth, tied them closed, and slid them inside her satchel with the Barov routes and Cyndia's estate sketches. She rose in one smooth movement and slung the strap across her body.

"When do you leave?" Lor'themar asked.

"Now," Sylvanas said.

"Of course."

There was nothing left in the ridge camp worth preserving except the shape of them, and even that had already split.

They saddled in the dark.

Jaina checked Sylvanas's bandage once more with quick, efficient hands and retied it tighter when she found blood under the edge.

"That will hurt," she said.

"Yes."

"Good."

Dry enough to be hers. Sylvanas let it stand.

Then Jaina stepped back, mounted, and turned her horse toward the Eastweald road without looking as though she expected someone to stop her.

Professional. Clean. Wrong in every way that mattered and necessary in all the others.

Sylvanas mounted more slowly than she would have liked. Cyndia was already in place on the left. When she settled in the saddle, Cyndia's eyes went right, then right again to the place where no one rode, then back ahead.

Jaina touched two fingers to the edge of her hood in something too small to be salute and too deliberate to be accident.

Sylvanas answered with the same economy. "Get through the door first."

"Send word before the paper walls close."

"You too."

Lor'themar took Jaina's horse by the rein long enough to turn her cleanly onto the eastern road. He did not touch Sylvanas's.

The roads divided below the ridge.

Jaina took the Barov line alone with the notes, the route evidence, and the incomplete ritual answer wrapped against the cold.

Sylvanas turned south-west with Cyndia and the wound under her ribs and the new price of every bad decision riding in the space where the right flank used to be.

No one looked back.
