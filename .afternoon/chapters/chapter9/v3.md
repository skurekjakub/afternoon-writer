# Chapter 9: The Trap

Eastweald had been kept.

Wild country made its own decisions. Hedges swallowed walls. Tracks softened back into field. Carts rotted where they broke. The land north of Andorhal had gone the other way. Roads stayed clear. Ditches had been cut back. Dead orchards stood in hard rows with their lower limbs trimmed for sightlines.

Sylvanas rode at the head of the formation and let her mare choose the firmer ground where wheel-ruts had dried hard. Horse sweat steamed under the cold. Pale fungus crusted the ditch stones. Up close it smelled like a cellar sealed too long.

Cyndia ranged left along a broken wall that vanished into an orchard gone black at the crown. Lyriel held the right, a little ahead when the ground opened, dropping back when the road narrowed. Jaina rode between them with her hood down, bare fingers on her staff. The crystal had been dark for an hour. That never lasted.

Weeks on the widened hunt had burned the explanations out of them. Cyndia said what she saw. Lyriel said what had changed. Jaina said left, or down, or there, and Sylvanas shifted the line without wasting breath on agreement. Repetition had made them quick.

At the next fork Cyndia lifted two fingers without looking back. Wagon scoring on the left branch, shallow and recent. Lyriel swung wide to cover the open field while Jaina leaned from the saddle, touched the pale mud at the edge of a rut, and said, "Grain weight. Two days, perhaps three." Sylvanas sent them left with a turn of the wrist.

Lyriel glanced toward a chapel ruin half-swallowed by nettles. "No birds."

"None since dawn," Cyndia said from the far side of the road.

Jaina kept her attention on a field stone. "Wrong place."

Sylvanas touched two fingers to her bowstring, then let the hand fall back to the reins.

The road bent north-west around another spread of abandoned trees. Beyond them sat a farmhouse with one side caved in, two outbuildings, and a yard too orderly for weather. The churn was old. The order was fresh.

Jaina went still before she lifted her head.

Jaina's grip tightened on the staff.

"Stop."

Cyndia checked left. Lyriel turned in the saddle to take the rear. Sylvanas studied the house.

From a distance it was one more ruin. Up close it had the tidy emptiness of a place left by people who had expected to return.

"What kind?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina drew a short breath. "Fresh. Deliberate."

Sylvanas watched the broken roofline, the gate hanging from one hinge, the dead apple trees beyond the well. Nothing moved.

"How fresh?"

"Hours, perhaps. Less if the cold had not slowed it."

Lyriel spat into the weeds. "Warm site in open country. Convenient."

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

Jaina's voice went flatter, faster. "This isn't spillover. They worked here in force. I can feel the shape of it from the yard."

That sharpened Sylvanas's attention. She let her gaze move once around the property. Orchard north. Low wall east. Wagon tracks in the yard, blurred but still there. A half-collapsed lean-to on the west side where a patient man could hide trouble.

"We take it," she said. "Cyndia left. Lyriel right and rear. No heroics around the buildings. Jaina with me."

"If this is what I think it is—"

"Then you get your look. Fast."

Jaina nodded. No argument.

They dismounted in the yard. The horses hated the place at once. Lyriel caught two sets of reins before the panic could spread. Cyndia led them back toward the road and tied them under the lee of the wall.

Sylvanas crossed the yard with her bow in hand. Cold air slipped under the collar of her leathers. The well bucket had split down one seam. Amber grit lay in the cracks of the stone rim. Reagent. Cheaply handled.

Inside, the house smelled of wet plaster, old straw, lamp smoke, and the metallic sweetness of magic worked too often in too small a room.

The kitchen floor had been scored in circles and cross-cuts deep enough to hold powder. Jaina crouched at the threshold and put two fingers near the nearest line without touching it.

"Binding work," she said. "Part of a larger shape. This section was built to survive handling."

Sylvanas let her speak and kept searching the room.

Shelves stripped. Crockery broken on purpose. A side table blackened where something hot had sat too long. Three cloudy bottles: one intact, one shattered under a chair, one stoppered with wax and a smeared seal.

Jaina moved deeper, careful as a woman crossing river ice.

"Door," Sylvanas said.

Jaina stopped, looked down, and swore softly. "You were right."

"Usually."

The threshold held another line of the same work, faint under soot. Jaina knelt, peered, and made a face. "Alarm line. Built to bite anyone trying to read from outside."

Sylvanas leaned one shoulder against the frame and watched the yard through the broken wall. "Can you kill it?"

"Already did."

"Good."

Jaina went to one knee in the center of the room, staff across her thighs, free hand hovering over the heart of the pattern.

"This is better than Andorhal," she said. "Smaller, cleaner. They left enough for me to read what lies under the carrying work."

Sylvanas took a position where she could see the yard, the back room, and half the kitchen window. "Read."

Jaina closed her eyes.

The air changed around her without flash. Heat shimmered low over the flagstones while the rest of the room stayed cold. Frost crept up the broken bottle and stopped.

Sylvanas counted breaths.

At thirty, Cyndia clicked once from outside the left window: clear. At forty-two, Lyriel answered from the yard wall: clear.

At fifty, Jaina said, "The body isn't only carrying it."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the orchard. "I know."

"No. The body is part of the lock. They aren't only poisoning people. They're teaching the flesh how to answer, then waiting for death to finish it."

The orchard stayed empty. Wind moved through the dead upper branches with a dry knocking sound.

"Can you use that?" Sylvanas asked.

"Part of it. Give me another—"

She cut off as the first crossbow snapped from the orchard.

The bolt struck the inner wall hard enough to punch chalk and plaster across the room. Two more followed, lower. One took the doorframe where Sylvanas had been a heartbeat earlier. She was already moving.

"Out."

Cyndia answered with an arrow from the yard. Lyriel shouted something sharp and ugly to the horses.

Jaina was still half inside the working. Sylvanas reached her as a man came through the shattered side door with a short sword in one hand and a buckler in the other. Living. Hooded. Fast enough to be trained, not fast enough.

She put an arrow through his throat at six feet, dropped the bow, caught Jaina by the front of the coat, and hauled her clear as the man behind him tried to force the same gap.

"Move."

Jaina blinked once and came back hard. "I have part of it."

The second man hit the frame with his shield and came through low. Sylvanas kicked the chair into his knees and drew her knife in the same motion. He saw the knife, lunged anyway, and died for it. Behind him, movement filled the yard. The orchard had grown men.

Jaina's staff flashed blue-white. Frost sealed the broken kitchen window in a jagged bloom just as a bolt tried to come through it. Wood smoked around the ice.

"Rear room," Sylvanas said.

"There isn't one."

"Then make one."

Lyriel was at the back threshold, bow half raised, one side of her face powdered with plaster. "Three left, two on the well, one roof."

"Roof first."

Cyndia's arrow answered from outside. A body struck shingles, then ground.

The line held for three breaths more. Then a bolt took Sylvanas in the side under the ribs, through leather and across flesh, and drove the air out of her.

Pain came white, then narrowed to a bright hard seam. She checked the blood once and ignored it.

Jaina checked it too.

"Sylvanas—"

"Later."

That one word cost more breath than she liked.

Jaina's face tightened. The panic hid. The speed did not. "I can give you what keeps it alive through storage and baking. I lost the last turn."

"Carry the useful half."

"I am."

Another bolt hit the frozen window and bounced away. Footsteps thudded on the outer wall. Lyriel loosed through the door and swore satisfaction a breath before someone screamed outside.

"Right side clear," Cyndia called.

"For now," Lyriel said.

Sylvanas snatched up the intact bottle from the table, caught lamp oil through whatever else had been in it, and hurled it into the back corner where the floor already sagged. Jaina understood at once. Frost bit the glass. It split with a crack that filled the room with fire, smoke, and the stink of burning oil.

They went through the smoke instead of the door. Cyndia met them in the yard with the horses already loose and Lyriel covering the orchard edge.

"North wall," Sylvanas said.

"Low enough to jump," Lyriel said.

"Then jump it."

Jaina caught Sylvanas's elbow before she put weight on the wrong side. "You'll tear it wider."

"I know."

Her hand was cold and steady. Sylvanas let her have one heartbeat to shove folded cloth under the cut and wrap the bandage once around her waist.

"That won't hold if you keep twisting," Jaina said.

"Then I won't."

They cleared the north wall on foot, caught the horses beyond it, and rode out through orchard rows where dead leaves had turned to black pulp underhoof.

No one spoke for half a mile.

When the trees thinned and the ground opened back to road and field, Sylvanas let the line slow. The bandage had already gone wet. Jaina rode close enough to watch it and far enough not to make a show of it. Cyndia had mud up both sleeves and someone else's blood on one boot. Lyriel kept checking behind them.

Sylvanas pulled the salvaged scraps from her coat and passed them back to Jaina without turning. "Tell me we got something worth bleeding for."

Jaina looked once. "Yes. Not enough. Yes."

"Define it."

"The part that keeps the treatment alive through storage and baking. Proof the body matters before death. The last turn is still missing."

"Still no waking turn."

"No."

"But enough to point higher."

"Yes."

Jaina unfolded one scrap fully. "There was a marking under the reagent shelf. Estate road, not Andorhal town use. Northeast approach. Private stores. Short labor rotation. Big enough to matter."

Cyndia held out a hand. Jaina passed the scrap over.

"Walled compound," she said. "Working road, but no village road. Someone wants traffic in and witnesses out."

Lyriel snorted. "Sounds charming."

The larger compound sat three hours farther on, above the road on a long low rise with scrub oak at its shoulders and grain fields gone to stubble around it. Two outbuildings. Main house. Cart shed. No banners. No life from the road.

Sylvanas reined in below the rise and studied it through a gap in the trees.

The perimeter was better than the farmhouse. No obvious watch. No men showing themselves where frightened men would have. Tracks kept to one lane. Yard clean, though not so clean it confessed fear. Professional ground.

Jaina drew up beside her and closed her eyes for half a breath.

Sylvanas did not wait for the report. She had already found the seam in the patrol logic: a shallow dip along the north side where the rise broke the line from the outbuildings. Too clean, perhaps, but still plausible.

Her side hurt. Every delay now came priced in food, watch rotations, spellwork, blood.

"There," she said. "North side. We move through the dip."

Jaina opened her eyes. "Wait."

Flat. Certain. Sylvanas disliked it.

"I need one sweep."

"You have half of one."

"Half won't do on this ground."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the dip. No movement. No glint. No sound beyond wind in the dead stubble and tack shifting on the horses behind them.

The seam still looked right.

Jaina touched her sleeve. "Sylvanas."

That decided it the wrong way. A touch like that made caution feel like a hand on the reins.

"Cyndia left," she said. "Lyriel right. Jaina with me. Quick and low."

Jaina made a noise of frustration that should have pleased her. It did not.

They moved on foot through the scrub oak. The ground dipped exactly where Sylvanas had read it: good cover, easy angle into the yard wall.

Halfway down, Jaina stopped so abruptly Sylvanas nearly hit her.

"No."

The word was for the ground, not for Sylvanas.

"What's wrong?"

Jaina's attention fixed on empty air. "The corridor isn't empty."

Lyriel was already stepping into it from the right.

There was no flash.

Only a hard bloom of cold light across the dip and the sound the air made when magic chose violence over warning.

Lyriel vanished.

No fire. No scream. One moment a body, the next a burst of white, a smell of ozone laid over sweet rot, and bare ground where the right flank had been.

Jaina's barrier went up a heartbeat later and caught the second pulse before it reached Sylvanas's chest. Blue-white light crawled over unseen lines in the air and showed the corridor for what it was: a throat lined with wards tuned to the spaces between moving bodies.

Farstrider spacing.

Built for them.

"Back," Jaina snapped.

This time Sylvanas obeyed before the sentence ended.

Cyndia had already dropped to one knee left of the dip, bow up, eyes locked on the empty place to the right with a ferocity that changed nothing.

There was nothing to recover. No body. No blood. Only scorched grass and after-light burning itself out.

Sylvanas shoved Jaina back behind the oak line as another ward fired where Jaina's knee had been.

Then the whole slope woke.

Crossbowmen rose from slit trenches in the field. One on the shed roof. Two behind the low stone wall. Living again. Always living when it cost them most.

Sylvanas fired on instinct. The man on the shed roof pitched backward without loosing. Cyndia took the left trench. Jaina held the barrier a fraction too low, corrected, and caught the next volley on the second try.

No time for the missing. The field asked for the living answer first.

Sylvanas used the oak trunks as cover, moved three paces, loosed, moved again. The wound in her side bit every turn. She kept her face blank and her breathing shallow.

Jaina spoke without looking at her. "Three layers. The outer one burns on spacing. The second waits for panic retreat. Don't go uphill blind."

Sylvanas adjusted at once. "Cyndia, on my arrow. Jaina, hold the left choke. Two steps only when I move."

"Understood."

They broke contact by inches instead of speed. Jaina peeled the second layer open just long enough to let them clear it. Sylvanas covered the exit. Cyndia answered every order, but her attention kept cutting right before it came back forward.

By the time they reached the tree line beyond the trap ground, the fight had turned too expensive uphill. No pursuit followed.

Sylvanas put one hand against a trunk and left it there until the world widened again.

Jaina came close, checked the fresh blood on the second bandage, and kept silent. Good.

Cyndia stood ten paces away scanning the right flank, then the road ahead, then the right again.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

They moved.

After that every choke point had teeth.

At the first narrow lane through the scrub, Sylvanas stopped and glanced at Jaina.

Jaina swept once. Nodded. "Clean."

Only then did Sylvanas take them through.

At the next broken fence line, Cyndia checked right before stepping over it.

Sylvanas kept the sight of it and said nothing.

They regrouped at dusk on a ridge above fields gone silver with frost. The road split below them. One branch bent toward Barov country and the deeper Eastweald. The other curved south-west toward Hearthglen.

Cyndia unsaddled with the hard economy of a woman making sure the horses still existed because looking anywhere else cost too much. Jaina spread the salvaged scraps and her copied notes across a flat stone. Sylvanas sat because standing pulled at the wound.

The absence in the formation had weight now. A shape ruined by subtraction.

Cyndia watered the horses, came back, and checked the right edge of the ridge before she looked at the road.

Jaina saw that too. Her mouth tightened. She bent back over the papers instead of speaking into it.

"What do we have?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina laid one scrap over another. "The farmhouse and the compound use the same family of binding. The compound's edge work is cleaner and better funded. More than one site. More than one labor crew. Someone upstream is making the method match."

"Barov?" Sylvanas asked.

"Barov roads. Barov access. Barov storehouses, probably. Nothing stronger than that yet."

"Good. Keep it there."

Cyndia tapped one route mark with a stripped fingernail. "Estate approaches. Three of them. Main road, north service lane, and a half-hidden cut through the orchard east of the wall. Old property. Built by people who trusted the land to show them strangers."

"And the other line?" Sylvanas asked.

Cyndia kept her eyes on the southern road. "I saw someone peel out of the trap site."

Jaina looked up sharply. "You saw someone get out?"

"Maybe. Tall. Hood up. Too far for a face. He moved before the crossbows did."

"You didn't say that earlier," Sylvanas said.

"We were leaving."

Fair.

"Anything else?"

Cyndia's gaze drifted once toward the space where Lyriel should have been. "When he broke the tree line he said something to the men by the lower ditch. I only caught the end."

Sylvanas waited.

Cyndia said, "'May the stars keep your road.'"

The phrase struck where it always did: nowhere useful and too deep.

The old seller. The man on the road south of Strahnbrad. Forgettable faces, memorable farewell.

Jaina heard the shift without fully knowing why. "You've heard that before."

"Yes."

"Same man?"

"I don't know."

Jaina let it lie. Another good choice.

Hooves came up the ridge road from the north at a pace too deliberate for enemies and too tired for a courier trying to impress anyone.

Cyndia was on her feet before the second stride. She checked right again, then drew on the approach.

Sylvanas knew the rider before the horse cleared the rise.

Lor'themar reined in below them on a lathered bay, one broken buckle hanging loose at his shoulder and road grime dulling what habit usually kept clean. He took in the camp in one sweep—the missing place, the blood on Sylvanas's side, Cyndia standing where two rangers should have stood.

"You look terrible."

"You've improved too."

He dismounted stiffly. That hurt to watch more than the words.

"Convocation?" Sylvanas asked.

"Among other delights."

Lor'themar reached inside his coat and produced two packets, one sealed in Silvermoon wax and the other in the narrow plain hand of a Dalaran copyist. He handed the second to Jaina and the first to no one. Sylvanas took it anyway.

"That one is supposed to sound official," he said. "The official line is grave concern over the widening scope of your authority, your absence from the northern defenses, and the number of reports arriving without the kind of proof a comfortable room can read."

Sylvanas broke the seal with her thumb. "And the real line?"

Lor'themar looked at the blood on her bandage. "I spent three days keeping them from recalling you by sheer precedent and wounded dignity. I bought you this much road by arguing that if they cut your line now, all they gain is ignorance with better posture."

Jaina opened her own packet. Her face barely changed.

"Antonidas?" Sylvanas asked.

"The Council wants the proof laid out clean before they'll move resources in force," Jaina said.

Lor'themar laughed once. "Remarkable. We are being buried by two institutions in exactly the same language."

He looked at the papers spread on the stone, the route marks, the roads below.

"Tell me what you have before someone older and more decorative than I am decides we should stop having it."

They told him.

Not in speeches. In pieces.

Barov route pressure through Eastweald storehouses and estate approaches, but no proof yet of willing guilt. The farmhouse read, incomplete but real: the binding, the host answer, no final turn. The compound trap built for disciplined scouts and tuned to the spaces between them. Hearthglen chatter rising from the south-west line, and with it Arthas—the one man outside their institutions likely to move before the rest finished discussing the danger. The repeated farewell again, still too thin to spend.

Lor'themar listened the way he always had, weighing each fragment before the next.

When they finished, he said, "You don't have one road anymore."

"No," Sylvanas said.

"And you don't have four women either," he said, still looking at the papers.

Jaina folded the Dalaran note once with great care. "If the Barov roads are real and still moving, I can get through doors you can't."

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

Jaina met her eyes over the map. "And if both your institutions keep turning inward, Arthas becomes a lever whether we like him or not."

"Also yes."

Lor'themar rubbed a hand over his mouth. "Then stop pretending there's a version of this where you stay one team and chase both before the paper walls close."

Cyndia checked the right flank again, then the south-west road.

Sylvanas caught it. So did Jaina.

No one named it.

"You take the Barov thread," Sylvanas said to Jaina.

Jaina held the silence for a beat.

"Alone," Lor'themar said, because someone had to.

"Because she's the right one," Sylvanas said. "Not because she's the spare body."

Jaina's chin lifted a fraction.

"Route evidence, estate approaches, the partial read," Sylvanas said. "You carry all of it. You do not push Barov beyond what the papers earn. If a door opens because they still think you're only a Kirin Tor problem, use it."

"I know how to walk into hostile houses politely," Jaina said.

"That was never the concern."

Lor'themar almost smiled. "Touching."

Sylvanas ignored him and looked to Cyndia. "You ride with me."

Cyndia nodded once.

"Hearthglen first," Sylvanas said. "Then Arthas if he's still within reach. We need rank, soldiers, and a man with a habit of moving before committees finish introducing themselves."

Jaina's eyes flicked to the bandage at Sylvanas's side. "You need that wound looked at before you try to bully a prince."

"He's human. It won't take much."

Lor'themar pinned the Convocation packet to the map with two fingers. "You understand what this means if either of you fails?"

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

"I understand what it means if we do nothing," Jaina said.

The light bled out of the fields by degrees. Frost thickened on the ridge grass. Cyndia checked the right again while tightening a saddle strap, then turned south-west where Sylvanas's road ran.

Jaina wrapped the farmhouse notes in oilcloth, tied them shut, and slid them into her satchel with the Barov routes and Cyndia's estate sketches. She rose in one smooth motion and slung the strap across her body.

"When do you leave?" Lor'themar asked.

"Now," Sylvanas said.

"Of course."

They saddled in the dark.

Jaina checked Sylvanas's bandage once more with quick hands and retied it tighter when she found blood under the edge.

"That will hurt," she said.

"Yes."

"Good."

Dry enough to be hers. Sylvanas let it stand.

Then Jaina stepped back, mounted, and turned her horse toward the Eastweald road without looking as if she expected anyone to stop her.

Professional. Clean. Wrong. Necessary.

Sylvanas mounted more slowly than she would have liked. Cyndia was already in place on the left. When Sylvanas settled in the saddle, Cyndia's eyes went right once, then forward.

Jaina touched two fingers to the edge of her hood in something too small for a salute and too deliberate for accident.

Sylvanas answered with the same economy. "Get through the door first."

"Send word before the paper walls close."

"You too."

Lor'themar took Jaina's rein long enough to turn her cleanly onto the eastern road. He did not touch Sylvanas's.

The roads divided below the ridge.

Jaina took the Barov line alone with the notes, the route evidence, and the incomplete answer wrapped against the cold.

Sylvanas turned south-west with Cyndia, the wound under her ribs, and the new price of every bad decision riding in the space where the right flank used to be.

No one looked back.
