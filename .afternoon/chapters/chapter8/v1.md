# Chapter 8: The Hunters Hunted

The west-line street still worked at night.

That was what made it possible.

Wagons rolled through the dark with hooded lamps swinging under their axles. Men cursed at balking mules. Flour dust hung pale in the air wherever a door opened and shut. A city afraid for its trade did not sleep; it narrowed its eyes and kept counting. Jaina waited in the seam between two storehouses and listened to that ordinary noise take the weight of their trap.

Across the lane, Lyriel stood in plain sight beside a stack of empty grain frames with her hood back and a porter’s hook over one shoulder, thin enough in the dark to look harmless. Cyndia had vanished entirely. Jaina could not see her, which usually meant no one else would until it was too late. Havel leaned in the shadow of a counting-house arch with the posture of a bored local officer. Corin kept his head down beside him, carrying a ledger board under one arm as if he had been sent to settle some tiresome discrepancy at an hour when decent people should already have gone home.

Sylvanas stood nearest Jaina, bow unstrung in her hand, pale hair hidden under a dark scarf. Her stillness had changed over the last days. At first Jaina had read it as reserve. Then as discipline. Tonight it read as readiness so complete it made speech feel wasteful.

"Second cart after the mill team," Havel murmured without moving his mouth. "Limp on the right. Checks exits before he checks men."

Jaina touched the ward-thread she had already laid over the lane. It was slight work, a muffling spread thin through nailheads, hinges, lamp brackets, anything with enough metal or built intention to hold a whisper of spellcraft. Not enough to hide a battle. Enough to put a hand over the bell if someone tried to ring one.

The cart came.

The first man walking beside it carried himself like labor. Loose shoulders. Slow feet. A man tired enough to forget his supper. The second glanced once at the alley mouth before he looked at the wheel rut in front of him.

There.

Sylvanas moved on Jaina's breath, not after it. One moment she was standing at Jaina's shoulder. The next she had stepped from the shadow behind the limping man with one hand over his mouth and the other locking his knife arm at the elbow. Lyriel left the stack of frames and took the cart horse by the bit before it could shy. The animal only snorted and stamped. Jaina caught the flare of cheap warding on the prisoner's collar and smothered it under her palm of cold.

It felt exactly like putting a hand over a bell as it began to ring.

The magic kicked once against her, startled and hard, then flattened under her grip. Cyndia rose behind the first escort and folded him down into the dark without spectacle. Havel turned his body just enough to block the lane's view from the counting-house arch. Corin did not look up at all. He kept walking, ledger under his arm, as if men disappeared in Andorhal between one breath and the next every night.

The whole snatch took less time than one wagon wheel turning.

Jaina was at the prisoner's face when Sylvanas dragged him backward into the seam between buildings. He bit down hard enough to bloody his own lip. She hit the hinge of his jaw with two fingers and whatever he had meant to break in his mouth stayed whole.

"Try again," Sylvanas said quietly. "I prefer not to pull teeth in the street."

His eyes cut to Jaina then, took in the glow left on her hand from the ward-suppression, and widened.

Good, she thought, with no pleasure in it at all. Fear was faster than persuasion.

They moved him through a side door Havel had already opened into a cooper's shed that smelled of damp wood, vinegar, and grain mold. Broken hoops leaned against the wall. A shutter hung crooked over the single back window. The room was too narrow for comfort and too ordinary for anyone to remember it if they lived.

Cyndia bound the unconscious escort in three efficient turns and left him under a tarp of spoiled sacking. Lyriel took the back corner near the window slit. Havel stood at the door with one hand on the bar. Corin, pale now that the motion had stopped, set his ledger down on a barrel and wiped his palm against his coat.

The operative sat because Sylvanas pushed him into the chair, not because he had agreed to anything. Jaina saw the first quick searches go across his face: window, door, rafters, Lyriel, the sword at Cyndia's hip, Jaina's hands, back to Sylvanas. Not panic. Inventory.

That chilled her more than panic would have.

His limp had been partly true. One boot was built up half an inch at the heel. His hands were laborer's hands if one ignored the scar-web under the calluses and the clean crescents where old ink had once sat. Not a dockside brute, then. A man trained into roughness and wearing it well.

"Name," Havel said.

The man spat blood at the floor.

"Local militia has already tried shouting at your people," Sylvanas said. "We can spare ourselves the repetition."

She crouched in front of him. No raised voice. No theatrics. Jaina had begun to understand that this, more than menace, was what made Sylvanas difficult to resist. She asked questions the way a fletcher tested wood grain, pressing where the break would tell her the most.

"Who handles your reliefs on west line?"

Nothing.

"Who signs spoilage after second bell?"

Silence again, but smaller this time. His shoulders had tightened at reliefs. Jaina saw it because Sylvanas had made the room so still there was nothing else to look at.

"You built the repack schedule to hide treated loads among damaged stock," Jaina said. "The preservative shell is elegant. Wasteful in one or two places, but elegant."

His gaze snapped to her.

There it was.

"You know enough to be useful," she went on. "Not enough to have designed it."

"Careful," Sylvanas said without taking her eyes off him. "He'll start enjoying being underestimated."

"He isn't enjoying anything."

The prisoner's mouth twitched. "You think this city belongs to clerks and wagoners because that's what it smells like."

"No," Sylvanas said. "I think you were taught to mistake concealment for ownership."

He looked at her then with something close to hatred, and the balance in the room shifted. The answer had found the pride under the fear.

Sylvanas felt it too.

"Better," she said softly. "Now we're speaking to the right man. Who sits above the yard cells?"

He breathed through his nose, too fast.

Jaina let a thread of magic slide over him, not into mind or blood, only near enough to read the charge moving through him. Sweat had gathered on his upper lip. His pulse struck high and hard in his throat. Fear, yes—but not the ordinary downward fear of pain, prison, or death. The shape of it ran elsewhere. Upward. Toward an absent thing with rank.

"You don't answer to a committee," Jaina said.

He kept his mouth shut.

"Nor to whatever petty idiot bought himself a private cellar and a handful of fools. This was designed. The grain treatment, the host trigger separated from the carrier work, the route discipline, the response lanes—months of them. One mind laid that structure down."

"Mind," he said, and laughed once through his teeth. "You say it like that's the part worth admiring."

Corin made a small sound behind her, half disgust and half vindication.

"No," Jaina said. "I say it because it means you are not important enough to die for."

That landed. She saw it land.

He knew it too. The contempt in his face broke for one bare instant, and the raw thing under it showed through. He had not been promised glory. He had been promised inclusion, perhaps. Proximity. The privilege of serving brilliance.

Men had built worse gods from less.

Sylvanas stood. The movement made him flinch though she had not touched him again.

"Field necromancers," she said. "How many in the city?"

He stared at her.

"Don't waste my time pretending you don't know what I mean. Not architects. Not theorists. Signal hands. The ones who sharpen obedience in whatever you've infected and keep your peasants useful."

For the first time, true surprise crossed his face.

"Three?" Sylvanas guessed. "No. More than that, or you would have hidden them better. Five? Rotating by district?"

He swallowed before he could stop himself.

Jaina felt Corin beside the barrel go still with amazement. Havel only looked angrier.

"They don't know the whole of it," the prisoner said suddenly. "None of us below the line do."

"Below whose line?" Jaina asked.

His eyes flicked to her, then shut tight, as if he could force the words back by closing one door in his own face.

"Good," Sylvanas said. "Stay with that mistake."

He opened his eyes again. They had gone wet around the edges, and somehow that made him less pitiful, not more. The fear in him had sharpened to the point where Jaina could almost feel it scraping the air.

"There's one hand above the city cells," he said. "One design. We build the lanes. We carry what we're given. We hold the mouths shut when the time comes. The acolytes keep the stock obedient. The true work sits higher."

"North?" Havel asked.

That got another involuntary look.

"How far north?" Jaina said.

"Far enough that Andorhal is only the throat, not the—"

He stopped. His chest jerked once.

For a fragment of a second Jaina thought he had simply swallowed wrong.

Then the air changed.

It came cleaner than the ambient taint she had spent the day tracing through dust and sackcloth, narrower too. A line driven hard through distance and stone with so much control inside it that its precision was more horrifying than force would have been. A needle through distance. A chosen point.

"Down!" she snapped.

Too late.

The strike went through him like a pin through wet parchment.

He made one soft sound, not even a cry, and every muscle in his body seized at once. Black flooded the veins at his throat so fast Jaina saw it happen. Blood spilled from his nose in two thin threads. His eyes locked on nothing. The word he had almost spoken died still shaped in his mouth.

Sylvanas was already moving, knife out, searching for the source no body in the room had made. Lyriel turned for the shutter. Cyndia hit the floor, then the wall beside the unconscious escort, then the back corner, checking angles on instinct while Havel slammed the bar harder across the door.

Jaina caught the prisoner's jaw and reached for life out of reflex more than hope. Cold punched into her palm. He was dying, and beyond her by the time she found the seam of the spell. Whoever had cast it had found the one brutal measure between certainty and economy and driven it straight through his heart.

"No," Corin whispered.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

Jaina looked up. "There are searchers already on the street."

She reached for the district again and found the answer waiting: motion. Living necromantic signatures traveled in disciplined lines through the district, each one leashed tight enough that she could distinguish it from the city's wider sickness. Three to the east. Two cutting south. Another spread across the roofs like a hand feeling for a throat.

The room was no longer an interrogation room. It was a marked point on someone else's map.

"Back window," Havel said.

"Not yet," Jaina answered. "Roofline north. Street team on the alley mouth in seconds."

Sylvanas nodded once and turned the warning into route at once. "Rear wall, then cut west before the cooper yard opens. Cyndia, take the body if you can do it cheap. If not, leave him."

"Cheap," Cyndia said, and hoisted the dead man's chair back with one hand so she could slice the wrist ties free.

They went out through the inner door into a storage run barely wide enough for two men abreast. Jaina did not waste power on concealment. Concealment had just failed. She spread her attention instead, feeling for the moving points of pressure sliding through the streets beyond the plaster and timber. It was a different use of her gift, one she would have called inelegant a week ago. Now it seemed like survival wearing an honest face.

"Right at the lane end," she said.

Sylvanas did not ask why. She only altered course half a step, caught Lyriel by the sleeve, and sent them left through a hatch into a yeast room rank enough to sting Jaina's nose. Havel shoved a grain cart aside, found the narrow service door behind it, and got them into a yard where sacks rose in pale towers under canvas.

Someone shouted two streets over.

Not alarm. Position.

The wrong kind of silence followed it, one where ordinary noise thinned instead of breaking. Teamsters stopped swearing. Hoists slowed. Men moved because movement had been ordered, not because they understood the danger.

Standing doctrine, Jaina thought, with a sick clarity. Breach. Seal. Search. Remove.

"Roof pair crossing," she said. "Three houses north."

Sylvanas cut them through an alley before Jaina had finished the sentence. Lyriel flowed ahead to the mouth, glanced once, then vanished around it. Cyndia kept the rear with the dead operative slung across her shoulders like spoiled grain. Corin stumbled on a rut and Havel caught him hard by the arm without even turning.

"Breathe later," Havel muttered.

They reached a broader street full of night carts and turned with them, not against them. Jaina kept her head down and let the city's work cover them while she listened with the skin behind her eyes. Two signatures split behind. One rose. One cut low. Another was waiting somewhere ahead where the street narrowed into a bottleneck between granary walls.

"Don't take the arch," she said.

"I wasn't going to," Sylvanas said.

Something in that steadied her more than praise could have done.

They wheeled instead into a side street so abruptly a baker's apprentice swore and dropped a basket. Lyriel caught the boy by the shoulder, set him upright again, and kept moving. Cyndia turned once at the far end of the lane and drew. Her shot went back into the dark with clean economy. Jaina heard a body strike timber and slide.

No slowing. Not for that.

The pressure ahead thickened.

"Two on the roofline above the dye house. One street level behind the rain barrel. Mage left hand, not right."

"How sure?"

"Enough."

Sylvanas planted a foot on the dye house wall, vaulted the rain barrel, and put an arrow into the place Jaina had felt before the hidden man fully rose from cover. Cyndia and Lyriel split without needing the order spoken, one taking the street line, one the flank stair to the roof. Havel shoved Corin through a door hanging half open on its latch. Jaina followed and found herself in a tailor's workroom with shirts strung from a line overhead and a woman staring at them over her own hand before common sense told her to get down.

"Stay there," Jaina said, and the woman's terror made obedience immediate.

They crossed that room, a back court, a midden passage, then another street. The escape became nothing but decisions. Left because Jaina felt a cold line gather at the right-hand corner. Down because Sylvanas saw boot marks fresh on the stair. Halt because Lyriel touched two fingers to the wall and heard men on the other side. Run because Cyndia's held line finally had to collapse inward.

Once, hostile magic skimmed so near her shoulder she tasted iron.

Sylvanas's hand closed on the back of her coat and yanked her through a doorway a breath before the bolt struck the brick where her throat had been.

The jolt spun her against Sylvanas's chest. For one instant there was no room in the world but the narrow dark between them, her own breath knocked thin, Sylvanas already looking past her for the next danger.

"Stair," Sylvanas said.

Jaina did not waste that second by staring. She took it and climbed.

They came out over a brew house, crossed three joined roofs under a moon dulled by dust, slid down into an alley lined with broken casks, and found the shelter only because Havel had prepared for losing the first shelter, and perhaps the second after that.

It was a washerwoman's abandoned store cellar under a chapel annex too poor for stained glass. The entrance hid under stacked coal bins in a yard with no line of sight from the street. Inside, the room was low, cold, and dry. Shelves held water jars, old blankets, lamp oil, two sealed crates of biscuits, and a packet of copied keys wrapped in waxed cloth.

Havel had never bothered naming the habit. He had simply lived in the city long enough to plan for it.

Corin bent over with his hands on his knees the moment the door shut. His whole body shook once.

"That was not a search," he said hoarsely. "That was a net."

"Yes," Havel said.

Cyndia lowered the dead operative to the floor. Lyriel stripped the collar from the corpse and handed it to Jaina. The cheap ward on it had burned out under her first suppression. The man himself had gone slack into the ugly peace of a problem no longer solvable.

Jaina knelt and touched the bruise-dark veins at his throat again, though she knew perfectly well what her fingers would find.

Nothing useful. No lingering hook to follow back. The strike had been made by someone who knew how not to leave himself on the work.

She sat back on her heels.

"This was ready," she said.

Corin looked up. "Ready?"

"Not improvised. Not a frightened local cell thrashing because we touched the wrong man. He was lost the instant he missed his report window, or crossed some line we never saw, and they had lanes in place to close the district around the failure."

Havel leaned against the shelf with both hands braced on the wood. "I thought I was looking at smugglers with a few grave-minded fanatics behind them."

"You were," Sylvanas said. "And a security doctrine."

The words sat in the cellar like a weight lowered carefully to the floor.

Corin went paler still. "If they planned for capture, then they planned for investigators. For us."

"Eventually," Jaina said. "Not for us by name. For the fact that someone competent would follow the books far enough to put a hand on one of theirs."

"Comforting."

"No."

She rose and cleared space on the central crate. Corin, grateful for any labor that was not terror, brought his copied route sheets at once. Havel added the spoiled manifest with its shift blocks and district notes. Jaina spread them flat, weighted the corners with lamp bases, and set the burned collar beside the map as if it were another piece of evidence and not a dead man's last silence.

The cellar lamp smoked. Grain dust still clung at the hem of her sleeves. When she laid two fingers on the route marks and called the faintest thread of power into them, the lines she had traced all day in fragments began to answer each other.

Out of Andorhal.

Not around it. Not within it. Out.

Road west to village ovens that could never have named the source of what arrived in their flour. North to storehouses smaller than this one, each thinking itself only a stop in commerce. East to market towns, to garrison bread, to kitchens where women would slap dough onto boards before dawn and never know what had been worked into the dust.

She went still.

Sylvanas saw it first. "What changed?"

Jaina did not answer immediately. She was looking at the map and at the room beneath the map, the whole city under her hands, the day's evidence at last stripped of any flattering lie.

"I kept treating Andorhal as the place where it became true," she said.

No one interrupted her.

"It isn't. This is only where they make it ordinary."

Corin shut his eyes.

Havel swore once under his breath.

Jaina traced one route line, then another, not because she needed to prove it now but because the body insisted on finishing what the mind had already accepted. The preserving shell, the repack work, the false spoilage, the shift discipline, the hidden caches: none of it was designed to poison Andorhal. It was designed to pass through Andorhal wearing the face of food.

"How far?" Sylvanas asked.

"Far enough." Jaina hated the vagueness in the answer and gave it shape. "North Lordaeron at least. Bakeries, settlements, probably way stations and garrison contracts. Not every route. Enough routes."

"Enough to start a war with bread," Havel said.

"The war started before we got here," Sylvanas said. "This is only the proof."

Jaina let out a slow breath and hated that too, because the proof felt like victory from one angle and from the only angle that mattered it was a ledger arriving after the grain wagons had left the yard.

Knowledge, again. Clean, hard, late.

Corin was staring at the northbound lines as if he could force them to shorten. "There were stories," he said. "From carters. About a scholar north of the city buying old houses through proxies and never showing his own face. I thought it was tavern rot."

Havel looked over. "And supply runs that vanished into farm roads they had no business using. Men got frightened when his people came up in drink. Wouldn't say much. Only that the name started with Kel."

Sylvanas's attention sharpened. "Kel what?"

Corin's head came up fast. "Kel'Thuzad."

The cellar changed around the word.

Jaina did not move. For a heartbeat she could not. The name had not lived in her mind for years, not as anything active. A dismissed conversation. A cautionary tale told in halls that liked to flatter themselves with the thought that expulsion solved corruption. Brilliant, difficult, increasingly grotesque in his appetites for forbidden study. Antonidas colder each time his former colleague's petitions crossed the table. Then the final break.

Kel'Thuzad.

The dead man in the cooper's shed had almost had the shape of it in his mouth. Enough of it for recognition to seize.

Corin licked dry lips. "I only know what apprentices repeat when they want to sound frightened on purpose. He was Kirin Tor once. That's all. Or close enough to all."

"More than enough," Jaina said.

Havel frowned. "You know him."

"I know what he was." Her eyes stayed on the map. "Archmage. Council of Six. Antonidas cast him out when necromancy stopped being argument and became work."

The route lines kept their shape beneath her fingers, severe and orderly. The plague's carrier side had always shown a mind trained to love durable solutions. Milling. Storage. Transport. Loss rates. Timing. She had spent days recognizing intelligence in the work without wanting to see whose kind of intelligence it was.

Now it had one.

A man trained under the same ceilings that had trained her. Taught to think in systems, in elegant sufficiencies, in the pleasure of solving what others considered impossible. Antonidas's world had rejected him, yes. It had also made him first.

Her body went still before her thoughts did.

She became aware of the others only by edges: Cyndia at the rear shelf, wiping her blade clean; Lyriel watching Jaina instead of the door for once; Havel grim and practical; Corin looking as if he had said something irreversible; Sylvanas nearest the map, nearest Jaina, saying nothing.

Nothing was the right choice. Jaina did not need comfort. She needed the next action before stillness hardened into delay.

She reached into her coat for the sending stone.

It had gone warm from her body. Warm enough to feel almost alive in her palm.

"Now?" Corin asked.

"Now," Jaina said.

"If the line isn't secure—"

"It never is."

She fed power into the stone. Light woke under her thumb, muted blue in the cellar dark. One breath. Two. Then Antonidas answered with no formal preamble, only her name and the roughness of interrupted sleep already stripped away by alarm.

"Jaina."

"We have the architect."

Silence.

She heard, very faintly, cloth move against stone on his end, as if he had sat down without meaning to.

"Say it," he said.

"Kel'Thuzad."

The silence that followed was worse than any curse would have been.

It lasted longer than courtesy, longer than surprise. Long enough for Jaina to understand that the name did not need explanation because it had never stopped living where Antonidas kept his failures. When he spoke again, all the academic padding had gone out of his voice. No lecture. No careful preface. Only age, recognition, and a speed too slow for the field but as fast as the institution knew how to move.

"Are you certain?"

"As certain as I can be without his hand on the worktable. Local fragments, northbound supply movement, the dead operative's near-confession, and the structure itself. It's him."

Another brief silence. Not disbelief this time. Calculation, perhaps, and memory cutting across it.

"Then leave Andorhal alive," Antonidas said. "Write everything. Carry nothing in one head that can be lost with the body. I will start what I can from here."

What I can.

Enough truth sat inside that phrase to make argument pointless.

"He's north of the city," Jaina said. "We turn that way next."

"I know."

Two words. Worn flat.

She had the strange, impossible urge to ask whether he remembered the first lecture Kel'Thuzad had ever given her class, whether he remembered the patience in that voice, the hands that never shook, the way brilliance made a room forgive its own disgust for longer than it should. She asked none of it. There was no room for that kind of grief yet. Perhaps there never would be.

"Master?" Corin whispered from somewhere behind her, forgetting himself.

Antonidas heard the title and ignored it.

"Report again once you're clear of the city," he said. "And Jaina—"

She waited.

Whatever had meant to follow the name failed him. Or he killed it before it could leave his mouth.

"Go," he said instead.

The stone dimmed.

The cellar seemed smaller after that, as if the silence he had left behind had taken space with it.

Jaina closed her fingers around the cooling stone until the edges bit her palm. Then she set it down beside the map, straightened the route sheets once because her hands needed a task, and looked at the others.

"Corin," she said, "you and Havel keep the records split. Nothing in one cache. Nothing in one hand. If they lose one site, they lose a fraction."

Havel nodded before Corin had found his breath.

"We'll salt false trails too," he said. "Make them spend men on ghosts."

"Good."

Sylvanas's gaze was still on her. Not prying. Measuring whether she had finished standing still.

Jaina met it. "Eastweald next."

"I assumed so."

"You usually do."

The corner of Sylvanas's mouth shifted, not enough to be called a smile. "Get some water first, or your splendid revelations will fall over on the road."

It was dry enough, and ordinary enough, that Jaina nearly laughed. The sound did not come, but something in her chest loosened anyway.

Outside the shelter the city would still be working, still loading bread onto wagons, still moving under routines that had learned how to hide murder inside commerce. They had the name now. They had the shape of the hand on the work. They also had the bitterer thing: proof that being early by every normal measure could still mean arriving after the grain had gone out through the gates.

She had wanted clarity to be the same thing as leverage.

Tonight had cured her of that.

The need to understand remained. That would never leave her. What died tonight was the comfort that understanding, by itself, would get ahead of men who had already built lateness into the design.

Jaina bent over the map once more and priced roads, time, and loss with the right hardness at last.
