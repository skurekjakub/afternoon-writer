# Chapter 4: The Sick Village

The village appeared through the tree line in ordinary morning light, smoke lifting straight from a handful of chimneys, a dog's bark cutting across the still air. Jaina heard the market before she saw it—wheel on cobble, a trader's voice carried thin by cold—and at first the sound settled her. After Millhaven's silence, noise meant life. Then the smell reached her, faint and sweet under wood smoke and horse dung and the particular wet-road smell of autumn mud, and she understood that life was not the same as safe.

She pulled her horse to a walk at the edge of the village common. Stalls were open on three sides of the square. A woman moved among them with a basket, and a boy trailed behind her dragging a muddy boot, and across the square a man was arguing with a carter over something in a crate. All of it looked exactly as it should, which was the problem. She knew now what it looked like when a village was sliding toward Millhaven and did not know it yet.

"Two coughing at the bread seller's stall," Sylvanas said beside her, without breaking her scan of the square. "One more at the well. Child near the common house kept upright by an adult who keeps checking the child's face. And the butcher's door is closed."

Jaina looked where Sylvanas was already looking. The child at the common house was perhaps seven, pink-cheeked in a way that was not quite healthy, a woman's hand braced against her small shoulder as though the child's weight needed managing. The bread seller—she found him, a stocky man with a floury apron—turned to cough into his elbow, and the woman nearest him shifted away without making it obvious.

The sweet smell was coming from somewhere inside all of it. Not strong enough yet to stop the market. Just present.

"Same as the south road villages," Jaina said. "Early stage. The gut contamination should be visible if we can examine anyone closely enough."

"That requires the militia captain's permission." Sylvanas had already found him—a blocky man in a worn leather half-coat posted near the well, watching their horses with the unhappy look of a man counting problems. "He'll have decided what we are before we reach him. I'll take the introduction."

Jaina did not argue. She had seen Sylvanas pry open three militia checkpoints on the south road by the time they left it, and the technique that worked there—let them ask the first question and answer only what the question required—was not Jaina's instinct. She would have explained. Sylvanas let authority talk itself into corners.

They dismounted at the square's edge. Cyndia took both horses' leads without being asked, angling the animals to a stance that looked calm and looked armed at the same time. Lyriel drifted right, picking a position near the well where she could see two streets and the common house door, her bow slung but her hands loose. Neither ranger made it visible. They simply arranged themselves the way careful people arrange themselves in unfamiliar places.

The captain met them halfway.

He was a head shorter than Sylvanas and carried his authority the way tired men carry it: close to the chest and ready to be offended. He looked at the pointed ears, the Farstrider armor, the swords, and settled into a posture that said he had a responsibility to this village and wasn't going to be rushed out of it.

"Farstriders don't come this far south for courtesy calls," he said.

"No." Sylvanas stopped far enough back that he wasn't crowded. "What's the name of the sickness you're managing."

He took that for what it was, which was a question that already knew part of the answer.

"Fever. Bad run of it. We've had it before."

"You had it in spring, when half a dozen people were sick for a week and the worst case was the tanner who lost two toes to secondary infection. What you have now spreads faster and gets further into the body. That's different."

The captain's jaw tightened slightly. "And you know that because—"

"Because we came from Millhaven."

The name landed the way she'd intended it to. He'd heard it, the same way every village north of the King's Road had heard it by now—some version of something bad happened there, the market closed, people left. Whatever version had reached him was softer than the truth, and Sylvanas let the gap between his version and hers sit in the silence between them.

His hands were work-roughened, calloused at the base of every finger, the kind of hands that had been swinging tools since before he was old enough to be given a real one. The knuckles of his right hand were lighter than the rest of his skin, a fading old break healed crooked. He folded them together, and she watched his mouth press into a line that was not refusal, just a man buying time.

"How many," he said.

"Everyone," Sylvanas said. "It did not move fast. It moved quietly, and by the time anyone understood it was not ordinary fever, it was past the point where understanding helped. I am not telling you that to frighten you. I am telling you because you have sick people in your village and a mage with me who can examine them and tell you what she finds."

He looked at Jaina for the first time.

Jaina met his gaze and said nothing, because Sylvanas had not finished yet and she was learning to stop filling silences that were already working.

"What's her read worth," he said.

"She identified the contamination pattern in Millhaven's grain stores, its water supply, and three dead bodies before she identified the first living survivor and confirmed the child was clean. Her read is worth the next six hours of your village's life if you let her work."

He stood with it for long enough that the dog at the far side of the square barked at something, then fell silent.

"Fine," he said. "The infirmary is the common house back room. Village medic is a man named Tomas Brek; he's been there four days without stopping. I'll take you in."

"Take the mage in," Sylvanas said. "I'll need someone to walk me through the stores and the well and wherever the deliveries have been coming in."

He looked at her with an expression that was not quite suspicious, not quite cooperative, and Sylvanas held it level until it resolved into a grudging nod.

"My wife handles the storehouse accounts. She's in the square."

---

The common house back room was not built to be an infirmary. Jaina could see that immediately—the space was sized for storing election ballots and tax ledgers and the kind of civic accumulation that ordinary villages carried through ordinary years, and someone had cleared the shelves and pushed three bunk beds against the wall and laid four more sleeping pallets on the floor and called it enough. A smell met her at the door: bodies, the chalky smell of fever-sweated bedding, and underneath it all the sweet-wrong note that matched what she'd caught at the square's edge.

Tomas Brek turned from the nearest pallet. He was a compact man somewhere in his fifties who had not slept in the four days the captain claimed, and he took her measure in one quick look—the staff, the crystal, the gloves that Jaina was already pulling off.

"Mage," he said.

"Jaina Proudmoore, Kirin Tor. Tell me what you've been seeing."

"Fever three to five days in, with sweating and weakness. Appetite gone by day two. The gut gets bad by day three—cramping, loose—I've been treating it as inflammation, but the usual preparations don't do much." He gestured at the nearest bed. "This one came in yesterday. She was well enough this morning."

The woman in the bed was perhaps thirty, hands lying open on the blanket in the limp way hands lay when the body had stopped bothering to close them. Jaina pulled a stool close and held her right hand out flat an inch above the woman's wrist, letting the crystal come up slowly so the light wouldn't startle anyone already in pain.

The contamination showed immediately.

She'd seen it in corpses, in Millhaven's cellar grain, in the black taint threading the well's submerged walls. Here it had a different quality—it was alive inside a living body, and that made it both more visible and more terrible. In Millhaven the pattern had been residue, a record of what the contamination had done after it finished. Here it was in the middle of doing it. She could see the concentration of it in the gut, the cluster around the stomach and the upper intestine that showed as a darker stain against the underlying blue of ordinary tissue, and she could see the way it was beginning to reach beyond that—not far yet, not past the early stage, but reaching.

The woman's breath sweetened slightly on exhale. Jaina held her hand steady.

"Has she been eating here," she asked Brek, keeping her voice low.

"She was eating fine when she came in. We have bread and broth from the communal pot."

"Stop both until I tell you otherwise. Just clean water. I need you to ask every patient whether they've been eating bread from the same source, and whether they've had bread at home before they came in." She pressed her attention back into the pattern, mapping it. "This contamination clusters in the gut first. It gets in through what people eat, and it settles there before it spreads. How are your worst cases distributed?"

Brek turned to the room, and Jaina watched him run through it the way a man runs through a list he knows too well. Seven patients. He ticked off names and notes in a voice kept flat for professional reasons.

The worst three were household heavies—a miller's assistant who ate bread with every meal, a baker's wife, a woman who'd been helping with baking preparations for the village's autumn gathering. The lightest cases were a shepherd who'd been out with his flock for three weeks eating dried provisions, and a child of four who had mostly been eating porridge made with oats from a separate family store.

Jaina went from bed to bed with the crystal, taking fifteen seconds at each, not interfering with anything yet, just reading. Every gut. The contamination was always there, always that same dense, sweet-rotten signature. The variation was in how far it had spread beyond the stomach—in the worst cases it was touching the bloodstream, and she could see the damage beginning in the tissue edges.

She stopped at the fourth bed and looked at the child watching her from a pallet in the corner. Eight years old, maybe, brown braids, sitting with her knees under her chin.

"I'm not here to hurt anyone," Jaina said. "I'm trying to understand what's making people sick."

"You're a mage," the child said.

"I am."

"Can you fix it."

"I can slow it down while I figure out what it is. Will you let me look at you?"

The child considered this with the gravity that children brought to important questions, then held out her arm.

The contamination in her was minimal—early, a faint shadow in the stomach only. Not progressed. Jaina pressed a gentle stabilizing thread through the layer and watched the shadow hold still and not push further.

"That helps some," she said. "Hold still for a moment."

She worked her way back through the room, laying the stabilization wards in each body—not a cure, not even close, but a slowdown, a pause in the progression that bought time. Her hands warmed and then ached with the sustained work. The crystal on her staff guttered blue through amber, blue through amber, fighting the contamination's frequency.

By the time she finished, the farmer's wife two beds over had gone still under her hands in a way that meant sleep, real sleep, the first in days.

Jaina stood and pressed her knuckles against the wall. The cold stone felt useful against her palms.

Through the window over the medic's desk, she could see part of the square. Sylvanas was across it, standing outside the granary's side door with a woman who must have been the captain's wife—stout, middle-aged, with account ledgers under her arm—and a older man in a work apron. Sylvanas was not speaking. She was asking something, pausing, and asking again, and the granary keeper was talking more than he had clearly intended to, his hands moving toward something behind him, the gesture of a man about to show something he hadn't planned to discuss.

Jaina could see it even from here: Sylvanas was not interrogating. She was creating space and letting the man fill it with what he already knew.

---

"How does a man who sells grain describe bread going wrong," Sylvanas said.

The granary keeper, whose name was Aldous, was not accustomed to this question. He was accustomed to questions about weight and measure and the condition of his roof and whether the autumn rains had gotten into the barley again. He turned the question over with the expression of someone who had thought about something but had not yet been asked to say it.

"Different," he said, finally. "Not spoiled. Just—the color was off a little, and the rise, and there was a sweetness in the finished bread that wasn't quite right. My wife noticed first. Thought I'd bought a different variety."

"When."

"End of summer. New grain supplier came through, pleasant man, good prices. I bought two deliveries. Thought I'd gotten lucky."

"What was different about the prices."

Aldous shifted his weight. "Too good, but not so good it felt like a scam. Just under what the usual man charges, and no long-term commitment required. That's the sort of thing you say yes to when you're trying to make the numbers work."

Sylvanas's captain's wife—her name was Hella, and she had the ledgers open now, running her finger down a column with the precise speed of someone who had been doing this for years—said, "He made two deliveries. First was twelve sacks of winter wheat. Second was eight. We used the second delivery first because it was fresher; the first is still in the back half of the store."

"The first delivery," Sylvanas said. "Still in the back half."

"Against the wall. Stacked. We haven't gotten to it yet."

Sylvanas turned back to Aldous. "The usual man. Older, established route. When did he last come through."

"Six weeks before the new one appeared." Aldous rubbed his jaw. "He sometimes skips a season. I didn't think much of it."

"Describe the new man."

"Pleasant." He said it in the way people said things that had started to sound different from how they sounded at the time. "Forgettable. Brown hair, brown coat, ordinary age, ordinary height. Knew enough about grain to sound like he'd been doing it long enough to know the roads."

Sylvanas's memory placed it without effort: *a newer grain man on the road before it started. A different one from the man who came every season. She said the bread tasted different after.*

Miri's words in the cellar. The same description—ordinary, pleasant, prices that just made sense—on a different road two villages south of here, coming from a child who had no way to have spoken to this man.

Not proof. But two villages pulling the same forgettable face from memory was a pattern that did not arrive by accident.

"Where is the back half of your store," she said.

---

Jaina heard the bread correlation click while she was still at the infirmary window.

She turned from the glass and looked at the room—the bread from the communal pot, the patients' histories that Brek had given her, the clear clustering of heavy bread-eaters against light ones—and reached the same place she had been circling for twenty minutes.

The contamination entered through the gut. It clustered in the stomach first. In every serious case, she had found the same density in the gut and the same distribution of the spread-lines, always radiating from the same interior point as though the body had ingested something and the ingested thing had settled there and started working.

This was not water. Water would have caught people variably—some days more, some days less, depending on how much they drank. This was more targeted than that. More cumulative. The worst cases were people who ate the most, specifically people who ate bread with every meal, and the lightest case was the shepherd who had not eaten bread at all.

She looked at the bakery visible from the corner of the window.

The grain went into bread. The bread went into people. If the grain was carrying it—if someone had put the contamination into the grain before it reached this village—

She looked back out the window at Sylvanas.

Sylvanas had the granary keeper showing her something inside the side door.

Jaina pressed a breath out slowly through her nose, resting the end of the staff against the stone floor.

She was right. She was almost certain she was right. But the piece that would make it certain—who had brought the grain, when, through what route—was on the other side of that square, in whatever Sylvanas had already mapped.

She went back to the patients, making herself slow down and be thorough, checking each ward she had laid before she let herself think about the square again. The farmer's wife was still sleeping. The baker's wife was breathing more easily than she had been thirty minutes ago. The wards would hold for hours if nothing disturbed them, maybe longer.

The child in the corner watched her with the careful attention of someone who had decided to trust this particular person and was verifying that the decision was correct.

"Better," Jaina said. "For now."

The child's expression did not change, but her shoulders dropped a fraction, which was the same thing.

Jaina picked up her staff and walked to the door.

---

Sylvanas came out of the granary side door as Jaina crossed the square. There was dust on her gloves—pale grain-dust, dry against the dark leather—and she moved with the particular quality of someone who had just found what she was looking for and was already three steps ahead of the finding.

They stopped two paces apart.

"Grain," Jaina said.

Sylvanas held still for half a beat. "New supplier, end of summer. Same description as the man the girl mentioned—ordinary, pleasant, prices slightly under market. Two deliveries. Second batch used first; first still in the back of the store." She glanced down at the dust on her gloves. "The granary keeper says the bread changed after the new man's first delivery. His wife noticed the taste."

"The contamination enters through ingestion. It clusters in the gut first, then spreads from there. The worst cases are the heaviest bread-eaters. The shepherd who's been out with his flock for three weeks on dried provisions has the lightest case. The four-year-old who hasn't been eating bread has the faintest shadow of it in her stomach, probably from shared household air or handling, nothing more."

Sylvanas looked at her. Not at the staff, not at the square—at her.

"The first delivery is still in the back of the store," she said.

"We need to examine it."

Jaina had thought she would feel something clean when the two halves of it met—the satisfaction of a hypothesis confirmed. Instead she stood in the grain dust of a village square and heard the morning coughing continuing from somewhere in the row of houses behind the well, and the feeling was less like satisfaction than like the moment when a locked room's key turns and you understand that you now have to open the door and walk in.

"Two things fit," she said, because she was not yet willing to call it more than that and Sylvanas would not want her to. "The contamination is in the grain. Someone brought the grain."

"That is not a coincidence if the same description appears in two villages."

"No." She adjusted her grip on the staff. "It isn't."

Sylvanas pulled her gloves off and turned them in her hands, looking at the grain dust. It caught the morning light, pale and dry.

"The granary," she said.

They went.

---

Aldous let them into the back section without argument. He had gone quiet after Sylvanas's questions, the particular quiet of someone processing a sequence of things they had individually seen as ordinary and was now being asked to view as a chain.

The first delivery was stacked against the rear wall: eight sacks of winter wheat, sealed, the seam stitching still intact. Ordinary grain sacks in an ordinary granary. Jaina cast her reading before she touched anything.

The contamination came up immediately.

Not the faint trace she read in living bodies, the pattern filtered through tissue and metabolism. This was the source material—dense, deliberate, a concentration of the same necromantic signature woven into the grain itself, layered through it the way a dyer layered pigment through fiber. It did not look like contamination on the surface. It was inside the grain, distributed through the kernels, designed to survive milling and baking and the heat of a bread oven and whatever other processing happened between the storehouse and the stomach.

The design was sophisticated. That was the word she kept landing on. Sophisticated in a way that spoke of extended development, of refinement over multiple versions, of someone who had thought very carefully about how much contamination a body could absorb without dying before it could pass it on, and had arrived at an answer and built the quantity into the delivery.

This was not a field expedient. Someone had made this.

She took a step back from the sacks.

"It's in the grain," she said. "All eight sacks. The concentration is—" She stopped. There was not a useful word for it in the vocabulary of field assessment. "Significant. Enough to explain every case in that infirmary, and more if the second delivery had remained unused."

Aldous stood in the doorway. His hands had stopped moving.

"The second delivery," he said. "That's what we were eating from."

"That's in the bread your village has been eating for the past two months, yes." Jaina looked at him directly. "You need to close the bakery. Anything baked with grain from this delivery, dispose of it. Don't let it into the food supply. The patients in your infirmary who consumed bread from this supply will need continued treatment to prevent the contamination from spreading through their bodies—what I've done will hold for some hours, but I'll need to return or find a way to make the stabilization permanent." She watched his face process each sentence. "Do you understand what I'm telling you?"

"Someone put sickness in my grain on purpose," he said.

"Yes."

The quiet in the room after that was different from ordinary silence.

Then someone moved in the corner.

The far corner of the storeroom was built wider than the rest, a loading alcove from the granary's original construction, and there was a man in it. Not a large man—young, actually, which was the first thing Jaina registered, younger than she'd expected from the shadow, a boy grown tall enough to pass for a man, in a plain brown coat with grain dust on his knees as though he had been crouching there long enough to settle.

He was moving toward the lamp.

Sylvanas was faster.

The arrow crossed the room before Jaina had fully processed the motion—Sylvanas had the bow in her hand, drawn, and the arrow flew on a line that was not aimed at the boy but at the lamp itself, shattering the oil reservoir against the wall a foot from his reaching hand and sending a spray of oil sideways onto the grain sacks without igniting.

The boy froze.

"Don't," Sylvanas said.

A second arrow was already nocked. She had not blinked.

The boy's hand was still extended toward nothing, the arm trembling slightly with the aborted momentum of a reach. He was perhaps seventeen. He had a young man's unfinished face and the look of someone who had been standing in that corner for a long time expecting something that had not quite come and now was not sure what to do because the thing that had come was not the expected version.

Jaina raised both hands palm-out, empty, and took one step left so she was not between Sylvanas and the boy and was also visible to him.

"What's your name," she said.

His mouth opened, closed.

"Don't ask him his name," Sylvanas said. "Ask him who sent him here."

"I'm going to ask him his name," Jaina said, keeping her voice the way she had kept it in the cellar with Miri—even, low, room enough in it for someone frightened to think without the space being filled with threat. "What's your name."

"Davan," he said, barely audible.

"Davan. What were you doing in this room."

His eyes moved to the shattered lamp, the oil-dark sacks, the arrow in the wall, and she could see him doing the arithmetic that told him the thing he had been supposed to prevent had not happened, and he did not know yet what that meant.

"Watching," he said.

"Watching for what."

"For anyone who looked too closely."

Sylvanas's second arrow did not lower. Jaina kept her hands where they were.

"And if someone looked too closely," she said.

He did not answer that, which was an answer.

Cyndia appeared at the granary's main door—she had been outside, she had heard the arrow, and she was here now with her own bow and a line on the boy that triangulated with Sylvanas's and did not need discussing. Lyriel came in behind her and moved to Davan's left, hand on her blade, not drawing.

Jaina said, "Davan. Who gave you this grain."

He was shaking now, and she was not sure if it was the chill of the room or the two arrows or something else—something underneath both of those, something that was not exactly fear of being shot but was perhaps a different kind of fear wearing a similar face.

"A man on the road," he said. "He told me to take the wage and keep watch and if anyone opened the back half to make sure they didn't leave with answers."

"What was his name."

"He didn't say."

"What did he look like."

"Ordinary." He said it with a kind of flat defeat, as though the word had been used so many times it had worn itself smooth. "Brown coat. Good prices. Pleasant."

Jaina held her breath for a moment.

"What else," she said. "Anything specific. Hair, height, a mark, a horse."

"He had a ring. On his right hand. Not a fancy one, just iron, but it had a mark on it, worn down—I couldn't see it clearly." He swallowed. "He said if anything went wrong, I should make sure no one could read the grain. He said it was for the greater good."

"What organization did he represent."

A flicker behind Davan's eyes. "He didn't say."

She looked at him for a long moment. The shaking had not stopped.

"We're going to step outside," she said. "You're going to come with us peacefully. The rangers will stay close. You're not going to be harmed if you cooperate."

"Can we—" He stopped.

"What."

"Can we do it fast," he said. "He said if I was caught—" His throat moved. "He said to be fast about it."

Sylvanas lowered her bow one inch, no more. Jaina caught the movement in the corner of her vision and understood it: not trust, not mercy, the calculation of someone deciding whether the thing behind the fear was exploitable.

"We'll keep you safe," Jaina said.

She looked at Sylvanas and they both understood simultaneously that she was not sure that was a promise she could keep.

---

They walked him to the common house and put him in the storage closet beside the medic's room, the one with a door that locked from the outside and a window too small to fit a person through. Lyriel sat outside the door with her hand on her sword. Cyndia checked the window from the outside and came back with one finger held up: one exit, already covered.

The captain came when he heard the commotion and stood in the hallway with his arms crossed and his face doing what faces did when the thing you had been hoping was not what you feared turned out to be exactly what you feared.

"You said watching for anyone who looked too closely," he said. "At my granary. A boy I've seen in the market."

"He was placed there," Sylvanas said. "He is a paid watcher, not an architect. He does not have names, hierarchy, or organizational structure, but he may have other details worth extracting if given time."

"And the grain."

"Seal it. Do not open the back half. Do not let it into the food supply." Jaina set the staff against the wall. "I can return to mark the contaminated stores with a ward that will be visible to any mage, but the seal should happen now regardless."

The captain left to do it, moving with the particular purpose of a man who had stopped trying to fit new information into the old frame and was simply acting on it.

Jaina and Sylvanas stood in the hallway for a moment.

The smell of fever came through the thin wall from the infirmary, mixing with the clean cold from the window at the hall's end. From inside the closet, nothing. No movement, no sound.

Too quiet.

Jaina was moving before she had fully named why, pushing through the door with her palm—Lyriel swung open the closet door before she reached it, already up from the bench—

The boy was on the floor.

She dropped to her knees beside him and pressed two fingers to his throat and then spread both hands over his chest, the crystal flaring up bright, searching. The contamination inside him was not the grain-contamination she had been reading all morning. It was something faster, something concentrated and targeted, working through his blood, and it had a head start of—

"Two minutes," she said. "Maybe less."

She pushed every ward she had at it and found nothing useful to hold on to—it was not a spell that could be countered the way a spell could be countered. It was chemistry. Something he had taken before they locked the door. Something he had been carrying for exactly this moment.

The light in the crystal turned cold.

She sat back on her heels.

Lyriel did not move from the doorway.

Sylvanas crouched beside Jaina without touching the body, her eyes going over it with the systematic attention of a woman reading a scene.

"He swallowed it when we walked him in," Sylvanas said. "Before we locked the door. That was what the 'be fast about it' meant."

"Yes."

"He was trained for this."

"He was seventeen."

"He was trained for this," Sylvanas said, not louder, not colder, simply placing the statement back down with more weight on it, because it needed that weight. "A frightened boy who barely knew the name of the man who hired him was given a way to kill himself before he could be questioned, which means whoever is running this operation considers village watchers expendable and anticipated capture. That is not a grain merchant with a side project. That is an organization with a supply chain and a doctrine."

Jaina's hands were still warm from the failed work. She pressed them flat against her thighs.

"He said the man on the road told him it was for the greater good," she said.

"Yes." Sylvanas stood. "People who want things for themselves say they want them for themselves. People who tell frightened boys it's for the greater good while handing them poison are either fanatics or building something they need fanatics to operate."

She stepped back from the doorway and looked down the hall toward the sealed granary entrance.

"Andorhal," she said. "That's where the grain comes from. If the supply chain moves through the region's main distribution center, it's not originating here, and it's not originating at Millhaven. We follow the network southwest."

The village coughing persisted from somewhere on the other side of the wall—not worse than it had been this morning, the wards were holding—and Jaina stayed on her knees in the storage closet doorway for another moment, looking at a dead boy in a brown coat with grain dust on his knees, and thought about the bakery down the street and the communal pot and a child in braids who had held her arm out and asked if she could be fixed.

"The wards I laid will hold through today," she said. "They'll need renewal by tomorrow evening, and the patients who are already past early stage will need further treatment before they're stable."

"Leave instructions with the medic." Sylvanas moved to the hall window, looking out. "The captain has resources we don't have time to deploy. He can send for a mage from the nearest garrison if you give him the method."

"It requires—" She stopped. Assessed. It was not a complicated method; she could write it out. The medic could not perform it, but anyone with a basic arcane kit and the right document could. "I can do that."

"Then we leave at first light."

Jaina stood.

The crystal on her staff had gone back to its ordinary dim glow—the color it held when it was resting, not working—and the warmth in her hands was fading to ordinary cold.

"We leave at first light," she said. "The grain road southwest."

Outside, the village went on coughing in the ordinary-sounding morning, bread smoke and horse dung and cold air, and somewhere at the far end of a road that ran through granaries and storehouses and loading yards they couldn't see yet, something was moving on a longer schedule than one frightened boy in a brown coat.

She picked up the staff, straightened, and went to write instructions for the medic.
