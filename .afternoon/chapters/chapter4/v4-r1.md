# Chapter 4: The Sick Village

The village came through the grey-green Lordaeron trees in ordinary morning light: two wagons pulled up beside the common, smoke lifting straight from damp thatch, a dog raising hell behind the bakehouse, somebody swearing at a wheel that had found a rut and decided to stay there. The Thalassian horses dropped from a road gait to a walk without needing the bit hauled on them, quiet leather creaking, one snort sending hot breath white into the chill.

After Millhaven's silence, noise should have meant safety.

Then the smell reached her, faint and sweet under bread smoke, wet mud, horse dung, and the cold iron reek of the common well, and safety vanished with insulting speed.

Jaina pulled her mare down beside Sylvanas at the edge of the square, where stalls stood open on three sides of the common, their boards darkened by last night's damp. A woman crossed between them with a basket on her arm, a boy dragging one muddy boot behind him while two men argued over a crate with the flat, tired venom of people who still expected the day to go on after the argument was done.

"Two coughing by the bread stall," Sylvanas said. "One at the well. Child by the common house being held upright. And the butcher's shutters are closed."

Jaina followed the line of her gaze. The child was maybe seven, cheeks flushed the wrong way, her mother holding her by one shoulder to keep her upright. The bread seller bent to cough into his sleeve over a row of still-warm loaves, and the woman nearest him took one step sideways without making a show of it. Ordinary caution, ordinary denial; Lordaeron still working because nobody in the square had yet accepted that work itself was part of the trap.

The sweet wrongness lay through all of it. Strong enough already.

"Same as the south-road villages," Jaina said. "Early enough to hide under fever if you want to be stupid about it. If I can get to the sick, I'll know whether it's already settling in the gut."

"If the captain lets you near them."

Sylvanas had already found him: thickset, middle years, leather half-coat gone dark at the cuffs, planted by the well with the look of a man counting every stranger against the number of problems he could still keep in hand. Jaina would have gone straight at him with the whole answer and ruined the room inside three sentences. Sylvanas never hurried to spend words she could make other people spend first.

"I'll speak first," Sylvanas said.

Jaina let that stand.

They dismounted at the square's edge. Cyndia took both reins without being asked, settling the horses where they looked calm and expensive and only incidentally capable of breaking a man's jaw if he came at them wrong. Lyriel drifted toward the well with her bow slung and one hand loose at her side, positioned to see the common-house door, the lane behind the bakehouse, and half the square without seeming to claim any of it. Neither ranger made a performance of readiness. That was the Farstrider version of announcing themselves.

The captain met them halfway, boots leaving black crescents in the mud.

He was shorter than Sylvanas and wore authority the way some men wore winter gloves: already stiff with use, never fully off. His eyes took in elven ears, green and gold armor, bows, blades, then shifted to Jaina's staff and blue robes with immediate dislike.

"Farstriders don't ride this far south for courtesy calls," he said.

"No," Sylvanas said, stopping with the well curb to one side and the common house at her back, giving the man the square and still making it feel as if he had stepped into her ground. "What are you calling the sickness?"

His answer came too fast. "Fever. Bad run of it. We've had it before."

"You had fever in spring," Sylvanas said. "Half a dozen sick for a week, one tanner losing toes after the rot set in. This spreads faster. It goes for the gut and keeps going. That's different."

The captain's jaw set, his work-rough hands folding over each other, one knuckle on the right hand healed sideways from an old break. Jaina could have taken his measure from that alone: a man who still used his own fists, a man who had been plugging holes with himself for too long.

"And you know that because—"

"We came from Millhaven."

He said nothing after that; he did not have to. The square said it for him: the woman at the bread stall pretending not to listen, the cough someone tried and failed to hide by the well, the child sagging against her mother's arm.

Sylvanas let the silence do the heavy work.

He swallowed once. "How many?"

"Everyone," Sylvanas said. "Quietly. By the time they admitted it wasn't fever anymore, admitting it changed nothing. I have a mage with me. Let her examine your sick."

Only then did he turn to Jaina.

She kept still. Sylvanas was still solving the room.

"What's her read worth?" he said.

"She found contamination in Millhaven's grain stores, well water, and dead before she found one living child clean. She can tell you what is poisoning your people before nightfall."

At the far side of the square the dog barked again, then cut off sharp.

The captain looked at Jaina's staff, at the crystal wired into the head, at the split skin over two of her knuckles where old frost work had not fully forgiven her yet.

"Fine," he said at last. "Back room of the common house. Tomas Brek's using it as an infirmary. He hasn't slept in four days."

"Take the mage in," Sylvanas said. "I'll need whoever handles your stores, your well, and your deliveries."

"My wife keeps the storehouse accounts," he said, jerking his chin toward the square. "Aldous keeps the grain. He's by the granary if he hasn't found another reason to disappear."

"Good," Sylvanas said. "Show my ranger."

No wasted reassurance, and no promise this would end well. Jaina approved of that against her better judgment.

---

The back room of the common house had been built for barrels, trestle tables, and winter meetings, not seven fever pallets and a man trying to keep a village alive with boiled water and stubbornness. The ale shelves had been stripped bare. Two benches stood on end against the wall to make room for borrowed bunks. Damp wool, old smoke, and fever-sour bedding lay thick enough in the air to feel on the tongue.

Under all of it, the same sweet wrongness from the square.

Tomas Brek turned from the nearest pallet at the sound of the door. Compact man, beard gone more grey than brown, sleeves rolled past the elbow, drying herbs and a butcher's knife sharing table space with a chipped mug of cold broth. Running on no sleep and habit.

"Mage," he said.

"Jaina Proudmoore. Kirin Tor." She crossed to the nearest patient, setting the butt of her staff against the stone floor. "Tell me what you've been seeing."

"Fever by the third day in most cases, and sweating with it. Weakness. No appetite at first, then sudden hunger in some, then the gut turns and they can't keep anything. Cramping. Loose bowels. Breath sweet enough you can smell it from the next cot if the room's shut up." He scrubbed a hand over his mouth. "I've tried willow bark, tansy, boiled mint, watered broth. It barely slows them."

He gestured to the woman on the nearest pallet. "She came in yesterday evening and walked here on her own. This morning her husband had to carry her the last ten steps."

The woman lay with her hands open on the blanket, too slack even for sleep. Jaina pulled up a stool, set two fingers above the wrist, and lifted the crystal with her other hand.

The contamination answered at once, flaring through the crystal before she had settled her second breath.

She had seen its trace in corpses, in spoiling grain, in the well at Millhaven. Here it was alive, threaded through living tissue and still moving. The stain lay densest in the stomach and upper gut, dark amber against the blue lattice of healthy flesh, already pushing outward in the worst places as if the body had begun carrying the thing deeper by the simple act of staying alive.

"Has she been eating here?" Jaina said.

"Bread and broth from the common pot."

"Stop both. Water only until I tell you otherwise." Jaina kept reading. "Ask every patient where their bread comes from and what they eat when they are not in this room."

She moved to the next cot, finding the same pattern: gut first, then farther.

Brek started through the room at once, asking questions in the low voice of a man who had long since stopped expecting anyone to answer him quickly. Seven patients, all told. The worst were a miller's assistant with flour still trapped under his nails, a baker's wife who smelled faintly of yeast even through the fever, and a woman who had spent the past week helping the village prepare for an autumn gathering still stupidly scheduled for three days from now. The lightest cases belonged to a shepherd only just in from the outer pastures and a child whose mother apologized three separate times for keeping the family on oats whenever the wheat stores ran dear.

Jaina went bed to bed with the crystal, giving each body fifteen seconds—no treatment yet, only pattern. Always the gut. Always that dense sweet-rot signature. In the worst cases it had begun to touch the blood.

At the fourth cot she paused by the child with brown braids pulled up under her chin.

"I'm not here to hurt anyone," Jaina said.

The girl watched the crystal rather than Jaina's face. "You're a mage."

"Yes."

"Can you fix it?"

That deserved better than comfort she had not earned.

"I can slow it down while I learn enough to stop it," Jaina said. "Let me see."

The child held out one thin arm from under the blanket.

Only a shadow in the stomach, early enough to matter, and Jaina fed a narrow stabilizing ward through it. The contamination pulled against the cold and stopped where it was, as if it had met glass.

"That helps," the girl whispered, surprised despite herself.

"It buys time," Jaina said. "Hold still."

She worked back through the room, laying the same slowing wards into each body. Heat gathered first in her palms, then the familiar ache in the forearms and behind the eyes that meant she was pushing too much too quickly and would keep doing it anyway. The crystal burned blue through amber, blue through amber, the rhythm of intervention and resistance.

By the end, one farmer's wife had gone slack under the blankets in the heavy sleep fever had denied her for days. Brek looked at that and did not know whether to thank Jaina or mistrust the miracle.

Jaina stood, pressing her knuckles to the wall beside the desk. Cold stone. Useful. The room had no business holding this many bodies. Somebody had stacked ledgers, candles, and packets of dried feverfew on the desk's far corner to make space for a basin. Somebody else had dragged a church stool in from somewhere and never taken it back. The village had been improvising around the sickness for days and still calling it ordinary.

From the small window above Brek's desk she could see the square at an angle. Sylvanas stood under the granary eaves with a stout woman holding a leather ledger open against one hip and an older man in a flour-dusted apron worrying the edge of his sleeve between thick fingers. Grain dust streaked Sylvanas's gloves where she had already put a hand on sackcloth or door plank or both. She asked something, waited, and asked again, and the older man started pointing behind him before he had finished deciding whether he should.

She let the silence hold until he filled it; of course she did.

Jaina turned back to the room.

Bread from the common pot. Bread at home. Bread with every meal in the worst cases, while the shepherd on dried travel provisions and the oat-fed child carried only the lightest shadow. Water would have scattered the pattern differently—sharper day to day, stranger house to house—but this was steadier: repeated meals, repeated settling, repeated doses.

Through the edge of the window the bakehouse roof showed beyond the square, smoke breathing slow into the autumn air.

The grain went into bread, and the bread went into people.

If the taint sat in the grain, loud enough to survive storage and ovens the way the field pattern suggested—

She looked back out. Sylvanas had one gloved hand on the granary latch now. The keeper was talking too quickly. The captain's wife had turned a page in the ledger and flattened it with her thumb, pinning down deliveries, prices, dates.

Jaina forced herself to the patients one last time, checking each ward. They would hold for hours if left alone. Maybe longer in the light cases. The girl with the braids still watched her from the corner with grave concentration.

"Better," Jaina said.

The child relaxed by a fraction.

Jaina took up her staff. The crystal was warm in her palm. The answer had edges now, and edges invited hurry before certainty was ready for it.

The granary would have to supply the certainty.

---

Outside, the square had shifted without changing shape—same wagons, same stalls, same coughs trying and failing to pass for ordinary. But now Jaina could feel the route under it all as cleanly as a ward line once someone had scraped the mud off the stone.

Sylvanas came out from the granary side door as Jaina crossed the common. Grain dust striped the dark leather of her gloves and one forearm. The captain's wife had the ledger tucked under her arm now; the keeper hovered two steps behind them with the look of a man trying to decide whether terror counted as an excuse.

They stopped under the granary eaves, close enough that Jaina could see the fine pale dust caught in the seams of Sylvanas's gauntlet.

"Grain," Jaina said, and Sylvanas answered at the same time.

"New supplier at summer's end," Sylvanas said, not looking at Jaina. "Pleasant. Forgettable. Prices just below the usual rate. Two deliveries so far. The bread changed after the first one."

Jaina glanced at the ledger, dates marching down the page in the captain's wife's blunt hand, then a different ink for purchases: wheat, rye, communal allotment, bakehouse issue.

"People eating the village bread are carrying the worst of it," Jaina said. "It settles in the stomach first, then spreads. The shepherd out on dried stores barely shows a trace. The oat-fed child in the common house is only just touched."

The keeper swallowed. "The first delivery's still stacked in the back half. We hadn't cracked all of it yet."

Sylvanas kept her hand on the latch. "Untouched since it came in."

"Then we read that before anyone moves a sack."

Jaina had expected satisfaction when the halves met. Instead she felt the speed of it—the ugly efficiency—and the village had gone on baking, serving, eating, and coughing while the answer sat twenty feet away behind a plank door.

"The sickness is in the grain," she said. "Someone put it there."

"In two villages now," Sylvanas said. "With the same sort of man behind both."

Not proof of identity, not yet, but no longer a frightened child's loose memory either.

Jaina tightened her grip on the staff. "Yes."

"Then the granary."

They went in with the keeper ahead of them, Cyndia and Lyriel peeling off from their corners of the square to take the doorways with the neat timing of people who had been reading the room since Jaina stepped out of the infirmary.

The back half of the granary smelled of old wood, dry chaff, mouse droppings, and the warm sweetness of winter stores. Eight sacks of wheat stood against the rear wall, their seam-stitching still tight, each marked in charcoal with the same account hand Jaina had just seen in the ledger. A loading alcove opened to one side where handcarts could back under shelter during rain. A clay lamp hung from an iron bracket on the post beside it.

Ordinary sacks in an ordinary village storehouse.

Jaina cast before she touched them.

The contamination flared bright enough to make her teeth ache.

This was the thing itself, dense and deliberate, worked through the kernels so evenly it would survive milling, kneading, baking, and the oven's heat before it ever reached a human stomach. The outer shell sang loudest, just as she'd suspected, but there was another layer buried deeper in the kernels, waiting for the body to finish the work.

She stepped back, hard enough for the hem of her robe to whisper through the grain dust.

"It's in all eight sacks," she said. "Enough to explain every patient in that room, and more."

Aldous stopped dead in the doorway when she said it. "The second delivery. That's what we've been eating from."

"Then shut the bakehouse and the common pot now," Jaina said. "Anything made from this grain stays out of mouths from this moment forward. The wards in the common house will hold till evening. After that the worst cases need renewing."

He stared at the sacks.

"Someone put sickness in my grain on purpose," he said.

"Yes."

No one moved for half a breath; then motion flickered in the loading alcove.

Jaina turned. A young man in a brown coat had been crouched behind a stack of split boards, grain dust thick on his knees and cuffs. One hand shot for the lamp bracket. The other jerked toward a squat clay vessel beside it, wax seal already broken at the lip.

Sylvanas's arrow shattered the lamp before Jaina finished drawing breath.

Oil burst across the post and stone without catching. The clay vessel went over with it, struck the floor, and broke open in a wet crack. A ribbon of dark syrup spread through the grain dust, and the sweet wrong smell that came off it was strong enough to sting the back of Jaina's throat.

Concentrated. Not spillover. This was what they poured in before it ever touched the grain.

"Don't," Sylvanas said.

Her second arrow was already on the string, aimed low and steady at the boy's ribs.

He could not have been more than seventeen. His hand still hung toward the ruined lamp, shaking with the force of the movement he had stopped too late.

Cyndia filled the main doorway with her bow up. Lyriel came in behind her and cut off the yard side, hand on her sword, leaving the boy nowhere to go except deeper into the grain and the arrow waiting there for him.

Jaina eased one step sideways so he could see her as well as the bow, keeping Sylvanas in the edge of his sight.

"What's your name?"

"Don't ask for his name," Sylvanas said. "Ask who sent him."

"Davan," the boy whispered, because fear made children and boys his age answer the wrong question first.

"Davan, then." Jaina kept her voice level. "What were you doing in here?"

His gaze jumped from the oil-slick post to the arrow to the black syrup creeping through the chaff at his boots. "Watching."

"For what?"

"For anyone who looked too closely."

"Who gave you the grain?"

He was shivering now, the tremor showing in the line of his jaw. "A man on the road. He paid me to watch the back half. Said if anyone opened it, I was to make sure they didn't leave with answers."

"His name?"

"He didn't give one."

"What did he look like?"

"Ordinary." Defeat flattened the word as he looked down. "Brown coat. Brown hair. Good prices. Pleasant."

Same better-price story. Same road face built to slide past memory. The thing had hands inside the road, and they wore whatever shape would get through a village gate.

"Anything else," Jaina said, leaning in a fraction. "Horse. Height. A mark."

"Iron ring on his right hand. Worn smooth. Couldn't read the sign." Davan swallowed. "He said if anything went wrong, I was to be fast about it. Said it was for the greater good."

Sylvanas did not lower the bow, her draw steady. "Come with us."

"Can we do it fast?" Davan asked, eyes fixed somewhere past Jaina's shoulder.

Jaina's skin went cold for reasons that had nothing to do with magic. "What?"

"If I get caught"—the words scraped out of him—"he said to be fast."

That landed wrong enough to matter. Jaina felt it; Sylvanas went still beside her.

"You are caught," Sylvanas said. "So do exactly as you're told and keep breathing."

Jaina looked once at the broken clay, the dark liquid soaking into dust and plank seams, then at the sacks stacked in their clean account order against the wall. Not one bad granary. Road and storehouse both. Hands moving it on purpose.

---

They locked him in the narrow storage closet beside the infirmary because it had one door, one high window, and stone walls that had once held winter ale instead of prisoners. Lyriel took the bench outside with her bow across her knees. Cyndia checked the yard side of the window, coming back with two fingers lifted. Covered.

The captain arrived hard-footed and angry enough to shake water from the hem of his half-coat.

"You said he was watching for anyone who looked too closely," he said. "At my granary."

"He was placed there," Sylvanas said. "A watcher, not the hand that tainted the grain."

"And the grain?"

"Seal the back half now," Jaina said. "Nothing from it goes into bread, porridge, or feed. Have every loaf already baked this morning pulled apart and kept from tables if you still can."

He stared at her. "If I still can."

Honesty had already cost him too much to sand down now. "Some of it is already in people."

His mouth hardened. The lie was gone from him at last, leaving the narrower misery of a man who knew what kind of fight he had been losing.

"Tell Brek what he needs," he said.

"Written instructions," Jaina said. "And a mage from your nearest garrison by tomorrow if one can be had."

He went at once, angry now at the granary, the road, the unseen hands behind both—anything except the strangers who had forced him to stop lying to himself.

Jaina and Sylvanas stood in the hall for one breath after he was gone, fever-sour air coming off the infirmary, cold sliding under the outer door, candle smoke sitting in the throat. Lyriel's glove scraped once on bow wood.

Nothing came from the closet.

Too quiet.

Jaina moved before the thought fully formed; Lyriel was faster to the latch, kicking the bench back against the wall as she yanked the door open.

Davan lay on the stone floor under the high window.

Jaina dropped beside him, fingers going to the throat first, then the crystal, then both hands spreading over chest and jaw as the magic flared white-blue. Warm still, a bare trace of foam at the mouth, something racing through the blood with a lead she could not claw back—and by the time she found it she already knew she was late.

"Two minutes," she said. "Less."

She threw ward after ward at it and found nothing to catch. Not necromancy. Not contamination. Poison already dissolving past the point where her work could pin it down.

The crystal's light went cold in her hands, and she sat back on her heels.

Sylvanas crouched beside the body without touching it, checking the mouth, the throat, the cuff seam, the inside of one sleeve with the economy of a ranger who had seen smuggled death before.

"Capsule," she said, checking the cuff seam again. "He took it when we walked him in."

"Yes."

"That was what he meant."

"Yes."

Lyriel swore once under her breath and went still again. Cyndia looked toward the outer door, fingers tightening on her bow until the leather creaked.

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the dead boy. "He was trained for this."

"He was seventeen."

"He was trained for this," Sylvanas said again, lower now. "Someone put a village boy in that granary with poison in his sleeve and orders for when to use it."

Jaina pressed her still-warm hands against her thighs because there was nowhere else useful to put them. The closet smelled of spilled ale long sunk into wood, old stone, and the sharp bitter trace the poison had left at the back of Davan's mouth.

"He said it was for the greater good."

"Of course he did." Sylvanas rose in one smooth motion. "Men building this kind of work always need a noble phrase for the part where children die."

She turned into the hall, dragging one dust-striped glove down the doorframe and leaving a pale streak in the old varnish. The cough from the infirmary carried through the wall as steadily as before, one house at a time translated into one room, one pallet, one bowl of water, one ward that would fail if Jaina did not leave instructions behind.

"Andorhal," Sylvanas said. "If tainted grain is moving village to village, we stop chasing single carts and go to the place that taught the road how to do this."

Andorhal. Not a skyline in Jaina's mind but loading yards, clerks, manifests, grain dust over everything, and enough storehouses to drown a kingdom by ledger.

She looked back once at Davan on the stone, brown coat bunched under one shoulder, then thought of the girl with the braids in the common room and the shepherd barely touched and the baker's wife already too weak to sit up on her own.

"The wards will hold through today," she said. "Tomorrow evening at the latest, the worst cases will need renewing, though the lighter ones may carry longer if the bread stops now."

"Then leave the method."

"It's careful work."

"Not beyond a trained garrison mage."

No. It was not. Any Kirin Tor journeyman with a steady hand and a serviceable kit could manage it. Brek could not; the village certainly could not. But a garrison mage might, if the captain found one before the road delivered another poisoned sack someplace farther south.

"I'll write it out," Jaina said.

"Then we leave at first light."

She rose. The crystal had dimmed to its resting glow. The warmth had already gone out of her fingers, leaving them stiff around the staff.

"At first light," she said. "Southwest."

Outside, the village kept coughing in the cold while bread smoke rose over the roofs unchanged. Jaina set her ward notes on Brek's scarred desk, pushed aside feverfew and ledgers to make room, and wrote until the instructions were clean enough for another mage to trust: sequence, pressure, where to place the hand, how long to hold the cold before the contamination answered back.

When she finished, the ink on the last line was still wet. Beyond the common-house window, carts groaned through the mud and somebody shouted at a mule that did not care.

Somewhere past the village, beyond more barns, more bakehouses, more storehouses, Andorhal was already loading the next answer onto wheels.
