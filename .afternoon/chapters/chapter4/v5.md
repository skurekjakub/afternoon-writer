# Chapter 4: The Sick Village

The road south had spent two days teaching Jaina how easily disaster could hide inside good weather.

Fields already cut for harvest. Sheds open to the air. Women snapping linens over fences while children ran under the cloth and came out laughing. Carts creaked. Dogs barked. Smoke rose where it ought to. If she had not known what to listen for, the country might have passed for healthy.

The horses knew first. Weariness, then refusal. Jaina's mare had started eyeing the village road hard half a mile out. By the first cottages she was tossing her head at a sweetness smeared under bread smoke and wet earth.

A child crossed the lane with kindling in both arms, fever-dark and still walking. A man at the pump coughed into his sleeve, spat in the mud, and put his shoulder back into the handle. Somewhere a baker knocked loaves from a board. The thump came through the morning noise clean and hard.

Alive, then. Worse for that.

"There," Jaina said, pointing toward the broadest building off the square. "That ought to be where they're keeping the worst."

Sylvanas swept lanes, roofs, alley mouths, the well, the men pretending not to stare before she looked where Jaina pointed. Cyndia and Lyriel had already spread just enough to show they were not careless.

"And why," Sylvanas said, "ought it be there?"

"If I had six cots and no infirmary, I'd put them where air can move."

"Useful. Stay here."

The refusal scraped Jaina's throat. "Absolutely not."

Blue-green eyes pinned her.

"You were told to stay here."

"And you were told Millhaven tells us nothing if the living die before I can read them."

One corner of Sylvanas's mouth twitched. "You improve. Keep that habit."

Then she turned her horse toward the square.

The militia captain met them halfway: thick through the chest, beard going iron at the chin, one hand already up in warning.

"Hold there. No need for weapons in the middle of my village."

No one had drawn. The mistake crossed his face at once. He had expected swagger. Men he could crowd. Instead he got an armored elf who sat her horse above argument, a travel-stained mage too tired for pride, and two silent rangers already seeing too much.

"Your village is coughing," Sylvanas said. "That makes a need."

"A fever run makes work. It doesn't make it your business."

Jaina's answer crowded her teeth. Sylvanas spent none of it.

"Millhaven is dead," she said.

The captain's jaw tightened. "I've heard stories."

"Then hear one from someone who walked through it. Tables laid. Doors open. No living voice left in the place but one girl under earth. The dead had already taken the north road."

The square shifted. Two women at the pump stopped pretending not to listen. The baker's board went quiet.

He looked to Jaina. Staff, robe, mage: the kind of proof he trusted.

"You can prove that?"

"I can prove your people are carrying the same wrongness," Jaina said. "Whether you have time to be offended depends on how long you keep me out of your sickroom."

The captain opened his mouth.

"You can argue after she's done," Sylvanas said.

He looked back at her.

"Captain." She lowered her voice until he leaned nearer to hear it. "If this is only fever, I leave and you keep your pride. If it isn't, every minute you spend proving your authority costs you people who still might live."

He blew air through his nose. "Dane Hall. We moved the worst there yesterday. The medic's inside. If you frighten them, that's on you."

"Everything in this village looks ready to frighten itself," Sylvanas said.

Dry enough that Jaina nearly missed the humor. The captain did not. The line in his face eased and he stepped aside.

Jaina swung down before her mare had fully settled.

The hall had been built for weddings and winter suppers. It smelled of rush mats, stale beer in old wood, lamp grease, boiled cloth, and under all of it the sweet wrongness on sick breath.

Cots jammed every space and still were not enough. Two children lay on pallets between benches. An old woman had been propped against the wall because no frame remained. A man sat over a bucket, shaking, while someone who must have been his wife held the back of his neck and stared at Jaina as if she had brought either salvation or insult.

The medic came at her fast. "If you're here to tell me it's marsh fever, I've already heard it from three farmers and a priest—"

"Good," Jaina said. "Then you have practice being wrong."

He stopped. Red eyes. Grey stubble. Sleeves rolled to the elbow. Hands scrubbed raw. He looked from her face to the staff and decided anything strange was better than another argument.

"What do you need?"

"Quiet. And whoever knows which of these people ate what."

He blinked. "Ate what?"

"Do you want the long version?"

"No."

"Then I need the room and your memory."

He gave her both.

Jaina woke the crystal in her staff properly for the first time that morning. Blue-white ran through it, then steadied into a thinner light she could hold without wasting herself. She moved from cot to cot, not touching at first, letting the spell skim skin, throat, belly, the hollows under the ribs. The pattern showed itself faster in the living than it had in Millhaven's aftermath. Alive, it was busier. Greedier.

Gut first.

She stopped over the man with the bucket. Sweet breath. Damp skin. Pulse skipping under the jaw. Amber filaments drew through his middle, a fouled knot under the stomach spreading outward in finer threads.

Something taken in again and again until the body had started helping it.

"How long?" she asked.

The wife answered. "Three days on the belly. Fever by last night."

"What has he eaten?"

"What does everyone eat?" Suspicion crept in before the words were out. "Broth. Bread. What we can keep down."

Jaina moved on.

Same knot in the gut. Worse in some, lighter in others. A boy with sunken eyes carried it only faintly. The old woman cursing Jaina's cold hands carried it deep. A child sleeping too hard under a patched blanket made her stop altogether. The contamination sat in her like a banked coal.

Jaina set the butt of her staff to the floor and built the ward carefully.

She had no cure. Only a way to slow a door from opening.

Blue light sank around the child in a thin shell, visible only in the way the air bent. The little body twitched once, then loosened. Breathing eased. In the corner the mother made a sound into both hands and held it there.

"That buys time," Jaina said. "Nothing more. Do not spend it by giving her more bread."

The medic stared from the child to her hands. "You can stop it?"

"I can slow some of them. If you want stopping, tell me where it enters."

"You think it's food."

"Their bellies know something the rest of the village hasn't admitted."

His mouth flattened. "We've had foul wells before. A spoiled cask can run through half a street."

"Show me the households that stayed light."

He had not expected the question.

"Marta and her boys," he said slowly. "They were sick, but not like this. The widow in the south lane. Two men from the tannery. One of the shepherd families."

"And what do they have in common?"

"How should I know?"

"Because you've been watching them live through it and I have only just arrived."

He scrubbed a hand over his mouth. Thought. To his credit, he thought instead of taking offense.

"The widow keeps oats. Hates mill prices. Marta's eldest broke a tooth and they've been feeding him broth more than bread. The shepherds don't buy much from the baker. They eat what they keep."

There.

A shape, at least.

Jaina pushed the shuttered window wider. The square lay beyond in slices: captain, frightened villagers, Cyndia at the pump, Lyriel by the lane mouth, and Sylvanas speaking to an old man in an apron dusted to the elbows.

Three short questions from Sylvanas; five useful answers out of the man's face before his mouth caught up.

Jaina looked back at the room.

Bread baskets. Crumbs. A knife left in a loaf on the trestle.

She crossed to the table and pressed two fingers into the cut loaf. Her spell answered at once, brighter than it had over the weaker patients.

"You idiot," she said softly, not to the medic.

He misheard it anyway. "I was trying to keep them alive."

"I know."

She turned the loaf over. Flour dusted her gloves. Warm room. Warm bread. Village life still going through its motions while every shared meal laid down another layer.

"How many households grind their own?"

"A few. Hardly enough."

"How many buy from the baker?"

He looked at the baskets and the pallets, the answer already in front of him.

"Most."

Jaina braced one hand on the trestle and shut her eyes long enough to fit the pieces together. Gut first. The spared households eating differently. The sweetness on infected breath. Repeated feedings until the body took the lesson and carried it inward.

When the pattern locked, her arm went still to the wrist.

"What?"

"Bread," she said.

"Bread can carry sickness."

"This bread. This flour. The body takes it in a little at a time and it settles where food settles first."

The medic stared at the loaf as if it might answer him.

"Sweet Light," he said.

"No," Jaina said, already moving for the door. "Grain."

Outside, the square had shifted again.

The captain stood with the older man in the apron, a narrow-shouldered woman dusted at both cuffs, and Sylvanas with one glove resting lightly on her saddle pommel. Grain dust filmed the glove.

Sylvanas turned as the hall door banged behind Jaina. "Well?"

"It's the grain," Jaina said. "Or what they've done to it before it becomes bread."

The captain swore. The older man went white.

"You say that now," Sylvanas said. No challenge in it. Only: what changed.

"Because now I can earn it. The worst cases cluster in the belly first. The people living light on bakery flour are ill, but they are not failing at the same speed."

"And outside the hall," Sylvanas said, "our new grain seller arrived three months ago with lower prices and no one can remember his face five breaths after describing it. Pleasant. Forgettable. Different wagon paint from week to week. Same stock. Same route. Millhaven's old man had company here after all."

Irritation flashed through Jaina. She had been right, but not finished. Magic had given her the carrier. Sylvanas had given her the path.

"It fits exactly," she said.

"It does."

She had the oddest urge to laugh. Nothing in the square deserved laughter. Still, hearing Sylvanas say what Jaina had already begun to think made the whole village tilt truer under her boots.

She looked to Sylvanas before she spoke again. New enough to sting.

"Where's the granary keeper?"

The dust-cuffed woman swallowed. "Inside. My father—"

"Good," Sylvanas said. "Then he can open it again."

The captain bristled. "Again?"

"If someone's feeding this through your stores on purpose, captain, then your granary is evidence."

"If there's treated stock left," Jaina said, "I need to see how it's carried. Mixed, washed, kept apart. If it started in liquid form, it would travel better."

Sylvanas's eyes flicked over her face once.

"Can you stand long enough to prove it?"

"Can you ask a useful question without sounding pleased with yourself?"

One of Cyndia's eyebrows rose. Lyriel looked away with suspicious care. The captain stared between them.

"Captain," Sylvanas said. "Two of yours with us. No one else goes near the grain until the mage tells us what part of it wants burning."

Then she was moving.

The granary sat behind the square with its long side to the road and its doors barred for the afternoon. Jaina smelled it before they reached it: dry grain, old wood, mouse droppings, and under all of it the same sweetness, now stripped of disguise.

The captain's men went ahead with the keeper. One lifted the bar. The other took the hanging lamp and held it shoulder-high as the first plank opened.

Movement broke inside at once.

Too tall for a rat. Too fast for any frightened animal in a grain room.

A young man in work clothes lunged from behind the nearest stack, one hand already on the lamp, the other reaching for a clay jug at his feet. He had the raw, cornered look of someone who had spent all morning pretending not to hear suspicion sharpening outside his walls and had run out of time.

"No," Jaina snapped.

Sylvanas was quicker.

The arrow left her bow with so little waste Jaina barely caught the draw. It took the young man through the sleeve and into the post behind him before the lamp could swing. He cried out. The lamp hit the floor in oil and sparks.

Frost sheeted across the boards. Flame bit once, hissed, died.

The clay jug flew from his other hand and shattered against a sack edge instead of the floor.

Sweetness hit the room hard and close.

Concentrated this time. Clean. Made.

Jaina stepped through the doorway before anyone could stop her. "Don't touch it."

Nobody looked eager.

The shards lay in a pale slick darkening the dust around them. The smell coming off it had none of the village blur she had been reading all morning. This was cleaner. Skilled. Made to ride quietly until the second half woke in flesh.

She knelt and held the crystal over it.

The light hated the stuff. It whitened hard, then broke into fine trembling lines that ran over the spill and back into her hand. Recognition came without a name. Warnings overheard in Dalaran corridors after lectures ended. A branch we do not teach. Methods better left buried.

She knew necromancy. Any apprentice worth the name knew the broad shape of it. This was handwork. Someone had learned how to make death travel as craft.

"What is it?" Sylvanas asked behind her.

"This isn't hedge poison," Jaina said. "It was made to survive a road, a storehouse, an oven, and still wake when the body gave it enough time. Whoever did it knew exactly what they were building."

The pinned young man had stopped struggling. Terror had left him almost limp.

"I didn't mean to spill it," he said.

The captain rounded on him. "Gods damn you, Tomas. What did you do?"

"Nothing. I watched the loads. That's all. I only watched."

Sylvanas stepped into his sightline and every other voice in the room flattened.

"Who told you to burn it?"

He looked at her with naked fear.

"No one."

"That's a lie."

"I—"

Jaina moved past the spill into the darker back half of the granary. Rows of sacks. Scoops. Ledgers on a narrow desk. Two barrels set apart from the rest and marked with chalk lines already half-rubbed away. Grain dust lay over everything, but not evenly.

On the nearest stack, burned into the weave, was a shipping mark she had not seen in Millhaven because Millhaven had left too little intact to read. A block letter over a mill-sign. Another stack bore the same mark, then a second stamp beneath it in darker ink: a warehouse sigil and an abbreviated place-name.

Andorhal.

Route, if not source.

The ledger told the same story in uglier handwriting. Dates. Weights. Lot numbers. Grain in by one road and out by six others. Jaina could not take in the whole page, only enough to see destinations radiating southwest and back again through the village.

"Sylvanas."

The room turned. Sylvanas came at once.

Jaina turned the ledger toward her and touched the stamped sacks with the crystal's light. "These are recent. Same store-mark over and over. Southwest route. Andorhal."

The captain took Andorhal harder than Millhaven. Millhaven was horror close enough to smell. Andorhal was scale.

"That's half the kingdom's flour," he said.

"Which is why someone put a watcher in your granary instead of trusting the work to carry itself," Sylvanas said.

The boy on the post made a broken noise.

"I didn't—"

"You are exactly what you look like," Sylvanas said. "A village pair of hands taught to spill a lamp and keep his mouth shut."

His face lost what little blood it had left.

Cyndia and Lyriel moved on him together. The arrow came out. He folded before he understood he was no longer pinned. Cyndia caught one arm. Lyriel took the other.

Cyndia's hand clamped over his jaw at once, thumb forcing his mouth open, while Lyriel ran quick fingers through sleeves, belt, and boots. Nothing. Tomas gagged, twisted, nearly bit Cyndia for the trouble.

"Keep him breathing," Sylvanas said. "Captain, where do you keep drunks or thieves when they need a door between them and the rest of you?"

"Store room off the yard."

"Then show us."

The room was little more than a lockable shed behind the hall: bench, wall ring, smell of old straw. They sat the watcher against the stone and bound his wrists before he found enough spine to throw himself about. Cyndia took the left. Lyriel the door. The captain stayed. The medic arrived because word outran them.

Jaina stood just inside the threshold with the ledger in one hand and the aftertaste of the spill in her mouth.

Sylvanas crouched in front of the prisoner, forearms on her knees, all her stillness gathered into a point sharp enough to enter skin.

"Name."

"Tomas."

"Who hired you?"

"No one hired me."

"Who told you when to watch the granary?"

"I always watched it."

"Who brought the jug?"

His lips pressed together. A child might have done it the same way, if that child had been taught silence would save his soul.

The captain swore again. "He was on barley counts last winter. My own sister's boy."

That, Jaina thought, was how these things spread best. Through nephews. Through boys told to mind one corner of the work and believe it small.

"How many villages?" Sylvanas asked.

He stared at the dirt between her boots. "I don't know."

"Lie smaller. It makes you sound brighter."

"I don't know," he said again, and this time Jaina believed at least part of it.

He knew just enough to keep the lamp near and the jug hidden.

Jaina stepped forward. "Tomas. Look at me."

He did.

"That liquid in the granary wasn't meant to make people ill for a week and let them recover. It was meant to fill them slowly until death came easier and did more work after. If you know a name, a road, a place where the next loads are being touched, this is the moment to stop serving the thing that picked you."

His eyes filled so fast she nearly mistook it for remorse.

"I never touched the loads," he whispered. "They said watch. They said if anyone came asking too much, I was to spoil the stock and keep my mouth shut."

"Who said?"

His throat worked.

Nothing came.

Then he bent forward in a convulsion so sharp Jaina thought for one absurd instant he was about to vomit up honesty.

Instead foam touched the corner of his mouth.

She moved before the captain finished shouting. She forced his jaw open and saw the blood where he had already bitten down on something hidden in the back of his cheek. Crushed kernel. Poison. Quick and thorough.

"No."

She reached for frost because cold was quickest and got a barrier over throat and chest fast enough to steal one more moment than the poison had planned.

Too late.

His eyes went wide at her, then wider still with surprise, as if even now he had expected a little more time.

The light in her staff fluttered, failed, came back thin.

By the time the medic knelt at her shoulder there was nothing left to slow.

Tomas sagged sideways against the wall ring, warm still, foam bright on his lip in the last slant of daylight.

No one spoke for several breaths.

The captain crossed himself once, hard. Lyriel was already looking to the yard, the hall, the lane beyond. Cyndia's face gave nothing.

"Then we take what he left us," Sylvanas said.

Jaina let go of the jaw carefully and got to her feet slower than she wanted seen. Fatigue had been stalking her since morning. Now it came in close, helped along by anger.

"The ledger. The marks on the sacks. The treated barrels. That's enough to move on."

"Then to Andorhal," Sylvanas said.

"To Andorhal," Jaina agreed.

The captain looked between them. "You mean to leave."

"I mean to keep your village from becoming the next place we save too late," Sylvanas said.

Jaina rubbed the heel of one hand across her brow. "Listen carefully. Anyone still walking and only feverish may have time if you cut the bread now. No bakery loaves. No common flour from the granary. Oats if households still have them. Root broth. Anything not milled from the recent stock. The worst cases can be slowed, not healed, if I mark them. The children first. Then anyone who turned bad in the last day."

He stared, face drawn tight around every person he was already counting.

"And if they beg for bread?"

"Then tell them no."

"You make that sound simple."

"It isn't. Do it anyway."

She went back through the hall once more with the last of her strength parceled meanly. Blue light over one child, then another. A younger man not yet fully taken. The old woman with enough spite to call her a heartless cow when Jaina slapped bread from her hand, which Jaina took as proof she might survive the night.

Outside, Sylvanas had turned the square back into an order.

The granary doors stood open while the captain's men dragged suspect sacks into a separate line under Lyriel's eye. Cyndia nailed broken shutters over the yard room until someone could decide what the village did with its dead. The baker wept over the nearest pile of flour as though grief might bleach it clean.

Sylvanas ignored the grief and used the useful parts.

"Seal the granary," she told the captain. "Nothing in or out without your hands on it. Post men who won't drink and won't steal."

"That leaves me three."

"Then pray three is enough until morning."

"And if the villagers fight me?"

"They will. Tell them their dead north road came from the same sacks as their supper."

The captain looked toward the hall, where the coughing had started again, soft and human and not yet lost.

"And if they still don't listen?"

Sylvanas's face did not change. "Then choose whether you want to command a village or bury one."

Jaina came down the hall steps with her staff braced across both palms. The world had narrowed to the next quarter hour: saddle, water, road, the light left in the sky.

Sylvanas read the calculation on her face. "How many did you buy?"

"Some." Jaina swallowed against a throat gone dry from spellwork. "Hardly enough."

"Good. I'd have worried if you claimed more."

It helped anyway.

They mounted in the failing light while the captain set men to the granary door and the medic began the ugly work of explaining to frightened people why the thing they trusted most on the table had to go.

From horseback the village looked ordinary again in patches. A woman crossing a lane with a kettle. A boy carrying split wood. Smoke from a cookfire climbing into evening. Only now Jaina could see the pattern under it, quiet and fed and waiting.

"Andorhal first?" she asked.

"Southwest by the road. Records if we can find them. More watchers if we cannot."

Jaina nodded. "You'll want the order of the sick written down before we lose the shape of this."

"I know."

Jaina looked once over her shoulder at the hall, the granary, the square still trying to behave like a square under a sky going to iron.

Millhaven had been aftermath. This was worse. This was the hour before a place understood it had already begun dying.

She drew in cooling bread smoke and dust. "The body helps it. The grain carries the first cut."

"Then we'll follow whoever keeps sharpening the blade," Sylvanas said.

Dry again. Flat enough to pass for simple sense. But useful.

No thaw. No trust spoken aloud. Only the ugly fact that the answer came clearer when the two halves met.

The quartet rode out as the first lamps came up in the cottages behind them.

On the road beyond the village the air cooled fast. Wheels had bitten deep into the mud here in recent days. Grain wagons, Jaina thought, and once the thought would have stayed with the magic of them alone. Now she found herself looking where the ruts forked, which turn took the heavier load, where a man would stop if he meant to watch a village without seeming to.

Ahead, the road bent southwest.

Andorhal waited somewhere beyond the dim fields, warehouse by warehouse, ledger by ledger, with half Lordaeron's bread running through its hands.
