# Chapter 4: The Sick Village

The village appeared through the trees in ordinary morning light: smoke rising straight from a handful of chimneys, a dog barking somewhere beyond the square, wagon wheels knocking over stone. After Millhaven's silence, noise should have meant safety. Then the smell reached her, faint and sweet under woodsmoke, horse dung, and wet autumn mud, and safety vanished.

Jaina pulled her horse down to a walk at the edge of the common. Stalls stood open on three sides of the square, a woman crossing between them with a basket on her arm while a boy dragged one muddy boot behind him and a man argued with a carter over a crate. It all looked normal.

"Two coughing at the bread seller's stall," Sylvanas said beside her, still scanning. "One at the well. Child by the common house being held upright. And the butcher's door is closed."

Jaina followed her line of sight. The child was maybe seven, pink-cheeked in the wrong way, her mother's hand fixed on her shoulder to keep her steady. The bread seller turned to cough into his elbow. The woman nearest him shifted away without making it obvious.

The smell threaded through all of it. Strong enough.

"Same as the south-road villages," Jaina said. "Early. If we get close enough, I can tell whether it's already in the gut."

"If the captain lets us near his sick." Sylvanas had already found him, thickset in a worn leather half-coat, planted by the well with the look of a man counting trouble. "I'll speak first."

Jaina let that stand. Sylvanas had opened three militia checkpoints since they left the south road, always by answering less than people expected and letting them step into their own silence. Jaina would have explained too much. Sylvanas never hurried to fill the gap.

They dismounted at the square's edge. Cyndia took both horses without being asked and settled them where they looked calm and dangerous at once. Lyriel drifted toward the well, loose-handed, bow slung, able to see two streets and the common-house door. Neither ranger made a show of it.

The captain met them halfway.

He was shorter than Sylvanas and wore his authority close, ready to defend it. He took in elven ears, Farstrider armor, then swords. His expression hardened.

"Farstriders don't come this far south for courtesy calls," he said.

"No," Sylvanas said. "What are you calling the sickness."

He heard the trap in that at once. "Fever. Bad run of it. We've had it before."

"You had fever in spring. Half a dozen sick for a week, one tanner losing toes after the infection turned. This spreads faster. It gets deeper. That's different."

He set his jaw. "And you know that because—"

"We came from Millhaven."

The name struck home. He had heard some softened version of it; she let him sit with the fact that hers would be worse.

He folded rough hands together. Old break in the right knuckle, healed crooked. The half-coat couldn't hide the hands or the break. He said nothing.

"How many," he said.

"Everyone," Sylvanas said. "Quietly. By the time they understood it wasn't ordinary fever, understanding no longer helped. I have a mage with me. Let her examine your sick."

Only then did he turn to Jaina.

Jaina kept still. Sylvanas was still working.

"What's her read worth," he said.

"She found the contamination in Millhaven's grain, water, and dead before she found one living child clean. Her read is worth the next few hours of this village."

The dog barked again at the far edge of the square, then stopped.

"Fine," he said. "The infirmary's the back room of the common house. Tomas Brek's in there. He hasn't slept in four days."

"Take the mage in," Sylvanas said. "I'll need someone who knows your stores, your well, and your deliveries."

"My wife handles the storehouse accounts. She's in the square."

---

Shelves gone, three bunks against one wall, four pallets on the floor. Fever-sour bedding, bodies, damp wool. Under all of it, the same sweet wrongness she had smelled in the square.

Tomas Brek turned from the nearest pallet. Compact man, grey in the beard, running on no sleep and habit.

"Mage," he said.

"Jaina Proudmoore, Kirin Tor. Tell me what you've been seeing."

"Fever by day three or so. Sweating. Weakness. No appetite. Then the gut turns. Cramping, loose bowels, worse by the day. I've tried the usual for inflammation. It barely touches it." He gestured to the nearest bed. "She came in yesterday. She was still walking this morning."

The woman on the pallet lay with her hands open on the blanket, too slack even for sleep. Jaina pulled up a stool and lifted one hand over the woman's wrist. The crystal rose with it.

The contamination showed at once.

She had seen its trace in corpses, in grain, in the well at Millhaven. Here it was alive. The stain lay thickest in the stomach and upper gut, dark against the blue of healthy tissue, already pushing outward in the worst places.

"Has she been eating here?" Jaina asked.

"Bread and broth from the communal pot."

"Stop both. Water only until I tell you otherwise. Ask every patient where their bread came from and what they've been eating at home."

She kept reading. The pattern held. Gut first. Then farther.

Brek ran through the room in a low voice. Seven patients. The worst were the ones who ate bread with every meal: a miller's assistant, a baker's wife, a woman helping prepare for the autumn gathering. The lightest cases belonged to a shepherd who had been out for weeks on dried provisions and a child mostly fed from a family store of oats.

Jaina moved from bed to bed with the crystal, fifteen seconds each, no treatment yet, only the shape of it. Always the gut. Always that dense sweet-rot signature. In the worst cases it had begun to creep into the blood.

At the fourth bed she paused by a child with brown braids drawn up under her chin.

"I'm not here to hurt anyone," Jaina said. "I need to see what's making this happen."

"You're a mage," the child said.

"I am."

"Can you fix it."

"I can slow it down while I learn enough to stop it. Let me see."

The child held out her arm.

Only a shadow in the stomach. Early. Jaina fed a thin stabilizing ward through it, and the stain stopped where it was.

"That helps," she said. "Hold still."

She worked back through the room, laying the same slowing wards into each body. Not a cure. A pause. Her hands warmed, then began to ache. The crystal guttered blue through amber, blue through amber.

By the end, one farmer's wife had gone slack under the blankets in the deep, heavy sleep fever had denied her for days.

Jaina stood and pressed her knuckles to the wall. Cold stone. Useful.

From the window above Brek's desk she could see part of the square. Sylvanas stood outside the granary with a stout middle-aged woman holding open ledgers and an older man in a work apron dusted with flour or grain. Sylvanas barely moved. She asked something, waited, and asked again. The man did the rest, already pointing behind him before he caught himself.

She let the silence sit until he filled it.

Jaina turned back to the room.

Bread from the communal pot. Bread at home. Heavy bread-eaters sicker than the rest. The shepherd nearly spared. The child scarcely touched.

The contamination entered through the gut and spread outward from there. Water would have struck harder and stranger, day to day. This was steadier. Repeated meals. Repeated doses. Bread.

The bakery was just visible through the corner of the window.

The grain went into bread. The bread went into people.

If the grain was carrying it—

She glanced back outside. Sylvanas was at the granary door now, one gloved hand on the wood while the keeper spoke too quickly.

Jaina forced herself back to the patients long enough to check each ward. They would hold for hours if left undisturbed, maybe longer. The child in the corner watched her with grave concentration.

"Better," Jaina said.

The girl relaxed by a fraction.

Jaina picked up her staff and went for the door.

---

Sylvanas came out of the granary side door as Jaina crossed the square. Pale dust streaked her gloves. She was already waiting for Jaina's half of it.

They stopped two paces apart.

"Grain," Jaina said.

"New supplier at summer's end," Sylvanas said. "Pleasant. Forgettable. Prices just below the usual rate. Two deliveries. The bread changed after the first one arrived. The unused sacks are still in the back."

"People are eating it," Jaina said. "It settles in the stomach first, then spreads. The worst cases are the ones living on bread. The shepherd who has been away from the ovens has barely a trace."

Sylvanas went still. "The first delivery is untouched."

"Then we need to read it before anyone moves it."

Jaina had expected satisfaction when the pieces met. Instead she heard coughing somewhere behind the row of houses.

"The sickness is in the grain," she said. "Someone put it there."

"In two villages, with the same grain seller behind it," Sylvanas said.

Jaina tightened her grip on the staff. "Yes."

"The granary, then."

They went.

---

Aldous let them into the back without argument. He had gone quiet in the square. The color had gone out of his face.

Eight sacks of winter wheat stood against the rear wall, seam-stitching still tight. Ordinary sacks in an ordinary storehouse.

Jaina cast before she touched them.

The contamination flared bright at once.

This was the thing itself, dense and deliberate, worked through the kernels so evenly it would survive milling, baking, and the oven's heat before it ever reached a stomach.

Someone had made it to do that.

She stepped back.

"It's in all eight sacks," she said. "Enough to explain every patient in that room, and more."

Aldous stopped in the doorway. "The second delivery. That's what we've been eating from."

"Then shut the bakery. Anything made from this grain stays out of mouths from now on. The patients will need their wards renewed before tomorrow evening."

He stared at the sacks as if they had become a different species of object in front of him.

"Someone put sickness in my grain on purpose," he said.

"Yes."

No one moved.

Then movement flickered in the far corner.

Jaina turned.

A young man in a brown coat had been crouched inside the loading alcove, grain dust thick on his knees. He was moving for the lamp on the wall.

Sylvanas's arrow shattered the lamp before Jaina finished drawing breath. Oil burst across stone and sackcloth without catching.

The boy froze.

"Don't," Sylvanas said.

Her second arrow was already nocked.

He could not have been more than seventeen. His hand still hung out toward the ruined lamp, shaking with the force of the movement he had stopped too late.

Jaina eased one step sideways so he could see her as well as the arrow.

"What's your name?" she said.

"Don't ask for his name," Sylvanas said. "Ask who sent him."

"Davan," the boy whispered.

"Davan," Jaina said. "What were you doing in here."

He tracked from the oil-dark wall to the arrow to the sacks.

"Watching."

"For what."

"For anyone who looked too closely."

Cyndia appeared at the main door with her bow already up. Lyriel came in behind her, hand on her sword, cutting off the other side.

"Who gave you the grain?" Jaina asked.

He was shivering now. Fear, cold, or both.

"A man on the road. He paid me to watch. Said if anyone opened the back half, I was to make sure they didn't leave with answers."

"His name?"

"He didn't give one."

"What did he look like."

"Ordinary." The word came out flat with defeat. "Brown coat. Brown hair. Good prices. Pleasant."

Same brown coat, same good prices, same pleasant face.

"Anything else," she said. "Height. Horse. A mark."

"An iron ring on his right hand. Worn. I couldn't make out the sign. He said if anything went wrong, I should be fast about it. Said it was for the greater good."

Sylvanas did not lower the bow.

"Come with us," Jaina said. "Quietly."

"Can we do it fast?" Davan asked.

"What."

"If I get caught." The words scraped out of him. "He said to be fast."

Jaina understood only half of that and hated the half she understood.

"Come with us," she said again. "We'll keep you alive."

She knew too well what the other half of that promise might be worth.

---

They locked him in the narrow storage closet beside the infirmary, the one with the small high window. Lyriel took the bench outside the door. Cyndia checked the window from the yard and came back with a raised finger. Covered.

The captain arrived hard-footed and angry.

"You said he was watching for anyone who looked too closely," he said. "At my granary."

"He was placed there," Sylvanas said. "A watcher, not the hand that built this."

"And the grain?"

"Seal the back half now," Jaina said. "Nothing from it goes into food. I'll leave written ward instructions for any mage your nearest garrison can spare."

He went at once, angry now at the granary instead of them.

Jaina and Sylvanas stood in the hall for a breath.

Fever smell from the infirmary. Cold air at the far window.

Nothing from the closet.

Too quiet.

Jaina moved before the thought finished. Lyriel was faster to the latch, swinging the door open as Jaina reached it.

Davan lay on the floor.

Jaina dropped beside him, fingers to his throat, hands spreading over his chest as the crystal flared white-blue. This was faster, cleaner, racing through his blood with a lead she could not claw back.

"Two minutes," she said. "Less."

She threw ward after ward at it and found nothing to catch. Poison. Something he had swallowed before they ever turned the key.

The crystal's light went cold in her hands.

She sat back on her heels.

Sylvanas crouched beside the body without touching it. She checked the mouth, the throat, the hands.

"He took it when we walked him in," she said. "That was what he meant."

"Yes."

"He was trained for this."

"He was seventeen."

"He was trained for this," Sylvanas said again, lower now. "Someone put a village boy in that granary with poison ready and orders for when to use it."

Jaina pressed her still-warm hands against her thighs.

"He said it was for the greater good."

"Of course he did." Sylvanas stood. "Men building this kind of thing always need a noble phrase for the part where children die."

She turned down the hall toward the granary.

"Andorhal," she said. "If tainted grain is moving village to village, we stop chasing single carts and go to the place all this grain is headed."

The coughing continued through the wall, steady as before. The wards were holding. Jaina stayed where she was a moment longer, looking at the dead boy in the brown coat and thinking of the child with braids in the infirmary.

"The wards will hold through today," she said. "Tomorrow evening at the latest, they'll need renewing. The worst cases will need more than that."

"Then leave the method," Sylvanas said. "The captain can fetch a garrison mage faster than we can keep circling back."

Jaina measured it. It was careful work. Any trained mage with a kit and the notes could do it.

"I can write it out."

"Then we leave at first light."

Jaina rose.

The crystal had dimmed to its resting glow. The warmth had already left her hands.

"At first light," she said. "Southwest."

Outside, the village kept coughing in the cold morning while bread smoke rose over the roofs unchanged. Somewhere down the road, beyond storehouses and loading yards she could not yet see, more grain was already moving.

She picked up her staff and went to write instructions for the medic.
