# Chapter 4: The Sick Village

The village showed through the grey-green Lordaeron trees in ordinary morning light: two wagons beside the common, smoke lifting from damp thatch, a dog raising hell behind the bakehouse, somebody swearing at a wheel sunk in a rut. The Thalassian horses eased from road gait to walk on their own, leather creaking, breath whitening in the chill.

After Millhaven's silence, noise should have meant safety.

Then the smell reached her, faint and sweet under bread smoke, wet mud, horse dung, and the iron tang of the common well. Safety vanished.

Jaina pulled her mare down beside Sylvanas at the edge of the square. A woman crossed between the stalls with a basket on her arm, a boy dragged one muddy boot behind him, and two men argued over a crate with the flat venom of people who still thought the day might go on after the argument.

"Two coughing by the bread stall," Sylvanas said. "One at the well. Child by the common house being held upright. And the butcher's shutters are closed."

Jaina followed her gaze. The child was maybe seven, cheeks flushed the wrong way, her mother holding her by one shoulder to keep her up. The bread seller bent to cough into his sleeve over a row of warm loaves, and the woman nearest him took one step sideways without making a show of it. Ordinary caution. Ordinary denial. Nobody in the square had admitted yet that work itself was part of the trap.

The sweet wrongness lay through all of it. Strong enough already.

"Same as the south-road villages," Jaina said. "Early enough to hide under fever if you want to be stupid about it. If I can get to the sick, I'll know where it's taking hold."

"If the captain lets you."

Sylvanas had already found him: thickset, middle years, leather half-coat dark at the cuffs, planted by the well like a man counting strangers against the number of problems he could still hold together. Jaina would have gone straight at him with the whole answer and ruined the square in three sentences. Sylvanas preferred to make other people spend words first.

"I'll speak first," she said.

Jaina let that stand.

They dismounted at the edge of the square. Cyndia took both reins without being asked, settling the horses where they looked calm and expensive. Lyriel drifted toward the well with her bow slung and one hand loose at her side, placed to see the common-house door, the lane behind the bakehouse, and half the square without seeming to claim any of it. Neither ranger made a show of readiness. That was announcement enough.

The captain met them halfway, boots leaving black crescents in the mud.

He was shorter than Sylvanas and wore authority like winter gloves: stiff with use, never fully off. His eyes took in elven ears, green-and-gold armor, bows, blades, then shifted to Jaina's staff and blue robes with immediate dislike.

"Farstriders don't ride this far south for courtesy calls," he said.

"No," Sylvanas said. "What are you calling the sickness?"

His answer came too fast. "Fever. Bad run of it. We've had it before."

"That child is being held upright to stand," Sylvanas said. "Your bread seller is coughing over the loaves, and I can smell the sickness from here. We came from Millhaven. This is different."

The captain's jaw set. His hands folded over each other, one knuckle healed crooked from an old break.

"And you think this turns into that."

"I think waiting to admit the difference buries people."

He said nothing after that. The square said it for him: the woman at the bread stall pretending not to listen, the cough someone tried and failed to hide by the well, the child sagging against her mother's arm.

Sylvanas let the silence work.

He swallowed. "How many?"

"Everyone," Sylvanas said. "Quietly. By the time they admitted it wasn't fever anymore, admitting it changed nothing. I have a mage with me. Let her examine your sick."

Only then did he turn to Jaina.

She kept still. Sylvanas was still solving the room.

"What's she worth?" he said.

"She found the taint in Millhaven's grain stores, the well, and the dead before she found one living child clean," Sylvanas said. "She can tell you what is poisoning your people before nightfall."

At the far side of the square the dog barked again, then cut off sharp.

The captain looked at Jaina's staff, the crystal wired into the head, the split skin over two knuckles where old frost work still punished her.

"Fine," he said. "Back room of the common house. Tomas Brek's using it as an infirmary. He hasn't slept in four days."

"Take the mage in," Sylvanas said. "I'll need whoever handles your stores, your well, and your deliveries."

"My wife keeps the storehouse accounts," he said, jerking his chin toward the square. "Aldous keeps the grain. He's by the granary if he hasn't found another reason to disappear."

"Good. Show my ranger."

No wasted reassurance. No promise this would end well. Jaina approved of that against her better judgment.

---

The back room of the common house had been built for barrels and winter meetings, not seven fever pallets and a man trying to keep a village alive with boiled water and stubbornness. The ale shelves had been stripped bare. Two benches stood on end against the wall to make room for borrowed bunks. Damp wool, old smoke, and fever-sour bedding coated the tongue.

Under it all, the same sweet wrongness from the square.

Tomas Brek turned from the nearest pallet at the sound of the door. Compact man, beard gone more grey than brown, sleeves rolled past the elbow, drying herbs and a butcher's knife sharing table space with a chipped mug of cold broth. Running on no sleep and habit.

"Mage," he said.

"Jaina Proudmoore. Kirin Tor." She crossed to the nearest patient and set the butt of her staff against the stone floor. "Tell me what you've been seeing."

"Fever by the third day in most cases, and sweating with it. Weakness. No appetite at first, then sudden hunger in some, then the gut turns and they can't keep anything. Cramping. Loose bowels. Breath sweet enough you can smell it from the next cot if the room's shut up." He scrubbed a hand over his mouth. "I've tried willow bark, tansy, boiled mint, watered broth. It barely slows them."

He gestured to the woman on the nearest pallet. "She came in yesterday evening and walked here on her own. This morning her husband had to carry her the last ten steps."

The woman lay with her hands open on the blanket, breathing so shallowly Jaina had to watch for it. Jaina pulled up a stool, set two fingers above the wrist, and lifted the crystal.

The taint answered at once. What she had seen as trace in corpses, spoiling grain, and well water at Millhaven was alive here, threaded through living flesh and still moving. It lay densest in the stomach and upper gut, dark amber against the blue lattice of healthy tissue, already pushing outward in the worst places.

"Has she been eating here?" Jaina asked, keeping the crystal over the woman's belly.

"Bread and broth from the common pot," Brek said.

"Stop both. Water only until I tell you otherwise. Ask every patient where their bread comes from and what they eat when they are not in this room."

She moved to the next cot. Same pattern: gut first, then farther.

Brek went through the room at once, asking questions in the low voice of a man long past expecting quick answers. Seven patients all told. The worst were a miller's assistant with flour still under his nails and a baker's wife who smelled faintly of yeast through the fever. The lightest were a shepherd only just in from the outer pastures and a child whose mother kept the family on oats whenever wheat ran dear.

Jaina went bed to bed with the crystal. No treatment yet, only pattern. Always the gut. In the worst cases it had begun to touch the blood.

At the fourth cot she paused by the child with brown braids tucked up under her chin.

"I'm not here to hurt anyone," Jaina said.

The girl watched the crystal instead of Jaina's face. "You're a mage."

"Yes."

"Can you fix it?"

That deserved better than comfort Jaina had not earned.

"I can slow it down while I learn enough to stop it," she said. "Let me see."

The child held out one thin arm from under the blanket.

Only a shadow in the stomach, early enough to matter. Jaina fed a narrow stabilizing ward through it. The taint pulled against the cold and stopped.

"That helps," the girl whispered.

"It buys time. Hold still."

Jaina worked back through the room, laying the same slowing ward into each body. Heat gathered in her palms, then the ache in the forearms and behind the eyes that meant she was pushing too hard and would keep doing it anyway. Blue through amber, blue through amber.

By the end, one farmer's wife had sunk into the heavy sleep fever had denied her for days. Brek looked at that and did not know whether to thank Jaina or mistrust the miracle.

Jaina pressed her knuckles to the wall beside the desk. Cold stone. Useful. The room had no business holding this many bodies. Ledgers and packets of dried feverfew had been shoved into one corner to make room for a basin. Somebody had dragged in a church stool and never taken it back. The village had been improvising around the sickness for days and still calling it ordinary.

From the small window above Brek's desk she could see the square at an angle. Sylvanas stood under the granary eaves with a stout woman holding a leather ledger open against one hip and an older man in a flour-dusted apron worrying the edge of his sleeve. Grain dust streaked Sylvanas's gloves. She asked something, waited, and asked again, and the older man started pointing behind him before he had finished deciding whether he should.

Bread from the common pot. Bread at home. Bread with every meal in the worst cases, while the oat-fed child carried only a shadow and the shepherd barely showed a trace. If water were carrying it, the spread would jump house to house. This was steadier than that. Repeated meals. Repeated settling.

The grain went into bread, and the bread went into people.

Jaina checked the wards one last time. They would hold for hours if left alone, longer in the light cases. The girl with the braids still watched her from the corner.

"Better," Jaina said.

The child eased by a fraction.

Jaina took up her staff. The answer had edges now. The granary would have to supply the rest.

---

Outside, the square had shifted without changing shape: same wagons, same stalls, same coughs trying and failing to pass for ordinary. Sylvanas came out from the granary side door as Jaina crossed the common, grain dust striping one glove and forearm. The captain's wife had the ledger tucked under her arm now; the keeper hovered behind them with the look of a man deciding whether terror counted as an excuse.

They stopped under the granary eaves.

"Grain," Jaina said, and Sylvanas answered at the same time.

"New supplier at summer's end," Sylvanas said. "Pleasant, forgettable, prices just below the usual rate, and two deliveries so far. The bread changed after the first one."

Jaina glanced at the ledger, dates marching down the page in the captain's wife's blunt hand.

"People eating the village bread are carrying the worst of it," she said. "It starts in the stomach and spreads from there. The shepherd on dried stores barely shows a trace, and the oat-fed child in the common house is only just touched."

The keeper swallowed. "The first delivery's still stacked in the back half. We hadn't cracked all of it yet."

Sylvanas kept her hand on the latch. "Untouched since it came in."

"Then we read that before anyone moves a sack."

"The sickness is in the grain," Jaina said. "Someone put it there."

"In two villages now," Sylvanas said. "With the same sort of man behind both."

Not proof yet. No longer a frightened child's loose memory either.

"Yes," Jaina said.

"Then the granary."

They went in with the keeper ahead of them, Cyndia and Lyriel peeling off to take the doorways with the neat timing of people who had been reading the room since Jaina stepped out of the infirmary.

The back half of the granary smelled of old wood, dry chaff, mouse droppings, and the warm sweetness of winter stores. Eight sacks of wheat stood against the rear wall, seam-stitching still tight, each marked in charcoal with the same hand Jaina had seen in the ledger. A loading alcove opened to one side. A clay lamp hung from an iron bracket on the post beside it.

Ordinary sacks in an ordinary village storehouse.

Jaina cast before she touched them.

The taint flared bright enough to make her teeth ache.

This was the thing itself, dense and deliberate, worked through the kernels evenly enough to survive milling, kneading, and the oven. The outer shell sang loudest, but another layer sat deeper, waiting for the body to finish the work.

She stepped back, robe hem whispering through the grain dust.

"It's in all eight sacks," she said. "Enough to explain every patient in that room, and more."

Aldous stopped dead in the doorway. "The second delivery. That's what we've been eating from."

"Then shut the bakehouse and the common pot now," Jaina said. "Anything made from this grain stays out of mouths from this moment forward. The wards in the common house will hold till evening. After that the worst cases need renewing."

He stared at the sacks. "Someone put sickness in my grain on purpose."

"Yes."

No one moved for half a breath.

Then motion flickered in the loading alcove.

Jaina turned. A young man in a brown coat had been crouched behind a stack of split boards, grain dust thick on his knees and cuffs. One hand shot for the lamp bracket. The other jerked toward a squat clay vessel beside it, wax seal already broken at the lip.

Sylvanas's arrow shattered the lamp before Jaina finished drawing breath.

Oil burst across post and stone without catching. The clay vessel went over with it, struck the floor, and broke open in a wet crack. A ribbon of dark syrup spread through the grain dust, and the sweet wrong smell came off it hard enough to sting the back of Jaina's throat.

Concentrated. Not spillover. This was what they poured in before it ever touched the grain.

"Don't," Sylvanas said.

Her second arrow was already on the string, aimed low and steady at the boy's ribs.

He could not have been more than seventeen, one hand still hanging toward the ruined lamp and shaking with the force of the movement he had stopped too late.

Cyndia filled the main doorway with her bow up. Lyriel came in behind her and cut off the yard side, hand on her sword, leaving the boy nowhere to go but deeper into the grain and the arrow waiting there.

Jaina stepped sideways so he could see her as well as the bow. "What's your name?"

"Don't ask for his name," Sylvanas said. "Ask who sent him."

"Davan," the boy whispered, because fear made him answer the wrong question first.

"Davan, then. What were you doing in here?"

His eyes jumped from the oil-slick post to the arrow to the black syrup creeping through the chaff at his boots. "Nothing."

"For what?"

"Watching the back half."

"Who put you here?"

He shook his head too fast. "I don't know."

"Lie better."

"I don't know," Davan said again, nearer to breaking now. "Please."

Jaina looked at Sylvanas. No name. No description. Nothing they could use.

Sylvanas did not lower the bow. "Move."

Jaina looked once at the broken clay, the dark liquid soaking into dust and plank seams, then at the sacks stacked in their clean order against the wall. Not one bad granary. Road and storehouse both. Hands moving it on purpose.

---

They locked him in the narrow storage closet beside the infirmary because it had one door, one high window, and stone walls. Lyriel took the bench outside with her bow across her knees. Cyndia checked the yard side of the window and came back with two fingers lifted. Covered.

The captain arrived hard-footed and angry enough to shake water from the hem of his half-coat.

"You said he was watching for anyone who looked too closely," he said. "At my granary."

"He was placed there," Sylvanas said. "A watcher, not the hand that tainted the grain."

"And the grain?"

"Seal the back half now," Jaina said. "Nothing from it goes into bread, porridge, or feed. Have every loaf already baked this morning pulled apart and kept from tables if you still can."

He stared at her. "If I still can."

"Some of it is already in people."

His mouth hardened. The lie was gone from him at last.

"Tell Brek what he needs," he said.

"Written instructions," Jaina said. "And a mage from your nearest garrison by tomorrow if one can be had."

He went at once, angry now at the granary, the road, the unseen hands behind both.

Jaina and Sylvanas stood in the hall for one breath after he was gone, fever-sour air coming off the infirmary, cold sliding under the outer door, candle smoke sitting in the throat. Lyriel's glove scraped once on bow wood.

Nothing came from the closet.

Too quiet.

Jaina moved before the thought finished. Lyriel was faster to the latch, kicking the bench back against the wall as she yanked the door open.

Davan lay on the stone floor under the high window.

Jaina dropped beside him, fingers to the throat first, then the crystal. Warm still, a trace of foam at the mouth, something racing through the blood with a lead she could not claw back.

"Two minutes," she said. "Less."

She threw ward after ward at it and found nothing to catch—not necromancy, not the grain-sickness, but poison already dissolving past the point where her work could pin it down.

The crystal's light went cold in her hands. Sylvanas crouched beside the body without touching it, checking the mouth, the throat, the cuff seam, the inside of one sleeve with a ranger's economy.

"Capsule," she said. "He took it when we walked him in."

Jaina sat back on her heels. "We should have searched harder."

Lyriel swore once under her breath. Cyndia looked toward the outer door, fingers tightening on her bow until the leather creaked.

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the dead boy. "He was trained for this."

"He was seventeen."

"He was trained for this," Sylvanas said again, lower now. "Someone put a village boy in that granary with poison in his sleeve and orders for when to use it."

Jaina pressed her hands against her thighs because there was nowhere else useful to put them. The closet smelled of old ale sunk into wood, cold stone, and the bitter trace the poison had left at the back of Davan's mouth.

"They sent him in with poison already on him," Jaina said.

"Of course they did." Sylvanas rose in one smooth motion. "Men building this kind of work always need a noble phrase for the part where children die."

She turned into the hall. The cough from the infirmary carried through the wall as steadily as before, one house at a time reduced to one room, one pallet, one bowl of water, one ward that would fail if Jaina did not leave instructions behind.

"Andorhal," Sylvanas said. "If tainted grain is moving village to village, we stop chasing single carts and go to the place that taught the road how to do this."

Andorhal. Not a skyline in Jaina's mind but loading yards, clerks, manifests, grain dust over everything, and enough storehouses to drown a kingdom by ledger.

She looked back once at Davan on the stone, then thought of the girl with the braids in the common room, the shepherd barely touched, the baker's wife already too weak to sit up on her own.

"The wards will hold through today," she said. "Tomorrow evening at the latest, the worst cases will need renewing, though the lighter ones may carry longer if the bread stops now."

"Then leave the method," Sylvanas said.

"It's careful work," Jaina said.

"Not beyond a trained garrison mage."

No. It was not. Any Kirin Tor journeyman with a steady hand and a serviceable kit could manage it. Brek could not. The village certainly could not.

"I'll write it out," Jaina said.

"Then we leave at first light."

She rose. The crystal had dimmed to its resting glow, the warmth gone from her fingers.

"At first light," she said. "Southwest."

Outside, the village kept coughing in the cold while bread smoke rose over the roofs unchanged. Jaina set her ward notes on Brek's scarred desk, pushed aside feverfew and ledgers, and wrote until the instructions were clean enough for another mage to trust: sequence, pressure, where to place the hand, how long to hold the cold before the taint answered back.

When she finished, the ink on the last line was still wet. Beyond the common-house window, carts groaned through the mud and somebody shouted at a mule that did not care.

Somewhere past the village, beyond more barns, bakehouses, and storehouses, Andorhal was already loading the next answer onto wheels.
