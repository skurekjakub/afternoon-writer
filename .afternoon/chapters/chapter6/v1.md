# Chapter 6: The Pattern

The south feeder road had changed under them overnight.

Yesterday it had been a line on a map, the cleaner answer among several ugly ones. This morning it wore itself openly: deeper wheel ruts holding last night's damp, fresh hoofcuts on the verge, dung trampled flat by too much traffic to belong to village carts alone. The mist lay low over the fields and the horses broke through it at the knee. Cold beads gathered on Jaina's gloves where she held the copied route page against the pommel. Damp leather. Horse sweat already rising beneath it. Somewhere ahead, past the white blur of morning, armor knocked once against armor.

Not empty country any longer. A road under orders.

Sylvanas rode a little ahead and left of her, where she could look south without giving up the quartet's shape. Cyndia ranged farther out when the hedges opened. Lyriel kept the right with the easy forward lean of someone who liked a road best when it asked for speed. Jaina sat inside their line and let the motion of the mare work up through her spine. The rhythm should have steadied the pounding behind her eyes. It did not.

The pattern would not stop arranging itself.

Grain first. That part had been plain for days now. Not plain enough for anyone useful in Lordaeron, perhaps, but plain to her: treated stock moving through ordinary kitchens, contamination laid into bread and porridge and the kind of daily hunger nobody bothered to think about. Yet the grain had never been the whole thing. The loudest sign, yes. The thing every crystal answered to first. But too durable, too elegant, too willing to survive sack, storehouse, oven, stomach. A carrier shell that did its work and then handed the body to something deeper.

She had known that yesterday in fragments. On horseback, with the road itself thickening under her and Strahnbrad's war traffic beginning to mix with grain teams, the fragments locked.

Not the full answer. The edges still refused her. The final turn remained hidden: the exact step where a living body's long corruption crossed the dark threshold and answered after death with motion instead of stillness. But the shape before that missing door stood clear enough to make her want to be sick.

Whoever had built this had not thrown poison into wells and hoped for panic. Poison was crude. Poison killed, announced itself, and was done. This work entered routine. It wanted the hand that kneaded dough, the mother cutting bread, the laborer eating twice because the fields had been long and the meal cheap. It wanted repetition. Time. The body learning the wrong lesson in little obedient increments until death no longer ended the instruction.

Her temples buzzed harder.

She shifted in the saddle and pressed two fingers against the place just above her brow. The pressure had moved deeper over the last days. No longer a headache. A second pulse instead, patient and wrong.

"Bad?" Sylvanas asked without looking back.

"Close," Jaina said.

"To the city or the answer."

"Yes."

That earned her the smallest turn of Sylvanas's mouth. Not warmth. Recognition of a shared inconvenience.

Jaina unfolded the copied route page again, though she no longer needed it. The marks were already under her skin: Andorhal tightening the roads into one knot, Barov paper appearing often enough to matter and not often enough to convict, Strahnbrad lying ahead because Lordaeron had committed men to the threat it could see. Grain teams south. Dispatch strings south. War and bread on the same road.

It should have comforted her to understand more than she had yesterday.

Instead it made the unseen mind behind it feel larger.

She had spent her life among difficult people and beautiful work. Dalaran bred both. She knew the pleasure of a theorem that held under pressure, of spellcraft compact enough to look effortless after months of failure. Some part of her, the one trained before it was taught to be afraid, could not help recognizing quality when she stood in it. That was the vilest part. Not just that this was monstrous. That it was good. Good in the way forbidden things often were: precise, layered, built by someone who understood exactly how far one discipline could be pushed before it became another.

Necromancy, yes. But not the student-level obscenity that animated a corpse by brute twitch and borrowed motion. Something cleverer. Something that stood uncomfortably close to questions Dalaran preferred to nail shut and call immoral rather than incomplete.

How much of a self could survive death?

How much could a body be taught to remember?

She had no right even to be thinking in that direction. Antonidas would have named the danger in the question before the words finished leaving her mouth. But the road kept asking it. Every village they passed, every sickroom, every body that had risen with more obedience than any dead peasant ought to possess, pressed the same answerless pressure against her mind.

Sylvanas slowed her horse and lifted one hand.

Ahead, the mist thinned enough to show movement: riders coming north at a trot, Lordaeron colors dark with damp, a wagon behind them carrying two men in bandages and one who was far too still. Jaina smelled iron before they reached the party. Not much. Old blood under wet wool. The leading soldier gave the Farstriders a long look, Jaina a shorter one, and kept going. Urgency had reduced curiosity to a luxury.

War first, Jaina thought. The visible wound first.

By the time Strahnbrad's outer camp took shape through the morning haze, she was no longer trying to persuade herself the prince would simply hear the truth and turn his entire command around. She still believed he should. That failure had not yet been beaten out of her. But the road had done enough work that hope now carried a bruise under it.

The camp sprawled across the lower ground in mud, wet banners, and hammered order. Horse lines. Supply carts. A forge going despite the damp, every strike bright as a snapped coin in the grey morning. Men in Lordaeron blue and Silver Hand white moved around one another with the practiced friction of an army that had been made to stop where it wanted to be moving. Wounded were being carried toward a canvas shelter at the far side. Nearer the center, a group of mounted knights waited around a map board propped on barrels, plate polished bright enough that the weak sun found it anyway.

Jaina knew him before she could make out his face.

That was the worst thing.

Not recognition by features. By use. By the way the command space bent around him. He had always carried attention as naturally as other men carried height or strength. Even in a yard full of armored cavalry and paladins he made a center of himself without trying.

Arthas turned at the stir of arriving horses.

Golden hair damp at the temples. Plate newly cleaned despite the churn everywhere else. Light's Vengeance resting across one forearm, effortless. He looked older than the last time she had stood this near him, though not by much. Harder at the mouth. The old open brightness narrowed by command and lack of sleep. Still handsome enough to make first impressions for him. Still every inch the prince men would follow into a stupid death, with victory promised him young.

He smiled when he saw her, and for one breath the years between simply failed to exist.

"Lady Proudmoore," he said. "Either the kingdom has grown very small, or I've gone farther south than I meant to."

It was easy. Of course it was. The charm had always been easy.

Jaina reined in hard enough that her mare tossed her head. "Arthas."

Sylvanas brought her own horse level and a little ahead, not enough to insult, enough to remind the camp that Jaina had not ridden in alone. Cyndia and Lyriel opened behind them with quiet efficiency, two green-and-gold answers to any question about whether the human soldiers should close in.

Arthas's glance flicked over the Farstrider leathers, the bows, Sylvanas's face. Surprise, then quick accommodation.

"Ranger-General," he said, with the polished speed of a man raised never to let confusion show longer than useful. "I didn't expect Silvermoon's aid in Hillsbrad."

"You don't have it," Sylvanas said.

The smile did not leave him. It landed anyway, that old princely trick of treating refusal as a thing the room would smooth over for him if he remained gracious enough.

"Then I've been corrected quickly." Arthas shifted the hammer in his grip and looked back to Jaina. "You've come a long way to find me. That usually means trouble."

"It means plague," Jaina said. "And if you have ten minutes, I'll explain why the orcs are not the only thing burning your time."

That changed him more than the greeting had. Not outwardly; the camp still had eyes, and Arthas had always known how to wear himself in public. But something in the set of his shoulders drew tight around the word.

"I've reports enough on sickness," he said. "Bad grain, bad wells, frightened villages. Ugly, but not unusual in a season like this."

"This isn't spoiled grain."

"No?" He tipped his head. "You crossed half the kingdom to tell me my quartermasters have a storage problem."

"I crossed half the kingdom because villages are dying and then getting up again."

The words landed. The men nearest the map board did not like hearing them in open air; that much showed plainly enough.

Arthas's gaze sharpened. "Come down, then."

She dismounted before the grooms could reach her stirrup. The ground took her boots with a wet suck. One of the horses somewhere nearby screamed and was answered by an irritated curse. Sylvanas came down more smoothly; even mud seemed to make terms with her on contact. Together they crossed into the command ring around the barrels and the map board. Jaina caught the quick discomfort of a paladin who had to step aside for an elf on his prince's signal. Good. Let him be uncomfortable.

Up close the board showed the southern settlements in grease pencil and knife-point corrections, black marks where raids had struck, supply lines drawn and redrawn in haste. Orc pressure. Civilian extractions. Burned farmsteads. Real losses. Real urgency. Arthas had not invented his competing fire. That, too, made it worse.

"Speak plainly," he said. "I'm short on time."

Plainly, then.

Jaina pulled the oilcloth packet free, unfolded the copied route sheet, and flattened it against the board with one hand. Her fingers left damp crescents on the page.

"The grain is carrying it," she said. "Not as simple poison. As contamination laid in deliberately and well enough to survive storage, baking, and repeated use. The villages we've tracked did not collapse because they ate one bad loaf. They were fed into it over time. The body takes it in, holds it, changes under it. By the time people begin dying, the wrongness is already everywhere that matters."

Arthas looked at the page. At her hand. At the map beyond both. His face stayed courteous. It was an old skill of his, listening with enough attention to make interruption feel ungenerous while still withholding belief.

"You've proven this?"

"I've watched it village by village."

"To what scale."

"Regional," Sylvanas said before Jaina could answer. "Depots, transfer books, storehouse routes, watcher discipline. The road behind us is part of it."

Arthas gave her a brief look, measuring the quality of the interruption. "And yet you've ridden into my camp to tell me about bread while Blackrock raiders are taking farmers out of their own homes."

"Because the bread will still be there when the orcs are dead," Jaina said. "Because it is already in Andorhal's road network and moving south under your war traffic. Because whoever built it understands supply better than panic, and if you keep striking only what draws blood in daylight you will hand them the rest of the kingdom in wagons and sacks."

That got through further. The stillness after told its own story.

Arthas rested the hammer's head against the mud. "And what exactly do you want me to do, Jaina."

He had used her name too softly. As if softness could make the question fair.

"Quarantine grain teams on the Andorhal approaches. Seize records. Warn the city authorities before the next shipments disperse. Put men on storehouses, not only roads. And stop treating every report of sickness as if it can wait until you finish the more visible war."

Silence around the board.

One of the knights shifted his weight. A banner snapped wetly overhead.

Arthas looked down at the route sheet again. When he spoke, his tone stayed maddeningly reasonable.

"I don't say you're wrong," he said. "I say I have a confirmed enemy in striking distance and civilians being dragged off by the hour. The grain, if it's as widespread as you claim, won't be solved by me turning half my mounted force toward counting sacks in Andorhal while the Blackrocks torch the countryside."

Jaina stared at him.

There it was. The gap. The old one, older now because command had fed it instead of teaching it fear.

"Counting sacks," she said.

"Do not do that," Arthas said quietly.

For the first time the courtesy showed its teeth.

"I am fighting a war in front of me."

"You're standing inside one and calling it something smaller because you know where to swing a hammer at orcs."

"And you are asking me to abandon civilians under active attack for an investigation you cannot finish naming."

That struck because it was partly true. Jaina hated him for knowing exactly where the bruise was.

"I can name enough."

"Enough for Dalaran, perhaps. Enough for the Kirin Tor to draft memoranda and decide who ought to be responsible. I need certainty before I move troops off a live front."

"You need a cleaner answer than the road is willing to give you."

His jaw hardened. There, at last, the human crack in the gold.

"What I need," Arthas said, "is to deal with the threat slaughtering people today. If your city proves what you think it will, send word to Capital City, to Hearthglen, to any magistrate along the line. I'll add my own authority to the warning once the raids are contained. But I will not gut this response on the strength of one argument, however well delivered."

One argument.

Weeks of villages, bodies, spoor, ledgers, poisoned bread, stripped depots, and the whole ugly road under her horse's hooves, reduced to that.

Her own laugh came out before she meant to give it voice. Short. Without humor.

"Well delivered," she said. "Thank you. I was worried clarity might fail on aesthetic grounds."

The nearest knight went rigid with offense. Arthas only looked tired.

"I know you think I'm refusing to listen."

"I think you have listened perfectly," Jaina said. "That's the problem."

For a beat she thought he might answer as Arthas and not as crown prince. The old private anger was there; she could see it moving behind the public one, quick and hot and almost young enough to remember what they had once argued about in safer rooms. But the camp held. The men held. So did he.

"You were always better at arriving with catastrophe than with timing," he said.

That one he should not have spent.

The line landed low and hard. Not because it was new. Because it was familiar. The same talent he had always possessed for mistaking the messenger's precision for arrogance when the message demanded too much of him.

Sylvanas stepped in before Jaina could answer, and did it so flatly the whole exchange seemed to sharpen around her.

"If the prince has heard the warning," she said, "then we've taken what we can from this stop."

Arthas turned his head. "I've heard it."

"Good," Sylvanas said.

No curtsy. No concession. Merely the acceptance of a fact already found wanting.

Arthas's mouth flattened. Jaina almost pitied him then, the way powerful men sometimes seemed startled by the existence of someone not invested in their opinion of themselves. Almost.

"If you reach Andorhal," he said to Jaina, all formal edges now, "be careful how you spread this. Panic kills as efficiently as plague."

"So does hierarchy," she said.

That did it. One of the paladins inhaled through his nose, scandalized on the room's behalf. Arthas's gaze held hers a moment longer, not warm, not kind, not even particularly angry now. Simply fixed. He had made his decision and settled into it with the whole of his weight. She remembered that too. The frightening part of him had never been temper. It had been conviction once the temper cooled.

"Captain," he said to one of the waiting officers without looking away from her, "send word to the quartermasters that any reports from Andorhal's grain exchanges come directly through the command tent by nightfall."

It was something. A token, perhaps. Or proof that some part of what she had said had found purchase under the armor. But the order came small, late, and obviously secondary to the matter already occupying the camp. Jaina could hear the shape of it even before the captain answered: yes, my prince, if time allowed, if messengers were free, if the more urgent map at his elbow did not eat the day whole.

Arthas lifted Light's Vengeance from the mud. "I cannot give you more than that now."

"No," Jaina said. "You can't."

She took the route sheet back, folded it once with too much force, and stepped away from the board. The conversation was over because he had declared it so, and there was nothing left in the camp that did not belong to him or to the war he preferred. Behind her, orders were already beginning again. Riders assigned. Villages named. Someone shouting for remounts. The machinery of visible urgency rolled forward without a hitch.

By the time she reached her mare, her hands were shaking.

Not much. Enough.

She pulled at the cinch strap harder than necessary and had to stop because the motion threatened to become ridiculous. The horse rolled an eye at her and shifted her weight. Jaina put a hand flat against the animal's neck until the tremor left her own fingers.

Troop movement went on all around them. Men passing with pikes. An acolyte of the Light carrying bandages in both arms. Somewhere to the west a horn sounded once, twice, and cut off. Strahnbrad kept being a place where people were trying not to die. Arthas had not invented that either. It would have been easier to hate him cleanly if he had.

Instead she had to stand in the churn of his camp and know two things at once: that the orcs were real, and that he had still chosen the wrong scale of disaster.

"Jaina."

Sylvanas's voice, close enough that Jaina did not have to turn to hear it.

"If you're about to say he never mattered—"

"No."

That stopped her. She looked over.

Sylvanas stood with one hand on her horse's reins, the morning light caught thin and cold along the curve of her cheekbone. No softness there. No false mercy.

"He mattered," Sylvanas said. "He matters now. That's why this is worse."

Jaina swallowed. The anger in her throat turned granular. Sand would have gone down easier.

"You heard him."

"I did."

"And."

Sylvanas's eyes flicked once toward the map board, where Arthas was already leaning over fresh reports, Jaina no more permanent than a weather interruption.

"He heard the warning," she said. "He chose the thing he could strike today."

There was no comfort in the line. Which was why Jaina believed it at once.

Not cruelty. Not some private wish to wound her. Something in many ways more dangerous: the old princely conviction that if a threat could not be solved by force immediately applied, then it could wait until force had leisure for it.

Jaina looked back toward the command ring. Arthas had one gauntleted hand planted on the edge of the board. An officer pointed. He nodded once and turned the whole of himself toward the next disaster.

Being right about him had never once been useful.

"Years ago," she said, mostly to the horse's shoulder, "I told myself that seeing the flaw early was enough. That if I stepped away before the worst of him had room to bloom, that counted as wisdom."

Sylvanas said nothing. The silence held because she let it.

"And now this." Jaina tightened the route packet in her fist. "The same man, the same refusal, only with more dead depending on it."

"Then stop asking him to save you from what he is."

Jaina laughed again, softer this time and more bitter for it. "That's almost kind, for you."

"No," Sylvanas said. "It's tactical."

That, of all things, steadied her.

Cyndia came in from the edge of camp at a jog, light on the mud despite the bow at her back. "Road south is clear enough for now. Two grain teams half a mile on, moving slow under escort traffic."

Lyriel followed a few steps later, mouth set in the expression she wore whenever humans had spent too long being human in public. "If we're staying, we should stable the horses. If we're leaving, now would be my preferred time."

Jaina drew one long breath through her nose. The air smelled of wet canvas, forge smoke, horse piss, and the copper edge of too many wounded men in one place. Her own anger sat inside it, no cleaner than the rest.

She could go back.

Not to Dalaran. That road had burned behind her long ago. But back to the map board, back to the prince, back to one more careful attempt at arranging evidence into an order he might choose to respect. She could spend another hour, perhaps two, trading urgency for hierarchy until either he relented in part or she degraded herself enough to hate the victory.

And while she did, the road would keep moving south without her.

She looked at Sylvanas. Not for permission. For the harder read of the room, the one she had been stubborn enough, until very recently, to treat as an accessory to real thought.

Sylvanas met her eyes and waited.

Not cynical, Jaina thought. Accurate.

The correction hurt in proportion to how badly she had needed it.

"No more sanction," Jaina said.

Lyriel's chin lifted a fraction. Cyndia's weight shifted already toward the horses. Sylvanas did not move.

"We go to Andorhal," Jaina said, and the choice settled through her body with the ugly cleanliness of something finally admitted. "If the city is what the road says it is, then the proof speaks there or it doesn't speak at all."

"Good," Sylvanas said.

This time the word carried no mockery. Only agreement, clean and hard as a blade edge.

Jaina mounted. Her legs complained on the first lift into the saddle. The headache behind her eyes had ripened into a bright, mean throb. None of it mattered enough to delay the horses. Around them the camp stayed busy and indifferent. One prince's refusal among ten thousand other moving parts. The world had not changed because her argument failed. Only her patience with the kind of room where it could fail and still call itself responsible.

They rode out of Strahnbrad without ceremony.

No one tried to stop them. A sentry watched the quartet pass and then looked back toward the south road, where a fresh file of infantry was already trudging in. Mud sucked at hooves. Wagon wheels bit deeper as the morning wore on. Once clear of the camp's outer churn, the feeder road opened again into long damp stretches bordered by winter-bare fields and low stone walls gone soft with moss.

For a while nobody spoke.

Jaina was grateful for it. The camp remained in her body after it dropped behind them, like the aftersound of a struck bell. Arthas's voice. Arthas's hands on the map board. Arthas saying he had heard and choosing otherwise. She had the childish urge to rehearse the argument and improve it by force, to discover some missed sentence that would have cut through rank, history, urgency, male pride, and all the rest of the old debris between them.

There was no such sentence.

The road gave her something else to do instead.

Traffic thickened by degrees. Farmers' carts yielding to troop columns. Grain wagons under patched canvas. A pair of priests riding north with their white tabards mud-streaked and their faces set into the particular fatigue of men who had spent too long deciding who could still be saved. Once, they passed a broken axle shoved off into the ditch and three boys trying to lever the wheel free while their father cursed the sky. Ordinary failure. Ordinary delay. The kind of life the plague preferred because it moved through it unnoticed until too late.

By noon the buzzing at Jaina's temples had become directional.

Not enough to point like a compass. Enough to load the air more heavily every mile south. The horses felt it before anyone said so. Jaina's mare shook her head twice and tried the bit. Sylvanas's gelding pinned his ears at nothing visible and needed a touch on the neck before he settled.

"They're feeling it," Jaina said.

"So am I," Lyriel muttered from the right flank, then, catching herself, added, "Only in the horse."

Even Jaina managed a thin smile at that.

The road curved around a copse of leafless alder and dropped into a busier stretch where side lanes joined it in quick succession. A watering trough stood near the junction, half full of grey sky. Beside it a wagon had stopped to adjust a binding on one rear wheel. The man doing the work looked up as they approached: middle-aged, broad through the shoulders, wrapped in the kind of serviceable brown that invited no memory at all. A woman sat on the bench seat beside him with a child asleep against her shawl.

"Andorhal?" Jaina called.

The man straightened, wiped his palms on his coat, and pointed down the road with the wheel wrench. "Keep the main line. You'll see the store roofs before the walls. Hard to miss it once the grain starts crowding in."

Useful. Plain. Exactly the sort of roadside answer a traveler expected.

Jaina thanked him and would have ridden on at once, but Sylvanas had let her horse slow almost to a stop.

"Busy ahead?" Sylvanas asked.

"Always." The man smiled, quick and neighborly. "Busiest place in the kingdom if the bakers are to be believed, and they've an interest in the argument."

The woman on the wagon laughed softly without waking the child.

"You'll have room enough," the man went on. "Road's packed, but it moves."

Nothing magical on him. Jaina checked without meaning to, the habit now as fast as blinking. Only the ordinary muddle every living body carried: warmth, fatigue, yesterday's ale in the blood, the minor ache in one shoulder from too much wrench work.

Then he dipped his head and said, "May the stars keep your road."

Jaina barely marked it. Odd wording, that was all.

Sylvanas did mark it.

Sylvanas's stillness changed the air with the suddenness of a temperature drop.

They rode on another twenty paces before she said, "What."

Sylvanas did not answer immediately. Her eyes stayed on the road ahead, but the focus had gone inward in the particular way Jaina had learned meant memory rather than scouting.

"You've heard it before," Jaina said.

"Yes."

"From him."

"Perhaps." Sylvanas's mouth hardened. "Or from someone very much like him."

Jaina turned that over. "You think it's the trader."

"I think it is the same phrase attached to the same kind of forgettable usefulness." Sylvanas exhaled once through her nose. "That is not the same thing."

No solved identity. No revelation. Only the breadcrumb sharpening because the road kept placing it under their boots whether they asked for it or not.

"Do you want to turn back."

"For a man changing a wheel with his wife beside him?" Sylvanas shook her head. "No. If he matters, he matters farther in."

Jaina looked behind them once. The wagon remained exactly what it had been: a stopped wheel, a sleeping child, two people glad of directions honestly given. By the time the road dipped again and the alder copse took them, it was gone.

The city's pressure arrived before the city itself.

The pain behind Jaina's eyes tightened through the bones of her face. Not sharp enough for panic. Worse than that. Steady. Accumulative. She shifted her grip on the reins and found her right hand colder than the left. The mare's neck went damp under her palm. Flies, absent all morning, had begun to gather in lazy loops anywhere the road's churn held standing water and manure together long enough to breed them.

No one spoke now because the road had become work.

Cyndia tightened back into the quartet's nearer orbit. Lyriel stopped her little under-the-breath commentary and gave the hedges the full attention of a drawn bow. Sylvanas kept one hand low on the reins and the other free, not yet reaching for the bow at her back, ready for the moment readiness needed a more visible shape.

Jaina leaned forward in the saddle. It did not ease the pressure in her skull. The wrongness was too broad here, too distributed. Not one house. Not one granary. The contamination sat in the air the way woodsmoke sat over a town in winter: not everywhere equally, never so dense you could seize it by one source alone, yet thick enough that the body could not help taking it in.

"How far," she said.

"Not long," Sylvanas replied.

Jaina did not ask how Sylvanas knew. The answer was in the road under them. The traffic had a city's impatience now. More carts. More wagons running empty one direction and heavy the other. Teamsters cursing time instead of weather. Men on the verges pissing without even the decency to step farther off because the city was close enough that everyone had begun behaving as if arrival itself would excuse them.

Then the rise came.

It was not much. A long shallow climb through rutted earth where the hedges thinned and the land opened. Jaina's mare dug in. Mud clung to the fetlocks in dark cuffs. At the top, Lyriel made a low sound that was not quite speech, and Jaina looked up past the pain long enough to see why.

Andorhal sat on the horizon exactly where the road had promised it would.

Not ruined.

That was the first shock.

Working.

Granary roofs lifted above the nearer buildings in a row of steep dark angles. Smoke rose from dozens of chimneys. Wagon lines crawled in and out of the gate roads like ants to a disturbed hill. Beyond the outer walls, storehouses and exchange yards spread wider than any sane village architecture had a right to, all of it built for weight, count, transit, repetition. Even at this distance Jaina could make out cranes, yard arms, stacked sacks under long awnings, men crossing catwalks between lofted storage bays. The city did not look frightened of itself. It looked fed. Busy. Competent.

And under that surface competence lay the same pressure now battering the back of her eyes.

Jaina stopped her mare without deciding to. The others drew up around her.

Below them the south road fed straight toward the gates, carrying grain, riders, traders, and all the ordinary faith of a kingdom that believed bread moved because decent men counted it correctly.

"There," she said, though everyone could see it.

Sylvanas followed the wagon traffic with her eyes instead of the skyline. "Alive," she said.

"That's the problem."

Jaina could not stop looking.

Millhaven had been aftermath. Dane Hall had been proof. The stripped depots and road books had been structure. This was the first time the whole mechanism stood in front of her still drawing breath, still doing business, still sending out flour and loaves and comfort to half a kingdom with commerce and contamination in the same hands.

The city looked normal enough to shame accusation.

Children would be running somewhere in there. Bakers shouting over ovens. Teamsters arguing rates. Clerks dipping pens, stamping tallies, cursing poor figures and late arrivals. A thousand small ordinary motions keeping the place alive while the plague rode outward through the exact same arteries.

No ruin to point at. No visible corruption to satisfy a prince who preferred the kind of crisis that smoked.

Only a working grain city carrying the kingdom's death in plain sight.

Jaina closed her eyes against the throb for one beat, opened them again, and made herself look harder. The roofs. The yards. The converging roads. The human swarm of it. If Arthas would not believe the road, then perhaps the city would prove rude enough to believe in itself for him.

"Chapter of your life," Lyriel said under her breath, "I would have preferred a cleaner destination."

"You and me both," Jaina said.

Cyndia shaded her eyes with two fingers and studied the outer traffic. "Gate lines are moving. No visible checks on grain."

Of course not.

Sylvanas turned in the saddle. "We go in quiet first. Learn the shape before we test it."

Jaina nodded once. This time the motion earned its keep. "Quiet," she said. "But fast."

Because the city was there. Because Arthas had heard and chosen otherwise. Because the old farewell had repeated and still refused to become an answer. Because all the roads narrowed now toward walls that held no visible alarm at all.

Jaina set her heels lightly to the mare's sides.

Below them, Andorhal went on working.
