# Chapter 6: The Pattern

The feeder road ran southwest through cold, and the horses had found their stride before the waystation was out of sight. Jaina rode with the map folded against her thigh and the evidence arrayed in her head like ledger columns, because the road was long and the pattern had started to settle in a way she did not want to let cool.

Grain first. Grain was the carrier—grain from the Barov-adjacent factor routes, pushed through ordinary trade channels on roads that already moved half the kingdom's harvest. The wheel-inside-wheel mark had appeared on eleven depots. The sickest places were the ones that kept buying. That was not fouled water or a bad season or one spoiled storehouse burning down unluckily. That was a principle.

The body second. Not just poisoned. She had spent two nights worrying that she was missing the main mechanism—the obvious one that a competent investigator would have seen at Millhaven and a good one would have seen at the first border village—but what she understood now was that the grain's role was not completion but preparation. Repeated ingestion. The body learning, slowly, to answer something it hadn't been built to answer. The corruption was not landing all at once. It was teaching the body to receive it, week by week, loaf by loaf, until the preparation was thorough enough that the last step could be small.

The last step she still could not quite see. Something happened at death—the conditioned body answered something, and what answered was neither the dead nor the living but a rough translation of both. She had tried to describe it to herself in the right vocabulary and the vocabulary kept running out before the mechanism did.

Death completing the lock, she thought. That was close enough to work with.

The headache had started within a mile of the waystation, not the sharp kind that meant exertion but the dull pressing kind she had come to read as ambient contamination—the field leaking through the road, through whatever she used to sense it. It had been manageable at the Crossbar and at Millbrook Spur. Manageable still, but heavier. The basin was ahead. They were moving into the heart of it, and the field grew denser with every mile.

She kept the evidence in order anyway. Grain, body, death, organization. Organization was what turned the first three from a catastrophe into a war. A lone wizard with necromantic ambitions and too much time could have fouled one storehouse. What was spread across eleven confirmed depots with a consistent factor mark, monitored roads, disciplined cleaning crews, and a body deployed at Millbrook Spur to vanish inconveniently in the night—that was coordinated. That had resources. That had someone at the top who understood both the grain trade and what the Kirin Tor forbade and why.

Cyndia rode ahead, keeping the road in view. Lyriel held the back trail as usual. The horses moved through the last of the dawn mist, breath and hoof beat and the wet quiet of a road before the day's traffic came onto it.

"Someone designed this," Jaina said.

Sylvanas rode two lengths away, the same spacing they had kept since Millhaven. "You said that yesterday."

"I mean the mechanism, not the plan." She almost reached for the map and stopped herself. She knew what was on it. "Grain carries a signature strong enough to survive baking. The body is not just the victim—it becomes part of the mechanism. Someone worked out that death is not where the process ends. It is where it resolves." She waited, watching the road ahead where it curved into a stand of bare-limbed ash. "That is not an accident. That is years of work by someone who has been asking questions the Kirin Tor declared unanswerable."

Sylvanas said nothing for a moment. A crow took off from the hedgerow to the left and crossed ahead of them, wings loud in the damp air.

"Can you explain how the sickness becomes the other thing," Sylvanas said. "What they are after they are dead."

"Not fully. Not yet." The headache pulsed once behind her eyes, then settled. "I can see the first three parts. The last step still—" She stopped, reforming the thought. "The body has been prepared to answer something when it dies. I do not know the exact mechanism for what answers. Only that it requires preparation, and that the preparation is the grain."

"Which means the design is someone's understanding of both."

"Yes. Alchemy, necromancy, food supply, and trade routes." She heard herself and recognized the ugly feeling underneath the precision—not quite admiration, but the reflex of it, the professional response that preceded the revision. Like finding a proof so elegant it took two breaths before you remembered what the proof was proving. "It is very well made."

Sylvanas turned her head once, brief, then back to the road.

"I know," Jaina said. "I know that is the wrong word."

"It is not the wrong word. It is true. The question is whether calling it that is useful."

It wasn't, particularly. But the word had arrived before the judgment, the way useful words sometimes did, and denying it felt less honest than saying it aloud and then setting it down. She had read the methodology in the grain, in the body counts, in the careful escalation from preparation to result, and the methodology was technically brilliant in the way a weapon could be brilliant—useful precisely because of the rigor applied to it, terrible precisely because of the same.

The Kirin Tor had banned certain questions about what the body could be taught to carry through death. The ban existed for reasons Antonidas had never fully explained to her, only gestured at with the careful vagueness of someone who has thought the problem all the way through and does not want to hand the path to someone else. She understood it now, in the way you understood the shape of a door only after it had closed on you. Someone had taken those banned questions into a workroom and answered them. Systematically. Patiently. In the language of grain routes and road factors and ordinary bread.

A useful property of a bread-based delivery system was that the target population ate bread every day. Three meals, sometimes more. The exposure was not accidental. It was guaranteed by the very ordinariness of what had been weaponized. She thought about the boy at the infected village—nine or ten, maybe, sitting at a table with an ordinary loaf and an ordinary meal in front of him, and his body patient as the weeks went on, doing what the grain was teaching it to do, entirely without knowing.

She cut that thought off before it finished.

Her grip on the reins had gone tight without her meaning it to. She released the pressure and the horse's ear tilted back toward her, curious.

"We need to reach a command authority before Andorhal," she said.

"Arthas is on this road."

"I know."

Sylvanas did not say anything further, which was its own kind of answer. Jaina heard it anyway. She heard the skepticism in the silence, the flat practical read that said: a prince committed to a visible military emergency is not a mind that receives invisible threats well. She heard it and she did not agree with it, or she was not ready to agree with it yet, because the alternative was arriving at Andorhal without a single Lordaeron command authority who understood what they were riding into.

The mist burned off as the sun came up behind cloud. The road dried from silver to pale grey, and the horses stretched their strides into the first real warmth of the day.

She kept the methodology in mind—brilliant, terrible, patient—and let the headache build.

---

By late morning the feeder road had bent north enough to put Strahnbrad's approach roads in sight: churned mud, wagon ruts deep as a hand's width, the distant clank of iron on iron and the kind of noise a camp made when it was past the staging phase and into actual business.

Cyndia dropped back from the front and matched pace with Jaina. "Scouts," she said. "About a quarter mile. Lordaeron sigils."

Jaina had already seen the banner, half-furled in the cold air, gold on blue. The sight of it produced an odd doubling in her chest—familiar and strange at once, the colors of a court she had spent two years adjacent to and never quite belonging to. She had known Arthas Menethil for the better part of those years. Not well, not the way you knew someone you had chosen—but well enough to remember the particular quality of his certainty, the way it filled a room before he did.

"We're not stopping," Sylvanas said, coming up on her other side.

"We're not," Jaina said. "But they have scouts on the road. We can't ride past a Lordaeron camp without showing credentials, and there is no world in which we ride past a Lordaeron camp while knowing what we know."

Sylvanas's jaw moved, brief. "He won't act on it."

"You don't know that."

"I know what a prince embedded in a visible military campaign looks like when you try to redirect him. I've briefed enough of them. They hear the invisible threat and they finish the visible one first."

"He may surprise you."

The silence after that was not skeptical. It was patient, which was worse, because patience in Sylvanas meant she had already made her assessment and was waiting for the evidence to arrive.

The road leveled and widened and a scout patrol moved into the track ahead of them, three riders in Lordaeron livery, weapons at ease. One of them raised a hand.

Jaina sat straight and let the blue of her coat do the talking for a moment before she spoke.

---

The camp had been built fast and built to stay. What had probably been farmland a month ago now ran canvas, rope lines, and stamped mud in every direction from the village proper, with infantry moving along the lanes between tents in the disciplined shuffle of men who had been doing this long enough to stop noticing the cold. A field forge was running somewhere behind the eastern tree line; she could hear it and smell it, the sharp clean smell of heated iron. Silver Hand sigils marked the pavilions nearest the village wall, but the camp sprawl was Lordaeron's—quartermasters, supply lines, the kind of organized military weight that took weeks to move.

The scout took them through the camp on foot, horses handed off to a boy at the supply line, and Jaina walked and tried to hold her reading of it cleanly. Wounded coming off the field, three men she counted being moved toward the healer's tent at a hurried walk—not critically, but recently, blood still fresh on one sleeve. A group of Silver Hand paladins at the well, two of them in full plate she recognized as campaign-heavy, scraped and re-stamped, the kind of armor that had seen serious use since it was last oiled. Orders being relayed in voices that carried the particular focused compression of field rank, where no one had time to say anything twice. The camp was busy in the way that said the emergency it had been built for was still ongoing, and she felt the argument she had prepared settle differently in her chest as she walked through it—slightly smaller, slightly harder to hand someone who had all this on his hands.

Arthas had not been difficult to find. The activity organized itself around the command tent near the village gates the way activity always organized itself around wherever Arthas happened to be.

He was standing at a map table when they brought them through, three officers around him and a runner waiting at his left, and he had Jaina's name on his lips before the tent flap dropped shut behind her.

"Jaina." His face held genuine warmth and genuine surprise in proportions that shifted in her chest in a way she did not particularly want to examine. He was wearing light field plate, the kind that meant he expected to move before noon, and the commander's sash across it was clean in a way the rest of the camp was not. He had always been able to look like he had just arrived from somewhere more dignified than wherever he actually was. "Word reached me you were riding south. I didn't think this far south."

"The road went farther than we expected," she said.

He glanced at Sylvanas with the easy read of a man who had been managing introductions since childhood. "Ranger-General." A nod, not a bow, calibrated and pleasant.

"Your Highness." Sylvanas had positioned herself two steps back and to the right, which Jaina had learned to read as her default in rooms where she had not yet established who needed to be watched most closely.

"I need a few minutes," Jaina said.

"Of course." He dismissed the officers with a gesture that was practiced without being ostentatious, and the runner went with them, and the tent became a smaller space. A candle lamp threw warm light across the map table. Two cups of something hot had been left on a camp stand by the entrance; she didn't touch them. "What are you following?"

She gave him the clear version, the one she had been refining since the first border village and that now had the Millbrook Spur evidence and the Barov name in it, and she kept it short because short was what a man in field plate with a runner waiting outside needed. Grain as the carrier. Repeated ingestion conditioning the body. The contamination designed to resolve at death. The network of roads and factor marks and depots, the cleaned shelves and blank books, the dead man at Millbrook Spur with no wound. The Barov connection unconfirmed but recurring.

He listened. That was not nothing; she had known men of equivalent rank who listened with their mouths already open for the response. Arthas listened with his eyes on her and his hands on the table, and when she finished he was quiet for three full seconds, which she knew was him giving the information its due rather than dismissing it.

"How confident are you," he said.

"In the mechanism, very. In the Barov connection, less—we have a name on a slate and a broken seal, not testimony. In the scale—" She spread her notes on the table beside his map. "Eleven confirmed depots. Forty to fifty communities in the supply chain. The worst outcomes are still ahead of us, not behind."

He studied her notes. He picked up the page with the route diagram and turned it once, reading her annotations in the margin, and she watched his face and read what she had always been able to read in it, which was that he was genuinely looking, not performing the act of looking. He had never been a stupid man.

She had built the route diagram carefully, the eleven depots laid out with their supply chains and factor marks in a web that made the distribution's scale legible to a field commander rather than a researcher, and he was reading it the way field commanders read maps—tracking the logic of movement, not just the geography. He set it down, picked up the Millbrook Spur page, read the room summary and the body position notes and the cleaned shelves. He read slowly. He asked no questions while he was reading, which was the mark of someone who preferred to hold their confusion until the whole picture was present.

"This is heading for Andorhal," he said.

"Yes. Andorhal is the distribution center for this basin. All the roads run through it."

"Then I need to clean the Blackrock problem out of this corridor before I can—"

"The Blackrock problem is happening now," she said, keeping her voice level. "The plague problem will still be happening in three months. If you don't cut the distribution now, there will be nothing to defend."

He looked up. The warmth was still there, but something had moved behind it—the shift from genuine attention to the particular kind of attention a man gave an argument he respected but was not going to change his course for. She had seen it before. She had told herself she had misread it, the first time she saw it.

"Jaina." He set the paper down gently, which was not a good sign. Gentleness was how he cushioned a conclusion rather than a question. "I hear you. I believe you believe this completely, and your methodology looks thorough. But the orc raiding parties are here. Today. They're hitting villages on this road, killing people while we're talking. If I pull my force to chase grain records, what I'm defending is an idea of a threat while the physical one burns down the house."

"The grain threat is already burning." She held his gaze because looking away would read as retreat. "You can see the orc raiders. You cannot see the grain. That is not evidence that the grain is smaller. It is evidence that it is better hidden."

"And you are asking me to deprioritize a known and immediate threat for one I can't yet see."

"I am asking you to send a rider to Andorhal's lord, to Hearthglen, to anyone who can pull the grain records and freeze the supply chain before the distribution runs to completion."

"I have two riders right now," he said. "Both of them are carrying requests for reinforcement from the capital, because the Blackrock numbers are higher than the initial count." He said it without apology, which she appreciated even while it landed as a wall. "I will note what you've told me. I will flag it to the command in Hearthglen on the next report. If you find Andorhal's distribution records—" he tapped her notes—"get them to me, and I will make sure they land on the right desk."

She looked at him for a moment, the notes still spread between them, the map of Andorhal's basin roads behind his shoulder.

He meant it. That was the part that required the most care not to let show on her face. He was not dismissing her out of contempt or blindness or personal history, and she could feel Sylvanas behind her, still and patient, not saying a word, and she understood with the particular clarity of someone who had just had the cleaner of two unwelcome readings confirmed that Sylvanas had seen this coming before the first sentence landed.

"Thank you for your time," Jaina said.

His face moved, brief. "Be careful in Andorhal. And come back when you have the records."

She gathered her notes and folded them into her coat without looking at them and walked out of the tent into the cold.

---

The horses were where they had left them, Cyndia and Lyriel standing easy with the reins, neither of them asking anything. Jaina did not look back at the tent. The camp continued its business around her—men with supply crates, a farrier's hammer, two clerks arguing over a wagon manifest at the supply line—all of it bearing its own kind of ordinariness against the cold.

Sylvanas came up beside her, not speaking.

Jaina walked the length of the horse line before she stopped, facing the camp's south road.

"He's not wrong about the raiders," she said.

"No."

"He's applying the correct logic to the wrong problem. The visible threat is immediate. The invisible threat is larger. He doesn't have a framework that makes the invisible one override the one bleeding in front of him."

"That is one way to say it," Sylvanas said.

"What is yours."

Sylvanas turned, briefly, to check the road behind them, the habit so trained it operated below attention. "He hears a plague problem and resolves it to a disease problem—something the physicians handle while the military handles the military problem. You were giving him a military problem in disease language, and command doesn't translate those in the field."

Jaina thought about the map. The eleven depots. The forty communities. The dead man at Millbrook Spur with the grain dust still in the room around him. "He was listening."

"He listened. He would have listened the same way if you were telling him that a wizard in Dalaran had solved how to transmute straw into gold. He takes you seriously. He does not take the problem seriously, because the problem does not yet look like his kind of war."

She had been right, years ago, about Arthas's certainty—the weight it carried, the way it organized a room. What she had not thought through at the time was that the same quality that made him compelling in a crisis also made him difficult to shift before the crisis became obvious enough to see from a camp tent on a cold road in the Hillsbrad foothills. Clarity should win on its own, she had thought, walking in. She could hear how that sounded now.

"I already knew that," she said. "I knew it when we came in."

"I know."

"Then why did I still think it would be different."

Sylvanas said nothing. A horse shifted behind them; one of the camp lines swayed in the wind. The question had not been a real question, which was probably why the silence fit it better than an answer would have.

She had wanted to be wrong about this. She had wanted the evidence to be enough, the argument to be enough, the old familiarity to provide some advantage. None of it had been enough, because none of it was what was blocking the road. What was blocking the road was not stupidity or malice. It was a prince with two riders who needed reinforcement and a visible war on his doorstep, making the correct calculation for the wrong problem.

She stood with one hand on her horse's neck and looked at the south road and held the anger level and did not let it land anywhere useful or useless. It had two parts—the failure of the argument, which she could work with, and the older irritant underneath it, the one she had thought too much road and too many dead villages had spent down. She had walked into that tent expecting evidence to make rank step aside. She had done that before, in a different form, with a different expectation, and the outcome then had also been technically civil and practically immovable and the only person surprised had been her.

She had been certain clarity would be enough. She had thought that about the other thing too, years ago. Apparently she had not learned it the first time.

The anger was useful if she kept it pointed at the right thing.

"The records," Jaina said.

"Yes."

"If we reach Andorhal and the main books are intact, we have something a rider can carry to Hearthglen without depending on his assessment to make it legible."

"If the books aren't cleaned."

"If they're not cleaned." She turned toward the horses. The south road ran out from the camp in a pale line between muddy verges. "Then we find out before whoever's running this ahead of us gets there first."

Sylvanas had her horse's reins already in hand. Cyndia passed Jaina her own without ceremony. The camp's noise dropped behind them in layers as they took the road south, thinning from clank and voice to the sound of their own hooves on cold ground, and by the time the camp banners were out of sight there was only the road and the wind and the southern sky going grey ahead of them.

---

The road south of Strahnbrad ran through harvested country, fields stripped back to frozen ground and hedgerows gone black with bare branches. The road itself was better than the feeder routes had been—wider, more often repaired, with the wheel ruts of serious traffic pressed into the verge. Grain wagons, mostly. They passed two between Strahnbrad and the mid-afternoon junction, both running south toward Andorhal with their cargo covered in oiled cloth, the drivers bundled to the ears, neither one interested in riders going the same direction.

Jaina watched the second wagon pass and thought about the holds under the oiled cloth and tried to think about something else instead.

Her head had been building since Strahnbrad—not the dull background pressure of the early morning but something more pointed, lodged behind her right eye and occasionally reaching for the left, the contamination field thickening as the road bent south and the basin roads came closer together and the distances from which she had been tracking the corruption shrank. At the border villages she had been reading it at range, the way you read a ward from outside its boundary. Now she was inside the boundary. The field was ambient. She was breathing it.

She kept her outer wards tight. It was mostly useful.

The junction arrived as a widening in the road where three lines met: the Strahnbrad road from the north, a feeder cut coming in from the west still carrying the marks of heavy use, and the main Andorhal approach running southeast, the road smoother and the verge signs more frequent. A milestone stood at the angle with the Andorhal distance carved into it and half its face gone green with old moss. Beside it, a grain wagon had pulled to the shoulder, its lead horse nosing at something in the verge while the driver stood in the road with a cartbook open on his forearm.

He looked up at the sound of hooves—an older man, broad through the chest, with a coat that had been patched on one sleeve and a hat that sat low against the wind. Road-man posture: easy without being careless, the kind of ease that came from spending more time moving than standing still.

"Andorhal?" he said, looking at their road dust and their packs.

"Yes," Jaina said.

He turned the cartbook so the back of it caught the light and tapped the route marked there. "This road goes straight in. Follow it past the second waymarker—the one with the old paint—and you'll see the granary towers before the city wall." He had the manner of someone who had given directions on this road before and had no particular interest in the people receiving them, which was the manner of a useful man. "Fair going today. Road's dry through to the gates."

"What's the trade on Andorhal now," Sylvanas said.

He considered this with the evaluating pause of someone who read trade questions for purpose. "Grain's moving. Mills are running. The bakery quarter's been busy since summer." He named a number for current bushel prices without being asked, which meant he had been pricing it. "Nothing unusual that I've seen."

"Good," Jaina said.

He flipped the cartbook closed. The lead horse had found what it was looking for in the verge and pulled against the tether again. He turned back to manage it, then stopped, half turned, with the familiar quality of a thought arriving at the point of departure.

"Safe roads to you," he said. "Keep the grain above damp, if you're carrying any."

He said it pleasantly. He said it the way a man said a thing he had said a hundred times, in the voice of easy habit—keep the grain above damp—and went back to his horse.

Sylvanas had gone still beside her. Not the kind of still that meant danger in the immediate sense. The kind that meant something had landed that she was not going to discuss until she had decided what to make of it.

Jaina had heard it too. Keep the grain above damp. An odd valediction, practical and specific in a way that snagged, the way a phrase snagged when it had been in your ear before. She turned the memory over. The north-road grain seller from Sylvanas's accounts—the one who had come out of the same web of roads before they knew grain was the carrier, the one Sylvanas had given a sharp read and let pass. She had not been there for the original encounter, only for Sylvanas's recounting of it. She could not be certain of the phrase.

Sylvanas was certain of something. She had gone still.

They rode south, and Jaina did not ask until the junction was a full quarter mile behind them.

"The same phrase," she said.

"Close enough," Sylvanas said.

"Two different men on two different roads weeks apart."

"The phrase is grain-trade practice. Common enough." Sylvanas's hands were easy on the reins, but she had turned her head once before speaking, the way she turned her head when she was pulling a memory for comparison. "The first time I let it pass because I had not yet connected grain to the carrier. It sat in the back because it was odd for a farewell."

"And now it's odd twice."

"Yes."

Jaina looked at the road ahead. She ran through the man at the junction—his bearing, his coat, his horse, his manner. Nothing had read as magical to her. No ward signatures, no necromantic residue, no wrongness at all under the ambient field that had been pressing at her since well before Strahnbrad. He had been a grain man on a grain road, or had read as one.

"I'm not getting anything from him," she said. "But I wasn't reading carefully."

"I know." Sylvanas said it without blame. "It may be nothing. It may be the phrase is common in this district and I am building a pattern from two points."

"Two points is the start of a pattern."

"Two points is also the start of a wrong one."

That was true enough that it settled the question for now, not into comfort but into the file where the unresolved things lived. The old seller. The phrase. The fact that neither of them had a third point to confirm it with.

The headache sharpened.

She adjusted her wards and the sharpness backed off by half, which was something. The horses had been growing restless for the last half hour—heads higher, ears working more, the particular quality of unease in their movement that meant the animals were reading something their riders were slower to process. The Thalassian geldings were better than average about arcane background noise; she had watched them tolerate Sylvanas's own considerable magic without fuss, and they had come through the worst of the feeder-road contamination without breaking stride. What they were reading now was not a single ward or field signature. It was cumulative saturation.

She pulled her outer wards closer and felt the field push back. It was not a single source—there was no granary ward burning at distance, no isolated contamination pocket she could name and route around. This was distributed pressure, the kind that came from a center that had been feeding outward for weeks, and it was strongest from the south.

Her right hand had gone numb at the fingers, which was the cold and also the effort of holding the wards flat against pressure. She flexed the hand without letting it show in the reins, checking the warmth would come back. It did, slowly.

"How far," she said.

"Two miles," Cyndia said from ahead. "Maybe less."

The last mile between the junction and the city was the worst. The contamination pressure built the way sound built near a forge—not sharply, but unavoidably, and by degrees that prevented adjusting for any one escalation because there was no single escalation, only the accumulated weight of all of them. The headache had moved from behind her eye to the crown of her skull, the kind of specific sustained pressure that meant her detection wards were working harder than they wanted to and complaining about it. She had a ward for heavy necromantic contamination that she had not deployed since the border villages; she deployed it now, and it helped at the cost of adding another demand to the field she was trying to hold together.

She was going to need to manage this carefully inside the city.

The horses were worse. She watched Cyndia's gelding take several steps of the half-collected, side-tilting walk of an animal whose instincts were voting strongly for reversal, and watched Cyndia ride it forward anyway with the patient insistence of long practice. Her own horse was holding its stride but its ears had gone flat in the direction of the city and stayed there, and she could feel the tension through the reins—not bolting-tension, but the suppressed decision, the animal holding itself to the agreement it had made with its rider while making clear the agreement was costing it.

"We may need to stable them outside the wall," she said.

"I know a family two roads east of the gate," Lyriel said. "If they're still there."

The road curved, and then the trees opened, and then the sky ahead was larger than it had been, and across the flat winter farmland the horizon broke apart into shapes that were not natural—walls, rooftops, granary towers in a row, four of them, tall enough to read their shape from here against the grey sky. Smoke from bakehouse chimneys. The sound of the road increasing: hooves ahead of them, a cart going toward the gates with loaded cargo, the distant low wash of a city at work.

Andorhal.

It looked alive. That was the first and most unsettling thing about it—alive the way a city was supposed to look, the way Strahnbrad looked under its camp, the way Fenwick Crossing looked on market days. The gates were open. Traffic moved through them. The granary towers were not ruins; they were working infrastructure, filled and drawing from what the roads fed them, and the roads were feeding them now, this day, with the city's ordinary commerce riding in on loaded wagons and empty ones going back out for more.

She could feel the field radiating from it.

Not through the wards, exactly. Not the sharp intrusive presence she had felt in the most contaminated depots. Something broader, something that had spread out from a center the way warmth spread from a forge, even through the walls, even across the approach road. The city was doing its ordinary work and the wrongness was built into the ordinary work, moving in the wagons the way it had moved through eleven depots and forty communities and every bag of flour that had come down the southwest feeder road under the wheel-inside-wheel mark.

One of the horses pulled up short and had to be urged forward. Then it settled, ears back, walking with the suppressed tension of an animal that had decided to obey rather than resolved its concern.

Jaina let her wards sit where they were, enough to hold the worst of the pressure out, and kept her eyes on the granary towers and the open gates and the people going in and out of them as if today were any other day.

"Still running," she said.

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

The distribution was still running. The grain was still moving. Wherever the cleaners had been at Millbrook Spur, they had not been here yet, or the schedule said not yet, or the work was not done enough to stop.

The city looked alive.

The city had no idea what it was doing.

She rode toward it and kept her wards tight and let the evidence of it replace the last argument she had been making to herself about clarity and command and whether being right was going to be enough.

It wasn't, on its own. Sylvanas had known that before they reached Strahnbrad. Jaina had known it after. What Andorhal added was the proof that the timeline was its own authority—the grain was still moving, the city was still working, and every hour before the records were in hand and the supply chain interrupted was an hour the distribution ran to completion.

The gates were close.

She kept riding.
