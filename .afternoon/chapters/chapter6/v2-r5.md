# Chapter 6: The Pattern

The feeder road ran southwest through the cold, and the horses settled into the long morning stride before the waystation dropped from sight. Jaina rode with the map folded against her thigh and the case laid out in her head so no part of it could blur.

Grain first. Grain from the Barov-side routes moving through roads already thick with half the basin's harvest traffic. The wheel-inside-wheel sigil on eleven depots. The worst villages always the ones that kept buying. Not bad water. Not one spoiled storehouse. Somebody had buried the taint in bread everyone would eat willingly.

The body next. She had spent two nights fearing she had missed the obvious turn, the one a better investigator would have seen at Millhaven, but she had finally seen enough on the road. The grain prepared the body for it. Week by week, loaf by loaf, teaching flesh to answer something it had never been meant to answer.

The last turn still lay in shadow. At death, something called and the prepared body answered, and what rose was neither the dead nor the living in any clean sense. She did not have the right words for that part. Not yet.

Death closes the lock, she thought.

The headache had started within a mile of the waystation, not the sharp flare of overuse but the slow press she had come to trust as contamination riding the land itself. She had felt it at the Crossbar and at Millbrook Spur. Here it was thicker. The basin was ahead. The roads converged with every mile, and the wrongness deepened with them.

She kept sorting the pieces. Grain. Body. Death. Organization. That was what turned a plague into a campaign. One mage with too much nerve could foul a storehouse. Eleven depots with one factor mark, cleaned shelves, watchers on the roads, and a body removed from Millbrook Spur before daylight—that was money, labor, discipline. Whoever planned eleven depots and road watchers knew trade as well as necromancy.

Cyndia rode ahead. Lyriel kept the back trail. Hoofbeats, cold breath, the road still damp with dawn.

"Someone built this," Jaina said.

Sylvanas rode two lengths off, the same spacing she had kept since Millhaven. "You said that yesterday."

"I meant the inner part, not the route map." Jaina kept her hand off the folded chart. She knew every notation on it. "The grain keeps enough of the taint to survive baking. Then the body takes over. It carries the taint the rest of the way until death finishes it." The road bent through a stand of bare ash. "Someone spent years on it."

A crow burst from the hedge and crossed in front of them, wings loud in the wet air.

"Explain the sickness becoming the other thing," Sylvanas said. "What they are after death."

"I can give you the first three steps." The ache pulsed behind Jaina's eyes and eased. "The last one is still dark. The body is taught to answer when it dies. I do not know what, exactly, answers back. Only that the grain is what teaches it."

"Then whoever designed it knew both halves."

"Yes." The word came with a taste she disliked. "Alchemy. Necromancy. Trade. Supply." She heard where her own mind had gone a beat before the judgment caught up. It was the same reflex that answered to elegant proofs and flawless wardwork. "It's well made."

Sylvanas turned her head once. "I know."

"I know that's a foul thing to say."

"It is also true." Sylvanas faced the road again. "Say what it is first. Curse it after, if there's time."

Sylvanas had said it plain. The thing had been built by a serious mind. Denying that did not make it smaller. The Kirin Tor had kept certain questions locked away for a reason—what could be carried through death, what a body could be taught to keep, what line separated memory from echo when magic got its hooks into flesh. Antonidas had never answered those questions directly. He had only closed the books and told her some doors were wiser as stone.

Someone had taken those sealed questions into a workroom and opened every one.

The cruelty reached people through bread. Everybody ate bread. Three times a day if they were lucky. More if they had grain enough. She thought of the boy at the infected village with his loaf on the table and cut the image off before it rooted.

Her hands had tightened on the reins. She loosened them. Her horse flicked an ear back at her.

"We need someone above the road captains before Andorhal," she said.

"Arthas is on this road."

"I know."

Sylvanas let the silence sit between them. Arthas would not turn for a threat he could not yet strike. Jaina was not ready to concede it. Not while there was still a chance to put the whole thing in front of a man with rank enough to move riders and freeze ledgers.

By the time the sun had climbed behind a bank of cloud, the mist had burned off the road. The mud silvered into pale grey. The horses lengthened their stride into the day's first thin warmth.

She kept grain, body, death, and the network behind them in mind and let the headache ride with her.

---

By late morning the feeder road had bent north enough to show the Strahnbrad approaches—churned mud, deep wagon ruts, banners above a scatter of tents, and the iron clang of a camp already past setup and deep into use.

Cyndia dropped back from the lead. "Scouts. Quarter mile. Lordaeron colors."

Jaina had already seen the blue and gold. The sight hit in two directions at once, familiar and not hers. She had spent two years near that court, near Arthas, near rooms where people yielded before he entered, always close to the edge of them.

"We're not stopping," Sylvanas said, drawing even on her other side.

"We are if they stop us first." Jaina kept her voice level. "And we cannot ride past a Lordaeron camp while carrying this."

"He won't turn for it."

"You don't know that."

"I know what happens when you try to pull a prince off the war in front of him." Sylvanas's tone stayed flat. "He will finish the thing burning in his sight before he chases the one hidden in grain."

"He may surprise you."

The pause after that held Sylvanas in her usual patient stillness, the one she wore when she thought an argument was finished.

The road widened. Three riders in Lordaeron colors took the track ahead of them, weapons low. One raised a hand.

Jaina sat straighter so the blue of her coat was the first thing they saw.

---

The camp had gone up fast, built for a stay. Farmland had become rope lines, pavilions, cookfires, mud lanes, wagon parks. Men moved through it with the blunt rhythm of soldiers who had stopped noticing the cold. Somewhere beyond the eastern trees a forge was running; she could hear the hammer and smell hot iron. Silver Hand sigils marked the tents nearest the village wall. The rest of the sprawl belonged to Lordaeron—clerks, quartermasters, supply carts, healers, runners, the whole weight of an army dug in for a stay.

The scout took their horses and led them through on foot. Jaina read as she walked. Wounded, but not in numbers that spoke of collapse. Paladins at the well with campaign dents still showing in their plate. Runners and officers were moving fast enough that nobody repeated an order. The fighting had already reached it.

Three men went past at a hurried walk toward the healer's tents, one of them with fresh blood down his sleeve, all of them still steady on their feet. At the cookfires nobody lounged; men ate standing and kept half an eye on the lanes. Even the mud looked worked hard, stamped flat by boots and cartwheels until it had lost the softness of farmland and taken on the shape of a place where decisions got made in a hurry. Jaina felt the argument she had built for Arthas change weight as she crossed the camp, harder to place in a hand already holding all this.

Arthas stood at a map table near the village gate with three officers around him and a runner waiting at his left. When the tent flap fell shut behind Jaina, he had her name ready.

"Jaina."

Warmth first, then surprise. It struck low in her chest before she could stop it. He wore light field plate, built for movement, and the command sash across it still looked cleaner than anything else in the camp. He had always managed that, looking like he belonged somewhere more orderly than the place where he stood.

He had also always filled a room in the same particular way, certainty first, charm second, with the sense that he had already chosen the line of advance and everyone else would do better if they stepped quickly into it. She had once mistaken that for the safest kind of strength there was.

"Word said you were riding south," he said. "I didn't expect this far."

"We kept riding south longer than I expected."

His glance cut to Sylvanas, easy and practiced. "Ranger-General."

"Your Highness." Sylvanas stood two steps back and to the right, already where protocol would put her before the others had fully settled.

"I need a few minutes," Jaina said.

"Of course." He dismissed the officers with a turn of the hand. The runner went with them. The space shrank. A lamp burned at the table. Two cups of something hot waited on a stand by the entrance. She left them there. "What are you following?"

She gave him the short version. Grain carrying the taint. Repeated eating teaching the body to hold it. Death finishing whatever came next. The tagged depots, the cleaned shelves, the blank books, the dead man at Millbrook Spur without a wound, the Barov name appearing too often to ignore and still not often enough to prove.

He listened. Arthas kept both hands on the table and let her finish.

"How certain?" he said.

"Of the grain, very. Of the Barov tie, less. Of the scale—" She spread her notes beside his map. "Eleven confirmed depots. Forty to fifty communities in the chain. The worst of it is still ahead."

He read. He turned the route diagram once and followed the lines the way commanders followed roads, in terms of movement, distance, and supply. He set that page down, took up her Millbrook Spur notes, and read the room summary, the body placement, the shelves. He did not interrupt. He never interrupted when the answer interested him. He had never been a fool, and he had never needed the room to mistake confidence for thought.

He lingered over the route map longer than she expected, fingertip resting once on the southern road where three lines fed into one. Then he picked up the page on the depots and read each place name instead of skimming for the total. That would have impressed her if she had needed impressing.

"This is running toward Andorhal," he said.

"Yes."

"Andorhal is the center."

"All the roads feed it."

He laid the sheet down. "Then I need to clear the Blackrock raiders out of this corridor before I can—"

"The Blackrock raiders are happening now," Jaina said. "The plague will still be happening in three months. If you let the grain keep moving, you'll be defending empty houses."

He raised his head. He had not stopped listening.

"Jaina." He set the paper down with deliberate care. "I hear you. I know you've done the work. But the orcs are hitting villages on this road today. If I pull men off that to chase ledgers and storehouses, I leave living people to burn for a threat I cannot yet put a sword through."

"The grain is already killing them." She kept her voice flat. "You can see the raiders. You can't see the loaves on a table in a village three days from here. That does not make the grain smaller."

"It makes the choice mine."

"I am asking for riders, then. Andorhal's lord. Hearthglen. Anyone who can seize the records and stop the grain before it finishes the route."

"I have two riders left to spare, and both are already carrying reinforcement requests to the capital." No apology in it. "I will put what you've told me into my next report. If you get the records out of Andorhal, bring them to me. I'll see they reach the right hands."

Sylvanas said nothing behind her. She did not have to.

That demanded more care on her face than contempt would have. It would have been easier to leave angry at foolishness. Harder to leave knowing he had heard the danger, measured it against the war in front of him, and chosen the nearer fire with clean hands and a prince's authority.

"Thank you for your time," Jaina said.

His face shifted, brief and human. "Be careful in Andorhal."

She gathered the pages, folded them back into her coat, and walked out into the cold.

---

Cyndia and Lyriel were waiting where the horses had been left, both of them wisely silent. Jaina did not look back at the command tent. Around her the camp kept moving with crates, hammer blows, and a pair of clerks arguing over a wagon count.

Sylvanas came up beside her.

"He's not wrong about the raiders," Jaina said.

"No."

"He's treating the raiders as the war and the plague as somebody else's problem."

"That is one way to say it."

Jaina turned to her. "Yours?"

Sylvanas checked the road behind them on instinct before she answered. "You gave him a war in healer's language. He heard plague and sorted it with the physicians. The orcs stayed with the soldiers. Commanders do that in the field."

"He was listening."

"He was." Sylvanas took the reins Cyndia held out and gave the mare a brief stroke at the jaw. "He would have listened just as hard if you told him some Dalaran archmage had learned to spin straw into gold. He takes you seriously. The plague still comes second."

Jaina had gone in believing a clean argument, laid out cleanly, would force Arthas to yield. She had believed that once before, in another room, with Arthas wearing a different face and the same certainty. He had listened, weighed it against the raiders, and refused anyway.

"I knew that," she said.

"Did you."

Jaina rubbed her thumb along the rein leather. "I knew it enough to fear it. Not enough to keep expecting better."

A horse shifted behind them. The nearest tent line rattled in the wind. She laid one hand on her gelding's neck and kept the anger where it could still be used. Arthas had a visible war and too few men. The older sting under it was not simple, but it did not need solving on this road.

She had wanted speed. A shortcut past ceremony. A moment in which he would take the shape of her thinking before the whole explanation had to be unpacked. Instead the years between them had only made the refusal sound courteous.

"The records," she said.

"Yes."

"If Andorhal's books are still there, we get something he can act on without choosing to believe me first."

"If they're already cleaned."

"Then we learn that too."

She took her reins from Cyndia. The south road ran pale between muddy banks.

"Either way," Sylvanas said, mounting in one smooth motion, "the argument is over."

They rode out before anyone in the camp pulled them into one more conversation that would do no good.

---

South of Strahnbrad the country opened into stripped fields and black hedgerows. The road improved with the traffic, broader and harder packed, with ruts set deep by loaded wagons. Grain mostly. They passed two before mid-afternoon, both headed toward Andorhal under oiled cloth.

Jaina kept her eyes off the second wagon's load as long as she could, failed, and then looked too hard.

The pain behind her eye had thickened since Strahnbrad. Not the dull early ache of a far-off taint, but something narrower and meaner, needling from behind the right eye toward the left as the basin roads drew tighter around them. At the border villages she had read the wrongness from outside it. Here she was riding inside its reach.

She cinched her outer wards closer. Enough to keep going. Not enough to stop the pressure altogether.

The junction came where three roads met: Strahnbrad from the north, a worn feeder cut from the west, and the main Andorhal approach slanting southeast. A milestone leaned at the angle, its carved face green with age. Beside it a grain wagon had pulled over. The driver stood in the road with a cartbook open against his forearm while his lead horse nosed at the grass.

He looked up at the sound of hooves. Older man. Broad chest. Coat patched at one elbow. The easy balance of somebody who had spent most of his life on roads and knew how not to waste motion on one.

"Andorhal?" he said.

"Yes," Jaina said.

He turned the cartbook and tapped the route. "Straight through. Pass the second waymarker—the one with the old paint—and you'll see the granary towers before the wall." He had the useful habit of a man who gave directions often and cared very little who received them. "Road's dry all the way in."

"What's moving there now?" Sylvanas asked.

He weighed the question in a brief pause. "Grain. Mills running. Bakery quarter busy since summer." Then, because he had been pricing it, he named the current bushel rate without being asked. "Nothing strange I've seen."

"Good," Jaina said.

He closed the cartbook. The lead horse tugged again. He turned back toward it, stopped half a step later, and tossed the farewell over his shoulder in the same easy tone.

"Safe roads to you. Keep the grain above damp."

He caught the lead horse's cheekpiece and checked the tack.

Sylvanas went still a beat late.

Jaina had heard the phrase snag too. Keep the grain above damp. Too specific for a casual farewell, and familiar in a way she couldn't yet place. The old seller on the north road—the one from Sylvanas's earlier account. Jaina had not been there; she had only heard the retelling. She could not trust the memory entirely.

Sylvanas rode with the stillness of a woman weighing it.

They rode on. Jaina left the question alone until the junction had sunk a quarter mile behind them.

"The same phrase?" she asked.

"Close enough."

"Two men. Two roads. Weeks apart."

"The phrase belongs to the grain trade." Sylvanas kept her hands light on the reins. "Common enough. First time I let it pass because we had not yet tied grain to the carrier. It stayed with me because it was an odd thing to say to strangers."

"And now it's odd twice."

"Yes."

Jaina ran back over the man at the junction: patched sleeve, good horse, road dust, weathered hands, nothing magical on him she had felt even through the worsening pressure in her head. A grain man on a grain road, unless he wasn't.

"I got nothing from him," she said. "But I wasn't looking for him."

"I know." No blame in it. "It may be nothing. It may be a district habit and we're building too much out of two points."

"Two men on two roads using the same farewell was enough to flag it."

"Or it was two grain men talking like grain men."

Fair enough. She set it beside the old seller in her mind and left it there.

The headache sharpened.

She tightened her wards again, shaved the pain down by half, and found the horses with heads higher and ears working. That thread of unease came from slow strain building through the body. Thalassian horses tolerated magic better than most; these had carried them through Sylvanas's presence and the feeder-road hum without complaint. The horses kept that posture as the miles fell away.

Jaina drew the wards closer. The wrongness pressed back. No single source to point at. No hot knot of magic she could circle wide around. Only the pull of one source somewhere ahead, feeding taint out through every road.

Her fingers had gone numb with cold and strain. She flexed them once against the reins until warmth returned.

"How far?" she asked.

"Two miles," Cyndia called from the lead. "Less, maybe."

The last mile hurt.

The pressure did not leap. It built by grains. Each time she tightened the ward, the ache slipped past it again. By the time they were nearly in sight of the city, the ache had spread from behind her eye to the crown of her skull. She put up the heavier ward she had used only at the worst villages and kept riding under the extra pull it laid across her senses.

Inside the walls would be worse.

The ward held some of it out and only thinned the rest. At the border villages the taint had struck like a bad smell caught on the wind: sharp, local, survivable at a remove. It pressed across the wards in a broad sheet. It sat on the skin and in the teeth. It came in with every breath. Every few minutes the wrongness pressed hard enough against the ward to make her jaw tighten.

The horses knew it already. Cyndia's gelding gave two side-tilting steps toward the shoulder, straining to turn back. Her own kept its pace, but its ears flattened toward the road ahead and stayed there.

"We may stable them outside the wall," Jaina said.

"I know a family two roads east of the gate," Lyriel said from the rear. "If they stayed."

The road bent. The trees broke. The sky opened.

Andorhal stood across the winter fields alive with walls, rooflines, four granary towers, bakehouse smoke, wagons at the gate, and the distant hush of work.

Gates stood open, traffic moved, wagons unloaded and left empty for more. The towers were full, and wagons were still pulling grain out toward the road. It looked less like a city under sentence than a market day that happened to wear winter light.

She could make out the order of it even from here. Tower first, then warehouse roofs, then the lower spread of workshops and houses shouldering together behind the wall. From here, the skyline looked ordinary. No smoke from a fire gone wrong, no bell in alarm, no knot of people massed in panic—only the basin's ordinary grain traffic under winter light.

She could feel the city from here.

It pressed across the wards in a broad sheet. The pressure had soaked so deeply into the place that flour in sacks, ledgers, bakers, carts, and clerks all carried it through the day's work.

The worst part was how cleanly the road and the city matched. Every depot they had traced, every symbol copied from a ledger, every village that had sickened without understanding why—all of it pointed here, and here was a functioning node in plain sight.

One of the horses balked and had to be urged on. Then it obeyed, tight-backed and miserable.

"Still running," Jaina said.

"Yes."

This was what she had tried to give him: towers full, wagons loading, a city still working. She had followed the evidence here all day. The grain was still moving through a city that still worked. Whatever hands had cleaned Millbrook Spur had not reached here, or had not needed to, or were still waiting their hour.

She stopped arguing with herself. A prince would ask for records; soldiers in camp would wait for orders.

Records first. Ledgers. Towers. Men who signed for the grain.

The gates were close enough now that, with her wards pulled tight, Jaina could ride straight toward them.
