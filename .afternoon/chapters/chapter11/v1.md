# Chapter 11: The Fuse

The road south carried two sets of hooves and the shape of four.

Cyndia checked right once, then again. The second turn of her head had become quick and ugly since the trap, meant to catch absence in the act and force it back into the line. Sylvanas let the habit stand. Naming it would not mend anything. Naming it would only make the air between them smaller.

Her bow rode against her spine. Each stride of the mare worked the half-healed cut under her bandage, a narrow drag that pulled when she twisted in the saddle to clear the verges and the ditches. Cold found the wound before her hand did. Eastweald wind came wet off the fields, soft enough to belong to ordinary harvest country, and still the land kept giving the game away.

Grain stood black on the stalk in one field and untouched in the next. An orchard had been trimmed clean for sightlines, every low branch cut back so the rows offered shade and nothing else. A cottage door hung open on one hinge. At the gate, a dog lay with its head across its paws and watched them pass without enough life left for a bark.

This should have been cart country. Men shouting over harness noise. Wheels sunk axle-deep at the bends. Children sent running from one yard to another with bread in their fists.

Instead there was the sweet wrong smell of treated grain drifting from shut granaries. Not rot. Not clean flour either. Something worked and sealed and meant to last.

Cyndia reined in at a crossroads where a saint's marker leaned under ivy. "Tracks west. Three riders, old. Wagon south, two days at least."

Sylvanas slid from the saddle long enough to touch the dirt where the ruts crossed. The wagon grooves had dried hard, then taken one night's damp. No panic in the spacing. No broken harness leather. Supply traffic, once. Human order going about its work while poison rode with it in sacks and bins and bread ovens.

"Keep the pace," she said.

Cyndia mounted silence the way other people wore wool. She turned the mare and moved on.

They rode through a village too tidy for plague country. Buckets stacked. A cart covered against rain. Laundry taken down before dark, folded nowhere she could see. Someone had done the normal work here and left the shell standing after. On the meeting-house wall, a notice sheet flapped under a nail. Sylvanas did not need to read it to know what would be written there. Requests. Measures. Reassurances. Human ink trying to catch up to a thing already inside their bread.

At the southern edge of the village, a figure watched from a field wall in patched brown and slipped down the far side the moment her horse angled toward him.

"Leave him," Sylvanas said before Cyndia could loose the reins.

The scout's mouth tightened. "He had line on the road."

"He had line on what was left to learn here." Sylvanas looked over the fields, the closed granary, the empty lane running toward Hearthglen. "If they still post watchers this lightly, the country is already doing half the work for them."

Cyndia checked right again.

Two scouts, one wounded commander, one missing mage, one dead ranger. That was the arithmetic now. Every turn in the road priced against what the formation no longer carried. Jaina would have read the wards in the orchard rows. Jaina would have told her whether the granary smell meant live work or residue. Jaina was not here, and the absence had long since stopped sounding like irritation in her own head. It sounded like cost.

By noon the road widened, deepened, and filled with military damage. Hoof-churned mud. Wheel ruts from supply wagons driven hard enough to cut the shoulders to slurry. Then the banners rose through the trees, blue and white under a low grey sky, and behind them the shape of Hearthglen's outer groundworks and a field camp spread across the wet.

It was force, properly gathered. Forge tents. picket lines. wagon circles. outriders moving in and out under shouted orders. Men in the colors of Lordaeron and the Silver Hand crossing one another's lanes with the practiced impatience of an army already weeks into its own emergency.

It was also pointed at the wrong enemy.

The sharpened stakes faced outward. The cavalry lanes left room for a charge. The healers' tents had been raised close to the center, where wounded men could be brought quickly from an open fight. Bread wagons stood under tarps by the quartermaster's rope line, close enough to the kitchens that steam already ghosted up from kettles beyond.

Sylvanas smelled horses, iron, damp wool, forge smoke, incense from the healers' side of camp, and under it all the same sick sweetness she had been riding through since dawn.

She swung down before the forward checkpoint and handed her reins to Cyndia. "No speeches."

"I never planned one."

The man who met them wore King's Guard plate worked bright despite the mud. He had the square shoulders of a door meant to stay shut until someone with more rank told it otherwise.

"State your business," he said.

"Ranger-General Sylvanas Windrunner," she replied. "I require Prince Arthas."

His gaze flicked from her face to the Farstrider leathers, then to the ears, then back. The plate settled on him an instant later. Not fear. Adjustment.

"Captain Falric," he said. "The prince is occupied."

"Then occupy him differently."

One of the men behind him failed at hiding a smile. Falric did not.

"With respect, my lady, I do not open the prince's command tent to every rider who arrives claiming urgency."

"Good. If you did, your camp would already be ash."

The answer bought exactly one heartbeat. In that beat Sylvanas looked past him. Two stretcher cases crossing toward the healers. A paladin kneeling beside a wagon boy whose hands shook too hard to hold a cup. Quartermaster scribes working from a stack of manifests with the attention of men who still believed ink could keep pace with catastrophe.

"And what," Falric asked, "am I to tell His Highness?"

"Tell him the grain road reached him before I did."

That landed.

Not comprehension. Weight. Enough of it showed in the captain's face that she knew the camp rumors had outrun the formal brief. He looked once toward the command section, then back to her.

"Wait here."

"No."

His brows drew together.

"I did not cross half a ruined province to stand in your mud while someone rehearses how gently to say plague to a prince." She pointed past him with two fingers. "I go as far as the tent line. If your men prefer to stop me there, they may explain the delay to him themselves."

Falric held her gaze for a long moment, measuring insolence against the risk of being the man who turned away the warning that had already reached his kitchens by scent if not by name.

At last he stepped aside. "With me, then."

The camp pressed around them as he led her inward. Hammer blows from the forge. Wet canvas slapping in the wind. Men speaking too low near the healer's line and too loudly by the horse pickets. A novice priest turned from one of the cots with blood on both sleeves and hopelessness already learning the shape of his mouth.

Jaina would have had the pattern of it inside a minute. Which tents held plague and which held ordinary camp sickness. Which men would die no matter how much Light got poured into them. Sylvanas could only count the bodies, the pace, the supply strain, and the number of ways the camp's structure assumed a visible war.

Falric stopped outside a broad command pavilion striped in Lordaeron blue, gave the guard the briefest sign, and disappeared within. He returned almost at once.

"The prince will see you."

Inside, heat from braziers fought wet wool and map ink and lost. Arthas Menethil stood over a trestle table crowded with markers, dispatch tubes, and a map of northern Lordaeron pinned flat with daggers. He looked up as she entered, and for a moment the years between Strahnbrad and here collapsed into the same golden face cut harder by command.

Not softer for having been right at last. Harder.

"Ranger-General," he said. "You've chosen a poor day to prove me wrong."

"If I wanted that, I would have come sooner."

Uther stood at the far side of the table, broad hands braced on the wood. Age had thickened him rather than slowed him. The room bent around Arthas's urgency, but it steadied around Uther's silence.

Good. At least one man here had learned the use of it.

Arthas gestured to the map. "Show me."

So she did.

Not everything. Not Jaina inside Caer Darrow. Not Kel'Thuzad's full shadow under the work. Not the parts that still belonged to the other road and the other mind.

She gave him the grain routes, the distribution hubs, the villages already gone quiet before authorities admitted anything larger than fever. She laid the Eastweald roads over his troop map and watched his expression change as one set of lines swallowed the other. She named Andorhal. Barov holdings. Contaminated stores embedded in normal supply traffic. Bread as delivery. Repeated exposure. The dead rising from bodies already taught what to answer after death.

At that, Arthas's hand flattened over Hearthglen.

"How far?" he asked.

"Far enough that you are already late."

He did not flinch from it. He moved two markers, then three, then looked to Falric without lifting his hand from the map.

"No bread from local stores until every granary within a day's ride is checked. Seal the quartermaster line. Anything suspect burns."

Falric turned at once.

"And if your men have eaten already?" Sylvanas asked.

Arthas's eyes cut back to her. "Then I would prefer to know before the dead tell me themselves."

There it was. The speed of it. No pause wasted on whether belief ought to hurt.

He kept asking questions, good ones, and not enough of them. Where the grain had moved fastest. Which holdings mattered. Whether the Cult favored roads, storehouses, or mills for insertion. She answered as cleanly as she could. Each answer sharpened him. Each one stripped something else out of the room that might have resembled caution.

"You said body conditioning," Uther cut in at last. "Not simple poison."

"Yes."

"And you know this how?"

Because Jaina read a kingdom through dead blood and spoiled bread and the shape of rot in a farmhouse corpse. Because the mage not in this tent had spent weeks assembling a theorem while institutions argued over permission.

"Because the land keeps proving it," Sylvanas said. "Poison kills where it lands. This teaches the body first and uses death second."

Uther absorbed that without any visible comfort from it.

Arthas had already reached for the next conclusion. "Then Hearthglen wasn't an isolated outbreak. It was a loaded stop on the road."

"Yes."

"Stratholme too."

"Almost certainly."

Tent heat and brazier smoke closed in around the table.

"My lord," Falric said from the entrance, "the riders—"

"Ready them." Arthas did not look away from the map. "Every mounted detachment that can keep a saddle. Uther, I want the paladins out in pairs with the vanguard. If a town starts turning, I do not intend to learn it from the smoke."

Uther's jaw set. "Arthas. We do not yet know the shape of this fully."

"We know enough."

"Enough to move," Uther said. "Not enough to stop thinking."

The prince's hand closed around the edge of the table until the knuckles showed white. "Thinking is why we're standing in a plague camp instead of a graveyard."

That was not true, and the speed with which he needed it to be true chilled her more than the order itself.

He looked back to Sylvanas. "Will you ride with us?"

No.

The answer came before the question finished. Not because he was wrong to ask, but because the other road had just flared hotter than the one in front of him.

"No," she said. "I came to light the fuse, not hold it."

Uther's gaze shifted to her then, sharply enough to count as thanks.

Arthas gave one short nod, as if he accepted refusal only because motion mattered more than pride. "Then get clear of the line before the column forms."

"Gladly."

Outside, the camp had already begun to turn itself inside out. Tents dropping. Harness thrown over sweating backs. Mess fires kicked apart. Six hundred horses would have sounded like thunder in another country. Here the noise came muffled through wet ground and low cloud, a kingdom driving itself toward the answer it wanted least and fastest.

Uther came after her before she reached Cyndia.

"Ranger-General."

She stopped.

Close up, the paladin looked less like a statue and more like an old campaign scar given shoulders and a voice. He had the face of a man who had spent half his life saving boys from the kinds of certainty they mistook for courage.

"You know what he is," he said.

Not a question.

"Yes."

Uther glanced once toward the mounting column. "He has never known how to step back from a fight once he believes the field chose him for it."

Camp noise swelled behind them—orders, buckles, hooves, men running because the prince had decided running was now virtue.

"Then why let him lead?" she asked.

His mouth changed. Not a smile. A grief too disciplined to call itself one.

"Because I am not the only man in Lordaeron who taught him that decisiveness was a holy thing."

There was no answer worth wasting breath on.

He held her there one moment longer. Then something in his face settled into weariness.

"May the Light keep you from needing him again," he said.

"It won't."

The lines at the corners of his mouth shifted once, without humor. Then he turned back toward his prince.

The column rode within the hour.

Dust and damp rose together, turning the southern road into a pale bruise under the sky. Arthas at the front beneath the Menethil blue. Falric near enough to hear his first change of orders before anyone else. Uther there too, not because agreement held, but because love and duty had both proved useless and he had no third thing left to try.

Sylvanas watched until the road swallowed the last wagons.

Catastrophe now had cavalry.

Cyndia brought the horses. "We go north?"

"After one thing."

The dispatch waited where Falric had told a runner to leave it, sealed in Farstrider wax laid over another hand's relay marks. Lor'themar's script cut across the page with less patience than ink usually tolerated from him.

Verification teams had reached the sites in Eastweald and found scrubbed floors, cold ash, missing records, empty basements. Witnesses gone. Local lords indignant. Convocation pressure tightening. Recall language no longer theoretical. Every proof that reached Silvermoon arrived half a day too late and already cleaned for inspection.

Lor'themar had underlined one sentence once, hard enough to nearly tear the parchment.

**If you mean to finish this in the field, finish it before procedure gets there first.**

Sylvanas folded the page back along its crease.

There it was. The border fiction dead in ink if not in policy. She had crossed too far into Lordaeron's collapse to pretend she was sending reports home from a safe remove. The work was here now, among princes and dead granaries and institutions losing to anyone ruthless enough to move first.

"Bad?" Cyndia asked.

"Predictable."

The second rider came in hard from the north before she could mount. Farstrider horse, Silvermoon tack, foam down the chest and blood at one fetlock from pace, not battle. Darrowmere mud striped the mare to the belly. The scout half-fell out of the saddle and thrust the message tube toward her.

"Ranger-General."

She broke the seal one-handed.

Barov estate entered under mage authority. Ferry watch at Darrowmere saw the mage cross to Caer Darrow by boat before dusk. Same watch held the landing through nightfall and saw no return crossing. No contact since.

No interior detail. No speculation. Good.

The page stayed very still in her hand. Around her, the southern road still held Arthas's dust like old smoke. North waited under mist and stone and the island keep Jaina had gone into alone because they had split the work like sensible women and paid for sensible work with blood ever since.

She handed the dispatch to Cyndia.

The other ranger read fast, once, and looked up already turning toward the horses.

"North," Cyndia said.

"North."

Sylvanas was in the saddle before the word finished falling.

The camp fell behind them. So did the last chance to pretend the plague could be solved by telling the right men and letting rank take it from there. Arthas rode south with an army and a flaw big enough to swallow cities. Jaina was somewhere beyond Darrowmere's mist with no archer on her flank and no one pricing the exits for her.

The road forked at the shrine marker and they took the northern branch at a gallop.

Cyndia checked right once, then again.

This time Sylvanas did not look away from it. The ghost had a place in the line. So did the missing mage. So did every choice already made.

Ahead, the country lowered toward lake ground and old stone. Mist lay in the hollows. Evening gathered under the trees. Caer Darrow waited somewhere beyond it with its island walls and whatever had swallowed Jaina whole enough that no one had seen her come back out.

Sylvanas leaned low over the mare's neck and drove harder.

South, the fuse burned.

North, the hand that had lit it reached for the only thing in this country she still trusted not to break.
