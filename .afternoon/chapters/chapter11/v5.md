# Chapter 11: The Fuse

The road south carried two riders and the shape of four.

Cyndia checked right once, then again. Since the trap, the motion had gone sharp. She kept trying to catch absence and drag it back into line. Sylvanas let her. Naming it would only stiffen her.

The mare's stride tugged at the half-healed cut under Sylvanas's bandage. Eastweald wind came wet off the fields and showed on every fence line.

One field stood black on the stalk. The next stood untouched. Someone had cut an orchard's lower branches back to open sightlines. A cottage door hung on one hinge. At a gate a dog watched them pass without the strength to bark.

This should have been cart country: harness noise, wheels sunk to the axle, children running errands with bread in their fists.

Instead she caught the sweet wrong smell of treated grain from shut granaries. Worked. Sealed. Built to keep.

At a crossroads Cyndia reined in beside a saint's marker gone soft under ivy. "Tracks west. Three riders, old. Wagon south, two days."

Sylvanas slid down and touched the wagon ruts. Hard-dried clay, then one night's damp. No panic in the spacing. No snapped leather. Supply traffic, once. Human order carrying poison in sacks and ovens.

"Keep the pace."

Cyndia turned the mare south.

They passed through a village too tidy for plague country. Buckets stacked. Cart covered. Laundry taken in before dark.

Sylvanas did not need the words. Requests. Measures. Reassurances. Human ink chasing a thing already in the bread.

At the far edge of the village a man watched from a field wall. When her horse angled toward him he dropped out of sight.

"Leave him," Sylvanas said.

Cyndia's mouth tightened. "He had line on the road."

"He had seen enough."

Two scouts. One wounded commander. One missing mage. One dead ranger. That was the count now. Jaina would have named what was wrong with the orchard rows and the granary stink before they reached the gate. Jaina was not here, and that absence cost.

By noon the road widened and broke under military traffic. Mud churned by hooves. Wagon ruts deep enough to drown a boot. Then blue-and-white banners rose through the trees, and Hearthglen's outer works spread ahead with a field camp crouched around them.

Proper force in a hurry: forge tents, picket lines, wagon circles, men in Lordaeron blue crossing paladins in white tabards, all moving with the impatience of an army weeks into bad news.

And all of it faced the wrong way.

Stakes outward. Cavalry lanes wide enough for a charge. Healers near the middle. Bread wagons tarped by the kitchens. Steam beyond them, thin and sour.

Under horses, iron, damp wool, forge smoke, and the healers' incense, Sylvanas caught the same sweet grain stink she had smelled all morning.

She swung down at the forward checkpoint and handed Cyndia the reins. "No speeches."

"I never planned one."

The man who met them wore King's Guard plate bright through mud. He had the shoulders of a door meant to stay shut.

"State your business."

"Ranger-General Sylvanas Windrunner. I require Prince Arthas."

His eyes flicked over the leathers, the ears, then back to her face. The rank landed a breath later.

"Captain Falric. The prince is occupied."

"Then occupy him differently."

"With respect, my lady, I do not open the prince's tent to every rider who arrives crying urgency."

"Good. If you did, your camp would already be ash."

She looked past him. Stretcher cases moving toward the healers. A paladin kneeling beside a wagon boy whose hands shook too hard to hold a cup. Scribes still bent over manifests.

"What am I to tell His Highness?"

"Tell him the grain road reached him before I did."

Enough showed in Falric's face. Rumor had beaten the brief.

"Wait here," he said.

"No."

Falric's jaw locked.

"I did not cross half a ruined province to stand in your mud while someone practices how softly to say plague to a prince. I go as far as the tent line. If your men stop me there, they can explain the delay themselves."

Falric held her gaze, weighing insolence against being remembered as the man who turned away the warning already in his kitchens.

Then he stepped aside. "With me."

The camp crowded close. Hammer blows from the forge. Wet canvas snapping in the wind. Men speaking too low by the healer's line and too loud by the horse pickets. A novice priest turned from a cot with blood on both sleeves and defeat on his face.

Jaina would have sorted the camp in a minute. Sylvanas could only count bodies, supplies, and how much of the place had been built for a visible war.

Falric stopped at a broad command pavilion striped in Lordaeron blue, spoke to the guard, and vanished inside. He came back almost at once.

"The prince will see you."

Braziers lost to wet wool and map ink. Arthas Menethil stood over a trestle table pinned with daggers, dispatch tubes, and a map of northern Lordaeron crowded with markers. He looked up when she entered, and for a moment Strahnbrad sat just under his face.

Command had cut it harder, not wiser.

"Ranger-General," he said. "You have chosen a poor day to prove me wrong."

"If I wanted that, I would have come sooner."

Uther stood across the table, big hands braced on the wood. Age had thickened him without slowing him. Every officer at the table leaned toward Arthas. Uther said nothing.

Arthas pointed at the map. "Show me."

So she did. Not all of it. Not Jaina inside Caer Darrow. Not Kel'Thuzad's full shape under the work. Those belonged to another road.

She gave him grain routes, storehouses, villages gone quiet before local lords admitted anything worse than fever. She laid Eastweald over his troop lines and watched his face change as one set of roads ate the other. Andorhal. Barov holdings. Tainted grain hidden in ordinary loads. Bread carrying it from table to table. Men falling sick in their own homes before the dead started rising.

Arthas's hand flattened over Hearthglen.

"How far?"

"Far enough that you are already late."

He took it cleanly. Two markers moved, then three.

"No bread from local stores until every granary within a day's ride is checked," he said to Falric. "Seal the quartermaster line. Anything doubtful burns."

Falric turned at once.

"And if your men have eaten already?" Sylvanas asked.

Arthas's eyes cut back to her. "Then I would rather know before the dead tell me."

He kept asking questions, good ones, and too few. Where the grain moved fastest. Which holdings mattered. Whether the Cult favored roads, storehouses, or mills. She answered cleanly. With each answer he sharpened, and the officers stopped qualifying their questions.

"You are describing more than poison," Uther said at last.

"Yes."

"And you know this how?"

Because Jaina had read a kingdom through dead blood, spoiled bread, and a rotting farmhouse corpse. Because the missing mage had spent weeks building the proof while institutions argued about permission.

"Because poison kills where it lands," Sylvanas said. "This travels first. Then the dead get up."

Uther's hands lifted from the table edge, then fell empty to his sides.

Arthas was already on the next turn. "Then Hearthglen was never an isolated outbreak. It was a loaded stop on the road."

"Yes."

"Stratholme too."

"Almost certainly."

Tent heat closed around the table.

"My lord," Falric said from the entrance, "the riders—"

"Ready them," Arthas said. "Every rider who can keep a saddle. Uther, send the paladins out with the vanguard. If a town starts turning, I will not learn it from the smoke."

Uther's jaw set. "Arthas. We do not know enough yet."

"We know enough."

"Enough to move," Uther said. "Not enough to stop thinking."

Arthas gripped the table edge until the knuckles went white. "Thinking is why we are standing in a plague camp instead of a graveyard."

That was a lie, and how fast he needed it chilled her more than the order.

He looked back at her. "Will you ride with us?"

No.

The answer came before the question finished. The southern road was already moving. The northern one mattered more.

"No," she said. "I came to light the fuse, not carry it."

Uther's gaze cut to her. Sharp enough to count as thanks.

Arthas gave one short nod. "Then get clear before the column forms."

"Gladly."

Outside, the camp had turned inside out. Tents dropping. Harness flung over sweating backs. Mess fires kicked apart. In another country, six hundred horses would have been thunder. Here the wet ground swallowed half the noise.

Uther caught her before she reached Cyndia.

"Ranger-General."

Close up he was no statue, only an old campaign scar given shoulders. Half his life had gone to pulling boys back from calls that would cost them everything.

"You know what he is," he said.

"Yes."

He glanced once toward the mounting column. "He has never known how to step back from a fight once he thinks the field chose him."

Behind them camp noise swelled. Orders, buckles, hooves, men running because the prince had decided running was virtue.

"Then why let him lead?"

His mouth tightened. Briefly. Then the discipline closed over it again.

"Because I was one of the men who taught him decisiveness was holy."

He held her a moment longer. When he let her go, weariness sat where grief had shown.

"May the Light keep you from needing him again."

"It won't."

The corner of his mouth moved once. Then he turned back toward Arthas.

Dust and damp rose over the southern road. Arthas at the front beneath Menethil blue. Falric close enough to hear the first changed order. Uther there too, because he could not save Arthas from himself and could not walk away.

Sylvanas watched until the last wagons vanished.

Cyndia brought the horses. "North?"

"After one thing."

Falric had left the dispatch where he promised, Farstrider wax over relay marks from other hands. Lor'themar's script cut across the page with less patience than ink usually got from him.

Verification teams had reached Eastweald: scrubbed floors, cold ash, missing records, empty cellars. Witnesses gone. Local lords suddenly offended by questions. And from Silvermoon, pressure: return, explain, wait.

One line had been underlined hard enough to bruise the parchment.

If you mean to finish this in the field, finish it before procedure gets there first.

She folded the page. No more pretending she was filing border reports from safe ground. She was inside Lordaeron's collapse now, with no clean way back out.

"Bad?" Cyndia asked.

"Predictable."

A second rider came from the north before she could mount. Farstrider tack. Foam down the mare's chest. Blood at one fetlock from pace, not battle. Darrowmere mud to the belly. The scout half-fell from the saddle and shoved over the message tube.

"Ranger-General."

She broke the seal one-handed.

Barov estate entered under mage authority. Ferry watch saw the mage cross to Caer Darrow before dusk and saw no return by nightfall.

No detail. No guessing. Good.

The page stayed still in her hand. Around her the southern road still held Arthas's dust. North lay under mist, stone, and an island keep Jaina had entered alone.

She handed the dispatch to Cyndia. The other ranger read once and turned for the horses before the page had settled.

"North," Cyndia said.

"North."

Sylvanas hit the saddle before the word finished.

The camp fell behind them. So did the last chance to pretend the plague could be solved by telling the right men and letting rank handle it. Arthas rode south with an army and the certainty Uther had learned to fear. Jaina was somewhere beyond Darrowmere with no archer on her flank and no one pricing the exits for her.

The road forked at the shrine marker. They took the northern branch at a gallop.

Cyndia checked right once, then again.

This time Sylvanas looked straight at it. The ghost had a place in the line. So did the missing mage.

Ahead, the country lowered toward lake ground and old stone. Mist filled the hollows. Evening gathered under the trees. Somewhere beyond sat Caer Darrow, island-walled, and whatever had kept Jaina off the road long enough that nobody had seen her come back out.

Sylvanas bent low over the mare's neck and drove harder.
