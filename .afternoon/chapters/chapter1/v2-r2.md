# Chapter 1: The Border

The troll came out of the fern with the axe already in flight.

Sylvanas hit the ground before her mare finished shying, bow in hand, world cut to the things that mattered: wet leaves, footing, one broad troll charging with another axe already up because the first had missed and buried itself in a tree behind her.

Good.

She drew on the move. The eye was too small through the bounce of his stride. Lower. She loosed and stepped left, turning the rush into a passing problem. The arrow punched in beneath the collarbone. He staggered. Two rangers had blades half out by then, late enough to be decoration.

The troll tore at the shaft and roared.

She was already drawing again. The second arrow went through his mouth and into the spine. Blood sprayed. He hit the ferns hard, twitched once, then lay still.

Silence returned in pieces: a horse blowing hard, tack settling, a bird starting up again above the amber canopy.

Lor'themar rode up, took in the corpse and the axe in the tree, and said, “You left that one for me on purpose.”

“You were thinking about tomorrow.”

“A failure of judgment. I won't repeat it.”

She braced a boot on the troll's shoulder, worked the arrow free, and said, “You will. The Convocation does that to your face.”

“My face is an innocent thing abused by bad company.”

She wiped the shaft on fern and slid it back into the quiver. Around them the Farstriders finished the sweep in the quiet way she trusted more than treaties. One checked the corpse for tokens, another the brush beyond. Their horses waited under the ward hum, dark at the knees with road mud in Eversong's mild false spring.

Clean enough. The rest of the day might still be hers.

She swung back into the saddle. This was the part of the world that made sense: bow across her back, road under hoof, a border that could be ridden and held by living hands instead of argued over by people who mistook maps for authority.

Lor'themar fell in beside her as the patrol turned south. Sunlight slid through the leaves and caught in his hair, the links at his throat, the smear of troll blood on his vambrace.

“Tomorrow,” he said, “the southeastern patrol cuts go before the Convocation.”

“My condolences.”

“Accepted. You may show your grief by attending.”

“I outrank grief.”

“You outrank nothing in that chamber. That is why they built it.”

The patrol laughed behind them. Sylvanas let it die. The road smelled of leaf mold, horse sweat, and the faint ozone of the ward-stones. A budget session could wait until after the patrol.

“We should check the southern stones,” she said.

“Should we.”

“The pass wind has been rough.”

“On the stones.”

“On everything south of the city, unless the weather's worsened there too.”

“Cyndia said you'd say that.”

“Cyndia has become insolent.”

“Competent. Close relation.”

“Dangerous relation.”

They rode on at the pace they had fallen into years ago, neither setting it, both keeping it. Around them: gold leaves that never quite turned, dark water somewhere off the road, the ward-stones' low note under everything. Sylvanas kept count because she tracked what guarded her lands. A gap in birdsong. Iron against leather. The hum deepening as they neared another node.

South suited her. The farther from Silvermoon, the easier her lungs worked.

“If you miss tomorrow,” Lor'themar said, “they'll cut the Amani corridor by a third out of wounded dignity.”

“Then talk them back to two-thirds.”

“Again.”

“You do love traditions.”

“One day I'm going to make you sit through the whole session.”

“No.”

“It has history on its side,” he said before she could.

The road dipped. A runestone stood off to the left under climbing vine, white surface scored with old Thalassian lines. Her mare sidestepped once at the bass vibration, then settled under the reins.

There. An excuse dressed as diligence. One more stretch south. One more hour without a ceiling over her head.

“When this runner from Cyndia appears,” Lor'themar said, “I want credit for prophecy.”

“If Cyndia sends a runner for a ward-stone check, I'll send her north to accountancy.”

“Cruel.”

“Effective.”

He was smiling when the sound came out of the south.

Hoofbeats. Hard, ugly, a horse ridden past pride and into need.

The runner rounded the bend on a lathered grey and hauled it down hard. Young ranger. Mud to the horse's chest. Sweat dark on her leathers.

“Ranger-General.” The words came clipped by training and short by breath. “Message from Cyndia's post. Human mage crossed the first ward ring before dawn. Reached the second. Caught at the stones.”

The pleasant day ended there.

“Inside the ward line?” Lor'themar asked.

“Yes, my lord. No credentials. No escort. No permit seal.”

Sylvanas caught the bowstring. Human mage. Inside her wards. That smelled of trouble with paperwork behind it.

“Name?”

“Wouldn't give one at first. Kept demanding to inspect the southern stones. Says the lines are wrong.” The runner forced another breath. “Says rot is riding the leylines north.”

Lor'themar closed his eyes for half a beat. “Of course Cyndia found you on this road.”

The runner risked a glance at him. “She said, my lord, that if the Ranger-General was not on the road to Silvermoon she would be on the road away from it, and the southern track was the sensible place to look.”

“Cyndia continues to justify her existence,” he said.

Sylvanas had already gone cold inside. A human mage alone might mean Kirin Tor arrogance, Lordaeron stupidity, or one gifted idiot with no sense. A human mage inside the ward network, talking about leylines at dawn, meant one of those and a problem with her stones besides.

“How far in?”

“Second ring. She had stopped at the nearest stone, kneeling with frost worked through a chalk circle and notes all over the ground. Cyndia says it wasn't breaching so much as...” The runner dragged in another breath.

“Choose.”

“Measuring, ma'am.”

Lor'themar looked at Sylvanas. “Worse.”

“Everything said by a human mage inside my borders is worse.”

“Some of them manage to be charming while doing it.”

She ignored that. “She's contained?”

“Yes, Ranger-General. Staff taken. Satchel taken. Holding room secured. Cyndia wants someone above her pay before she goes further. The prisoner won't stop saying the lines are carrying rot.”

There it was again. Rot. A word for spoiled grain and bad wells, not ward-stones.

Which made it either nonsense or a warning about the wards in the language of spoiled grain and wells.

“The Convocation will enjoy this,” Lor'themar said.

“It can enjoy hearing about it after I'm done with it.”

“Someone has to tell them before Antonidas's apprentice vanishes under Farstrider guard.”

“You know who she is?”

“I know which human names are worth keeping in the dossiers. A young mage with enough nerve to cross alone and enough confidence to lecture ward-stones narrows the list.”

Jaina Proudmoore, maybe. Still no proof. Still no proof this was anything more than an intrusive scholar with too much magic and no sense.

But it had reached her road now.

“Take the patrol north,” she said.

“You say that as if you've stolen my line.”

“You were going to say it badly.”

“I was going to say it diplomatically.”

“Exactly.”

He turned in the saddle and gave the order. The rangers moved at once, and old satisfaction moved through her at the sight of it.

He reined in beside her one last time. “If it is Proudmoore, the chamber will call it court business before it calls it a threat.”

“Then don't let them.”

“As always, your political guidance is invaluable.”

“Go be expensive, Lor'themar.”

That won the breath of a laugh she wanted. “Try not to start a war before noon.”

“No promises.”

He looked south once, then took the north road at a canter with the patrol streaming after him.

Gone.

Lor'themar was gone, and she knew it at once.

Sylvanas turned her mare south. The runner wheeled the grey after her; Sylvanas cut a hand back. “Beside me. Talk while we ride.”

They went hard.

The southern track broke rougher than the road behind them, gravel loosening under the first sprint, trees thinning as the land sloped toward the pass. Wind found them where the canopy opened.

“Everything,” Sylvanas said.

“First sighting just before dawn. Cyndia and the watch felt a drag in the hum at the outer stones. She took two rangers and found the prisoner kneeling at the second marker with a chalk circle under the stone and frost cracked white through it. No attempt to flee. She argued when ordered back.”

“Argued what.”

“That it was carrying damage from farther south and nobody was listening. She tried to keep working while she said it.”

“Any sign she forced the first ring?”

“No blast marks. No burned grass. The stones reacted, but wrong for an outside strike. More like...” Another pause.

“You've had your warning.”

“That she found an opening that was already there and kept leaning on it.”

Not better.

They passed between two old standing stones. The ward hum thickened until it buzzed in Sylvanas's teeth. Her mare tossed her head. South of here the land lost some of Eversong's soft false warmth and became only air.

Someone else's country lay a few miles beyond. That had always been the point.

“Anything else on her?” Sylvanas asked.

“Field satchel. Notes. Staff. No court trim. Travel-worn.” The runner found plainer words with effort. “Hands marked up. Ink on them. Frost burns too.”

“Frost burns.”

“Knuckles. Two fingers. Worked raw by cold magic.”

She held onto that.

Court mages came polished. Frost-bitten knuckles and ink stains belonged to scholars and field workers who let the job split their hands and stain their fingers.

The track dropped again. Far ahead, the southern stones showed pale through the wind where the pass narrowed into defended ground. Sylvanas leaned lower and gave the mare more head. The horse lengthened without fuss.

Mountain wind met them there, carrying dust, worked stone, and under both the old song of the wards.

Rain slicked the ground here, riders had to go single file, and scouts liked to wait when raiders or smugglers stirred beyond the line. Human farms and roads crowded the border in their usual untidiness.

At the border, she went exact.

And now a human mage had put hands on her stones.

By the time the outpost came into full view, anger had worn down into hard focus on the outpost. Stone buildings against the slope. Watch platforms in the trees. A lean-to stable. Three ward monoliths marking the line with pale fire. Rangers on alert, bows close, waiting to hear whether this was still a holding problem or had become a killing one.

Cyndia met her outside, braid dark at the temples, posture still straight despite the drag of fatigue at the corners of her mouth.

“Ranger-General.”

“Ranger.”

Sylvanas dismounted.

“You sent the runner well,” she said.

“I know your habits, ma'am.”

“A risk to discipline.”

“A convenience to the border.”

There. Insolent. Competent too. She let it live.

“Report.”

Cyndia led her to the nearest ward-stone. Chalk still dusted the base. Frost glazed one side thin and bright. Heel marks scored the dirt nearby.

“First disturbance at fourth bell. Not a breach signal. The tone dragged low and doubled back on itself. Lyren, Fela, and I found the prisoner here.” She pointed to the frost-webbed chalk. “Working some kind of reading spell. Not at the ward itself. At what it was carrying.”

“She said that too.”

“Yes, Ranger-General. Repeatedly.”

“Did she try to break through when challenged.”

“No. She tried to finish the line she'd started. Then she tried to explain.”

“Worse.”

“Considerably.”

They crouched by the stone. Sylvanas touched the carved base. Warm as ever. The hum steady now. No crackle of a recent attack, no residue of anything forced in from outside. Whatever the human had done, she had done it with the ward rather than against it.

“You took everything?”

“Staff, satchel, notes, three chipped focus crystals, and a knife small enough to pass for decorative until you touched the edge. No seal case. No diplomatic packet. No escort tokens.”

“Name.”

“She wouldn't give one at first. Objected to every question that slowed her down.” A beat. “Jaina Proudmoore.”

Sylvanas knew the name at once.

Antonidas's apprentice. Kul Tiras blood. Too much talent for her age and enough nerve to make it everyone's business.

“How did you confirm it.”

“Her notes. One crystal answered to her hand when Lyren tried it. I left the rest sealed.”

“Everything feels above your pay.”

“Not troll arrows.”

Fair.

Sylvanas looked again at the frost at the base of the stone. If it was Jaina Proudmoore, trust still had to be earned; the name only made her stranger than expected, and strange problems became personal if you left them alone.

“How did she take confinement.”

“Angry, with any fear buried too deep to show. She wanted back at the stones. Kept asking whether we'd felt the bad patch in the lines on the north sweep. When I told her she could answer questions after she sat down and stopped reaching for the staff, she said I was wasting time we did not have.”

“Charming.”

“Not especially.”

“Did you hit her.”

“Only enough.”

Also fair.

“Show me the satchel.”

A guard brought it over. Weathered leather, tied with ordinary cord. Sylvanas opened it herself.

Paper. More than she had expected. Notes in a cramped hand blotted by cold or haste. Charcoal rubbings of runes. Sketches of the southern stones from several angles, each crossed with lines she did not need to understand to distrust. No ornamental nonsense. No diplomatic letters tucked between the pages in case manners failed.

Fieldwork, then. Ugly, hurried, sincere-looking in the least convenient way possible.

One sheet had been folded and refolded until the edges softened. Sylvanas opened it.

A map of the southern approaches with notes at several ley crossings. More ink stains. A blue-black thumbprint. A line in the margin: spread northward along existing channels—faster near population routes.

She folded it shut.

“Did she write all this here.”

“Some on the road. Some before she crossed. She had miles on her before we took her.”

Boots. Ink. Frost burns.

“Holding room?”

“Secured.”

“Any trouble since we put her in it.”

“Less once the staff was out of reach. More once she realised that wouldn't get it back.”

The post had gone quieter while they talked. Nothing visible had changed. Voices had dropped around the yard.

“Who has seen her besides you, Lyren, and Fela.”

“Only the two door guards. I kept the rest back.”

Good. Fewer tongues.

“Lor'themar rides north already,” Sylvanas said. “The Convocation will hear some version of this before sunset. I'd prefer it to be mine.”

“Understood.”

“And Cyndia.”

“Ma'am.”

“If this turns out to be a scholar's tantrum dressed in expensive frostwork, I will remember that you interrupted a good patrol day.”

Cyndia's mouth twitched once. “If it's real, you'll remember I interrupted it early.”

Dangerous relation.

Sylvanas turned toward the holding room.

Stone box at the edge of the yard, built for smugglers, spies, and the occasional fool who mistook elven borders for suggestions. Narrow window. Iron-bound door. Extra power blue around the threshold. Beside the wall, where nobody could miss what had been taken, stood a pale staff with its crystal head gone cloudy in the outpost light.

It should have pleased her more than it did.

Cyndia kept pace half a step back. “One more thing, Ranger-General.”

“Say it.”

“She was furious we wouldn't let her keep working.”

“That makes her reckless.”

“Yes, ma'am.”

“Or convinced.”

“Yes, ma'am.”

“I dislike both.”

At the door the guards straightened. Blue light climbed Sylvanas's boots and sharpened the iron bands. Inside: one person breathing too tightly to pass for calm.

She gave a short nod. A guard opened the door.

Small room. Bench. Bucket. One slit window losing cold afternoon light to the ward glow. The prisoner stood too fast for indifference, too controlled for panic. Travel robes, dusty hem, boots grey with miles. Bare hands.

Ink stained one finger.

The knuckles were worse. Frost had left them red and split in two places where magic had bitten back. Her braid was coming loose at the neck. Hair the color of late wheat, more weather than care in it now. Younger than the dossier suggested. Not soft. Nothing soft in the set of the shoulders. She held herself braced for disbelief and finished with courtesy about it.

A field mage, then—travel-worn, split-knuckled, and miles past court polish.

The prisoner met Sylvanas straight on at once. Tired at the edges. Furious enough to hide it.

Jaina Proudmoore, then.

Just a hard-used young mage in a border cell, hands chewed raw by her own work, all of her bent toward the ward-stone instead of this room.

Sylvanas stepped inside. Door open behind her. Cyndia at her shoulder. Staff out of reach. Exits known.

Good.

Ink. Split knuckles. Frostbite.

She let the silence pull tight until the ward-light was the loudest thing in the room.

Then she said, “You crossed my ward line alone. Start there.”
