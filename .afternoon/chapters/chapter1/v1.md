# Chapter 1: The Border

The troll came out of the fern with the axe already in flight.

Sylvanas was off the saddle before the horse had finished shying. One hand caught the pommel, the other the bow, and the world narrowed to the simple parts that always pleased her most: distance, footing, line. Wet leaves under her boots. Amber light broken by the canopy. One troll, broad through the chest, blue tattoos gone slick with sweat, another axe coming up from his belt because the first had already missed.

Missed by inches. It buried itself in a tree behind her with a wet chop and a tremor through the bark.

The troll charged.

Good.

She drew as she moved, the string biting into fingers hard with long use, and the shot settled in her hands before she loosed it. The eye was too small through the bounce of his stride. Lower. She let the arrow go and stepped left at the same time, turning his rush into a passing problem.

The shaft struck under the collarbone with a sound like a fist driven into soaked cloth. He staggered. Two more rangers had blades half out, too slow by the time they understood the kill was already hers.

The troll ripped at the arrow and roared.

Sylvanas had another nocked before the first snapped in his grip. This one took him through the mouth and into the spine. The roar broke into a spray of blood. He went down hard enough to flatten the crushed ferns beneath him, limbs twitching once, twice, and then not at all.

Silence came back in layers. A horse blowing hard through its nose. Tack whispering as the patrol mounts settled under familiar hands. Somewhere high in the amber canopy, a bird that had wisely stopped singing started again.

Lor'themar rode up at an easy walk, looking from the dead troll to the axe in the tree and then to her. “You left that one for me on purpose.”

“You were thinking about tomorrow.”

“A failure of judgment. I won't repeat it.”

She planted one boot on the troll's shoulder and freed the second arrow with a twist. Blood sheeted down the blue-black hide, sharp and coppery in the still air. “You will. The Convocation inspires the same expression on your face every time.”

“My face is an innocent thing abused by bad company.”

She wiped the arrow on fern and slid it back into the quiver. Around them the Farstriders moved through the last of the sweep with the quiet efficiency she trusted more than most treaties. One ranger checked the dead for tokens, another the brush beyond for a second body that was not there. Their horses waited in the track under the ward hum, fine-boned field animals gone dark at the knees with road mud, breathing steamless in Eversong's mild false spring.

Finished. Cleanly enough to leave a body on the ground and the rest of the day still hers.

For the moment.

She crossed to her mare and swung up. Leather settled. So did she. This was where the world made sense: the weight of the bow across her back, the road under hoof, a border that could be ridden and checked and held by living hands instead of debated by people who believed maps obeyed them out of courtesy.

Lor'themar fell in beside her as the patrol turned south. Sunlight worked loose through the high leaves, washing the road in gold. It caught in his hair, in the polished links at his throat, in the damp smear on his vambrace where troll blood had landed and been ignored.

“Tomorrow,” he said, because he lacked the tact to leave a good morning alone, “the southeastern patrol projections go before the Convocation.”

“My condolences.”

“Accepted. You may demonstrate your grief by attending.”

She looked ahead, where the road slipped between pale trunks and long strips of shade. “I outrank grief.”

“You outrank nothing in that chamber. That is why they built it.”

The patrol behind them laughed in the careful, short way rangers did when the joke involved the Convocation and therefore truth. Sylvanas let the sound run and die. The easy stretch after a kill had opened in her chest, clean and bright. She had bled that troll's band off the forest floor. The road was hers. The day still smelled of leaf mold, horse sweat, and the faint ozone of the ward network threaded through the old trees. A budget session could wait until it developed legs and hunted her down itself.

“We should check the southern stones,” she said.

Lor'themar's mouth moved at one corner. “Should we.”

“The pass wind has been rough this week.”

“On the stones.”

“On everything south of the city, unless the weather has learned respect.”

“Cyndia said you'd say that.”

“Cyndia has become insolent.”

“Competent. Close relation.”

“Dangerous relation.”

“To councils, certainly.”

They rode on at the shared pace they had fallen into decades ago, neither setting it, both obeying it. The patrol stretched and compressed behind them with the curves of the road. Eversong held around them in her usual way, all curated wilderness and old power: golden leaves that never properly turned, dark water running somewhere off the track, the bass note of ward-stones too low for human ears and too familiar for most elves to mark. Sylvanas marked it because she marked what defended her lands. A gap in birdsong. The scrape of iron on leather. The small change in the hum when a node came nearer on the road.

South suited her. The farther from Silvermoon, the easier her lungs worked.

“If you miss tomorrow,” Lor'themar said, “they'll cut the Amani corridor complement by a third out of wounded dignity.”

“Then talk them back to two-thirds.”

“Again.”

“You do enjoy traditions.”

He snorted. “One day I'm going to let you suffer through the full session.”

“No.”

“A persuasive counterargument.”

“It has history on its side.”

The road dipped. A runestone stood off to the left, half wrapped in climbing vine, white surface scored with old Thalassian lines that glimmered only when the light struck them wrong. Her mare's ears twitched as they passed. The horse sidestepped once at the bass vibration, then settled when Sylvanas shortened the reins and gave the quiet pressure that meant enough foolishness.

There. An excuse dressed as diligence. One more stretch south. One more hour with no ceiling overhead and no councillor asking whether troll arrows could be made to do the work of patrols more economically.

The next turn brought open track and the long amber tunnel of road beyond. Lor'themar let his horse draw even with hers, close enough that the stirrups nearly brushed.

“When this runner from Cyndia appears,” he said, “I want credit for prophecy.”

“If Cyndia sends a runner for a ward-stone check, I'll transfer her north to accountancy.”

“Cruel.”

“Effective.”

He was smiling when the sound came out of the south.

Hoofbeats. Hard, ugly, wrong for the road. No patrol rhythm in them, none of the measured pace of elves on known ground. This was a horse pushed past dignity and into need.

They hit Sylvanas first. Head up, reins shortening, every pleasant loose thought gone at once.

The runner burst around the bend on a lathered grey with foam at the bit and mud to the chest. Young ranger. Field leathers dark with sweat. She hauled the horse down so fast the animal nearly sat and saluted in the same motion.

“Ranger-General.” The words came clipped by training and short by lack of breath. “Message from Cyndia's post. Human mage crossed the first ward ring before dawn. Reached the second line. Caught at the stones.”

The easy day died where it sat.

“Inside the grid?” Lor'themar asked.

“Yes, my lord. No credentials. No escort. No permit seal.”

Sylvanas's fingers found the bowstring without asking permission. Human mage. Inside her ward line. That smelled less like trespass and more like paperwork with ambitions.

“Name?”

“Wouldn't give one at first. Keeps demanding to inspect the southern stones. Says the lines are wrong.” The runner swallowed, got her breath back by force. “Says something's moving through the leylines from the south.”

Lor'themar's expression did a private, tired thing. “Of course Cyndia found you on this road.”

The runner risked a glance at him. “She said, my lord, that if the Ranger-General was not on the road to Silvermoon the Ranger-General would be on the road away from it, and that the southern patrol track was the sensible place to look.”

“Cyndia continues to justify her existence,” Lor'themar said.

Sylvanas was already past irritation and into the colder part of thought. A human mage alone might mean Kirin Tor arrogance, Lordaeron stupidity, or one ambitious idiot with more talent than judgment. A human mage inside the ward network, talking about wrong leylines at dawn, meant those possibilities plus a problem with her own stones. That narrowed her temper without improving it.

“How far in?”

“Second ring. She wasn't forcing her way north. She was working at the nearest stone with some kind of frost spell and written notes all over the ground. Cyndia says it looked less like breaching and more like...” The runner hesitated.

“Choose a word, ranger.”

“Testing, ma'am.”

Lor'themar looked at Sylvanas. “Well. That's worse.”

“Everything said by a human mage inside my borders is worse.”

“Some of them speak very prettily while being worse.”

She ignored that. “She's contained?”

“Yes, Ranger-General. Staff confiscated, satchel confiscated, holding room secured. Cyndia requests senior authority before proceeding further. The prisoner won't stop insisting the lines are carrying contamination.”

There it was again. Not invasion. Not demand for asylum. Contamination. A word that belonged to crop rot and poisoned wells, not to ward networks.

Which made it either nonsense or the wrong kind of truth.

Lor'themar exhaled through his nose. “The Convocation will enjoy this.”

“No. It will enjoy hearing about it after I've finished with it.”

He gave her a look. “Someone has to tell them before a human archmage's apprentice turns up missing under Farstrider supervision.”

“You know who she is?”

“I know which human names make the border dossiers worth reading. A young mage with enough nerve to cross the line alone and enough confidence to lecture ward-stones probably narrows the list.”

That did not improve her mood either. The Farstriders kept records on powerful human mages for a reason. Some because they were useful. Some because they were dangerous. A few because they were both. Jaina Proudmoore lived in the second list and, if reports from the south were to be trusted, was walking toward the first with indecent speed.

Still no proof it was her. Still no proof this was anything except an intrusive scholar with more magic than sense.

But it had reached her road now.

“Take the patrol north,” she said.

Lor'themar's head turned a fraction. “You say that as if you've stolen my line.”

“You were going to say it badly.”

“I was going to say it diplomatically.”

“Exactly.”

He took the rebuke with the patience of long practice and swung his horse around to face the patrol. “You heard the Ranger-General. We split here. North with me. Quickly enough to look efficient, slowly enough not to kill the horses before we reach people who only value them on paper.”

The rangers moved at once. Saddles turned. A brief confusion of hooves and green cloaks and shifting light, then order again. Old satisfaction moved through her at the sight of command well obeyed.

Lor'themar reined in beside her one last time. Up close she could see the thing she had mocked before the runner came: the Convocation already on him, like weather a day ahead of itself.

“If it is Proudmoore,” he said quietly, “the chamber will call it a diplomatic problem before it allows it to become a security one.”

“Then don't let them.”

“As always, your political guidance is invaluable.”

“Go be expensive, Lor'themar.”

That won the breath of a laugh she wanted. “Try not to start a war before noon.”

“No promises.”

He looked south once, toward the pass, toward whatever had climbed inside her wards and named itself trouble. Then he nodded, not to agree but because there was nothing left to say, and took the north road at a canter with the patrol flowing after him.

Gone.

The space beside her opened like a missing board in a bridge.

Sylvanas turned her mare south. The runner wheeled the grey without being told and fell in behind her for half a length before Sylvanas cut a hand back. “Beside me. Talk while we ride.”

They went hard at once.

The southern track ran rougher than the road behind them, gravel loosening under the first sprint, trees thinning as the land angled down toward the pass. Hoofbeats changed with it. Not the muffled drumming of Eversong loam now, but sharper strikes, stone and packed dirt, the sound of a border instead of a forest. Wind found them where the canopy broke. Cooler. Less tamed.

“Everything,” Sylvanas said.

The runner did not waste breath on preface. “First sighting just before dawn. Cyndia and the post watch felt a disturbance at the outer stones, not a breach exactly — a drag in the hum. She took two rangers and found the prisoner kneeling at the second marker with a chalk circle under the stone and frost worked through it like cracks in lake ice. No attempt to flee. She argued when ordered back.”

“Argued what.”

“That the ward wasn't under attack. That it was carrying damage from farther south and nobody was listening.” The runner's mouth tightened around whatever comment wanted out. “She tried to keep working while she spoke.”

There was a kind of arrogance in that Sylvanas respected against her better judgment, which annoyed her more than the arrogance itself.

“Any sign she forced the first ring?”

“No blast marks. No burned grass. The stones reacted, but Cyndia said it looked wrong for an external strike. More like...” Again the pause.

“You've had your warning.”

“More like she found an opening already there and kept pressing it, ma'am.”

Not better.

The road narrowed between two standing stones older than Silvermoon and less patient with trespass. As they passed, the ward hum thickened until it sat in Sylvanas's teeth. Her mare tossed her head. South of here the enchantment on the land thinned enough to notice if you had ever been raised inside it. The amber cast faded. Greens went duller. The air lost some of its soft false warmth and became only air.

Someone else's country began a few miles beyond. That had always been the point.

Not today, apparently.

“Anything else on her?” Sylvanas asked.

“Field satchel. Notes. Staff. No court trim on her. Travel-worn.” The runner searched for the right plain words and found them. “Hands marked up. Ink on them. Frost burns too.”

“Frost burns.”

“Knuckles. Two fingers. Like she'd been working cold magic too hard.”

That lodged.

Court mages came polished. Even the dangerous ones. They wore their power with enough care to pass among diplomats and still terrify anyone worth terrifying. Frost-burned knuckles and ink stains belonged to a different breed: scholars who had let the work eat the body around it, field mages who forgot to stop before their hands paid for it.

Which again suggested either sincerity or a more talented lie than she had expected.

The track dropped another grade. The trees opened. Far ahead, the southern stones showed pale through the wind, tall and regular where the pass tightened into defended ground. Sylvanas leaned lower over the mare's neck and gave her more head. The horse answered like a Farstrider mount should — no drama, just longer stride and harder breath.

Mountain wind came at them now, steady through the bottleneck. It carried dust, the cold mineral smell of worked stone, and under both the low old song of the wards. The nearer they came, the more the road hardened under that sound.

She knew this stretch. Knew where the ground ran slick after rain, where the trail narrowed enough to take riders single file, where scouts preferred to post when the southern line grew restless. Knew, too, where the human lands began to lean against hers with all their usual untidiness: greener leaves, rougher roads, fewer centuries of being arranged by people who intended to keep them.

The border never made her sentimental. It made her exact.

And now a human mage had stepped across it and put hands on her stones.

No wonder the day had curdled.

By the time the outpost came into full view, her anger had worn itself into a colder, more useful thing. Stone-built structures tucked against the slope. Watch platforms in the trees above. A stable lean-to. Three ward monoliths marking the line with pale fire under the carved runes. Rangers on alert, not panicked but extended — bows close, shoulders set, the particular stillness of people waiting for senior authority to tell them whether this remained a holding problem or had become a killing one.

Cyndia was already outside, braid darkened at the temples, posture straight enough to count as respect and tired enough to count as truth. She took the report from Sylvanas's horse before the mare had fully stopped.

“Ranger-General.”

“Ranger.”

Sylvanas dismounted. Gravel shifted under her boots. The ward-light painted blue along the edges of the stones and turned the lower hems of every cloak colder than the day deserved.

“You sent the runner well,” she said.

“I know your habits, ma'am.”

“A risk to discipline.”

“A convenience to the border.”

There. Insolent. Competent too. She let it live.

“Report.”

Cyndia led her toward the nearest ward-stone as she spoke, not because Sylvanas needed the walk but because some reports landed better with the evidence in sight. The stone still bore a scattering of white chalk ground into its base. Frost had glazed one side in a delicate skin that hadn't yet fully melted, though the air was warm enough for it. Nearby, the dirt showed the heel marks of a struggle that had been brief and efficient.

“First disturbance at fourth bell. Not a breach signal. The tone dragged low and doubled back on itself. I took Lyren and Fela from watch and found the prisoner here.” She pointed to the frost-webbed chalk. “Working some kind of mapping spell. Not at the ward in the usual sense. At what the ward was carrying.”

“She said that too.”

“Yes, Ranger-General. Repeatedly.”

“Did she try to break through when challenged.”

“No. She tried to finish the line she'd started.” Cyndia's mouth tightened, not with judgment but with remembered irritation. “Then she tried to explain.”

“Worse.”

“Considerably.”

They crouched by the stone. Sylvanas put her fingertips to the carved base. Warm, as always. The hum steady now. No crackle of recent attack, no residue of a blast forced from outside. Whatever the human had done here, she had done it in conversation with the ward rather than against it.

Conversation remained an offensive practice in the wrong hands.

“You confiscated everything?”

“Staff, satchel, notes, three chipped focus crystals, a knife small enough to be decorative and sharp enough not to be.” Cyndia lifted her eyes from the chalk. “No seal case. No diplomatic packet. No escort tokens.”

“Name.”

“She wouldn't give one at first. Objected to the question. Objected to all questions that delayed her work.” A pause, then: “Jaina Proudmoore.”

The name landed without spectacle. It did not need any.

Antonidas's apprentice. Kul Tiras blood. The young mage whose reports crossed more desks than her age should have allowed, because talent made its own introductions if it was large enough. Sylvanas had expected court polish, expensive irritation, the smugness of a human who believed magic excused border lines.

“How did you confirm it.”

“Her notes had the name. Later one of the crystals answered to her hand when Lyren tried it.” Cyndia straightened. “I left the rest sealed. This had your name on it.”

“Everything feels above your pay.”

“Not troll arrows.”

Fair.

Sylvanas looked again at the frost etched around the base of the stone. Jaina Proudmoore. Alone. Travel-worn. Hands damaged by her own work. That still did not make the woman honest. It only made her stranger than expected, and strange problems had a way of becoming personal if left alone too long.

“Tell me how she acted once you had her.”

“Angry,” Cyndia said at once. “Not frightened. Or frightened under the anger, maybe, but the anger did most of the visible work. She wanted back at the stones. Kept asking whether we'd felt the wrongness in the lines on the north sweep. When I said she would answer questions after she sat down and stopped trying to reach for her staff, she told me I was wasting time we did not have.”

“Charming.”

“Not especially.”

“Did you hit her.”

“Only enough.”

Also fair.

The outpost around them kept moving in quiet lines. A ranger crossed the yard with a bucket. Another checked the stable knots. Above, on the platform, someone shifted weight and the wood answered with a soft complaint. The world had not narrowed to the holding room yet. That mattered. A border post lived or died by what it could keep routine while trouble waited inside.

“Show me the satchel first,” Sylvanas said.

Cyndia signaled to one of the guards. A moment later a weathered leather satchel arrived, sealed with ordinary cord, not magic. Sylvanas crouched again and opened it herself.

Paper. More of it than she'd expected. Notes in a fast, cramped hand, ink blotted where someone had written in motion or cold. Charcoal rubbings of runes. Diagrams of the southern stones sketched over and over from different angles, each marked with lines she did not need to understand to distrust. No ornamental nonsense. No diplomatic correspondence tucked between the pages in case intelligence failed. This was fieldwork, ugly and urgent and sincere-looking in the least convenient way possible.

One sheet had been folded and refolded until the edges had gone soft. Sylvanas opened it.

A map of the southern approaches with notations at several ley crossings. More ink stains. A thumbprint in blue-black. A line of hurried script at the margin: spread northward along existing channels—faster near population routes.

She shut the page.

“Did she write all this here.”

“Not all. Some on the road, I think. Some before she crossed. She had mileage on her before we put hands on her.” Cyndia's gaze flicked down to the satchel, then away again, professional enough not to read over the Ranger-General's shoulder. “The boots told that much.”

Boots. Frost-burned knuckles. Ink.

This was becoming inconveniently human.

“Holding room?”

“Secured.”

“Any trouble since confinement.”

“Less once we took the staff out of reach. More once she realised that wouldn't return it.”

Sylvanas handed the satchel back and rose. The post had gone quieter by a shade. Nothing visible had changed. Everyone with ears had simply understood this had crossed from routine detention into whatever came next.

“Who has seen her besides you, Lyren, Fela.”

“Only the two door guards. I kept the rest at distance.”

Good. Fewer tongues. Fewer future explanations.

“Lor'themar rides north already,” Sylvanas said. “The Convocation will hear a version of this before sunset whether I like it or not. I'd prefer the version to be mine.”

“Understood.”

“And Cyndia.”

“Ma'am.”

“If this turns out to be a scholar's tantrum dressed in expensive frostwork, I will remember that you interrupted a good patrol day.”

The ghost of a smile threatened one corner of Cyndia's mouth and thought better of it. “If it turns out to be real, you'll remember I interrupted it early.”

There it was again, the dangerous relation.

Sylvanas looked toward the holding structure.

Stone room at the edge of the yard, built for smugglers, spies, and the occasional idiot who mistook elven borders for suggestions. Narrow window. Iron-bound door. Ward-light leaking blue around the frame because the post had thrown extra power on the threshold once the prisoner's name had settled. Beside the wall, propped in plain view where no one could mistake what had been taken, stood a staff of pale wood with a crystal head gone cloudy in the outpost light.

Confiscated staff by the rack.

It should have pleased her more than it did.

Cyndia moved with her, one half-step behind and to the side. “One more thing, Ranger-General.”

“Say it.”

“She wasn't trying to run. She was furious we would not let her go on working.”

Sylvanas kept walking.

“That makes her reckless,” she said.

“Yes, ma'am.”

“Or convinced.”

“Yes, ma'am.”

“I dislike both.”

They reached the door. The guards straightened. Blue light from the threshold ward climbed Sylvanas's boots and sharpened the edges of the iron banding. Up close she could hear more than the hum of the stones outside. Breath within. One person, seated or standing still enough to make little noise, but not calm. The sound had a catch in it now and then, the body held too tight after too many hours on fury alone.

Sylvanas gave the guards a short tilt of her chin. One of them opened the door.

The room was small and all of it useful. Bench against the wall. Bucket in the corner. Single slit window giving up a band of cold afternoon light that lost against the blue from the threshold wards. The prisoner stood when the door opened — too fast for someone trying to look unconcerned, too controlled for panic. Practical travel robes, hem dusted white from the southern road. Boots gone grey with miles. Hands bare.

Ink stained the side of one finger.

The knuckles were worse. Frost burn had left them red and raw, the skin on two split enough to show where magic had bitten its owner on the way through. Her braid was coming apart at the neck. Hair the color of late wheat, no court servant's labor in it now, just haste and weather. Younger than the dossier made her feel. Not soft, though. There was nothing soft in the set of her shoulders. She held herself like a woman braced against being disbelieved and long past courtesy about it.

Wrong. Entirely wrong.

Which was its own offense.

The prisoner's gaze struck her at once and stayed there. Blue eyes. Tired enough to darken at the edges. Furious enough to hide it.

Jaina Proudmoore, then. The notes had said so, but the room said it louder. Dangerous names changed a room exactly this way — no grandeur, just the simple irritation of finding reality less convenient than rumor.

No jewels. No velvet. No polished Kirin Tor escort. Just a hard-used young mage standing in a border cell with damaged hands and the posture of someone who would rather be at the ward-stones than alive anywhere else.

Sylvanas stepped inside. The rangers cleared behind her. Door open. Cyndia at her shoulder. Space controlled, exits known, staff out of reach, prisoner's eyes fixed where they belonged.

Good.

This had stopped being paperwork the moment she saw the hands.

She let the silence stretch until it tightened, until the ward-light was the loudest thing in the room, until the prisoner's anger had time to show itself in the set of her mouth and nowhere else.

Then Sylvanas said, “You crossed my ward line alone. Start there.”
