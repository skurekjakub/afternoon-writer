# Chapter 1: The Border

The troll came out of the fern with the axe already in flight.

Sylvanas hit the leaf mold before her mare finished shying, bow in hand, world cut to footing, range, and one broad Amani charging with another axe already up because the first had buried itself in a birch behind her.

Good.

She drew on the move. The eye was too small through the bounce of his stride. Lower. She loosed and stepped left, turning the rush into a passing problem on the Farstrider road. The arrow punched in beneath the collarbone. He staggered. Two rangers had blades half out by then, late enough to be decoration.

The troll tore at the shaft and roared.

She was already drawing again. The second arrow went through his mouth and into the spine. Blood sprayed dark across the fern bed. He hit the ground hard, twitched once, and went still under Eversong's amber canopy.

Silence returned in pieces: a horse blowing hard, quiet leather tack settling, one bird trying the air again above the gold leaves.

Lor'themar rode up, took in the corpse and the axe in the birch, and said, “You left that one for me on purpose.”

“You were thinking about tomorrow.”

“A failure of judgment. I won't repeat it.”

She braced a boot on the troll's shoulder, worked the arrow free, and said, “You will. The Convocation does that to your face.”

“My face is an innocent thing abused by bad company.”

She wiped the shaft on fern and slid it back into the quiver. Around them the Farstriders finished the sweep in the quiet way she trusted more than treaties, one checking the corpse for tokens while another worked the brush beyond. Their horses waited under the ward hum, dark at the knees with road mud, fine-boned heads lifting and settling as the next runestone answered somewhere deeper in the trees.

Clean enough. The rest of the day might still be hers, and she swung back into the saddle thinking this was the part of Quel'Thalas that made sense: bow across her back, road under hoof, a border that could be ridden and held by living hands instead of argued over under painted ceilings in Silvermoon.

Lor'themar fell in beside her as the patrol turned south. Sunlight slid through the leaves and caught in his hair, the links at his throat, the smear of troll blood on his vambrace.

“Tomorrow,” he said, “the southeastern patrol cuts go before the Convocation.”

“My condolences.”

“Accepted. You may show your grief by attending.”

“I outrank grief.”

“You outrank nothing in that chamber,” Lor'themar said, sunlight catching the links at his throat as he glanced over. “That is why they built it.”

The patrol laughed behind them, and Sylvanas let it die while the road gave her leaf mold, horse sweat, and the faint ozone that always lived around the ward-stones. A budget session could wait until after the patrol.

“We should check the southern stones,” she said.

Lor'themar adjusted his reins, still riding easy beside her. “Should we.”

“The pass wind has been rough.”

“On the stones.”

“On everything south of the city, unless the weather has worsened there too.”

“Cyndia said you'd say that.”

“Cyndia has become insolent.”

“Competent,” he said. “Close relation.”

“Dangerous relation.”

They rode on at the pace they had fallen into years ago, neither setting it, both keeping it. Gold leaves that never turned all the way overhead, dark water somewhere off the road, the ward-stones' low note under everything. Sylvanas kept count because she tracked what guarded her lands: a gap in birdsong, iron against leather, the hum deepening as they neared another node in the ring.

South suited her. The farther from Silvermoon, the easier her lungs worked.

“If you miss tomorrow,” Lor'themar said, “they'll cut the Amani corridor by a third out of wounded dignity.”

“Then talk them back to two-thirds.”

“Again.”

“You do love traditions.”

“One day I'm going to make you sit through the whole session.”

“No.”

“It has history on its side,” he said before she could.

The road dipped. A runestone stood off to the left under climbing vine, its white surface scored with old Thalassian lines. Her mare sidestepped once at the bass note coming off it, then settled under the reins. There: an excuse dressed as diligence, and one more stretch south—one more hour without a roof over her head.

“When this runner from Cyndia appears,” Lor'themar said, “I want credit for prophecy.”

“If Cyndia sends a runner for a ward-stone check, I'll send her north to accountancy.”

“Cruel.”

“Effective.”

He was smiling when the sound came out of the south.

Hoofbeats. Hard, ugly, a horse ridden past pride and into need.

The runner rounded the bend on a lathered grey and hauled it down hard, young ranger and horse both spattered to the chest with mud, sweat dark on her leathers and foam flecking the bit.

“Ranger-General.” The words came clipped by training and short by breath, and the runner hauled in another breath before pressing on. “Message from Cyndia's post. Human mage crossed the first ward ring before dawn, reached the second, and got caught at the stones.”

The pleasant day ended there, dropping out from under her as cleanly as a cut line.

Lor'themar's hand stilled on the pommel. “Inside the ward line?”

“Yes, my lord. No credentials, no escort, no permit seal.”

Sylvanas caught the bowstring. Human mage inside her wards—that smelled of trouble with paperwork behind it.

“Name?”

“Wouldn't give one at first. Kept demanding to inspect the southern stones, said the lines were wrong, and”—the runner forced another breath—“said rot is riding the leylines north.”

Lor'themar closed his eyes for half a beat. “Of course Cyndia found you on this road.”

The runner risked a glance at him. “She said, my lord, that if the Ranger-General was not on the road to Silvermoon she would be on the road away from it, and the southern track was the sensible place to look.”

“Cyndia continues to justify her existence,” he said.

Sylvanas had already gone cold inside. A human mage alone might mean Kirin Tor arrogance, Lordaeron stupidity, or one gifted idiot with no sense; a human mage inside the ward network, talking about leylines at dawn, meant one of those and a problem with her stones besides.

“How far in?”

“Second ring. She had stopped at the nearest stone, kneeling with a chalk circle under the base and frost worked white through it, notes all over the ground. Cyndia says it wasn't breaching so much as...” The runner dragged in another breath.

“Choose.”

“Measuring, ma'am.”

Lor'themar looked at Sylvanas. “Worse.”

“Everything said by a human mage inside my borders is worse.”

“Some of them manage to be charming while doing it.”

She ignored that. “She's contained?”

“Yes, Ranger-General. Staff taken, satchel taken, holding room secured, and the prisoner won't stop saying the lines are carrying rot.”

There it was again. Rot. A word for spoiled grain and bad wells, not runestones, which made it either nonsense or a warning about the wards in the language of spoiled grain and wells.

“The Convocation will enjoy this,” Lor'themar said.

“It can enjoy hearing about it after I'm done with it.”

“Someone has to tell them before Antonidas's apprentice vanishes under Farstrider guard.”

She turned to him. “You know who she is?”

“I know which human names are worth keeping in the dossiers. A young mage with enough nerve to cross alone and enough confidence to lecture ward-stones narrows the list.”

Jaina Proudmoore, maybe, but still no proof this was anything more than an intrusive scholar with too much magic and no sense.

It had reached her road now.

“Take the patrol north,” she said.

“You say that as if you've stolen my line.”

“You were going to say it badly.”

“I was going to say it diplomatically.”

“Exactly.”

He turned in the saddle and gave the order. The rangers moved at once, tack whispering, horses stepping through the turn as one body. The sight of it steadied her.

He reined in beside her one last time. “If it is Proudmoore, the chamber will call it court business before it calls it a threat.”

“Then don't let them.”

“As always, your political guidance is invaluable.”

“Go be expensive, Lor'themar.”

That won the breath of a laugh she wanted. “Try not to start a war before noon.”

“No promises.”

He looked south once, then took the north road at a canter with the patrol streaming after him.

Gone. Lor'themar was gone, and the space at her right hand stayed empty; the road lost its ease with him.

Sylvanas turned her mare south, the runner wheeling the grey after her until Sylvanas cut a hand back. “Beside me. Talk while we ride.”

They went hard, and the southern track broke rougher than the road behind them, gravel loosening under the first sprint as trees thinned and the land sloped toward the Pass. Wind found them where the canopy opened.

“Everything,” Sylvanas said.

“First sighting just before dawn. Cyndia and the watch felt a drag in the hum at the outer stones, and she took two rangers to the second marker, finding the prisoner kneeling with a chalk circle under the base and frost cracked white through it. No attempt to flee. She argued when ordered back.”

“Argued what.”

“That it was carrying damage from farther south and nobody was listening. She tried to keep working while she said it.”

“Any sign she forced the first ring?”

“No blast marks. No burned grass. The stones reacted, but wrong for an outside strike—more like...” Another pause.

“You've had your warning.”

“That she found an opening that was already there and kept leaning on it.”

Not better.

They passed between two old standing stones, the ward hum thickening until it buzzed in Sylvanas's teeth, and her mare tossed her head before lengthening under the bit when Sylvanas steadied her with one hand. South of here the air lost some of Eversong's false spring and became only border wind coming through the cut.

Someone else's country lay a few miles beyond. That had always been the point.

“Anything else on her?” Sylvanas asked.

“Field satchel. Notes. Staff. No court trim. Travel-worn.” The runner searched for plainer words, riding hard to keep pace. “Hands marked up. Ink on them. Frost burns too.”

“Frost burns.”

“Knuckles. Two fingers. Worked raw by cold magic.”

She held onto that.

Court mages came polished, while frost-bitten knuckles and ink stains belonged to scholars and field workers, the sort who let the job split their hands and stain their fingers.

The track dropped again. Far ahead, the southern monoliths showed pale where the pass narrowed into defended ground. Sylvanas leaned lower and gave the mare more head. The horse lengthened without fuss, ears set forward against the wind.

Mountain air met them there, carrying dust, worked stone, and under both the old song of the wards.

Rain slicked the ground here, riders had to go single file, and scouts liked to wait along the cut when raiders or smugglers stirred beyond the line; human farms and roads crowded the border in their usual untidiness.

At the border, she went exact, and now a human mage had put hands on her stones.

By the time the outpost came into full view, anger had worn down into hard focus. Stone buildings against the slope, watch platforms in the trees, a lean-to stable, three ward monoliths marking the line with pale fire. Rangers waited on alert with bows close, ready to hear whether this was still a holding problem or had become a killing one.

Cyndia met her outside, braid dark at the temples, posture still straight despite the drag of fatigue at the corners of her mouth.

“Ranger-General.”

“Ranger.”

Sylvanas dismounted.

“You sent the runner well,” she said.

“I know your habits, ma'am.”

“A risk to discipline.”

“A convenience to the border.”

There—insolent, competent too, and she let it live.

“Report.”

Cyndia led her to the nearest ward-stone. Chalk still dusted the base, frost glazing one side thin and bright; heel marks scored the dirt nearby, and a ranger's boot had half smeared one note without erasing the line beneath it.

“First disturbance at fourth bell. Not a breach signal. The tone dragged low and doubled back on itself. Two rangers and I found the prisoner here,” she said, pointing to the frost-webbed chalk. “Working some kind of reading spell—not at the ward itself, at what it was carrying.”

“She said that too.”

“Yes, Ranger-General. Repeatedly.”

“Did she try to break through when challenged.”

“No. She tried to finish the line she'd started, and then she tried to explain.”

“Worse.”

“Considerably.”

They crouched by the stone. Sylvanas laid two fingers against the carved base. Warm as ever, the hum steady now, with no crackle of a recent strike and no stink of forced magic driven in from outside. Whatever the human had done, she had done it with the ward rather than against it.

“You took everything?”

“Staff, satchel, notes, three chipped focus crystals, and a knife small enough to pass for decorative until you touched the edge. No seal case, no diplomatic packet, no escort tokens.”

“Name.”

“She wouldn't give one at first. Objected to every question that slowed her down.” A breath, and then: “Jaina Proudmoore.”

Sylvanas knew the name at once: Antonidas's apprentice, too much talent for her age and enough nerve to make it everyone's business.

“How did you confirm it.”

“Her notes. One crystal answered to her hand when one of the rangers tried it. I left the rest sealed.”

“Everything feels above your pay.”

“Not troll arrows.”

Fair.

Sylvanas looked again at the frost at the base of the stone. If it was Jaina Proudmoore, trust still had to be earned; the name only made her stranger than expected, and strange problems became personal if you left them alone.

“How did she take confinement.”

“Angry, with any fear buried too deep to show. She wanted back at the stones. Kept asking whether we'd felt the bad patch in the lines on the north sweep. When I told her she could answer questions after she sat down and stopped reaching for the staff, she said I was wasting time we did not have.”

“Charming.”

“Not especially.”

“Did you hit her.”

“Only enough.”

Also fair.

“Show me the satchel.”

A guard brought it over, weathered leather tied with ordinary cord, and Sylvanas opened it herself, peeling back the flap with her thumb.

Paper, more than she had expected: notes in a cramped hand blotted by cold or haste, charcoal rubbings of runes, sketches of the southern stones from several angles, each crossed with lines she did not need to understand to distrust. No ornamental nonsense, and no diplomatic letters tucked between the pages in case manners failed.

Fieldwork, then. Ugly, hurried, sincere-looking in the least convenient way possible.

One sheet had been folded and refolded until the edges softened, and Sylvanas opened it, smoothing the crease with her thumb.

A map of the southern approaches with notes at several ley crossings, more ink stains, and a blue-black thumbprint. A line in the margin: spread northward along existing channels—faster near population routes.

She folded it shut.

“Did she write all this here.”

“Some on the road. Some before she crossed. She had miles on her before we took her.”

Boots. Ink. Frost burns.

“Holding room?”

“Secured.”

“Any trouble since we put her in it.”

“Less once the staff was out of reach. More once she saw that wouldn't get it back.”

The post had gone quieter while they talked. Nothing visible had changed, but voices had dropped around the yard, and a horse stamped once in the lean-to and was answered by another.

“Who has seen her besides you.”

“Only the two door guards. I kept the rest back.”

Good. Fewer tongues.

“Lor'themar rides north already,” Sylvanas said. “The Convocation will hear some version of this before sunset, and I'd prefer it to be mine.”

“Understood.”

“And Cyndia.”

“Ma'am.”

“If this turns out to be a scholar's tantrum dressed in expensive frostwork, I will remember that you interrupted a good patrol day.”

Cyndia's mouth twitched once. “If it's real, you'll remember I interrupted it early.”

Dangerous relation.

Sylvanas turned toward the holding room.

Stone box at the edge of the yard, built for smugglers, spies, and the occasional fool who mistook elven borders for suggestions: narrow window, iron-bound door, blue ward-light caged along the threshold. Beside the wall, where nobody could miss what had been taken, stood a pale staff with its crystal head gone cloudy in the outpost light.

It landed flat.

Cyndia kept pace half a step back. “One more thing, Ranger-General.”

“Say it.”

“She was furious we wouldn't let her keep working.”

“That makes her reckless.”

“Yes, ma'am.”

“Or convinced.”

“Yes, ma'am.”

“I dislike both.”

At the door the guards straightened, blue light climbing Sylvanas's boots and sharpening the iron bands while inside one person breathed too tightly to pass for calm.

She gave a short nod, and a guard opened the door, stepping back from the threshold.

Small room: bench, bucket, one slit window losing cold afternoon light to the ward glow. The prisoner stood too fast for indifference and too controlled for panic, in travel robes with a dusty hem and boots grey with miles. Bare hands, one finger stained with ink.

The knuckles were worse. Frost had left them red and split in two places where magic had bitten back, and her braid was coming loose at the neck; hair the color of late wheat had more weather than care in it now. Younger than the dossier suggested. Not soft. Nothing soft in the set of the shoulders—shoulders set, courtesy still in place.

A field mage, then—travel-worn, split-knuckled, and miles past court polish.

The prisoner met Sylvanas straight on at once, tired at the edges, jaw set hard enough to show what courtesy covered.

Jaina Proudmoore, then: just a hard-used young mage in a Farstrider cell, hands chewed raw by her own work, all of her bent toward the ward-stones instead of this room.

Sylvanas stepped inside, door open behind her, Cyndia at her shoulder, staff out of reach, exits known.

Good.

Ink, split knuckles, frostbite, confiscated staff outside the door like a second accusation.

She let the silence pull tight until the ward-light was the loudest thing in the room.

Then she said, “You crossed my ward line alone. Start there.”
