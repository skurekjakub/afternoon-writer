# Chapter 14: The Revelation

The road south led back into country that ought to have been ordinary.

Late sun over harvested fields. Orchard rows on the low rises. Wagons groaning north under sacks and trunks. A dog barking behind a hedge. People still hurrying home for supper because they thought supper would matter.

Chalk still sat in the ridges of Jaina's fingertips. Lake water had not taken it. Canteen water had not taken it.

Cyndia held the left. Sylvanas rode ahead and to the right by half a step, close enough to hear every word.

Jaina kept the usable stack ready: grain carried the shell; the body learned the rest; death woke it; Kel'Thuzad expected to return; the chain ran north to the Lich King and farther still. Under that sat the room. The board. The cup gone cold. Kel'Thuzad warming as she kept pace with him. Sylvanas in the doorway, seeing the shape of it at once.

She could say the first stack aloud.

The second would hurt.

"Kel'Thuzad was serving more than Lordaeron," she said.

"Go on."

"He named the Lich King in Northrend. He named the Burning Legion above that. This isn't local. They're softening Lordaeron first, turning it into someone else's war."

"A conquest."

"Yes."

"For whose army?"

"The dead first. After that, whatever answers the hand above them."

"The grain," Sylvanas said.

"Only the shell." Jaina kept her eyes on the ruts. "The treated grain survives the road, the granary, the oven. People eat it and keep eating it. The first layer takes hold in the gut, then spreads. The body starts learning a pattern it should never know. When the sickness turns obvious, death stops ending the process."

"Releases what."

"Motion. Hunger. Direction. Enough to make a corpse stand when it should stay down."

"And the wards?"

"Built to stop force from outside. They fail against a thing carried in living bodies until it answers. A city can ward a gate. It cannot ward every loaf on every table."

Sylvanas's hand tightened once on the reins. "Hearthglen."

"Yes."

"Stratholme."

"Yes."

A farmer at a trough looked up as they passed, measuring their speed and Sylvanas's bow and how close the trouble was to his own field.

"He built it in layers," Jaina said. "That's why the first reads kept catching only the shell. The grain screamed. The deeper part stayed quiet until it had time to take hold."

"You sound as if he had you at a board."

"He had a room built for instruction."

"I saw the school under it."

Dust lifted behind a merchant cart ahead and shredded in the crosswind.

"I came in through Jandice," Jaina said. "Books. Ledgers. Archive space. She wanted an audience."

"I came in through stock."

"I gathered that."

"Did you. There were drawers of hands. Bodies still breathing on tables. Parts sorted into crates. He was teaching you over that floor."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the reins. "Jandice chose my route."

"Yes."

"She wanted me impressed before I was useful."

"And was she right?"

Jaina stared at the seam between two wheel tracks until it blurred. "Useful enough to keep listening."

"Good," Sylvanas said. "That's the true shape of it."

Jaina faced forward.

"I did not go there to be impressed."

"No. You went there to understand."

"Yes."

"And he built the room for exactly that kind of visitor."

There was no defense in that she could use without sounding small.

"How it works matters," she said.

"Then give me the part that matters next."

Jaina drew a slow breath. "He expected death to be temporary."

Sylvanas turned a fraction. "Speak plainly."

"He built a higher version for himself. Most dead rise crude. Herd-pulled. Enough for motion and command. He thought he'd solved the part that keeps memory and mind intact through death."

"Believed."

"He took an arrow through the throat and still sounded inconvenienced."

Cyndia checked right again out of old reflex.

"You should have told me that first," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

"But you did tell me."

They rode on. Sunset turned copper on distant glass.

Jaina had chosen the omission in the sanctum, on the island, and again when the road began. Each mile made it harder to keep the room boxed.

Warmth struck from Cyndia's side.

"Relay."

The crystal in her hand had gone amber at the core. She drew closer and held it out. Lor'themar's voice came through thin with distance.

"Ranger-General."

Sylvanas took the stone. Her fingers brushed Jaina's knuckles, quick as a spark.

"Speak."

"The Convocation has issued formal recall. Immediate return to Silvermoon for review and direct report. I am required to tell you the order stands above your current discretion."

Flat country gave Jaina nowhere to hide from the words.

"There is more pressure behind this than yesterday," Lor'themar went on. "They heard enough of Hearthglen to get afraid and enough of the rest to think distance will still save them."

No one spoke.

"Sylvanas," he said, dropping title and rank together. "If you have a reason I can spend, give me one."

She kept her eyes on the south road. "Tell them Hearthglen held for an hour and the dead kept standing back up inside the walls. Tell them the plague answers through the body and no ward line ends at the skin. Tell them I am not riding north while traffic is still moving south."

"That will buy me noise, not votes."

"Then buy noise."

The crystal hummed once.

"Understood," Lor'themar said.

The light died. Sylvanas handed the stone back to Cyndia and put her mare on.

No one suggested slowing.

Farm smoke flattened under the evening damp. Traffic thinned. A priest on a mule. Two men carrying a broken wheel. A woman walking north with a basket on one hip and a child on the other side.

The sending stone at Jaina's belt warmed next. Violet light leaked around the filigree. Antonidas never called twice in quick sequence unless the space between those calls had been full of people arguing.

She pulled it free. "Archmage."

"Jaina. I have your earlier report. I need the fuller version now."

Need. Good.

"Kel'Thuzad confirmed the plague is part of a larger invasion," she said. "The Lich King is real. Northrend sits above the outbreak. There is another power above him still, called the Burning Legion."

The pause after that was long enough for her to picture Antonidas standing somewhere with all of Dalaran behind him and none of it moving quickly enough.

"And how does it work?"

"The grain carries the first layer. Repeated eating teaches the body the rest. Death completes it. What rises in most cases is low and crude, but it answers. Enough for motion. Enough for command. Enough for a city to kill its own defenders while the walls are still standing."

"Can it be stopped?"

"Broadly? Not with what I have in hand." The road dipped; red sun burned in the ditch water. "There may be a few places to cut it. Storehouses. Feed points. Anywhere the shell can be broken before the body finishes the work. I don't know enough yet to promise more."

"Yet," he said, and beneath the weariness the old teacher in him was already there, braced for this answer from her.

"Hearthglen is already in crisis. Arthas is moving. I am moving toward him."

"Come home."

The mare stepped around a stone and for a second the world still tilted the way it had in the sanctum.

"I can't."

"Child."

"If I ride north now, I bring you a theory and leave the south to burn with it. If I keep riding, there may still be a chance to use part of it before Stratholme starts proving me right."

Wind hissed through the hedges. Cyndia's mare tossed foam from the bit. Sylvanas gave no sign of listening, but she was taking every word.

"You are not obliged to solve this alone," Antonidas said.

Kel'Thuzad's room flashed up so hard her mare hit the next rut badly: one desk, one cup, Kel'Thuzad looking at her the way Antonidas used to when she reached the next line before he had to say it.

"No," Jaina said, quieter. "I know that."

The stone stayed warm in her palm.

"Then live through the next answer," Antonidas said.

His voice roughened only once.

"I will do my best."

"That is never the comforting version with you."

Her chest loosened a fraction. There was the dry impatience again.

"No. It isn't."

"Send what you can when you can. I will move what I am able."

"Antonidas."

He waited.

She could not give him the room. Could not give him Kel'Thuzad's approval, or the one heartbeat in which the design had shown its elegance before judgment caught up.

"He wasn't mad," she said instead.

Another pause.

"No," Antonidas said softly. "I know."

The light died in her hand.

She slid the stone back into its pouch. The leather felt clammy. Her hand felt worse.

"You told him almost all of it," Sylvanas said.

"Almost."

"Not the room."

"No."

"Very well."

Dusk thickened around them. The road dipped between hedges, and hoofbeats broke from a side lane.

Cyndia moved first. Her mare slid left across the road, bow already up. Sylvanas checked hard and turned half-sideways. Jaina caught her own reins short as a rider burst out of the lane with mud to the knees and smoke in his cloak.

"Farstrider relay! South track!"

"Speak," Sylvanas said.

"Hearthglen held, then nearly failed again after dark. Uther is on the field. Arthas did not remain. He has taken the main body south-east at speed. Stratholme is his line now."

"How bad?"

"Bad enough that the wounded are being burned before their kin are done saying the words over them."

Jaina saw the northbound wagons again. The child asleep on bedding. The farmers at the trough. Ordinary work all across a kingdom feeding itself poison.

"Numbers."

The rider dragged himself back from what he had seen. "No count worth trusting. Too much churn. Too many civilians in the withdrawal. Priests say bodies keep answering after any lull longer than a few minutes. Captain Falric's line calls it a held city. The men coming off the wall call it a furnace with gates."

"How long ahead is the prince?" Sylvanas asked.

"Hours. Maybe less if he pushed through the last relay without halting."

Of course he had.

Stratholme opened in Jaina's mind all at once: granaries, bakehouses, market lanes, doors still unlocked because no one yet believed the grain had made those doors dangerous.

"Go back," Sylvanas told the rider. "Tell the next north station we are on the south road without stop. Tell them to keep the relays hot."

He wheeled his horse around so hard it skidded in the mud before finding purchase.

Cyndia lowered her bow only when the noise faded.

"Hours," Jaina said.

Sylvanas turned fully toward her for the first time since the road began. "If you have the ugly narrow possibility, now is when it becomes either a plan or dead weight."

Jaina pushed damp hair back from her temple. She laid the Stratholme map over the city she remembered: grain yards, bakeries, warehouse lanes, the service roads feeding all of it.

"The feed points. Granaries first, if the grain hasn't already been broken up and sent out. Bakehouses after that. Main holding yards. Anywhere the outer shell still sits in enough quantity to break before it disappears into bodies."

"Break how."

"Cold, if I can get enough of it into the stock at once. Fire if I can't, and if the choice comes down to grain or people. It won't cure what has already settled. It could keep part of the city from taking the next dose. It could blunt the turn if we beat the army in."

"Could."

"Yes."

"And if we're late."

Jaina looked south. Smoke smudged the horizon there, too thin to be evening haze.

"Then we learn that in time to do something with it."

Sylvanas held her gaze another beat.

"Good," she said. "That's a road answer."

Approval hit Jaina crookedly enough to feel like pain before it settled.

The ditches went dark first, then the fields. Horses breathed harder. Cyndia checked the empty right again and said nothing about it.

"How far to the next relay?" Jaina asked.

"One hour at this pace," Cyndia said.

"Half," Sylvanas said.

No one argued.

They drove the horses harder. Eastweald darkened around them. Orchard shadows thickened into bands of black. Windows kindled in farmhouses behind them, one here, three there. Somewhere off the road a bell rang for evening meal. Farther south another bell answered in alarm.

Jaina rode in the middle with Stratholme open behind her eyes. Sylvanas kept the half-step, close enough to listen and far enough to watch.

Kel'Thuzad had worked alone in a closed room, pleased with himself.

They ran south into the deepening dusk, smoke ahead of them, ordinary Lordaeron behind.
