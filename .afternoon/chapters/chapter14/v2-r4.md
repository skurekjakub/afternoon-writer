# Chapter 14: The Revelation

The road south led them back into country that ought to have been ordinary.

Late sun over the harvested fields. Orchard rows climbed the low rises in dark green bands. Wagons groaned north under sacks and household trunks. A dog barked beyond a hedge. Eastweald kept working. They had come out of a school built over butcher's rooms and back into mud, fences, and people still thinking supper would happen if they got home before dark.

Chalk still sat in the ridges of Jaina's fingertips. She had scrubbed at it in lake water, then from her canteen while they rode, and the pale line kept coming back.

Cyndia held the left as she always did. Once, while a farm cart rattled past, the ranger's head flicked right before coming forward again. Habit. Older than the last few hours and sharper for them.

Sylvanas rode ahead and to the right by half a horse, close enough to hear every word.

Jaina kept one stack ready for the road: grain as carrier, the body learning the deeper work, death as the point where it woke, Kel'Thuzad expecting to return, the chain reaching north to the Lich King and farther still. And under it, the room. The chalkboard. The cold cup on the side table. Kel'Thuzad's voice warming when he realized she could keep up. Sylvanas in the doorway, seeing the whole wrong shape of it at once.

She could say the first stack aloud.

Speaking the second stack would hurt.

Jaina shifted in the saddle. Leather creaked. Speak from the first stack, then. Keep the rest boxed until the miles and the questions stopped landing together.

"Kel'Thuzad was serving something larger than Lordaeron," she said.

Sylvanas did not turn. "Go on."

"He named the Lich King in Northrend. He named the Burning Legion above that. This isn't a local outbreak. Lordaeron is being softened, converted, and pushed into someone else's war before most of the kingdom knows it's in one."

Hooves beat their four-count into the dirt.

"A conquest," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

"For whose army?"

"The dead first. After that, whatever answers the hand above them."

Sylvanas went still in the way she did before a shot. Her horse kept the half-step. So did she.

Jaina kept going without letting the pause stretch. "The grain is only the shell. That much we knew. He filled in the rest."

"Then fill it in."

She shaved off the parts that sounded too much like the board downstairs. "The treated grain survives transport, storage, baking. People eat it and keep eating it. The first layer settles in the body, mostly through the gut, then wider. The body starts learning a pattern it should never learn. When the sickness turns obvious, death stops ending the process. Death releases it."

"Releases what."

"Motion. Hunger. Direction." Jaina kept her voice even. "Enough to turn a corpse into something that can rise when the body stops."

Cyndia's mare stamped through a wetter rut. Harness rings stayed almost silent; Thalassian tack was built for that. Her own gear came through more clearly: a pack strap at her shoulder, the sending stone knocking at her hip, the staff butt tapping her stirrup leather when the road jarred.

"And the wards?" Sylvanas asked.

"Built to blunt force, breach, direct pressure. They fail against something taught into living tissue and carried inside ordinary bodies until it answers. A city can ward a gate. It cannot ward every loaf on every table."

Sylvanas's left hand tightened once on the reins.

"Hearthglen," she said.

"Yes."

"Stratholme."

"Yes."

A crossroads opened ahead, marked by a weather-grey shrine post and a trough where two farmers watered their teams. One looked up as the riders passed. He watched their speed, their faces, and the bow on Sylvanas's back. Armed people riding hard in one direction always meant the trouble was ahead.

Jaina went on. "He built it in layers. That's why the early reads kept stalling. The grain screamed because the outer shell is bright to anyone looking for magic. The deeper part stays in the body until it has had time to settle."

"You sound as if he had you at a board for an afternoon lecture."

Jaina kept her eyes on the ruts ahead. "He had a room built for instruction."

"I saw the school built under it."

Road dust lifted behind a merchant cart ahead and broke apart in the crosswind. Jaina fixed on its wobbling rear wheel because if she turned now she would read the half-step as judgment instead of measure.

"I came in through Jandice," she said. "Books. Ledgers. Archive space. She wanted an audience."

"I came in through stock."

"I gathered that."

"Did you. There were drawers of hands. A room of bodies still breathing on tables. Another with parts sorted into crates. He was teaching you over that floor."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the reins. "Jandice chose my route."

"Yes."

"She wanted me impressed before I was useful."

"And was she right?"

Jaina stared at the muddy seam between two wheel tracks until it blurred. She could have lied. Instead she found herself back in the room, chalk under her thumb, Kel'Thuzad smiling because he had finally found someone who did not need the crude version.

"Useful enough to keep listening," she said.

Nothing moved except the horses.

Then Sylvanas said, "Good. That is at least the true shape of it."

Jaina faced forward.

"I did not go there to be impressed."

"No. You went there to understand."

"Yes."

"And he built the room for exactly that kind of visitor."

There was no defense in that she could use without sounding pathetic. The cart ahead turned onto a hedge-cut lane. The road opened into stubble and orchard shadow, too broad and too open after stone corridors and sealed doors.

"How it works matters," she said. "Even now."

"Then tell me the part that matters next."

Jaina drew a slow breath. "He expected death to be temporary."

This time Sylvanas turned a fraction. "Speak plainly."

"He built a higher version for himself. Village dead rise crude. Herd pull. Enough for motion and command. He believed he had solved the layer that keeps memory and mind intact through death."

"Believed."

"He was very calm about being shot through the throat."

Cyndia's head shifted left toward a stand of ash, then right again out of old reflex.

Sylvanas's bow-hand opened and closed on the reins. "You should have told me that first."

"Yes."

Sylvanas faced forward again. "But you did tell me."

They rode on between the fields. Sunset had started turning toward copper. Window glass on a distant farmhouse flashed once like a signal.

She had chosen the omission hours ago in the sanctum and again on the island and again when the road began. Each time keeping it back got harder. She stayed with the useful part; there was no time for confession on the ride south.

A warmth struck from Cyndia's side.

"Relay," the ranger said.

The crystal in her hand had gone amber at the core. She drew her mare closer and held it across. Lor'themar's voice came through clipped thin by distance.

"Ranger-General."

Sylvanas reached for the stone. Her fingers brushed Jaina's knuckles in passing, quick as a spark, then were gone.

"Speak."

"The Convocation has issued formal recall. Immediate return to Silvermoon for review and direct report. I am required to tell you the order stands above your current discretion."

Jaina wished for hedges, trees, anything but flat country and air.

Lor'themar went on, weariness rough in his voice. "There is more pressure behind this than there was yesterday. They heard enough of Hearthglen to get afraid and the wrong kind of enough from the rest to think distance will still save them."

Sylvanas said nothing.

No one answered.

"Sylvanas," he said, dropping every title. "If you have a reason I can spend, give me one."

She kept her eyes on the south road. "Tell them Hearthglen held for an hour and the dead kept standing back up inside the walls. Tell them the plague answers through the body and no ward line ends at the skin. Tell them I am not riding north while traffic is still moving south."

"That will buy me noise, not votes."

"Then buy noise."

The crystal hummed once under her grip.

"Understood," Lor'themar said.

The light went out.

Sylvanas handed the stone back to Cyndia and touched her heels to her horse. The mare lengthened at once.

No one suggested slowing.

They rode on.

Farm smoke flattened under the evening damp. Wagons thinned away until the three of them had the road mostly to themselves, passing only stragglers now: a priest on a mule with soot on his hem, two men carrying a broken wheel, a woman walking north with a basket on her hip and a child's hand in the other.

The sending stone at Jaina's belt warmed next.

The pulse was unmistakable before she touched it. Violet light leaked around the metal filigree. Her stomach tightened with recognition. Antonidas never called twice in quick sequence unless the space between those calls had been full of people arguing over maps.

She pulled the stone free. "Archmage."

"Jaina." His voice came thin with range and strain. "I have your earlier report. I need the fuller version now."

Need. Good.

Jaina steadied her mare with her knees and spoke into the stone while the horses kept moving. "Kel'Thuzad confirmed the plague is part of a larger invasion. The Lich King is real. Northrend is the seat above the outbreak. There is another power above him still, called the Burning Legion."

Antonidas did not answer at once.

A short pause meant calculation. A longer one meant fear dressed as deliberation. This one lasted just long enough for her to picture him somewhere far from her with the whole weight of Dalaran at his back and none of it moving quickly enough.

"And how does it work?" he asked.

"Finished." Her thumb ran once over the engraved edge. "The grain carries the first layer. Repeated eating teaches the body the rest. Death completes it. What rises from common cases is low and crude, but it answers. Enough for motion. Enough for command. Enough for a city to kill its own defenders while the walls are still standing."

"Can it be stopped?"

There it was, stripped of manners at last.

A red-gold line of sun burned in the ditch. "Broadly? Not with what I have in hand."

Antonidas made no sound.

"There may be narrower interventions," she said. "Storage points. Feed points. Somewhere the shell can be broken before the body finishes the work. I don't know enough yet to promise more than that."

"Yet," he repeated.

The word came out worn, and beneath it her old teacher was already there, braced for this answer from her.

"Hearthglen is already in crisis," she said. "Arthas is moving. I am moving toward him."

"Come home."

The mare stepped around a stone and for one strange second she still felt the world tilt. Jaina kept facing forward because if she shut her eyes she would see Antonidas at a library table in Dalaran, sleeves inked at the cuff, pushing a dangerous text across to her because he wanted the pleasure of watching her understand it.

"I can't."

"Child."

"If I ride north now," she said, "I bring you a theory and leave the south to burn with it. If I keep riding, there may still be a chance to use part of it before Stratholme becomes another proof."

Wind hissed through the orchard hedges. Cyndia's mare tossed foam from the bit and settled. Sylvanas gave no sign of listening, but she was taking every word.

"You are not obliged to solve this alone," Antonidas said.

Kel'Thuzad's room flashed up in her mind so hard her mare hit the next rut badly. One desk. One cup. One scholar's pleasure in being fully understood.

"No," Jaina said more quietly. "I know that."

The stone stayed warm against her palm. At last Antonidas said, "Then live through the next answer."

His voice roughened at the edges, just once.

"I will do my best."

"That is never the comforting version with you."

Her chest loosened a fraction. Antonidas sounded dry and impatient again.

"No," she said, and managed a thin smile. "It isn't."

"Send what you can when you can. I will move what I am able."

"Antonidas."

He waited.

She could not give him the room. Could not give him Kel'Thuzad's voice turned warm with approval. Could not give him the one heartbeat in which the design had shown its elegance before judgment caught up.

"He wasn't mad," she said instead.

Antonidas answered with a longer pause than before.

"No," Antonidas said at last, very softly. "I know."

The light died in her hand.

Jaina slid the stone back into its pouch. The leather was clammy. Her hand worse.

"You told him almost all of it," Sylvanas said.

Jaina turned then, because she could not ignore the accusation. Sylvanas still faced forward, profile clean against the copper sky, jaw set in the hard line that meant control rather than anger.

"Almost."

"The room again."

"The room again."

"Very well."

They rode another quarter hour in gathering dusk before the next interruption found them.

This one came as a rider cutting out of a side lane at a reckless angle, horse blowing hard, cloak tied wrong for speed. Cyndia moved first. Her mare slid left across the road, bow already in hand. Sylvanas's horse checked and turned half-sideways. Jaina's own mare bunched under her as she caught the reins short.

"Farstrider relay!" the newcomer shouted. "South track!"

Cyndia kept the bow up until he was within ten paces and panting hard enough to lose any pretended disguise. Mud striped his boots to the knee. Smoke had gotten into his cloak; Jaina caught old ash and hot pitch even across the road.

"Speak," Sylvanas said.

"Hearthglen held, then nearly failed again after dark. Uther is on the field. Arthas did not remain. He has taken the main body south-east at speed. Stratholme is his line now."

Jaina hated the look on the man's face.

"How bad?" Sylvanas asked.

"Bad enough that the wounded are being burned before their kin are done saying the words over them."

All at once she saw the dead in every mile behind them. The northbound wagons. The child asleep atop bedding. The farmers at the trough. Ordinary Lordaeron work went on while poisoned grain moved through the kingdom.

"Numbers," Jaina said.

The rider blinked, dragged himself back from whatever he had seen. "No count worth trusting. Too much churn. Too many civilians mixed into the withdrawal. The priests say bodies keep answering after any lull longer than a few minutes. Captain Falric's line calls it a held city. The men coming off the wall call it a furnace with gates."

Sylvanas leaned forward over her saddle horn. "How long ahead is the prince?"

"Hours. Maybe less if he pushed through the last relay station without a halt."

Of course he had.

Jaina saw it whole at once: Hearthglen reduced to relay smoke and secondhand horror, Arthas already out beyond it, Stratholme still living its evening routine while contaminated flour sat in bins, on shelves, in kneaded dough, in cooling loaves.

"Go back," Sylvanas told the rider. "Tell the next north station we are on the south road without stop. Tell them to keep the relays hot."

He snapped a nod and wheeled his horse around so sharply the animal skidded in the mud before finding purchase.

Cyndia lowered her bow only when his noise faded around the bend.

"Hours," Jaina said.

Sylvanas turned to her then, fully for the first time since the road began. "If you have the ugly narrow possibility, now is when it becomes either a plan or dead weight."

Jaina pushed damp hair back from her temple. Her thoughts were already ordering themselves against the Stratholme map she carried from old lessons and newer reports: markets, bakers, warehouse lanes, the service roads that fed the city its grain.

"The feed points," she said. "Granaries first, if the stores aren't fully dispersed already. Bakehouses after that. Main holding yards. Anything where the outer shell still sits in enough quantity to break before it disappears into bodies."

"Break how."

"Cold, if I can get enough of it into the stock at once. Fire if I can't and if the choice becomes grain or people." The words came faster now because the problem had finally taken a shape she could use on the road. "It won't cure what has already settled. It could keep some of the city from taking the next dose. It could blunt the worst of the turn if we beat the army in."

"Could," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

"And if we're late."

Jaina faced south. Smoke smudged the horizon there, too thin to be evening haze.

"Then we learn that in time to do something with it."

Sylvanas stayed turned toward her for another second. Jaina waited for the cut. Instead Sylvanas said, "Good. That sounds like a road answer."

Something in Jaina's chest went unsteady for one beat and righted itself.

The ditches went dark first, then the fields. Horses breathed harder now, white beginning to show at the nostrils. Cyndia checked the empty right again before looking south.

No one mentioned it.

Jaina gathered the reins and let her mare lengthen. Her back hurt from hours of stillness in the sanctum. Ahead and to the right, Sylvanas moved with the same hard economy she had brought to the border, to Millhaven, to every room Jaina had misread before understanding what it cost to be corrected by another mind in motion.

Kel'Thuzad had worked alone in a closed room, pleased with himself.

She needed something else from it.

"How far to the next relay?" she asked.

"One hour at this pace," Cyndia said.

"Half," Sylvanas said.

No one argued.

They drove the horses harder.

Eastweald darkened around them. Orchard shadows thickened into bands of near-black. Windows kindled in the farmhouses, one here, three there, square yellow lives lit against the coming night. Somewhere off the road a bell rang for evening meal. Somewhere farther south another bell answered in alarm.

Jaina rode in the middle with the map of Stratholme opening behind her eyes. Grain yards. Bakeries. Market lanes. Doors still unlocked because no one yet believed they were the hinges of a massacre.

Sylvanas still kept the half-step, close enough to listen and far enough to watch.

Three horses ran south into the deepening dusk, smoke ahead of them, ordinary Lordaeron behind.
