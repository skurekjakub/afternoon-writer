# Chapter 14: The Revelation

The road south took them back into the sort of country that ought to have been ordinary.

Late sun lay warm on the harvested fields. Orchard rows climbed the low rises in dark green bands, apples still hanging where the pickers had not quite finished. Wagons groaned north under sacks and household trunks. A dog barked from somewhere beyond a hedge. Eastweald kept working. That was the offense of it. Caer Darrow had spat them out of a school built over butcher's rooms and set them back among mud, fences, and people still thinking supper would happen if they got home before dark.

Damp reins rubbed against Jaina's palms. Chalk still lived in the ridges of her fingertips. She had scrubbed at it in the lake water and then again from her canteen while they rode, and a pale line kept surfacing around the nails anyway.

Cyndia held the left as she always did. Once, while a farm cart rattled past on the other side of the road, the ranger's head turned right before she brought it forward again. The motion happened fast. Habit, older than the last few hours and sharper for them.

Sylvanas rode ahead and to the right by half a horse.

Close enough to hear every word. Far enough that the space showed.

Jaina kept two stacks in her head because one would carry and the other would not. The first stack held the pieces that mattered on the road: grain as carrier, the body taking the deeper work into itself, death as the threshold that woke what had been prepared, Kel'Thuzad's expectation of returning, the chain reaching north to the Lich King and farther still. The second stack held the room. The chalkboard. The cup gone cold on the side table. The ease in Kel'Thuzad's voice when he heard a mind keeping pace with him. Sylvanas framed in the doorway with the whole wrong shape of it laid bare in a single glance.

The first stack could be spoken.

The second had teeth in it.

Jaina shifted in the saddle. Leather creaked under her. Her mare tossed her head once against the bit, picked her footing through a churned patch of road, and settled again into the southbound rhythm. Speak from the first stack, then. Keep the rest boxed until the road stopped asking for miles and answers at the same time.

"Kel'Thuzad was serving something larger than Lordaeron," she said.

Sylvanas did not turn. "Go on."

"He named the Lich King in Northrend. He named the Burning Legion above that." Jaina kept her eyes on the wheel ruts ahead. "This isn't an isolated outbreak. It is preparation. Lordaeron is being softened, converted, and moved into someone else's war before most of the kingdom understands it's in one."

No answer came at once. Hooves beat their four-count into the packed dirt. A hay wagon rolled by northbound, driver hunched over the reins, two children asleep on top of a blanket pile behind him. The younger one's hand hung limp over the side, fingers brown with road dust.

"A conquest," Sylvanas said at last.

"Yes."

"For whose army?"

"The dead first. After that, whatever answers the hand above them."

Sylvanas absorbed that in the same still way she drew a bow, by taking the whole line cleanly and wasting nothing on visible reaction. Her horse kept the half-step. So did she.

Jaina pushed on before the silence could harden. "The grain is only the shell. That much we had before. He filled in the rest."

"Then fill it in."

She had already started arranging the words into the plainer road shape, shaving off the parts that sounded too much like the board downstairs. "The treated grain survives transport, storage, baking. People eat it and keep eating it. The first layer settles in the body, mostly through the gut, then wider. The body starts learning a pattern it should never learn. When the sickness turns obvious, death stops ending the process. Death releases it."

"Releases what."

"The answer." Jaina forced herself to keep going instead of correcting the word into something safer and thinner. "Motion. Appetite. Direction. Enough to turn a corpse into something that can rise when the body stops."

Cyndia's mare stamped once through a wetter rut. Harness rings stayed almost silent; Thalassian tack was built for that, leather fitted close, buckles kept from chattering against themselves. Jaina heard her own gear far more clearly: a pack strap working at her shoulder, the faint knock of the sending stone at her hip, the staff butt touching her stirrup leather whenever the road jarred.

"And the wards?" Sylvanas asked.

"Built to blunt force, breach, direct pressure. They fail against something taught into living tissue and carried inside ordinary bodies until it answers. A city can ward a gate. It cannot ward every loaf on every table."

That landed. Jaina knew it from the way Sylvanas's left hand tightened once on the reins and loosened again.

"Hearthglen," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

"Stratholme."

"Yes."

The word sat between them like a stone too large to kick aside.

A crossroads opened ahead, marked by a shrine post gone weather-grey and a trough where two farmers were watering their teams. One looked up as the three riders passed. His gaze stuck on their speed, their faces, perhaps the bow on Sylvanas's back, perhaps the fact that nobody in the middle of ordinary work ever liked seeing armed people ride hard in one direction. It meant the trouble was there, not elsewhere.

Jaina went on because stopping now would only throw her back toward the other stack. "He built it in layers. That is why the early reads kept stalling. The grain screamed because the outer shell is bright to anyone looking for magic. The deeper part stays in the body until it has had time to settle."

"You sound as if he had you at a board for an afternoon lecture."

The words were level. That made them land harder.

Jaina's mare took another uneven patch. Her knee knocked the saddle roll. "He had a room built for instruction," she said, and hated how spare that sounded the instant it left her mouth.

"I saw the school built under it."

There it was.

Road dust lifted in a thin brown sheet behind a merchant cart ahead of them, then broke apart in the crosswind. Jaina kept her eyes fixed on the cart's wobbling rear wheel because Sylvanas was beside her and because if she turned now she would see the half-step as a judgment instead of a measure.

"I came in through Jandice," she said. "Books. ledgers. Archive space. She wanted an audience."

"I came in through stock."

Short, clean, unbearable.

Jaina swallowed against a dry mouth. "I gathered that."

"Did you." Sylvanas's voice stayed mild. "There were drawers of hands. A room of bodies still breathing on tables. Another with parts sorted into crates. He was teaching you a theorem over that floor."

Jaina's fingers bit into wet leather. She had one use of silence left here, perhaps two, and could spend neither if she meant to keep the road from breaking under them. "Jandice curated my route."

"Yes."

"She wanted me impressed before I was useful."

"And was she right?"

The question struck lower than anger would have. Jaina stared at the muddy seam between two wheel tracks until it blurred. She could have lied. She could have said no with enough force to make the rest of the answer seem childish for wanting. Instead she found herself back in the room, chalk under her thumb, Kel'Thuzad watching the line of her thought and smiling because he had finally found someone who did not need the crude version.

"Useful enough to keep listening," she said.

Nothing moved for three heartbeats except the horses.

Then Sylvanas said, "Good. That is at least the true shape of it."

Jaina almost looked at her then. Did not. Truth from Sylvanas was a blade; you took the cut cleanly or you made it worse by flinching into it.

"I did not go there to be impressed."

"No. You went there to understand."

"Yes."

"And he built the room for exactly that kind of visitor."

Jaina let the words strike and settle. There was no defense in them she could use without sounding like a woman trying to negotiate with the evidence. Ahead, the cart finally turned off toward a lane cut between hedges. The road opened. Eastweald rolled south in bands of stubble and orchard shadow, all of it too broad and too open after stone corridors and sealed doors.

"The mechanism matters," she said. "Even now."

"Then tell me the part that matters next."

Jaina drew a slow breath through her nose and set the second stack further back, somewhere she would have to work to reach it. "He expected death to be temporary."

This time Sylvanas did turn a fraction, not enough for Jaina to catch more than the pale line of cheek and the edge of one ear through loose fair hair. "Speak plainly."

"He built a higher version for himself." Jaina's throat scraped raw on the words. "Village dead rise crude. Herd pull. Enough for motion and command. He believed he had solved the preserving layer that keeps memory and mind intact through death."

"Believed."

"He was very calm about being shot through the throat."

Cyndia's head shifted left toward a stand of ash trees, then right again out of old reflex before settling forward. Her mare's ears flicked back at Jaina's voice and went up once more.

Sylvanas's bow-hand opened and closed against the reins. "You should have told me that first."

"Yes."

The answer came too fast to sound graceful.

Sylvanas faced forward again. "But you did tell me."

No absolution in it. No open condemnation either. Only the line as it stood.

They rode on between the fields. Sunset had started the long turn toward copper. Window glass on a distant farmhouse caught it and flashed once like a signal.

Jaina understood then that she had chosen the omission hours ago in the sanctum and again on the island and again when the road began. Each time it grew heavier and cleaner at once. The thing she could say usefully kept moving farther away from the thing that had actually happened. She stayed with the useful one because the south road needed that more than it needed her confession.

A warmth struck from Cyndia's side.

"Relay," the ranger said.

The crystal in her hand had gone amber at the core. She drew her mare closer without crowding Jaina and held it across. Light crawled under the polished facets in slow bands.

Lor'themar's voice came through clipped thin by distance. "Ranger-General."

Sylvanas reached for the stone. Her fingers brushed Jaina's knuckles in passing, quick as a thrown spark, and were gone before the contact had finished becoming real.

"Speak."

"The Convocation has issued formal recall. Immediate return to Silvermoon for review and direct report. I am required to tell you the order stands above your current discretion."

Jaina looked straight ahead and wished the road would choose, for once, to be less flat. Anything with cover would have helped. Open country gave silence too much room.

Lor'themar went on before Sylvanas could answer or refuse to. Weariness roughened the edges of his voice. "There is more pressure behind this than there was yesterday. They heard enough of Hearthglen to get afraid and the wrong kind of enough from the rest to think distance will still save them."

Sylvanas said nothing.

Only the hoofbeats answered.

Jaina could imagine the other side of the line: Lor'themar somewhere under a relay awning or on a road verge, one hand on the crystal, shoulders set the way exhausted men set them when there is nobody left to be tired for.

"Sylvanas," he said, dropping every title. "If you have a reason I can spend, give me one."

She kept her eyes on the south road. "Tell them Hearthglen held for an hour and the dead kept standing back up inside the walls. Tell them the plague answers through the body and no ward line ends at the skin. Tell them I am not riding north while the road south is still moving."

"That will buy me noise, not votes."

"Then buy noise."

The crystal hummed once under her grip.

"Understood," Lor'themar said.

He did not waste them with farewell. The light went out.

Sylvanas handed the stone back to Cyndia and touched her heels to her horse. The mare lengthened at once.

No one suggested slowing.

The sun dropped lower over the fields.

For a while the road gave them nothing but distance.

Farm smoke flattened under the evening damp. One by one the wagons thinned away until the three of them had the south road mostly to themselves, passing only the stragglers now: a priest on a mule with soot on his hem, two men carrying a broken wheel between them, a woman walking north with a basket on her hip and a child's hand clenched in the other. Ordinary people going the wrong way from where the chapter wanted them to go. Each northbound face added one more quiet argument for haste.

The sending stone at Jaina's belt warmed next.

She knew the pulse before she touched it. Violet light leaked around the metal filigree. Her stomach tightened in the same instant, not from surprise, from recognition. Antonidas never called twice in quick sequence unless the space between those calls had been full of people arguing over maps.

She pulled the stone free. "Archmage."

"Jaina." His voice came thin with range and strain, the familiar careful cadence pared down so hard the care showed as absence rather than style. "I have your earlier report. I need the fuller version now."

Need. Not requested. Not advised. Good. That at least meant he had stopped pretending the Council was still in the stage where language could afford upholstery.

Jaina kept her mare steady with her knees and spoke into the stone while the road moved under them. "Kel'Thuzad confirmed the plague is part of a larger invasion frame. The Lich King is real. Northrend is the seat above the outbreak. There is another power above him still, called the Burning Legion."

Silence came back over the crystal.

Antonidas's silences had always done more work than other men's speeches. Jaina had learned them at fourteen and hated them by fifteen. A short one meant calculation. A longer one meant fear dressed as deliberation. This one went on just long enough to show him standing somewhere far from her with the whole weight of Dalaran pressing at his back and no part of it moving quickly enough.

"And the mechanism?" he asked.

"Finished." Her thumb ran once over the engraved edge. "The grain carries the first layer. Repeated eating teaches the body the rest. Death completes it. What rises from common cases is low and crude, but it answers. Enough for motion. Enough for command. Enough for a city to kill its own defenders while the walls are still standing."

"Can it be stopped?"

There it was. The question beneath all the others, stripped of manners because the road had burned those off for both of them.

Jaina looked ahead at the red-gold line of the westering sun caught in a ditch. "Broadly? Not with what I have in hand."

Antonidas made no sound.

"There may be narrower interventions," she said. "Treatment points. Storage points. Somewhere the shell can be broken before the body finishes the work. I don't know enough yet to promise more than that."

"Yet," he repeated.

She heard the weariness in the single word. Heard the age. Heard, under both, the teacher who knew exactly what sort of answer that was from her and had probably known it would arrive before she finished saying it.

"Hearthglen is already in crisis," she said. "Arthas is moving. I am moving toward him."

"Come home."

No title. No council. No distance.

The mare gave one small sideways step around a stone, and for a strange second the whole road tilted anyway. Jaina kept her gaze fixed ahead because if she shut her eyes she would see Antonidas not as the Grand Magus but at a library table in Dalaran, sleeves inked at the cuff, pushing a text across to her with that patient look which meant he had already decided to let her read the dangerous thing and only wanted the pleasure of watching her understand why.

"I can't."

"Child."

The word might have softened another woman. It only made the refusal hurt in a different place.

"If I ride north now," she said, "I bring you a theory and leave the south to burn with it. If I keep riding, there may still be a chance to use part of it before Stratholme becomes another proof."

Wind hissed through the orchard hedges. Cyndia's mare tossed foam from the bit and settled. Sylvanas gave no sign of listening, but Jaina knew she was taking every word. The half-step had not altered since the relay from Lor'themar.

"You are not obliged to solve this alone," Antonidas said.

Kel'Thuzad's room flashed up in her mind with such force she almost missed a rut. One desk. One cup. One scholar's pleasure in being fully understood.

"No," Jaina said, more quietly. "I know that."

That, too, was true now, and too late to make anything easier.

The stone stayed warm against her palm. At last Antonidas said, "Then live through the next answer."

His voice roughened at the edges, just once. The crack sealed over almost before it had formed.

"I will do my best."

"That is never the comforting version with you."

Something tight in her chest shifted a fraction. Not release. Nothing so kind. Only the old familiar angle of his dry impatience surfacing long enough to remind her he was still himself under all the pressure.

"No," she said, and almost managed the shape of a smile without earning it. "It isn't."

"Send what you can when you can. I will move what I am able."

He would. She believed that. The horror lived in how often what he was able to move arrived after the moment that needed moving had gone.

"Antonidas."

He waited.

She could not give him the room. Could not give him Kel'Thuzad's voice turned warm with academic approval. Could not give him the one intolerable heartbeat in which the theorem had shown its elegance before her judgment caught up. Some truths had no road shape yet. Some perhaps never would.

"He wasn't mad," she said instead.

The silence that answered her was longer than the others.

"No," Antonidas said at last, very softly. "I know."

The light died in her hand.

Jaina slid the stone back into its pouch. The leather was clammy. Her hand worse.

"You told him almost all of it," Sylvanas said.

Jaina turned then, because the words demanded it. Sylvanas still faced forward. Her profile was cut clean against the copper sky, fair hair pulled loose in a few strands by the wind, jaw set in the hard line that meant control rather than anger.

"Almost."

"The room again."

"The room again."

Sylvanas nodded once, the small economical dip she gave when a range had been confirmed. "Very well."

That was all.

They rode another quarter hour in gathering dusk before the next interruption found them.

This one came not as warmth from a carried stone but as a rider cutting out of a side lane at a reckless angle, horse blowing hard, cloak tied up wrong for speed. Cyndia moved first. Her mare slid left across the road, bow already in her hand. Sylvanas's horse checked and turned half-sideways. Jaina's own mare bunched under her as she caught the reins short and held.

"Farstrider relay!" the newcomer shouted before an arrow could make the distinction irrelevant. "South track!"

Cyndia kept the bow up until the man was within ten paces and panting hard enough to lose any pretended disguise. Mud striped his boots to the knee. Smoke had gotten into his cloak; Jaina caught it even across the road, old ash and hot pitch.

"Speak," Sylvanas said.

The rider swallowed once. "Hearthglen held, then nearly failed again after dark. Uther is on the field. Arthas did not remain. He has taken the main body south-east at speed. Stratholme is his line now."

There was more in the man's face than the message and Jaina hated him a little for it because she already knew the shape before he said it.

"How bad?" Sylvanas asked.

"Bad enough that the wounded are being burned before their kin are done saying the words over them."

All at once the road filled with the dead it had not shown them yet. The northbound wagons. The child asleep atop bedding. The farmers at the trough. Ordinary Lordaeron work spread over a kingdom already baking its own knife.

"Numbers," Jaina said.

The rider blinked at her, dragged his thoughts back from whatever he had seen. "No count worth trusting. Too much churn. Too many civilians mixed into the withdrawal. The priests say bodies keep answering after any lull longer than a few minutes. Captain Falric's line calls it a held city. The men coming off the wall call it a furnace with gates."

Useful enough. Useless enough.

Sylvanas leaned forward over her saddle horn. "How long ahead is the prince?"

"Hours. Maybe less if he pushed through the last relay station without a halt."

Of course he had.

Jaina saw it whole at once: Hearthglen as relay smoke and secondhand horror, Arthas already out beyond it, Stratholme still living in its own evening routines while contaminated flour sat in bins, on shelves, in kneaded dough, in cooling loaves. The window did not narrow. It collapsed.

"Go back," Sylvanas told the rider. "Tell the next north station we are on the south road without stop. Tell them to keep the relays hot."

He snapped a nod and wheeled his horse around so sharply the animal skidded in the mud before finding purchase.

Cyndia lowered her bow only when the rider was half lost behind the hedge line and the lane had swallowed his noise.

"Hours," Jaina said.

Sylvanas looked across at her then, fully for the first time since the road began. The expression gave nothing away except attention. "If you have the ugly narrow possibility, now is the moment when it becomes either a plan or dead weight."

Jaina pushed damp hair back from her temple with the back of one wrist. Her thoughts had already begun ordering themselves against the map of Stratholme she carried from old lessons and newer reports: markets, bakers, warehouse lanes, the service roads that fed the city bulk grain, the places where ordinary work became mass delivery.

"The feed points," she said. "Granaries first, if the stores aren't fully dispersed already. Bakehouses after that. Main holding yards. Anything where the outer shell still sits in enough quantity to break before it disappears into bodies."

"Break how."

"Cold, if I can get enough of it into the stock at once. Fire if I can't and if the choice becomes grain or people." The words came faster now because the shape of the problem was at last one the road could use. "It won't cure what has already settled. It could keep some of the city from taking the next dose. It could blunt the worst of the turn if we beat the army in."

"Could," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

"And if we're late."

Jaina looked south. Smoke smudged the horizon there, a grey-brown mark so thin it might have been evening haze if she had not known better.

"Then we learn that too in time to do something with it."

Sylvanas held her gaze another second. Jaina waited for the cut. The reminder of Kel'Thuzad's room. The half-step made verbal. Instead Sylvanas said only, "Good. That sounds like a road answer."

Something in Jaina's chest went unsteady for one beat and righted itself.

Night began sliding up from the ditches. The warmth drained out of the fields. Horses breathed harder now, white beginning to show at the nostrils. Cyndia shifted in the saddle and checked the empty right again before looking south.

No one mentioned it.

Jaina gathered the reins and let her mare lengthen. Leather tightened under her hands. Muscles complained along her back where the sanctum's stillness had sat too long. Ahead and to the right, Sylvanas moved with the same hard economy she had brought to the border, to Millhaven, to every room Jaina had misread before understanding what it cost to be corrected by another mind in motion.

Being right alone had a shape after all. A desk. A closed room. A man pleased to find himself mirrored.

The shape of it demanded something else.

"How far to the next relay?" she asked.

"One hour at this pace," Cyndia said.

"Half," Sylvanas said.

None of them argued with the order buried in it.

They drove the horses harder.

Eastweald darkened around them by degrees. Orchard shadows thickened into bands of near-black. Windows kindled in the farmhouses, one here, three there, square yellow lives lit against the coming night. Somewhere off the road a bell rang for evening meal. Somewhere farther south another bell answered in alarm, thin enough that it might have been wind playing tricks if the smoke on the horizon had not kept its place.

Jaina rode in the middle with the map of Stratholme opening and reopening behind her eyes. Grain yards. Bakeries. Market lanes. Doors that would still be unlocked because no one yet believed they were the hinges of a massacre. She could spend what she had learned. Perhaps only in pieces. Perhaps too late for half the city. Perhaps late enough that the work would look like triage rather than victory. It was still something.

Sylvanas kept the half-step.

The spacing no longer read as oversight. It read as what it was: trust still working under damage, functional enough to carry a kingdom's worth of bad news, sharp enough to keep a hand on every missing piece.

That would have to be enough for this road.

For now, it was.

Three horses ran south into the deepening dusk, smoke ahead of them, ordinary Lordaeron behind.
