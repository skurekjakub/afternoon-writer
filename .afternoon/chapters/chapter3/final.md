## Chapter 3: The Dead Village

From the rise it had been a knot of dark roofs and dead air. Down among the buildings, distance stopped helping. A cart stood half across the lane with one wheel sunk in mud. A door on the nearest house tapped its latch when the wind found it. The stream under the little bridge still ran. Nothing else did.

Sylvanas raised one hand. The line stopped.

The mage's mare went stiff again. Listening this time, not shying.

"Cyndia. Left side to the bridge and back. Lyriel with her. Neral, rear lane. Keep sight on the north road. You"—she glanced at the fourth ranger—"stay with the horses unless I say otherwise. If anything moves, call it before you loose."

The ranger saluted.

The mage sat with the staff across her thighs, too still for comfort. Blue lay under the surface like banked fire.

"And me?" the mage asked.

"With me." Sylvanas swung down. "You get your turn when I say."

The mage's mouth tightened. She dismounted anyway.

No smoke from the chimneys. No goat in a pen. No dog warning strangers off. Only the little sounds that should have had company: hooks knocking in the breeze, loose boards shifting, water under the bridge. Millhaven had all the shapes of a living place and none of its life.

She crossed to the nearest house and pushed the door wider with two fingers on the wood, not the iron ring. Cold met her from inside: house-cold, a stove dead for hours.

One room below, loft above. Table set for two. A crust of bread hard enough to tap. A cup on its side. The stain had soaked into the boards. Dust settling on the sill. Not much. Enough to tell her the meal had been broken off, not cleared away.

Leather creaked behind her. The mage had stopped at the threshold.

"Don't touch anything yet."

"I wasn't going to."

The mage had gone pale in the low light, not from nerves. From whatever she was reading in the place itself.

Sylvanas tipped her head. The mage stepped in, slower now, staff angled ahead. The crystal brightened. Her free hand moved over the table, the air above the cup, the dark stove mouth.

"Well?"

"Everywhere," the mage said quietly. "Not strongest here. Just everywhere."

Sylvanas stepped back outside.

The rest of the lane gave her more of the same. A wash line left strung, shirts stiff with old rain and newer dust. A gate hanging open. A child's hoop near the ditch. In one house, stew dried to the pot. In another, a shawl half dragged from a bed and tracked with mud. No visible blood. No bodies. The absence sharpened the more houses she crossed.

Cyndia came out of a stable, bow in hand, mouth flat.

"Two cattle in the far pen, dead where they fell. No cuts. No one moved them."

"How long?"

"A day. Two at most."

Sylvanas nodded and kept moving.

A grey cat streaked across the lane and vanished under a cart, ribs showing, tail low.

By the stream the little mill wheel sat still, one spoke broken, weeds snagged in the lower rim. Lyriel crouched at the bank, fingers near the current, then stood.

"No bodies in the stream," she said. "No washdown either. If they died here, the water didn't take them."

"If they died here," Sylvanas said.

Lyriel's eyes flicked toward the houses. She did not answer.

The granary stood apart from the rest of the village, broad-shouldered and shut. The mage reached it first, then stopped with her hand on the latch and looked back instead of opening it.

Sylvanas came up beside her. Grain dust lay thick in the threshold grooves. No rats.

"Open it."

The latch lifted. Stale heat pressed out. Dry grain, mouse piss, old burlap—and under them, the wrong sweetness the mage had named at the pass.

The mage went to one knee in the dust without being asked. Her staff touched the floorboards. Blue whitened through the crystal and climbed her wrist. For an instant the room sharpened: slat walls, stacked sacks, a shovel in the corner. Sweat darkened the mage's neck.

"Here?" Sylvanas asked.

"Loudest here," the mage said, voice gone thin. "Not deepest. Just loudest."

"Explain."

The mage opened her eyes without looking up. "A bell struck in one room doesn't make it the wound, even if you hear it loudest."

"Can you tell what brought it in?"

"No." Frustration roughened the word. "The grain's carrying too much of the outer stain. The whole village is full of it. Soil. Trough. Cellar stone. If I call the grain the answer because it's loudest, I'll be lying to you."

"Up," Sylvanas said. "Outside."

The mage rose with less grace than she'd want seen. Her left hand found a post. Back in the lane, she blinked against the air.

"You look ill," Sylvanas said.

"This place is."

They circled behind the last row of houses, where kitchen scraps ought to have gone and burial plots ought to have started. The ground there had been worked over by too many feet and no order at all. Dirt broken to clods, old mud kicked up under new. Drag marks and heel gouges. One place where a body had gone down hard enough to leave a shoulder in the earth before being pulled on or climbing up again.

Cyndia crouched and counted under her breath.

Sylvanas read what the ground would give her first. No clean column. Too much overlap. But the story held. A great deal of movement away from the village: feet dragging, crossing, wandering, then finding one pull and following it.

Many of them barefoot.

Lyriel came up on her right. "No grave field. No burn pit."

"I know."

The air smelled of broken dirt and old sickness.

The mage stopped at the edge of the churn and stayed out of it.

"Can your magic tell where they went?" Sylvanas asked.

"Not from this mess."

"Can it tell anything useful?"

A pause. "This was not one rush. Several passes. Some while the ground was wetter."

Sylvanas cut her eyes sideways.

The mage answered the look before it landed. "I'm not tracking the feet. I'm reading what they disturbed. The older churn had time to settle. The fresher breaks haven't."

Enough.

Cyndia rose. "A lot of them."

"How many."

"Fifty if I lie low. More likely sixty, and I still wouldn't swear that's all of them."

Sylvanas looked north. The road bent behind alder and a long rise. Quel'Thalas that way. Home that way. She was not sending a warning on churned dirt and magical disgust alone.

"Not enough," she said.

Cyndia's mouth hardened. Agreement changed nothing.

From under the nearest house came a sound.

Wood knocking softly against wood. A scrape. Then stillness so complete every ranger turned at once.

The bow was in Sylvanas's hand before the thought finished.

"There," Lyriel said.

Sylvanas moved for the house.

It leaned toward the lane, old settle in the frame, with a root-cellar door built into the packed earth under the kitchen. One plank had shifted half an inch.

"Back," she said.

She planted one boot beside the doors, not on them, and hooked two fingers under the iron ring. The wood stuck, then yielded. Cold breathed up, wet and earthen. The dark below had shape but not detail.

"Come out," Sylvanas said into it. "Now."

Silence.

Then a tiny catch of breath. Human.

The mage raised one hand. Light gathered in her palm, blue and sour. Sylvanas hated the color in this village. It made everything look bruised.

"Again," she said.

"Come out," the mage echoed, voice lower than usual. "You're safe enough for the next ten seconds. Take them."

Movement below. Small. Careful. A face appeared between the doors, dirt pasted across one cheek, eyes too large for the thin face. Eight, perhaps nine.

When she saw the bows she made one trapped sound and tried to go backward. Her heel slipped.

Neral was there before Sylvanas chose her, kneeling at the lip of the cellar with both hands empty and lifted where the child could see.

"Easy," she said. No field in her voice now. "No one is going to drag you anywhere. Stay there if you like."

The girl froze, breathing through her mouth.

"What's your name?"

Nothing.

"You don't have to tell me yet. Can you look at me?"

Barely, but she could.

"Good. That's enough. Anyone else down there?"

A tiny shake of the head.

"Any dead ones moving?"

The girl's stare went past her to whatever the question had brought back.

"No," she whispered.

"Then come up," Neral said. "One step. Then another. If you fall, I've got you."

She came because Neral had given her one small thing to do. One step. Another. On the third she wavered. Neral took her under the arms and lifted her the rest of the way into the light.

She smelled of turnip cellar and fear. She weighed nothing.

The mage crouched at once. "Water."

Sylvanas passed her canteen without comment. The girl drank too fast, coughed, then locked both hands around the skin and clutched it to her chest.

"Food first," Neral said.

"Questions first," Sylvanas said.

The girl flinched so hard water ran down her chin.

"Then allow a compromise, my lady," Neral said without heat. "One bite. Three questions. Another bite."

"Fine."

Lyriel brought dried apple from her pouch. The girl gnawed it in little jerks, swallowing before she finished chewing.

Sylvanas went down to one knee.

"Look at me."

The child did.

"Your house. Who was in it?"

"Mam. Da. Hester. Me."

"Which died first?"

The apple stopped. Her eyes fixed on Sylvanas's gorget, on the silver edge of it.

"Da," she said. "Then Hester. Mam said it wasn't fever—fever doesn't smell sweet."

The mage went very still beside Sylvanas.

"After they died?"

The girl's fingers closed until her knuckles whitened. "They got up."

"How long after?"

"Night. Maybe." She looked toward the house and did not see it. "I was under the table. Mam put me there. Hester was making that noise with her teeth. Da had stopped. Then he was standing."

"Did they leave the house alone?"

The girl shook her head. "Others were outside. I heard them. The lane was full."

"Going where?"

"North," the child whispered after drinking. "All north. Past the wagons. Past old Paret's shed. Like they were listening."

Cyndia made a tiny sound beside them.

"Did anyone lead them?"

"I don't know," she said. "There was shouting before. Not after."

"What shouting?"

"Men in the square. About grain. About price." She squeezed her eyes shut. "Mam said stay away from traders when men got mean over pennies."

"Which traders?"

"Old Paret. And the new one maybe. I don't know. I heard Mam say there'd be trouble if cheap sacks kept coming. Da laughed."

Sylvanas stood. "Cyndia. Find Paret's shed."

"Already guessing which one," Cyndia said, and moved.

The mage stayed crouched with the girl. "What was your name?"

A ragged pause. "Mira."

"Mira, I'm Jaina. This is Neral. The woman in steel is helping too."

Mira made a strangled little hitch that almost managed a laugh.

Sylvanas ignored the mage and walked the north lane with Lyriel at her shoulder. Paret's shed was a broad lean-to two lanes past the square. Empty now except for a broken axle, flour dust, and a scale set crooked on a crate. Cyndia was already crouched beside it, fingers tracing ruts.

"Wagons here," she said. "Several. One heavier than the rest. Recent."

"North?"

"In and out both. That's trade, not flight."

Sylvanas followed the lane beyond the shed to where the road widened for carts to turn, then narrowed again between alder and ditch. Here the marks cleared.

Bare feet, boot heels, dragging hems. One deep set of wagon ruts older than the rest. The road looked ordinary until the crystal at her staff head took on that cold underglow. Then it looked used up.

The mage shut her eyes. "Yes."

"Most of it runs this way," the mage said. "Bodies. Many together. They kept to the road once they found it."

"How many?"

"Don't ask me for numbers from what's left on it."

"Then give me shape."

The mage opened her eyes. "Enough to foul the line all the way up the rise. More than a household. Less than an army."

"No. But it sounds like Cyndia."

Cyndia snorted.

Lyriel had gone a little farther on, bow lowered but ready. "Clearer prints here where the ditch kept the edge from drying. Toes dragging. Heels biting wrong. No weight shifts. They didn't walk like living people."

The village dead had moved north in a body: toward farms, then roads, then the passes, then Quel'Thalas.

Behind them Mira gave a thin cry. Neral spoke over it at once, not loud, just steady.

"Take her back from it," Sylvanas said.

Neral did, one arm around her shoulders, head bent so she only had to hear that steady voice and not the world.

The mage watched them, then said quietly, "You have enough now."

Sylvanas measured time in distances. The nearest mounted post. How fast one rider became ten. How many villages lay between this ditch and the first walls she was not willing to gamble.

"Yes," she said. "Now I do."

Sylvanas wrote the warning herself on a strip from her field packet, hand hard enough to score the page.

Millhaven lost. Civilian dead mobile. Track confirmed northbound in significant number. Contamination saturates site and road. Quarantine villages on the line. Alert ranger posts and dispatch riders toward Silvermoon at once.

She sanded it, rolled it, sealed it over Cyndia's travel lamp, and slid it into the narrow case behind Neral's saddle.

"You ride north," she said. "Do not stop in any settlement longer than it takes to hand over a copy and demand fresh horses. If a gate captain argues, tell him I told him to choke on the seal and move anyway."

Neral's mouth twitched. "A direct quote?"

"Exact."

Neral sobered as she took the tube. "The girl?"

Mira stood beside her mare with both fists knotted in the blanket over the saddle. One side of her face was washed. The other still held dirt in the crease beneath her eye. She chewed because it gave her mouth work other than trembling.

"She goes with you," Sylvanas said. "One more rider."

The fourth ranger touched fist to breastplate at once.

"Get her ahead of the road as fast as you can. If she starts screaming at the sound of hooves, you let her scream."

"Yes, captain."

Mira looked from one armed stranger to another and settled on Neral. She crouched to the girl's height.

"You'll ride in front of me," she said. "Can you hold a pommel?"

She nodded.

"Good. My mare bites men and never children. If you feel like falling, tell me first so I may object."

That got the broken hitch of breath again.

The mage stood a few paces off with her arms folded against the chill. At Sylvanas's count of riders she turned.

"One more rider north," she said. "That leaves four south."

"You can count. I had hopes you might manage it."

"I was checking whether your brilliance extended to arithmetic."

Sylvanas looked at her. The mage looked back, pale under the skin from the granary.

"If the road ahead is this fouled," the mage said, "you'll want me."

"You'll do until I find a better mage."

"Comforting."

Lyriel came in from the south lane. "Movement on the north road. One wagon. Slow."

Every hand went to a weapon. Sylvanas stepped into the center of the square and sighted down the lane.

The wagon appeared between the alder trunks a moment later: single horse, old bay, head hanging. The wagon rode empty except for a rolled tarp and a shovel. The driver sat bundled in brown wool with a cap low over white hair at the temples. He reined in at the sight of armed strangers in his village.

His face changed by degrees: surprise, confusion, then flat shock.

"Stars preserve us," he said. Country accent. Age rough in it. No false cheer. "What happened here?"

Sylvanas did not lower the bow. "Name."

"Paret Dane." He looked from her to the houses, then to Mira by the horses, and the last color left his face. "No."

Mira shrank behind Neral's leg and whispered, "Old Paret."

The man heard it. His grip on the reins failed for a second, then hardened.

"Mira? Sweetheart, where's your mam?"

No one answered. Mira could not. Neral did not.

Paret shut his eyes once. Opened them old.

"I was south on the mill road," he said. "Two days' trade. Flour, salt, lamp oil. Came back empty because the fools cleaned me out at Harbrook." He swallowed. "What happened?"

"Sickness," the mage said before Sylvanas chose how much to give. "Then worse."

Paret looked at the houses again. Counting it.

"We found tracks leading north," Sylvanas said. "Many of them. Tell me what settlements lie nearest on that road."

He answered at once.

"Alder Run first, though that's hardly more than six farms and a shrine. Then Three Willows. Then the old relay post before the border lane turns east. If whatever took this place kept moving, it'll find folk all along that line."

"Any trouble reported before you left?"

"Coughing. Sick cattle. People arguing over spoiled sacks and whose fault it was." His brow drew in. "Nothing like this."

Sylvanas lowered the bow a finger's breadth, no more. "You sold grain here?"

"When they could pay." A bitter shrug moved under the wool. "Less this month than usual. A younger trader was cutting under everybody from the south road. Didn't know his name. Red wagon once, plain boards the next time. Men change paint when debt gets ahead of them."

"He came through Millhaven often?"

"Often enough for the farmers to curse him." Paret rubbed at his stubble. "Could've been cheap stock. Could've been theft. In a lean season, people don't ask much once the price turns kind."

"You're riding north?" Sylvanas asked.

"Home's north." He looked at Mira again. "Or was. Brother's place past Alder Run, if the road hasn't taken that too."

Neral said, "We're sending warning that way now."

"Good." Paret nodded too fast, trying to turn motion into action. "Good. I'll spread it where I can. Tell them not to let kin open the door after dark. Tell them not to go close if someone they buried comes scratching." His mouth worked once. "Tell them to burn what gets back up."

Mira made a small wrecked sound. Neral laid a hand over her hair.

The old man gentled his voice at once. "Easy, little pip. None of that for you now."

Then he looked at Sylvanas properly, at the armor and ears and the hard shape of the square.

"You'll be heading south, then?"

"Yes."

"There's still folk on that road. Scared, hungry, stupid in the usual ways. Not dead yet." He gestured with the reins. "If you find them before this does, maybe you'll keep them from turning into another of these."

Sylvanas gave one short nod. "If you see any village standing quiet like this one, you do not enter. You turn around."

"At my age?" Paret said. "I turn around from chairs if they look unfriendly."

"Go," she said.

He touched two fingers to the brim of his cap. "May the stars keep your road, captain."

Sylvanas watched him go until the wagon cleared the rise. Nothing in it tripped the part of her that hunted ambush, though the farewell still sat oddly in her ears.

By the time the wagon vanished from sight, Neral had Mira wrapped in her cloak and mounted, the fourth ranger up behind her. The child sat stiff with both hands locked around the pommel.

"Ride," Sylvanas said.

They did. North at once, hooves striking the road that had already taken too much north. Mira never looked back. Neral did once, enough to catch Sylvanas's eye and touch two fingers to the warning tube at her shoulder. Then the rise took them.

Silence flowed back into the square, thinner now for lacking horses and people and one frightened child's breathing.

Four remained.

Sylvanas mounted and turned her mare south.

The mage fell in beside her without invitation, staff across the saddle bow instead of upright in her hand. Cyndia and Lyriel took rear and flank by habit. The company felt smaller now, sharper, harder to hold if the road threw a hundred dead at them in the dark.

The village slid past. Open door. Dead mill. Shirts on the line still hung. The world had stopped around it.

At the edge of the lane the mage said, "You were right not to send the warning on guesswork."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the road.

"A rare honor," the mage added. "Cherish it."

"Don't ruin the moment."

"Too late."

They crossed the little bridge and took the south road under a sky gone the color of old steel. Behind them Millhaven stayed still. Ahead, the land opened in long fields already touched by evening, each fence line and stand of trees another place death might have paused before moving on.

South, then. Toward the next village with smoke still rising. Toward people not yet dead enough to walk.

Sylvanas set her heels to the mare and did not look back again.
