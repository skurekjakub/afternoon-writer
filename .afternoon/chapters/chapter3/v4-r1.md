# Chapter 3: The Dead Village

From the ridge Sylvanas had read Millhaven as cold chimneys and silence, the wrongness a field commander could price from half a mile off. Down in the mill lane, with river damp off the bend and the wheel sitting dead over its race, the price kept climbing. A door stood open on the first house past the mill, swinging on good iron strap hinges in the wind. Inside, the stove was cold. A pot on the plate had gone to crust and pale mold, and flies worked the rim without hurry, the loudest thing on the street.

She held at the threshold and listened.

No dog behind a fence. No goat in a pen. No voice from a kitchen. No child called in before dark. The river slipped under the mill pilings with a thin secretive sound. Behind her the Thalassian horses pinned their ears flat and shifted under saddle leather gone dark with sweat. Even the elven mounts, steady through blight-sign and old death on the south road, wanted nothing from this ground. Jaina's mare sidled twice before Neral caught the rein, and the crystal in the mage's staff pulsed in the half-dark, unbidden and unwelcome.

"Sweep," Sylvanas said. "Pairs. Cyndia, take Lyriel. North side first, south after. Report what you find, not what you think it means."

Cyndia went at once, Lyriel half a step behind with her bow strung. They moved through Millhaven's muddy lanes in Farstrider rhythm, one forward and one covering, angles crossing where the gaps between cottages, sheds, and split-rail pens opened wide enough for a clean shot. The unnamed escort ranger held the road at the village mouth, horse turned broadside across the lane, ready to whistle if the approach changed.

"Neral, hold the mage's horse. She'll want to get down."

Neral caught the rein before Jaina could dismount the way she always did—too fast, one boot catching, the staff already flaring. The mage dropped to one knee in the road, pressing her free hand to the packed dirt without waiting for the perimeter to close, the same habit she had shown at every stop since the ward-stones fell behind them.

The habit still grated. Jaina knelt in exposed places because the reading mattered more to her than the angles, and two days of riding together had not changed that arithmetic. Useful and careful were separate facts.

Sylvanas took the first house alone.

The latch iron was greasy with old hands and cold now. Rough Lordaeron boards under her boots, river-stone hearth, smoke-black rafters, the whole room built for weather and work rather than grace. The half-eaten loaf, the shoved-back chair, and the untouched dust fixed the timing at a glance. The loaf sat on a bread board near the stove, the cut side furred with mold thick enough for days of abandonment. Dust lay on surfaces that should have been touched that morning: the bucket bail by the hearth, the broom propped in its corner with grit still in the twigs, the crock beside the door where someone had left a ladle standing upright in cold grease.

She crossed into the sleeping room. Straw pallet. Blankets thrown aside. The body's impression still pressed into the ticking. A child's wooden horse lay on its side beside the bed, one wheel split, dropped from a hand that had never come back for it. The room held the stale chill of a hearth gone dark for days.

She came back into the lane as Cyndia's whistle carried from two streets north—the short rising note for all clear, nothing active. Lyriel answered a moment later from farther on: same note, same nothing.

"Every house is the same," Cyndia said when she returned, mud to the ankle on her boots and a streak of soot on one bracer where she had checked a forge. "Meals left out. Beds slept in. Work abandoned in the middle of it. The blacksmith's tongs are still clamped around a bar on the dead coals. The tanner's yard has hides pegged on the frames. I counted fourteen doors open and not one body in any of them."

The only living thing Sylvanas had found was a tabby cat on a windowsill, cleaning its paw while the village rotted around it. A dog's water dish sat full and scummed outside the cooper's shed. The dog was gone. Whatever had emptied Millhaven had taken the people and left some of their animals behind. Some creatures had scattered before it finished; others had stayed to starve.

"Lyriel."

"Twenty-two dwellings on my side, including the mill house and the chandler's shop." Lyriel had slung her bow to report, a risk she took only when the houses stayed quiet long enough to earn it. "Same picture—life interrupted, no bodies, no blood, no sign of a fight. The mill stores have grain sacked and tied for market, still waiting."

Thirty-six households and not a single corpse. The houses still held meals, tools, and beds; only the people were gone.

Jaina was standing now, dirt ground into her palm, the staff pulsing low in her other hand.

"The granary," she said. "That reads strongest."

Sylvanas looked once at the mill, the river loading door, the path behind the storehouse where carts would have come in from the fields. "Show me."

The granary sat behind the mill on river-stone foundations, timber-framed and squat, its heavy plank door propped open by a split grain sack leaking barley across the threshold. Inside, dust and dry rot sat in the air with the flat sweet smell of grain gone past its season. Sacks lined the walls in straight rows. Nothing had been ripped open by looters or kicked over in a fight. Everything sat where traders had left it for a market day that never came.

Jaina knelt in the spill by the door, set her staff against the wall, and laid both palms against the packed-earth floor. The crystal guttered once. Sylvanas cleared the room while she worked—corners, loft ladder, loading door over the river chute, rafters broad enough to hide a man if he was thin and stupid enough to try it.

"The whole village is soaked through," Jaina said at last. Dust clung to the heels of her hands when she lifted them. "Well water, root cellar floors, the trough by the smithy. This building reads three or four times stronger than anything else."

"How long."

Jaina rubbed grain dust from one thumb with the heel of the other hand. "Days. A week, maybe more. The traces are stacked too tightly to split clean. The granary reads loudest, but loudest is not the same thing as first."

She was already giving Sylvanas the road answer now. No apology in it. No attempt to soften the uncertainty.

"Say the rest."

"Grain stored here may be the thickest patch. It may be one of a dozen." She looked up. Her hair had slipped loose at the neck, and stale heat from the closed building had brought color into her face. "Something brought it in. After that, people spread it through the whole village."

On the farms two days back, Sylvanas would have called that scholar's caution. Here, with rot under every roof in Millhaven, it looked more like discipline. Jaina had evidence under her hands and still refused the easiest conclusion.

"What else carries it in."

"Water. Runoff. Anything they drank, cooked with, or fed to livestock." Jaina pushed herself upright and steadied once against a grain stack. "The well is fouled. The blacksmith's trough is worse. If it reached the kitchens and the stables together, the village did the rest itself."

That was more than Jaina had been giving her on the road. Harder to dismiss, too, when it came from a woman kneeling in tainted barley and refusing her own neatest answer.

Sylvanas turned from the granary and walked back into the lane.

She found the rest behind the village, where the last cottages gave way to kitchen plots and the lane narrowed into a muddy track between bean poles, dead cabbage rows, and low stone walls. Too many feet had gone through a space built for one cart at a time, churning the earth to paste. Sylvanas crouched and pressed her fingertips into the nearest print.

Shallow. Sliding. A foot dragged through soft mud instead of planting cleanly. The gait ran wrong—half walk, half lurch, legs moving by force of habit after the rest had gone.

She followed the track north twenty paces, reading where mud met firmer ground. Crust had snapped along both sides where bodies wandered off the path and came back. Garden fences were broken in two places where the press had widened and narrowed again. She tried to count and gave it up. The overlap buried the number, but dozens still showed in the churn.

Cyndia appeared beside her without being called and crouched lower over the same dirt, one knee sinking into the edge of a cabbage bed gone to slime.

"Sixty, maybe more," she said. "I can't separate them. Too many passes through the same ground, and scavengers have chewed the edges after—rats or dogs, I think. Direction is mostly north, but past the last garden wall the track fans out. I can't give you a line I'd stake a warning on."

At least sixty dead had walked out of Millhaven, and north covered too much country for a bad dispatch.

She stayed crouched a moment longer with her hand in the mud, feeling cooling air and broken crust strip the sign away. A warning sent on bad direction was worse than none. It pulled riders to the wrong road and left the right one open.

She needed a witness or a cleaner spoor before she spent riders on a warning.

"Keep working the track," Sylvanas said. "Take it as far as the road cut and mark anything you trust. We need someone alive who saw where they went."

The cellar lay under the fourth house from the river, below a trapdoor half-hidden by a fallen shelf and a scatter of broken crocks.

Lyriel found it on the second pass through the houses, after the first sweep and the ground read had given them all they would. She came back at a near-silent run, stopping beside Sylvanas with her voice pared down to what mattered.

"Someone alive. Fourth house. Under the floor."

Sylvanas took the steps first.

Stone stairs dropped into damp dark, and the smell hit before the shapes did—old urine, stale fear, the sour reek of somebody too frightened to climb out for days. She went down with bow in hand and an arrow nocked, because cellars in dead villages did not earn the benefit of doubt.

The girl was behind a barricade of flour sacks and a water barrel gone mostly dry, knees pulled to her chest, blanket scrap clenched in one fist. A cracked jar of beans lay tipped beside her, and she made no sound at all.

"Neral," Sylvanas said up the stairs. "Come down. Slowly."

She stayed back from the barricade, armor and a drawn bow the wrong first shape for a child already cornered. Sylvanas knew that from long practice at reading what made living things hold still and what made them bolt.

Neral came down with her bow slung and her hands empty, crouching at the foot of the steps and waiting until the girl's breathing came down from a raw rasp before she spoke.

"I know," Neral said. "It's dark and it stinks and you've been down here too long. My name is Neral. I'm a ranger. I have clean water and a horse with a terrible temper. What's your name?"

The girl's mouth worked once against the blanket wool.

"Miri," she said.

Jaina stood at the top of the stairs with the staff dimmed low, and when Sylvanas gave her a short nod the mage came down carefully, kneeling beside Neral no nearer to the girl than the ranger, one hand on the wall to keep from brushing the sacks.

"Miri," Jaina said. "Can I look at you? I won't touch you."

The child measured the stairs, the trapdoor, the women between her and the way out. Then she held out her free hand, palm up, in the practiced motion of someone who had seen a village healer work.

Jaina held her own hand an inch above the skin. The crystal pulsed once, blue light catching in the damp mortar between the stones.

"She's clean," Jaina said. "Dry, hungry, bruised, but clean. The plague never reached her."

"The cellar."

"Stone kept it below the taint, most likely. The water table may not have carried it this deep." Jaina drew her hand back. "She was lucky."

They brought Miri into the last of the light, and Neral wrapped her in a ranger's cloak before putting a waterskin in her hands, correcting the first desperate gulp with the same firm touch she used on skittish horses. Jaina set her staff aside against the table and stayed low to the girl's eye line. Sylvanas gave Miri a few minutes, enough for the shaking in her hands to ease and her breath to stop skittering at every shadow across the trapdoor.

"Miri," Sylvanas said. "What happened here."

The child answered in scraps.

Sweetness came first—the thing Miri remembered earliest, turning up on people's breath and in the bread and the water and the morning porridge. Honey in the wrong place. The girl's fingers locked harder in the cloak every time she said the word bread, knuckles whitening at the sound of it.

Then sickness, house by house: the baker first, then the miller's family, then the tanner and his wife and two of their boys, then more. People stopped eating and stopped getting out of bed. The village healer made rounds until the healer stopped making them too.

"And then?" Jaina asked, low and steady, one hand braced against the table so she would not lean too near.

"They died." The blanket scrap tightened in Miri's fist until her knuckles whitened. "And then they got up."

No one in the room moved when she said it.

"My mother told me to go to the cellar," Miri said. Her voice had gone hoarse again, the words catching where she had to drag them past dry fear. "She told me take water and bread and not come out until somebody came. She told me not to look." Her stare fixed on the square of dusk above the steps, then slipped past it toward the north side of the village. "I looked. Through the crack in the trapdoor. At night. They walked. The dead ones came out into the lane and walked toward the north road. Slow, like they had forgotten how legs worked. Some came back for the sick who hadn't died yet and carried them."

"North," Sylvanas said, and Miri swallowed water, coughing before she clutched the skin back to her chest. "The north road. Not to the river. Not to the fields. North."

Witness and ground now pointed the same way, not proof yet but closer.

"Anything else?" Jaina asked, and Miri shut her eyes hard, chasing the pieces back into order. "Mama said there was a newer grain man on the road before it started. A different one from the man who came every season. She said the bread tasted different after."

Sylvanas took that for what it was: a frightened child's loose thread, not an answer.

"Neral," she said. "Stay with her."

She walked to the north road cut, where Cyndia had already driven cloth markers into the mud, and Jaina followed with the staff dimmed again, stepping where the markers spared her the worst of the muck.

The road cut was worse than the lane behind the houses. Wagon ruts from weeks of village trade lay over the dead's passage—hoof prints, rain damage, fox, rat, crow. The shoulder had broken where carts coming off the fields habitually shaved the bend, and the ditch beside it held black water under a skin of chaff.

Cyndia's markers picked out the places where the dead could still be separated from ordinary traffic: torn strips of cloak cloth on thorn twigs, quick field notation from a ranger who had been reading ground since Jaina was still learning to hold a pen.

"I can give you north-northeast past the second field wall," Cyndia said. "That's as clean as the ground gets."

"Jaina. Can you strip the tainted sign from the rest of it."

Jaina knelt at the verge where Cyndia's first marker stood. She planted the staff in the mud, spread both hands over the road, and the crystal went dark. Sweat broke along her hairline in the cold. The strain showed first in her forearms, cords standing out under the skin as she held one layer of the road apart from another.

The light was nearly gone now. Beyond the cut, the land had flattened into dark fields and hedge lines, open country for anything moving north in numbers.

"Here," she said without looking up. "This layer carries the taint. The rest doesn't. If I hold it, can you read only the ground that answers?"

Sylvanas crouched beside her and put her own fingers into the marked earth, reading past the wagon ruts once Jaina had stripped the rest away.

With the road's other scars pushed back, the drag marks stood out under the wagon ruts—the shuffle-step of bodies that did not bend their knees correctly, the places where feet had caught the crust and torn it instead of stepping over. North-northeast. Parallel grooves. A rough column, shoulder to shoulder in a lane meant for carts, pressing toward the road that led to the Quel'Thalas border. Jaina had cut the noise away for her. The sign lay clean enough now to spend riders on.

Sylvanas stood.

"Sixty at least. North-northeast. Column, not rout. They had direction, and somebody was keeping them in it."

Cyndia's fingers closed around the nearest cloth marker.

"That is toward home," she said.

Toward the border—toward the wards, the pass, the patrol lines. Cyndia did not say more. She did not have to. Sixty bodies moving in column toward Quel'Thalas was warning enough.

Sylvanas looked once more at the broken crust, then committed.

"Neral carries the warning."

She wrote the dispatch in the miller's house on a bread board angled toward the last grey light. Ink from her field kit. Steel nib unfolded from its own cap. The board still held a dusting of old flour at one corner where somebody had meant to slice supper before the village stopped. She kept the message short. Field dispatches were for numbers, direction, and threat. Anything else belonged to desks.

*Millhaven emptied. Sixty-plus reanimated, moving NNE in column toward Thalassian border. Contamination confirmed in village water, soil, grain stores. No living population remaining. One survivor recovered, child, uncontaminated. Dispatch rider Neral carries this warning and the survivor north. Recommend border patrol double on northeastern approach and Farstrider command be notified at earliest relay. Investigation continues south. —Windrunner.*

She rolled the paper tight, sealed it with the wax stub from her kit, and pressed the field mark flat with her thumb.

Outside, Miri sat wrapped in a ranger's cloak on the well curb while the unnamed escort ranger held both northbound horses steady and Neral checked cinches. The child had both hands buried in the horsehair at the nearest neck, taking warmth where talk would not help.

Sylvanas handed over the dispatch tube. "Straight north. Do not stop at any settlement between here and the border. If you meet a patrol, hand this off and keep riding with the girl until you reach a garrison. If you do not meet a patrol, ride until you do."

Neral strapped the tube to the saddle ring and checked the leather twice. "And the girl?"

"She stays with you until you reach someone who can keep her safe. You were good with her. That's why you're carrying this and not Cyndia."

Neral nodded, turning to Miri and holding out a hand.

Neral kept one hand on the mare's girth while she spoke, letting the horse feel her there. "We're riding north now. Long ride. Mostly boring if we're lucky. There'll be walls when we get there, hot food, and stables cleaner than this village well. My mare pulls left when she's tired, so if we drift that's her being lazy, not me losing the road."

That finally pulled the child's eyes off the horse.

"Can you sit in front and hold the mane?" Neral asked. "Or the saddle horn if you hate the mane."

Miri let herself be lifted. Neral settled behind her, gathered the cloak around her shoulders, and put the girl's hands where they needed to go. The unnamed escort ranger mounted beside them. One more rider gone north, one more horse out of the line south. Reduced margin, as plain as counting arrows after a long fight.

"Ride well," she said.

"Captain." Neral touched two fingers to her brow and turned the mare north.

They rode until the bend took them, Neral keeping the horse to a working trot and pacing for distance instead of speed. The unnamed escort ranger stayed close on the off side. In two minutes the curve of the river road and the falling dark hid all three.

Four riders left: Sylvanas, Jaina, Cyndia, and Lyriel, one less horse in the southbound line, Corin's Crossing ahead and Millhaven at their backs.

She had just turned toward the saddled horses when a wagon came around the north bend at the slow creak of a draught horse pulling light.

An open-bed farm wagon came first: slatted sides, canvas rolled back and lashed, grain dust and old chaff silvering the joints. An old man sat on the bench in a patched road coat and broad hat, broad-shouldered and sun-darkened, his nose broken once and set badly enough to stay crooked.

The horse saw them first and slowed, ears flicking at the empty lane. The trader pulled up and squinted at the armed women standing in a village that should have had people in it.

"Evening," he said.

His gaze went past Sylvanas to the dark houses, the still well, the dead mill wheel at the bend. His mouth eased open a fraction.

"Millhaven's quiet tonight."

"Millhaven is empty."

He sat with that, reins loose in his hands and gaze still searching for the people who should have been there.

"Empty," he repeated. "I was here six weeks ago. Full market. The baker bought two sacks of barley flour and complained about the price for twenty minutes. What happened?"

"Plague," Sylvanas said, letting the word stand between them before she asked, "You trade grain on this road?"

"Thirty years, give or take." He pushed his hat back with the heel of one hand. "Millhaven. Corin's Crossing. The farms between. I know every sack of flour and every bushel of seed barley between here and Andorhal." His eyes took in her bow, her armor, her ears. "You're Farstriders."

That much rang true; a local grain man would know the baker by name, remember who haggled, know which settlements bought seed and which bought bread flour because they had already run through their own.

"We are. The settlements south of here—what condition are they in."

He took a moment over that, picking through what he had seen before he answered, while the draught horse shook its bridle and stamped once in the lane.

"Corin's Crossing had a cough going through when I came north four days back. Nothing that shut the market. The farms between are still trading. Flour shipments have been short, so prices are high, but people are eating. I didn't see anything like this."

"Were you the only grain seller on this stretch?"

"For years." One hand lifted from the reins in a brief loose turn. "Lately there's been another. Younger fellow, came up from the south, started making the same rounds maybe two months back. I lost a few customers to him. Prices were lower. It happens."

That matched Miri's memory closely enough to keep and little else.

"The road south. Passable for mounted riders?"

"Clear to Corin's Crossing, last I saw. Bridge at the mill fork is sound. After that I couldn't say. I turned north there." He gathered the reins back into both hands. "If plague did this, there are people south who don't know it's coming."

"We intend to tell them."

He clicked his tongue, and the wagon creaked forward through the lane.

"Safe roads," he said. "What's left of them."

The wagon rolled north past the last houses, pale grain dust in the bed catching the last light. The trader never looked back. The horse leaned into the collar with the patient rhythm of an animal that had made that road a hundred times and expected to make it a hundred more. On any other road, Sylvanas would have taken him for one more grain seller and kept riding.

She watched the wagon long enough for a second look and no more, then turned south, where the horses were saddled and waiting. Cyndia had already broken the saddlebags down from six riders to four, redistributing weight with the quick practical economy of a woman who had done it on campaign and in storms. Lyriel held the rear watch with her bow across the saddle. Jaina mounted without asking where they were going and settled the staff across her thighs, mud still drying dark on the hem of her robes and grain dust caught at one sleeve.

Four riders now, with darker farmland ahead, fallow fields edged with bare hedgerows, the road flattening as it left the river valley. Corin's Crossing still trading, still sick, not yet dead. Behind them, Millhaven sat silent with empty houses, a dead mill, and a granary full of tainted grain. Somewhere ahead, people in another market still took the cough in their streets for ordinary sickness and the bread on their tables for safe food.

Jaina drew up on Sylvanas's left without being asked. Not trust. Not warmth. The mage had simply stopped waiting to be invited when the ground turned strange, and that was enough.

Sylvanas put her heels to the horse and rode.
