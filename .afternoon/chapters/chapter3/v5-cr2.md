# Chapter 3: The Dead Village

From the ridge Sylvanas had read Millhaven as cold chimneys and silence. Down in the mill lane, with river damp off the bend and the wheel dead over its race, the price kept climbing. A door stood open on the first house past the mill, swinging on good iron in the wind. Inside, the stove was cold. A pot on the plate had gone to crust and pale mold, and flies worked the rim without hurry, the loudest thing on the street.

She held at the threshold and listened.

No dog behind a fence. No goat in a pen. No voice from a kitchen. No child called in before dark. The river slipped under the mill pilings with a thin secretive sound. Even the Thalassian horses wanted nothing from this ground. Jaina's mare sidled before Neral caught the rein, and the crystal in the mage's staff pulsed in the half-dark, unbidden and unwelcome.

"Sweep," Sylvanas said. "Pairs. Cyndia, take Lyriel. North side first, south after. Report what you find, not what you think it means."

Cyndia went at once, Lyriel half a step behind with her bow strung. They moved in Farstrider rhythm, one forward and one covering. The unnamed escort ranger held the road at the village mouth, horse turned broadside across the lane, ready to whistle if the approach changed.

"Neral, hold the mage's horse. She'll want to get down."

Neral caught the rein before Jaina could dismount the way she always did—too fast, the staff already flaring. The mage dropped to one knee in the road, pressing her free hand to the packed dirt without waiting for the perimeter to close.

The habit still grated. Jaina cared more about the reading than the angles. Useful and careful were separate facts.

Sylvanas took the first house alone.

The latch iron was greasy with old hands and cold now. River-stone hearth, smoke-black rafters. The half-eaten loaf, the shoved-back chair, and the untouched dust fixed the timing at a glance. Dust lay on the bucket bail by the hearth, the broom in its corner, the crock beside the door where someone had left a ladle standing upright in cold grease.

She crossed into the sleeping room. Straw pallet. Blankets thrown aside. The body's impression still pressed into the ticking. A child's wooden horse lay on its side beside the bed, one wheel split. The room held the stale chill of a hearth gone dark for days.

She came back into the lane as Cyndia's whistle carried from two streets north—the short rising note for all clear, nothing active. Lyriel answered a moment later from farther on: same note, same nothing.

"Every house is the same," Cyndia said when she returned, mud to the ankle on her boots and soot on one bracer. "Meals left out. Beds slept in. Work abandoned in the middle of it. Fourteen doors open and not one body."

A tabby cat cleaned its paw on a windowsill. The rest of the animals were gone or left to starve.

"Lyriel."

"Twenty-two dwellings on my side, including the mill house and the chandler's shop." Lyriel had slung her bow to report. "Same picture—life interrupted, no bodies, no blood, no sign of a fight. The mill stores are still tied for market."

Thirty-six households. No corpses.

Jaina was standing now, dirt ground into her palm, the staff pulsing low in her other hand.

"The granary," she said. "Reads strongest."

Sylvanas took in the mill, the river loading door, the path behind the storehouse. "Show me."

The granary sat behind the mill on river-stone foundations, squat and timber-framed, its plank door propped open by a split grain sack leaking barley across the threshold. Inside, dust and dry rot hung over straight rows of sacks. Nothing had been ripped open or kicked over. The place waited for a market day that never came.

Jaina knelt in the spill by the door, setting her staff against the wall before she laid both palms against the packed-earth floor. The crystal guttered once. Sylvanas cleared the room while she worked—corners, loft ladder, loading door, rafters.

"The whole village is soaked through," Jaina said. Dust clung to the heels of her hands when she lifted them. "Well water, root cellar floors, the trough by the smithy. This building reads three or four times stronger than the rest."

"How long."

Jaina rubbed grain dust from one thumb with the heel of the other hand. "Days. A week, maybe more. The traces are stacked too tightly to split clean. This reads loudest, not earliest."

She was already giving Sylvanas the road answer now. No apology in it. No attempt to soften the uncertainty.

"Say the rest."

"Grain stored here may be the thickest patch. It may be one of a dozen." She tipped her chin up. Her hair had slipped loose at the neck. "Something spread it through the whole village. I can't tell what the first source was."

On the farms two days back, Sylvanas would have called that scholar's caution. Here, with rot under every roof, it read more like discipline. Jaina had evidence under her hands and still refused the easiest conclusion.

"What else carries it in."

"Water. Runoff. Anything they drank, cooked with, or fed to livestock." Jaina pushed herself upright and steadied once against a grain stack. "The well is fouled. The blacksmith's trough is worse. If it reached kitchens and stables together, the village did the rest itself."

That was more than Jaina had been giving her on the road. Harder to dismiss from a woman kneeling in tainted barley and refusing her own neatest answer.

Sylvanas turned from the granary, walking back into the lane.

She found the rest behind the village, where the last cottages gave way to kitchen plots and the lane narrowed into a muddy track between bean poles and dead cabbage rows. Too many feet had gone through a space built for one cart at a time, churning the earth to paste. Sylvanas crouched, pressing her fingertips into the nearest print.

Shallow. Sliding. A foot dragged through soft mud instead of planting cleanly. The gait ran wrong—half walk, half lurch, legs moving by force of habit after the rest had gone.

She followed the track north twenty paces. Crust had snapped where bodies wandered off the path and came back. Garden fences were broken where the press had widened and narrowed again. She tried to count and gave it up. Dozens showed in the churn.

Cyndia appeared beside her without being called and crouched over the same dirt, one knee sinking into the edge of a cabbage bed gone to slime.

"Sixty, maybe more," she said. "I can't separate them. Too many passes through the same ground, and scavengers have chewed the edges after. Direction is mostly north, but past the last garden wall the track fans out. I can't give you a line I'd stake a warning on."

At least sixty dead had walked out of Millhaven, and north covered too much country for a bad dispatch.

She stayed crouched a moment longer with her hand in the mud, feeling cooling air and broken crust strip the sign away. A warning sent on bad direction was worse than none.

She needed a witness or a cleaner spoor before she spent riders.

"Keep working the track," Sylvanas said. "Take it as far as the road cut and mark anything you trust. We need someone alive who saw where they went."

The cellar lay under the fourth house from the river, below a trapdoor half-hidden by a fallen shelf and a scatter of broken crocks.

Lyriel found it on the second pass through the houses. She came back at a near-silent run, stopping beside Sylvanas with her voice pared down to what mattered.

"Someone alive. Fourth house. Under the floor."

Sylvanas took the steps first, descending into damp dark with bow in hand and an arrow nocked.

The smell hit before the shapes did—old urine, stale fear, the sour reek of somebody too frightened to climb out for days.

The girl was behind a barricade of flour sacks and a water barrel gone mostly dry, knees pulled to her chest, blanket scrap clenched in one fist. A cracked jar of beans lay tipped beside her.

"Neral," Sylvanas said up the stairs. "Come down. Slowly."

She stayed back from the barricade, armor and a drawn bow the wrong first shape for a child already cornered.

Neral came down with her bow slung and her hands empty, crouching at the foot of the steps until the girl's breathing eased from a raw rasp.

"I know," Neral said. "It's dark and it stinks and you've been down here too long. My name is Neral. I'm a ranger. I have clean water and a horse with a terrible temper. What's your name?"

The girl's mouth worked once against the blanket wool.

"Miri," she said.

Jaina stood at the top of the stairs with the staff dimmed low, and when Sylvanas gave her a short nod the mage came down carefully, kneeling beside Neral no nearer to the girl than the ranger, one hand on the wall to keep from brushing the sacks.

"Miri," Jaina said. "Can I look at you? I won't touch you."

The child measured the stairs, the trapdoor, the women between her and the way out. Then she held out her free hand.

Jaina held her own hand an inch above the skin. The crystal pulsed once, blue light catching in the damp mortar between the stones.

"She's clean," Jaina said. "Dry, hungry, bruised, but clean. The plague never reached her."

"The cellar."

"Stone kept it below the taint, most likely. The water table may not have carried it this deep." Jaina drew her hand back. "She was lucky."

They brought Miri into the last of the light, Neral wrapping her in a ranger's cloak before putting a waterskin in her hands and correcting the first desperate gulp with the same firm touch she used on skittish horses. Jaina set her staff aside against the table and stayed low. Sylvanas gave Miri a few minutes, enough for the shaking in her hands to ease.

"Miri," Sylvanas said. "What happened here."

The child answered in scraps.

Sweetness came first, turning up on people's breath and in the bread and the water and the morning porridge, honey in the wrong place. The girl's fingers locked harder in the cloak every time she said bread.

Then sickness, house by house: the baker first, then the miller's family, then the tanner and his wife and two of their boys, then more, each house going still in turn. The village healer made rounds until the healer stopped making them too.

"And then?" Jaina asked, low and steady, one hand braced against the table so she would not lean too near.

"They died," Miri said, the blanket scrap tightening in her fist until her knuckles whitened, "and then they got up."

"My mother told me to go to the cellar," Miri said, her voice gone hoarse again. "She told me take water and bread and not come out until somebody came. She told me not to look." Her attention fixed on the square of dusk above the steps. "I looked, though. Through the crack in the trapdoor. At night they walked into the lane and headed for the north road, slow like they had forgotten how legs worked, and some came back for the sick who hadn't died yet and carried them."

"North," Sylvanas said, and Miri swallowed water, coughing before she clutched the skin back to her chest. "The north road. Not to the river. Not to the fields. North."

"Anything else?" Jaina asked, and Miri shut her eyes hard, chasing the pieces back into order. "Mama said there was a newer grain man on the road before it started. A different one from the man who came every season. She said the bread tasted different after."

Sylvanas took that for what it was: a frightened child's loose thread, not an answer.

"Neral," she said. "Stay with her."

She walked to the north road cut, where Cyndia had already driven cloth markers into the mud, and Jaina followed with the staff dimmed again.

The road cut was worse than the lane behind the houses. Wagon ruts from weeks of village trade lay over the dead's passage—hoof prints, rain damage, fox, rat, crow. The ditch beside it held black water under a skin of chaff.

Cyndia's markers picked out the places where the dead could still be separated from ordinary traffic: torn strips of cloak cloth on thorn twigs, quick ranger marks from a woman who had been reading ground since Jaina was still learning to hold a pen.

"I can give you north-northeast past the second field wall," Cyndia said. "That's as clean as the ground gets."

"Jaina. Can you strip the tainted sign from the rest of it."

Jaina knelt at the verge where Cyndia's first marker stood, planting the staff in the mud before she spread both hands over the road. The crystal went dark, sweat breaking along her hairline in the cold.

Beyond the cut, the land had flattened into dark fields and hedge lines.

"Here," she said without looking up. "This layer carries the taint. The rest doesn't. If I hold it, can you read only the ground that answers?"

Sylvanas crouched beside her and put her own fingers into the marked earth.

With the road's other scars pushed back, the drag marks stood out under the wagon ruts—the shuffle-step of bodies that did not bend their knees correctly, the places where feet had caught the crust and torn it instead of stepping over. North-northeast. Parallel grooves. A rough column pressing toward the road that led to the Quel'Thalas border. Jaina had cut the noise away for her. The sign lay clean enough now to spend riders on.

Sylvanas stood.

"Sixty at least. North-northeast. Rough formation toward the Quel'Thalas road. That's all the ground gives me."

Cyndia's fingers closed around the nearest cloth marker.

"That is toward home," she said.

Toward the border. Cyndia did not say more. She did not have to. Sixty bodies moving in column toward Quel'Thalas was warning enough.

Sylvanas took one last measure of the broken crust, then gave the order.

"Neral carries the warning."

She wrote the dispatch in the miller's house on a bread board angled toward the last grey light. Ink from her field kit. Steel nib unfolded from its own cap. She kept the message short, writing for numbers, direction, and threat because anything else belonged to desks.

*Millhaven emptied. Sixty-plus reanimated moving NNE in column toward Thalassian border. Contamination confirmed in water, soil, grain stores. One uncontaminated child survivor recovered and sent north with rider Neral. Recommend doubled patrols on northeastern approach and Farstrider command at first relay. Investigation continues south. —Windrunner.*

She rolled the paper tight, sealed it with the wax stub from her kit, and pressed the field mark flat with her thumb.

Outside, Miri sat wrapped in a ranger's cloak on the well curb while the unnamed escort ranger held both northbound horses steady and Neral checked cinches. The child had both hands buried in the nearest neck.

Sylvanas handed over the dispatch tube. "Straight north. Do not stop at any settlement between here and the border. If you meet a patrol, hand this off and keep riding with the girl until you reach a garrison. If you do not, ride until you do."

Neral strapped the tube to the saddle ring, checking the leather twice. "And the girl?"

"She stays with you until you reach someone who can keep her safe. You were good with her. That's why you're carrying this and not Cyndia."

Neral turned to Miri and held out a hand.

Neral kept one hand on the mare's girth while she spoke. "We're riding north now. Long ride. Mostly boring if we're lucky. There'll be walls when we get there, hot food, and stables cleaner than this village well. My mare pulls left when she's tired, so if we drift that's her being lazy, not me losing the road."

That finally pulled the child away from the horse.

"Can you sit in front and hold the mane?" Neral asked. "Or the saddle horn if you hate the mane."

Miri let herself be lifted. Neral settled her in front of the saddle, gathered the cloak around her shoulders, and put the girl's hands where they needed to go. The mare sidestepped once and Neral steadied her with a hand on the girth instead of mounting yet.

Then a wagon creaked around the north bend.

An open-bed farm wagon: slatted sides, canvas rolled back and lashed, grain dust silvering the joints. An old man sat on the bench in a patched road coat and broad hat, his broken nose set crooked for good.

The horse slowed first, ears flicking at the empty lane, and the trader pulled up squinting at the armed women standing in a village that should have had people in it.

"Evening," he said.

He took in the dark houses, the still well, the dead mill wheel at the bend, and his mouth eased open a fraction.

"Millhaven's quiet tonight."

"Millhaven is empty."

He sat with that, reins loose in his hands, still searching for the people who should have been there.

"Empty," he repeated. "I was here six weeks ago. Full market. The baker bought two sacks of barley flour and complained about the price for twenty minutes; what happened?"

"Plague," Sylvanas said, letting the word stand between them before she asked, "You trade grain on this road?"

"Thirty years, give or take." He pushed his hat back with the heel of one hand. "Millhaven. Corin's Crossing. The farms between. I know every sack of flour and every bushel of seed barley between here and Andorhal." He took in her bow, her armor, her ears. "You're Farstriders."

That much rang true; a local grain man would know the baker by name and which settlements had already burned through their own flour.

"We are. The settlements south of here—what condition are they in."

He took a moment over that before he answered.

"Corin's Crossing had a cough going through when I came north four days back. Nothing that shut the market. The farms between are still trading. Flour shipments have been short, so prices are high, but people are eating. I didn't see anything like this."

"The road south. Passable for mounted riders?"

"Clear to Corin's Crossing, last I saw. Bridge at the mill fork is sound. After that I couldn't say; I turned north there." He gathered the reins back into both hands. "If plague did this, there are people south who don't know."

"We intend to tell them."

He clicked his tongue, the wagon creaking forward through the lane.

"Safe roads," he said. "What's left of them."

The wagon rolled north past the last houses, pale grain dust in the bed catching the last light. The trader never looked back. On any other road, Sylvanas would have taken him for one more grain seller and kept riding.

She watched the wagon long enough for a second look, then turned back to Neral.

"Ride well," she said.

"Captain." Neral touched two fingers to her brow and swung up behind the girl. The unnamed escort ranger mounted beside them.

They rode until the bend took them, Neral keeping the horse to a working trot and pacing for distance instead of speed while the unnamed escort ranger stayed close on the off side. In two minutes the curve of the river road and the falling dark hid all three.

Four riders left: Sylvanas, Jaina, Cyndia, and Lyriel, one less horse in the southbound line, Corin's Crossing ahead and Millhaven at their backs.

She turned south, where the horses were saddled and waiting. Cyndia had already broken the saddlebags down from six riders to four. Lyriel held the rear watch with her bow across the saddle. Jaina mounted without asking where they were going, the staff across her thighs.

Four riders now, with darker farmland ahead and the road flattening as it left the river valley. Corin's Crossing still trading, not yet dead. Behind them, Millhaven sat silent with empty houses and tainted grain. Somewhere ahead, people in another market still took the cough in their streets for ordinary sickness and the bread on their tables for safe food.

Jaina drew up on Sylvanas's left without being asked. Not trust. Not warmth. The mage had simply stopped waiting to be invited when the ground turned strange, and that was enough.

Sylvanas put her heels to the horse and rode.
