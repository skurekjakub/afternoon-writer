## Chapter 3: The Dead Village

Millhaven did not look any better close.

From the rise it had been a knot of dark roofs and dead air. Down in among the buildings, the wrongness lost distance and turned practical. A cart stood half across the lane with one wheel sunk into mud. A door on the nearest house moved against its latch whenever the wind found it. The stream under the little bridge still ran. Nothing else did.

Sylvanas raised one hand. The whole line stopped with the clean obedience she expected from elves and was still not used to getting from the human between them.

The mare under Jaina went stiff again. Not shying now. Listening. Good animal.

"Cyndia. Left side to the bridge and back. Lyriel, with her. Neral, rear lane. Keep sight on the road north. You." She looked once at the fourth ranger. "Stay with the horses unless I tell you otherwise. If anything moves, call it before you loose."

The ranger saluted.

Jaina sat waiting with the staff across her thighs, too still for comfort. The crystal at its head had gone dim again, though not all the way. It held a line of blue under the surface like banked fire.

"And me?" the mage asked.

"With me." Sylvanas swung down. "You get your turn when I say."

Jaina's mouth tightened. She dismounted anyway.

The village met Sylvanas in pieces. No smoke from the chimneys. No goat complaining from a pen. No dog announcing strangers. The silence had weight because every place like this ought to have been full of useless little sounds. Iron hooks knocking in the breeze. Someone splitting kindling. A child being called inside before dark. Millhaven had all the shapes that should have carried noise and none of the noise itself.

She crossed to the nearest house and pushed the loose door wider with two fingers on the wood, not the iron ring. Cold met her from inside. Not night-cold. House-cold. A stove gone dead hours ago.

One room below, a loft above. Table set for two. A crust of bread hard enough to tap. A cup on its side, long since bled dry into the boards. Dust had started settling on the sill. Not much. Enough.

Interrupted, Sylvanas thought. Not emptied cleanly. Interrupted.

Behind her, leather creaked softly. Jaina had stopped at the threshold.

"Don't touch anything yet."

"I wasn't going to."

That bought a glance. The mage's face had gone pale in the low light, not from nerves. From whatever she was reading under the skin of the place.

Sylvanas let her have the room with a small turn of her head. Jaina stepped in, slow now, staff angled forward like a blind woman's cane. The crystal brightened a shade. Her free hand hovered over the table, the air above the cup, the stove mouth.

"Well?" Sylvanas asked.

"Everywhere," Jaina said quietly. "Not strongest here. Just everywhere."

Not useful enough yet. Sylvanas moved back outside.

The lane gave up more of the same. A wash line left strung, shirts stiff with old rain and newer dust. A yard gate hanging open. A child's hoop near the ditch. In the second house she found stew dried to the pot. In the third, a bed unmade and a shawl dragged half off it, one corner in the dirt where boots had tracked through. No blood worth naming. No bodies. That absence kept sharpening instead of dulling.

Cyndia came out of a low stable with her bow in her hand and her mouth set flat.

"Two cattle in the far pen, dead where they fell. No cuts. No one moved them."

"How long?"

"A day. Two at most."

Sylvanas nodded once and kept walking.

Something streaked across the lane ahead of her. She turned in time to catch the end of it: a grey cat sliding under a cart, ribs showing, tail low. Alive. Alone.

That was almost worse than the cattle.

By the stream the little mill wheel sat still, one spoke broken, weeds snagged around the lower rim. Lyriel crouched at the water's edge with two fingers near the current and then stood.

"No bodies in the stream," she said. "No washdown either. If they died here, the water didn't take them."

"If they died here," Sylvanas repeated.

Lyriel's eyes moved once toward the houses. She did not answer. She did not need to.

The granary stood a little apart from the rest of the village, broad-shouldered and shut. Jaina reached it before Sylvanas gave permission, then had the sense to stop with her hand on the latch and look back instead of opening it.

Progress.

Sylvanas came up beside her. Grain dust lay thick in the grooves of the threshold. No recent sweep. No rats. That caught harder than it should have. Rats belonged where grain lived.

"Open it."

The latch lifted. Heat pressed out, stale and close, the kind trapped spaces kept after holding a day's worth of autumn sun. The smell should have been dry grain, mouse piss, old burlap.

It was those things. And under them, the wrong note Jaina had named at the pass. Sweetness gone bad. Not rot exactly. Something taught to imitate food after forgetting what food was for.

Jaina went down to one knee in the grain dust without being asked. Her staff touched the floorboards. Blue whitened across the crystal and climbed her wrist. For an instant every seam in the room stood out to Sylvanas's eye: slat walls, stacked sacks, the shovel propped in the corner, the sweat-darkened hair at the back of Jaina's neck where the braid had loosened.

"Here?" Sylvanas asked.

"Loudest here," Jaina said. Her voice had gone thinner, pulled toward concentration. "Not deepest. Just loudest."

That was new enough to matter. Sylvanas filed it.

"Explain."

Jaina opened her eyes but did not look up. "If someone struck a bell in one room and left blood in another, you'd hear the bell first. That doesn't make the bell the wound."

Plain, then. Better.

"Can you tell what brought it in?"

"No." Frustration roughened the word. "Grain's carrying too much of the outer stain. The whole village is full of it. Well. Soil. Trough. Cellar stone. If I call the grain the answer because it's loudest, I'll be lying to you."

Sylvanas watched her a beat longer.

The whole village. Not just the lines, not just the water, not just the animals on the road. This place had been soaked through until every surface answered the same way. She had trackers. She had rangers. She did not have this. She could dislike that fact later.

"Up," she said. "Outside."

Jaina rose with less grace than she'd want observed. Her left hand found one of the support posts for a second before it let go. When she came back into the lane, she blinked hard against the open air.

"You look ill," Sylvanas said.

"This place is."

Good enough.

They circled behind the last row of houses where kitchen scraps ought to have gone and burial plots ought to have started beyond them. The ground there had been worked over by too many feet and not enough direction. Dirt broken to clods, crust kicked apart, patches of older mud dug up from under newer. Drag marks here and there. Heel gouges. One place where something had gone down hard enough to leave a shoulder-shape in the earth before being pulled onward or getting up under its own power.

Cyndia crouched. Counted under her breath.

Sylvanas did not interrupt. She read what the ground would give her first. Not a clean column of tracks. Too much overlap, too much collapse, too many places where the earth had been churned after the first marks set. But the story held. A great deal of movement away from the village. Not panicked flight. Not villagers loading carts. Feet that dragged, wandered, crossed, then found one pull and followed it.

Many of them barefoot.

Lyriel came up on her right. "No grave field. No burn pit."

"I know."

The air here smelled different. Broken dirt. Human waste. Old sickness. Under that, the faint metallic edge that had dogged them since the border.

Jaina stopped at the edge of the churned ground and did not step into it. Sensible, for once.

"Can your magic tell where they went?" Sylvanas asked without looking at her.

"Not from this mess."

"Can it tell anything useful?"

A pause. "That this was not one rush. Several passes. Some while the ground was wetter."

Sylvanas's eyes cut sideways.

Jaina shrugged with one hand, impatient with herself before Sylvanas could be. "I'm not tracking the feet. I'm reading where the pressure is strongest in what they disturbed. The older churn has had longer to settle. The fresher breaks are still wrong in a brighter way."

Useful enough.

Cyndia straightened slowly. "A lot of them."

"How many."

"Fifty if I lie low. More likely sixty, and I still wouldn't swear that's all of them."

Missing from the houses. Missing from the yards. Missing from the cattle paths and the stream bank and every place a village should have held its dead if the dead had stayed put.

Sylvanas looked north. The road bent out of sight behind a stand of alder and a long rise beyond it. Quel'Thalas that way. Home that way. She was not sending a warning on churned dirt and magical disgust alone. Not yet.

"Not enough," she said.

Cyndia's mouth hardened. She agreed. So did Sylvanas. The problem was agreement changed nothing.

From somewhere under the nearest house came a sound.

Not loud. Not dramatic. Wood knocked softly against wood, then a little scraping pause, then stillness so complete Sylvanas might have doubted it if not for the way every ranger's head turned at once.

She had the bow in her hand before the thought finished.

"There," Lyriel said.

The sound did not come again.

Sylvanas moved for the house.

The building leaned a little toward the lane, old settle in the frame, with a root cellar door built into the packed earth under the kitchen. One of the planks had shifted half an inch. Nothing more. Enough.

"Back," she said, and the others gave it to her.

She set one boot beside the doors, not on them, and hooked two fingers under the iron ring. The wood stuck before it yielded. Cold breathed up, wet and earthen. The dark below had shape but not detail.

"Come out," Sylvanas said into it. "Now."

Silence.

Then a tiny catch of breath. Human.

Jaina had already lifted one hand. Mage-light gathered at her palm, blue enough to sour the dark instead of warming it. Sylvanas hated the color in this village. It made every surface look bruised.

"Again," she said.

"Come out," Jaina echoed, voice pitched lower than usual, quick edges filed off. "You're safe enough for the next ten seconds. Take them."

Sylvanas gave her a flat look.

"What?" Jaina murmured. "It was true."

Movement below. Small. Careful. A face showed first between the cellar doors, white with dirt pasted across one cheek, eyes too large for her thinned face. A girl of perhaps eight or nine. Too old to be carried, too young for this.

When she saw the bows, she made a sound like an animal in a trap and tried to go backward. Her heel slipped on the step.

Neral was there before Sylvanas chose him, kneeling at the lip of the cellar with both empty hands up where the child could see them.

"Easy," he said. His voice had none of the field in it now. No soldier. No ranger. "Easy. No one is going to drag you anywhere. Stay where you are if you like."

The girl froze, breathing through her open mouth.

"What's your name?" he asked.

Nothing.

"You don't have to tell me yet. Can you look at me?"

She could. Barely.

"Good. That's good enough. Do you have anyone else down there?"

A head shake. So small Sylvanas almost missed it.

"Any dead ones moving?"

The girl's stare went past him to the lane. Past the lane to whatever she'd brought up with the question.

"No," she whispered.

Her voice sounded sanded raw.

"Then come up," Neral said. "One step. Then another. If you fall, I've got you."

She came because he had given her something narrow enough to do. One step. Another. On the third she wavered. Neral took her under the arms and lifted her the last pace into the light.

She smelled of turnip cellar, fear-sweat, and old urine. She weighed almost nothing.

Jaina crouched at once. "Water."

Sylvanas passed her canteen without comment. The girl drank too fast, coughed, then locked both hands around the skin and held it tight against her chest.

There would be no clean questioning. Sylvanas had known that before the child cleared the cellar. Still, they needed something firmer than churn and absence.

"Food first," Neral said.

"Questions first," Sylvanas said.

The girl flinched so hard water splashed down her chin.

Jaina's head turned. "If you break her now, you'll get crying and nothing else."

"If she collapses, I get the same."

"Then allow a compromise, my lady," Neral said without heat. "One bite. Three questions. Another bite."

Sylvanas let him keep the title for the child's sake.

"Fine."

Lyriel brought dried apple from her pouch. The girl gnawed it with desperate little jerks, swallowing before she had finished chewing.

Sylvanas went down to one knee so she did not have to loom for this.

"Look at me."

The child did.

"How many were here when the sickness came?"

She swallowed. "All."

Worthless. Sylvanas changed angle.

"Your house. Who was in your house?"

"Mam. Da. Hester. Me."

Hester likely the sister. The girl had answered fast enough for truth.

"Which of them died first?"

The apple stopped. Her eyes fixed on Sylvanas's gorget, on the silver line at the edge of it.

"Da," she said. "Then Hester. Mam kept saying it wasn't the same fever. She said fever doesn't smell sweet."

Jaina went still beside Sylvanas.

"After they died?" Sylvanas asked.

The girl's fingers closed until her hands went white. "They got up."

No one spoke over that.

"How long after?"

"Night. Maybe." She looked toward the house behind them. Not seeing it. "I was under the table. Mam put me there. Hester was making that noise with her teeth. Da had stopped. Then he was standing."

Enough to make the shape. Not enough yet to send riders. Sylvanas kept her own voice flat, something to push against instead of drown in.

"Did they leave the house alone?"

The girl shook her head. "Others were outside. I heard them. The lane was full."

"Going where?"

She licked cracked lips. Jaina gave her the canteen back before she asked.

"North," the child whispered after drinking. "All north. Past the wagons. Past old Paret's shed. Like they were listening."

The name meant nothing to Sylvanas. Cyndia made a tiny sound, filing it.

"Who told you that was north?"

The girl's brow pinched. "It's just north."

Fair. Villagers usually knew their own roads.

"Did anyone lead them?"

This time the pause lasted longer.

"I don't know," she said. "There was shouting before. Not after."

"What shouting?"

"Men in the square. About grain. About price." She squeezed her eyes shut. "Mam said stay away from traders when men got mean over pennies."

Jaina's gaze flicked to Sylvanas and away.

Not proof. Not even close. Villages argued over grain whenever weather turned poor.

"Which traders?"

The girl took longer still over that one, rummaging through what terror had not already kicked apart.

"Old Paret. And the new one maybe. I don't know. I heard Mam say there'd be trouble if cheap sacks kept coming. Da laughed."

There it was. A thread and no more.

Sylvanas stood. "Cyndia. Find Paret's shed."

"Already guessing which one," Cyndia said, and moved.

Jaina stayed crouched with the girl. "What was your name?"

A ragged pause. "Mira."

"Mira, I'm Jaina. This is Neral. The unpleasant woman standing over us is helping too."

Mira made a strangled little sound that might have been the start of a laugh and failed halfway through. Good enough.

Sylvanas ignored Jaina and walked the lane north with Lyriel at her shoulder. Paret's shed turned out to be a broad lean-to open on one side, two lanes beyond the square. Empty now except for a broken axle, flour dust, and a scale set crooked on a crate. Cyndia was already on the ground beside it, tracing the ruts with two fingers.

"Wagons here," she said. "Several. One heavier than the rest. Recent."

"North?"

"In and out both. That's trade, not flight."

Sylvanas followed the lane past the shed to where the road widened enough for carts to turn and then tightened again between alder and ditch. Here the marks cleared. Not much. Enough.

Foot traffic had thickened on the north road in a way no sane caravan would choose. Bare feet, boot feet, dragging hems, one deep set of wagon ruts older than the rest. Over it all lay the same ugly disturbance the mage had named without naming. The dirt looked ordinary until Jaina came up behind them and the crystal at her staff head took on that cold underglow. Then the road showed its memory.

Jaina shut her eyes. "Yes."

Sylvanas did not ask yes to what. She watched the road instead.

"Most of the pressure runs this way," Jaina said. "Not carts. Bodies. Many together. They kept to the road once they found it."

"How many?"

"Don't ask me for numbers from residue."

"Then give me shape."

Jaina opened her eyes. "Enough to foul the line all the way up the rise. More than a household. Less than an army. Does that satisfy your need for poetry?"

"No. But it sounds like Cyndia."

Cyndia snorted once.

Lyriel had gone a little farther ahead, bow lowered but ready. "There are clearer prints here, where the ditch kept the edge from drying. Toes dragging. Heels biting wrong. No weight shifts. They didn't walk like living people."

That settled what had already settled in Sylvanas's mind. The village dead had not vanished. They had moved north in a body. Toward farms that fed larger roads, toward larger roads that fed the passes, toward Quel'Thalas.

Behind them, Mira gave a thin cry. Neral spoke over it at once, not loud, just steady. Sylvanas turned. The child had seen the road.

"Take her back from it," she said.

Neral did, one arm around her shoulders, his head bent so she only had to hear him and not the world.

Jaina watched them and then said, very quietly, "You have enough now."

Sylvanas looked north another moment, measuring time in distances. The nearest mounted post. The border patrol lines. How fast a warning from one rider became ten. How many villages sat between this ditch and the first walls she was not willing to gamble.

"Yes," she said. "Now I do."

They used the square because it gave room for horses, sightlines, and no shelter for nonsense.

Sylvanas wrote the warning herself on a strip from her field packet, the script fast and hard enough to cut the page. Millhaven lost. Civilian dead mobile. Track confirmed northbound in significant number. Contamination saturates site and road. Quarantine villages on the line. Alert ranger posts and dispatch riders toward Silvermoon at once.

She sanded it, rolled it, sealed it with wax softened over Cyndia's travel lamp, and slid it into the narrow case Neral kept fixed behind his saddle.

"You ride north," she said. "Do not stop in any settlement longer than it takes to hand over a copy and demand fresh horses. If a gate captain argues, tell him I told him to choke on the seal and move anyway."

Neral's mouth twitched. "A direct quote?"

"Exact."

He sobered as he took the tube. "The girl?"

Mira stood beside his mare with both fists knotted in the blanket draped over the saddle. She had washed one side of her face. The other still held dirt in the crease under the eye. Cyndia had found her a heel of bread from somewhere. She was chewing because chewing gave the mouth work other than trembling.

"She goes with you," Sylvanas said. "One more rider."

The fourth ranger touched fist to breastplate at once.

"Get her ahead of the road as fast as you can. If she starts screaming at the sound of hooves, you let her scream."

"Yes, captain."

Mira looked from one armed stranger to another and landed, sensibly enough, on Neral. He crouched to her height.

"You'll ride in front of me," he said. "Can you hold a pommel?"

She nodded.

"Good. My mare bites men and never children. If you feel like falling, tell me first so I may object."

That got him the little broken hitch of breath again. Closer to a laugh this time.

Jaina had been standing a few paces off with her arms folded against the chill, gaze on the lane they had come in by. At Sylvanas's count of riders her head turned.

"One more rider north," she said. "That leaves four south."

"You can count. I had hopes you might manage it."

"I was checking whether your tactical brilliance extended to arithmetic."

Sylvanas looked at her. Jaina looked back, cold-eyed and tired and still carrying that washed-out cast the granary had left under her skin. Useful, then ruined by conversation. A pity.

"If the road ahead is as saturated as this one," Jaina said, "you'll want me."

"You'll do until I find a better mage."

"Comforting."

Lyriel came in from the south lane where she had been checking harness and perimeter. "Movement on the north road," she said. "One wagon. Slow."

Every hand went to a weapon. Sylvanas stepped into the center of the square and sighted down the lane.

The wagon appeared between the alder trunks a moment later: single horse, old bay, head hanging with the patience of creatures long accustomed to bad roads and worse masters. The wagon itself rode empty except for a rolled tarp, two spare sacks, and a shovel. The man driving it sat bundled in brown wool with a cap pulled low over white hair at the temples. He reined in the instant he saw armed strangers in his village.

His face changed by degrees. Surprise. Confusion. Then the flat shock of a man who had left home thinking of axle grease and supper and returned to find death had beaten him there.

"Stars preserve us," he said. His voice carried well enough. Country accent, age rough in it, no tremor of false cheer. "What happened here?"

Sylvanas did not lower the bow. "Name."

"Paret Dane." He looked from her to the houses, then to Mira by the horses, and all the color that age had left him went out of his face. "No."

That landed honest. Mira shrank behind Neral's leg and whispered, "Old Paret."

The man heard it. His grip on the reins failed for a second, then tightened until the leather creaked.

"Mira? Sweetheart, where's your mam?"

No one answered. Mira could not. Neral would not.

Paret shut his eyes once. Opened them old.

"I was south on the mill road," he said. "Two days' trade. Flour, salt, lamp oil. I came back empty because the fools cleaned me out at Harbrook." He swallowed. "What happened here?"

"Sickness," Jaina said before Sylvanas chose how much to give. "Then worse."

Paret looked at the houses again. Not performing grief. Counting it.

"We found tracks leading north," Sylvanas said. "Many of them. Tell me what settlements lie nearest on that road."

He answered at once, because locals always did when the question touched roads and stores and other things that kept them fed.

"Alder Run first, though that's hardly more than six farms and a shrine. Then Three Willows. Then the old relay post before the border lane turns east. If whatever took this place kept moving, it'll find folk all along that line."

"Any trouble reported before you left?"

"Coughing. Sick cattle. People arguing over spoiled sacks and whose fault it was." His brow drew in. "Nothing like this."

Nothing like this. Again, honest enough.

Sylvanas lowered the bow a finger's breadth, no more. "You sold grain here?"

"When they could pay." A bitter little shrug moved under the brown wool. "Less this month than usual. A younger trader was cutting under everybody from the south road. Didn't know his name. Red wagon once, plain boards the next time. Men change paint when debt gets ahead of them."

Jaina's gaze slid to Sylvanas, then away.

"He came through Millhaven often?"

"Often enough for the farmers to curse him." Paret rubbed a hand over his stubble. "Could've been nothing but cheap stock. Could've been theft. In a lean season, people don't ask much once the price turns kind."

Still not proof. Still no more than a thread. Sylvanas took it and left it unknotted.

"You're riding north?" she asked.

"Home's north." He glanced at Mira again. "Or was. Brother's place past Alder Run, if the road hasn't taken that too."

Neral said, "We're sending warning that way now."

"Good." Paret nodded too fast, trying to turn motion into action by force. "Good. I'll spread it where I can. Tell them not to let kin open the door after dark. Tell them not to go close if someone they buried comes scratching." His mouth worked once. "Tell them to burn what won't stay down."

Mira made a small ruined sound. Neral set a hand over her hair.

The old man heard that too and gentled his voice by instinct, not skill. "Easy, little pip. Easy. None of that for you now."

He looked at Sylvanas again. Really looked this time, at the armor and the ears and the hard set of the square.

"You'll be heading south, then?"

"Yes."

"There's still folk on that road. Scared, hungry, stupid in the usual ways. Not dead. Not yet." Paret gestured vaguely with the reins. "If you find them before this does, maybe you'll keep them from turning into another of these."

Useful. Practical. Precisely what a frightened local ought to say.

Sylvanas gave one short nod. "If you see any village standing quiet like this one, you do not enter. You turn around."

"At my age?" Paret said. "I turn around from chairs if they look unfriendly."

Dry amusement brushed her, brief and unwelcome. It did not last, but it existed.

"Go," she said.

He touched two fingers to the brim of his cap. "May the stars keep your road, captain."

The words were ordinary. The way he said them was not quite. Too soft on keep, perhaps. Too intimate for strangers. Or perhaps he had buried a daughter once and every farewell now came out touched in the same place.

Sylvanas watched him long enough to mark the wagon's line and the steadiness of the horse. Nothing in it tripped the part of her that hunted ambush. Only that faint hitch in the ears, a tug on a thread she could not see the far end of.

Not enough.

She let him pass.

By the time the wagon had gone from sight, Neral had Mira wrapped in his cloak and mounted before the fourth ranger swung up behind him. The child sat stiff with both hands locked around the pommel just as promised.

"Ride," Sylvanas said.

They did. Northbound at once, hooves striking the road that had already taken too much north. Mira never looked back. Neral did once, enough to catch Sylvanas's eye and touch two fingers to the warning tube at his shoulder. Then the rise took them.

Silence flowed back into the square after them, thinner now for lacking horses and people and one frightened child's breathing. It still sat wrong.

Four remained.

Sylvanas mounted and turned her mare south.

Jaina fell in beside her without invitation, staff across the saddle bow this time instead of upright in her hand. Cyndia and Lyriel took rear and flank by habit. The shape of the company changed with the missing weight at its back. Smaller. Sharper. Easier to hide, harder to hold if the road spat a hundred dead at them in the dark.

The village slid past one last time. Open door. Dead mill. Shirts on the line. A life interrupted badly enough that the whole place held itself around the missing end of it.

At the edge of the lane Jaina said, "You were right not to send the warning on guesswork."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the road.

"A rare honor," Jaina added. "Cherish it."

"Don't ruin the moment."

"Too late."

Yet the answer had less acid in it than before. Fatigue, perhaps. Or the simple relief of motion after that square.

They crossed the little bridge and took the south road under a sky gone the color of old steel. Behind them Millhaven stayed still. Ahead, the land opened in long fields already touched by evening, each fence line and low stand of trees another place death might have stopped to listen before moving on.

South, then. Toward the next village with its smoke still rising. Toward people not yet dead enough to walk.

Sylvanas set her heels to the mare and did not look back again.
