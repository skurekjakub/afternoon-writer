# Chapter 3: The Dead Village

The village was worse at street level.

From the ridge Sylvanas had read Millhaven as cold chimneys and silence, the kind of wrongness a field commander could price from a distance and decide how many riders it was worth. Down here the distance collapsed and the price kept climbing. A door stood open on the first house past the mill, swinging on good hinges in the wind off the river, and nobody had come to shut it. The stove inside was cold. A pot of something had gone to crust and pale mold on the iron plate, and flies worked the rim without urgency, their fat unhurried circling the loudest sound on the street.

She held at the threshold and listened.

Nothing. No dog behind a fence, no goat in a pen, no voice raised in a kitchen, no child calling through the dusk. The river slipped past the mill pilings with a thin secretive sound, water moving through a place that had stopped being worth passing through. Behind her the horses had their ears pinned flat. Even the Thalassian mounts, bred for the border and steady past blight-sign and old death in the wards, wanted nothing from this ground. The grey mare under Jaina sidled twice before Neral caught the bridle, and Jaina's staff crystal pulsed in the half-dark, unbidden and unwelcome.

"Sweep," Sylvanas said. "Pairs. Cyndia, take Lyriel. North side first, south after. Report what you find, not what it means."

Cyndia went at once, Lyriel half a step behind with her longbow strung and her pale eyes already cutting left toward the granary. They moved the way Farstriders moved through hostile ground: one forward, one covering, angles crossed, voices dropped to hand-signs when the distance between houses opened. The unnamed ranger held the road at the village entrance with her horse turned broadside, close enough to whistle if something moved on the approach.

"Neral, hold the mage's horse. She'll want to get down."

Neral caught the rein before Jaina could dismount the way Jaina always dismounted---too fast, one boot catching, the staff already flaring. The mage dropped to one knee in the road and pressed her free hand flat against packed dirt without waiting for the perimeter to close, the same gesture she had made at every stop since the ward-stones fell behind them.

The habit still grated, and Sylvanas let it. The mage knelt in exposed places because the readings mattered more to her than the angles, and two days of riding together had not changed that arithmetic. Useful did not mean careful.

She dismounted and walked the first house alone, leaving the mage on her knees in the road behind.

The kitchen told the quickest story. A half-eaten loaf sat on a board near the cold stove, the knife beside it, the cut surface furred with mold that said days rather than hours. A chair was pushed back at the angle of someone who had stood in the middle of a meal and not sat down again. Dust lay on surfaces that should have been touched today---the door handle, the water bucket's bail, the broom propped in its corner with a day's grit still on the bristles.

She moved through to the sleeping room. A straw pallet, blankets thrown aside, the body's impression still pressed into the ticking. On the floor beside the bed a child's wooden horse lay on its side, dropped from a hand that had not come back for it. The air was close and stale, carrying the chill of a house whose hearth had not been lit in days while the walls held every cold night that followed.

She came back into the lane as Cyndia's whistle carried from two streets north---the short rising note that meant all clear, nothing active. Lyriel's answer came a moment later from further on: same note, same nothing.

"Every house is the same," Cyndia said when she returned, her face holding the flat discipline of a ranger giving a report she did not like. "Meals left out. Beds slept in. Work abandoned in the middle of it. The blacksmith's forge has iron still in the tongs, cold. The tanner's yard has hides pegged and rotting on the frames. I counted fourteen houses open and not one body in any of them."

Fourteen households. The only living thing Sylvanas had found in any of them was a tabby cat on a windowsill of the third house, cleaning its paw with the bored precision of a creature that had outlasted the inconvenience of its owners. A dog's water dish sat full and scummed outside the cooper's workshop; the dog was not there and neither was the cooper. Whatever had emptied Millhaven had taken the people but not all of their animals, which meant the leaving had been slow enough for some creatures to scatter and fast enough that others had stayed to starve.

"Lyriel."

"Twenty-two dwellings on my side, including the mill house and the chandler's shop." Lyriel had slung her bow to report, which she only did when the immediate threat had passed. "Same picture. Life interrupted, not packed up. No bodies. No blood on any surface I checked. No sign of a fight that broke furniture or doors. The mill stores have grain sacked and tied for market, still waiting."

Thirty-six households and not a single corpse. A whole village emptied of its people the way a cup empties of water---from the inside out, leaving only the shape of what had been there.

Jaina was standing now, dirt ground into her palm, the staff pulsing low in her other hand. She had not moved from the road.

"The granary," she said. "That reads strongest."

"Show me," Sylvanas said.

The granary sat squat and solid behind the mill, river-stone foundations and timber frame, with a heavy plank door propped open by a grain sack that had split and spilled its load across the threshold. Inside, the air was thick---stale dust, dry rot, the flat sweet smell of grain gone past its season. Sacks lined the walls in disciplined rows. Nothing had been opened by looters or scattered by fighting. The stores sat exactly as they had been placed, waiting for a market day that had not come.

Jaina knelt in the grain spill by the door, set her staff against the wall, and laid both palms flat against the packed-earth floor. The crystal guttered and flared once. She held still long enough for Sylvanas to clear the room behind her---corners, the low loft, the narrow loading door at the back that opened over the river---before she spoke.

"The whole village is saturated. Well, soil, root cellar floors, the river trough by the blacksmith's. But this building reads three or four times stronger than anything else." Jaina's hand came up from the floor with dust on her fingers, and she turned her wrist, studying the grit the way a tracker studied a bent blade of grass. "The contamination is everywhere here. It has been for a while."

"How long."

"Days at the least. Possibly a week, possibly more---the readings layer on each other, and I cannot separate the oldest deposit from the newest without time I do not have." She looked up. The careful dumbed-down version she had used at the border post was gone from her voice; what remained was faster, harder-edged, and it trusted Sylvanas to follow. "The granary screams loudest. But I will not mistake the loudest layer for the whole answer. Something carried this in, and everything the village touched became part of it after. The grain stored here may have been the thickest single deposit, or it may have been one of a dozen sources that kept adding to the same well."

Sylvanas watched the way Jaina held herself back from the obvious call. The mage had evidence under her hands and she was refusing to close on it. On the farms, two days back, Sylvanas would have read that as academic hedging---the kind of caution that kept scholars arguing while the thing they studied spread. Here, surrounded by a village that had soaked in contamination bad enough to empty every house without leaving a body behind, the restraint looked less like hesitation and more like the discipline of a reader who had been wrong before and did not intend to be wrong about this.

"What else would carry it in," Sylvanas said.

"Water. Soil runoff. Anything the villagers drank, cooked with, or fed livestock. The well is contaminated. The river trough is worse than the well." Jaina stood, brushing grain dust from her knees. "This is not one source doing one thing. Everything in this village is doing the same thing at once, and the granary is just where it bunched thickest."

That was a different kind of reading than the mage had been giving on the road---the full thought, not the soldier-sized version Jaina had been building for an officer she assumed needed the smaller pieces. It was harder to dismiss when the woman delivering it was kneeling in contaminated grain and did not flinch.

Sylvanas turned from the granary and walked into the lane.

###

The ground behind the village told the part of the story that Jaina's magic could not.

She found it at the edge of the settlement where the lane turned to a muddy track heading north between garden plots. Trampled earth, not by livestock, not by cart wheels. The imprints were wrong. Too many feet in too narrow a lane, overlapping, dragging---the kind of churned ground that happened when a crowd moved through a space meant for one cart at a time. Sylvanas crouched and put her fingertips into the nearest print.

Shallow. Sliding. A foot dragged through soft mud rather than placed on it. The print was too wide for a running person and too narrow for a stumbling one---half walk, half lurch, the gait of a body that remembered the motion of legs without understanding why they moved.

She followed the track north twenty paces, reading where mud met firmer ground. The crust was broken along both sides, snapped and re-frozen in patches where bodies had stumbled off the track and come back to it. She tried to count prints and gave it up---the overlap was too dense for a clean number, but the sheer volume of displaced earth said dozens, not a handful.

Cyndia appeared beside her without needing to be called. She crouched lower, her braid swinging forward over one shoulder, reading the same dirt with the same frustration Sylvanas could feel tightening her own jaw.

"Sixty, maybe more," Cyndia said. "I can't separate them. Too many passes through the same ground, and scavengers have chewed the edges after---rats or dogs, I think." She traced a broken crust line with one finger. "Direction is mostly north, but past the garden wall the ground opens and the trail fans out. I cannot give you a line I would stake a warning on."

Sylvanas looked at the dirt under her hand. Sixty dead, at the least, walked out of this village. The ground said north. But north covered a lot of country, and a warning sent on bad direction was worse than no warning at all, because it pulled patrols to the wrong approach and left the real one open.

She needed either a witness or a cleaner line of spoor before she spent riders on a dispatch.

"Cyndia, keep working the track. Go as far as the road cut and mark anything that reads fresh." She wiped mud from her fingers against her thigh and stood. "We need something alive in this place that saw which way they went."

###

The cellar was under the fourth house from the river, behind a trapdoor half-hidden by a fallen shelf.

Lyriel found it. She had been walking the houses a second time---Sylvanas's order, now that the ground search had hit its limit and they were scouring for living people rather than evidence---when she stopped in the doorway of a house with a collapsed herb-drying rack and cocked her head. The rest of the party was fifty paces out by the road cut. Lyriel came back at a run that was almost silent, her long stride eating the distance between the houses and the lane, and her voice when she reached Sylvanas was stripped to the minimum.

"Something alive. Fourth house. Under the floor."

Sylvanas took the stairs first.

The trapdoor gave onto stone steps worn smooth by generations of boots carrying preserves and root vegetables into the dark. The smell hit before her eyes adjusted---damp, old urine, the sour reek of someone who had been too frightened to leave for days. She descended with her bow in her hand and an arrow nocked, because cellars in dead villages did not earn the benefit of doubt.

The girl was behind a barricade of flour sacks and a water barrel gone mostly dry, knees pulled to her chest, eyes so wide in the dark that the whites caught what thin light fell through the open trap above.

Small. Eight years old, perhaps nine. Hair matted with sweat, the blanket scrap in her fist clutched tight enough to turn her knuckles pale. She stared at Sylvanas and made no sound at all, which was worse than screaming, because it meant she had already spent her screams on the days before.

"Neral," Sylvanas said up the stairs. "Come down. Slowly."

She kept her distance from the barricade. A Farstrider in fitted armor with a bow in her hand was not the shape a terrified child needed first, and Sylvanas knew it from the other side of the equation---the same way she knew kill ranges and approach angles, from long practice reading what made living things hold still or bolt.

Neral descended the stone steps with her bow slung and her hands empty. She crouched at the foot of the stairs, not approaching the barricade, and waited until the girl's breathing had come down from the raw panicked rasp to a rhythm closer to human before she spoke.

"I know," Neral said. "It's dark and it smells bad and you've been down here too long." The voice was low and practical, stripped of the careful softness people used on children when they wanted to sound kind rather than be it. "My name is Neral. I'm a ranger. I have a horse with a terrible personality and a bag with clean water in it. Can you tell me your name?"

The girl's mouth opened and closed. Her fingers worked the blanket.

"Miri," she said, the word scraped raw at the edges.

Jaina was at the top of the stairs, silhouetted against grey sky, staff in hand. Sylvanas gave her a short nod, and the mage came down with the care of someone who understood that a cellar with a frightened child in it was not a place for a flaring crystal. She dimmed the staff with a twist of her wrist and knelt beside Neral---not closer to the girl than the ranger was, not farther.

"Miri," Jaina said. "Can I look at you? I won't touch anything."

The girl's eyes moved between them, measuring distances, tracking which way the stairs were. Then she held out her hand---the one not gripping the blanket---palm up, in the practiced gesture of someone who had been examined by a village healer before and knew the routine.

Jaina held her own hand an inch above the girl's outstretched skin. The staff pulsed once, low and dim.

"She's clean," Jaina said to Sylvanas. "Dehydrated, malnourished, bruised on her shins and forearms. But the plague did not reach her."

"The cellar."

"Sealed stone below the contamination layer, most likely. The water table may not have carried it this deep." Jaina withdrew her hand. "She was lucky."

Lucky was not the word Sylvanas would have chosen for a child who had survived by hiding under the floor while her village emptied above her. But the mage meant the body, not the rest, and the distinction mattered enough to let stand.

They brought Miri up into the last of the light. Neral wrapped her in a ranger's cloak and put a waterskin in her hands, correcting the girl's desperate gulping with the same steady authority she used on panicked horses---small firm pressure, not unkind, just clear about the pace. Miri drank in small sips, shaking, and would not let go of Neral's sleeve between them.

Sylvanas gave her time. Not much---a dead village with its population marching north did not leave room for much---but enough for the girl's hands to stop shaking on the waterskin and for her eyes to stop tracking every shadow as if the dead were still moving through it.

"Miri," she said, keeping her voice flat and carrying nothing but the question. "What happened here."

The girl's account came in pieces---not a story but images, disconnected and out of order, the way memory broke when the thing it held was too large for the sequence.

Sweetness first. That was what Miri remembered earliest: a sweetness on people's breath that turned up in the bread and the water and the porridge her mother made in the mornings. Not a bad sweetness. Just wrong, like honey put somewhere honey did not belong.

Then sickness. House by house, not all at once. The baker first, then the miller's family, then the tanner and his wife and two of their boys, then more, spreading the way a winter cold moved through a village except that it was not winter and the sick did not recover. They stopped eating. They stopped getting out of bed. The village healer made rounds until the healer stopped making them too.

"And then," Jaina said, pitched low and steady.

"They died." The blanket scrap tightened in Miri's fist. Her voice had gone thin and flat, pushed past the place where shaking could reach it. "And then they got up."

Silence held the lane. The river ran past the mill pilings, and nobody moved.

"My mother told me to go to the cellar," Miri said. "She told me take water and bread and not come out until someone came. She told me not to look." The girl's eyes went to the lane, to the north, to the dark space between the garden walls where the track disappeared. "I looked. Through the crack in the trapdoor. At night. They walked. The dead ones came out into the lane and walked toward the north road. Slow, like they had forgotten how legs worked. Some of them came back for the sick who hadn't died yet and carried them."

"North," Sylvanas said.

"The north road," Miri said again, with the certainty of a child who had watched through a crack in the floor while everything she knew emptied in one direction.

Jaina caught Sylvanas's eye. No warmth in the look---it carried coordinates. The girl's broken testimony and the churned ground outside said the same thing, and two kinds of evidence pointing the same direction was worth more than either on its own.

"The grain man," Miri said then, unprompted, in the way of memory dragged loose by the image before it. "Mama said there was a newer grain man on the road before it started. Not the one who came every season. A different one. She said the bread tasted different after."

One child's memory of one mother's complaint. It went into the pile with the rest---no louder, no quieter, and not yet heavy enough to bear weight.

"Neral," Sylvanas said. "Stay with her."

She walked to the north road cut, Cyndia already there with her cloth-flag markers driven into the mud, and Jaina following with her staff dimmed and her boots tracking through the same churned ground Sylvanas had been reading since they arrived.

The road cut north of the village was worse than the lane behind the houses. Wagon ruts from weeks of ordinary trade layered over the dead's passage. Hoof prints from draught horses and riders. Rain damage, two days' worth at least. Scavenger sign---fox, rat, crow---picking at whatever the dead had left behind. The trail was buried under everything a north road accumulated in a season.

Cyndia had staked her markers where she could isolate the dead from the living traffic: small torn-cloth flags on twigs, the field notation of a ranger who had been reading ground since Jaina was learning to hold a pen. Even so, her face held a frustration that mirrored Sylvanas's own.

"I can give you north-northeast past the second field wall," Cyndia said. "That's as clean as the ground gets."

"Jaina." Sylvanas turned to the mage. "Can you strip the contaminated sign from the ordinary traffic."

Jaina knelt at the verge where Cyndia's first marker stood. She planted the staff in the mud, spread both hands flat against the road surface, palms down, fingers wide. The crystal went dark. Her shoulders tightened, and sweat broke along her hairline despite the cold---the effort of holding the separation between contaminated resonance and ordinary dirt visible in the tendons standing out along her forearms.

Sylvanas watched the road while the mage worked. The light was gone now; the farmland northeast had settled into blocks of dark fallow field and hedge, the kind of open country where sixty walking dead could move at night and leave traces only a ranger would think to search for.

"Here," Jaina said. She did not look up. "This layer is all contaminated movement. The ordinary traffic does not carry the same signature. If I hold this, can you read only the ground that answers?"

Sylvanas crouched beside her, one knee in the mud, and put her own fingers into the earth Jaina had marked.

The difference was not magic. It was what thirty years of reading ground could do when someone else cleared the noise. The drag marks stood out beneath the wagon ruts---the shuffle-step of bodies that did not bend their knees correctly, the places where feet had caught on road crust and torn it rather than stepping over. With the ordinary traffic stripped away, the dead's passage lay open like a track in fresh snow.

North-northeast. Parallel grooves where feet had dragged. A rough column, not scattered milling---bodies moving with direction, shoulder to shoulder in a lane meant for carts, pressing toward the road that led to the Quel'Thalas border.

Sylvanas stood.

"Sixty at least," she said. "North-northeast, moving in a column rather than a rout. Not scattered. Not milling. They had a direction, and a force was keeping them in it."

Cyndia's marker count went from frustration to alarm. Her gaze moved from the cloth flags to Sylvanas's face, and the set of her jaw said she had already done the math: sixty bodies in column formation did not happen by accident, and a dead village's worth of walking corpses moving in a coordinated line toward the border meant someone or something was pulling the strings from a distance neither of them could see from a road cut in the dark.

"That is toward home," Cyndia said.

Toward the border. Toward the wards, the pass, the patrol lines that Sylvanas had ridden for decades before she had been given a title and the title had given her the authority to worry about things beyond her section of the wall. Toward everything behind them that still thought this was a contained problem on someone else's road.

Sylvanas looked at the ground one final time---the parallel grooves, the broken crust, the evidence of a dead village walking north in rough formation toward her country---and committed.

"Neral carries the warning," she said.

## III

She wrote the dispatch in the miller's house, using the flattest surface she could find---a bread board, wiped clean, angled toward the last grey light through the window. The ink was her own field kit: a small bottle of lampblack she carried in a belt pouch, with a steel nib that folded into its own cap. The message was short. Field dispatches did not explain themselves; they delivered coordinates, confirmed numbers, and stated the assessed threat in language a border captain could act on without waiting for permission.

*Millhaven emptied. Sixty-plus reanimated, moving NNE in column toward Thalassian border. Contamination confirmed in village water, soil, grain stores. No living population remaining. One survivor recovered, child, uncontaminated. Dispatch rider Neral carries this warning and the survivor north. Recommend border patrol double on the northeastern approach and Farstrider command be notified at earliest relay. Investigation continues south. ---Windrunner.*

She rolled the paper tight, sealed it with the wax stub from her kit, and pressed the seal with her thumb---no signet, just the flat oval of a ranger's field mark, which would tell the border post this came from the road and not from a desk.

Neral was outside with the horses, the girl already sitting wrapped in the ranger's cloak on a rock by the well. The unnamed fourth rider---a quiet Farstrider called Taelis who had spoken perhaps ten words since the border crossing---held both northbound horses steady while Neral checked cinches and stirrup lengths.

Sylvanas handed Neral the dispatch tube. "Straight north. Do not stop at any settlement between here and the border. If you meet a patrol, hand this off and keep riding with the girl until you reach a garrison. If you do not meet a patrol, ride until you do."

Neral took the tube, strapped it to the saddle ring without looking at it. She had done this before; the muscle memory was clean. "And the girl?"

"She stays with you until you reach someone who can keep her safe." Sylvanas paused, watching Neral settle the leather strap. "You were good with her. That is why you are carrying this and not Cyndia."

It was not sentiment. Neral was the strongest relay rider in the unit, and she had demonstrated in the cellar that the girl would hold still for her voice. A dispatch rider who also had to manage a terrified child on horseback needed both: the speed and the steadiness. The tactical logic was clean. That was the only kind Sylvanas trusted.

Neral pulled the strap tight and turned to the girl. "Miri. We're going to ride north now, to where there are walls and warm fires and people who are not dead. It will be a long ride and most of it will be boring. My horse pulls to the left when she's tired, so if you feel us drifting, that is her being lazy, not me being lost." She held out a hand. "Can you ride in front of me and hold the mane?"

Miri stared at the horse---a dark bay mare with a white blaze, standing patient while Taelis held the bridle. Her fingers worked the blanket scrap. Then she reached for Neral's hand and let herself be lifted into the saddle, settling in the space between the pommel and Neral's chest with the stiff-legged uncertainty of a child who had ridden farm ponies but never a ranger's mount.

Neral swung up behind her, adjusting the cloak so the girl was tucked against the cold, and gathered the reins. The bay shifted under the doubled weight and settled. Miri's hand found the mane and gripped. Neral covered the small fingers with her own for a moment---steadying, not comforting---and then both hands went to the reins and the horse stepped out.

"Ride well," Sylvanas said.

"Captain." Neral touched two fingers to her brow---the ranger's field salute, not the formal one---and turned the mare north.

Sylvanas watched them go: Neral's straight back, the girl's small shape bundled against it, Taelis flanking close on the off side. They moved at a working trot, not a gallop---Neral pacing for the distance, not the first mile. In two minutes they rounded the bend past the garden walls, and the road was empty again.

Four riders left. Sylvanas, Jaina, Cyndia, Lyriel. Two rangers and a mage, heading south into country that was getting worse.

She was turning back toward the horses when the wagon appeared on the north road.

It came around the same bend Neral had just disappeared beyond, rounding the curve at the slow, creaking pace of a draught horse with an empty load and nowhere particular to hurry. An open-bed farm wagon, the kind used for bulk grain delivery between villages---slatted sides, a canvas cover rolled back and lashed, the bed empty except for dust and chaff caught in the joints. An old man held the reins. Broad-shouldered, sun-darkened, with the kind of face that accumulated decades without acquiring distinction: heavy brow, grey stubble, a nose that had been broken once and set badly. He wore a trader's road coat, patched at the elbows, and a wide-brimmed hat pulled low against the evening air.

The horse saw their party before the man did. It slowed, ears forward, and the old trader pulled up with a creaking of axles and leather, squinting at the armed women standing in the middle of a village that should have had people in it.

"Evening," he said. His voice was pitched for road conversation---carrying, unhurried, the kind of voice that expected to be answered by farmers leaning on fence posts. He looked past Sylvanas at the dark houses, the empty lane, the well with nobody drawing from it. His expression shifted. Not alarm---too slow for that. Surprise, the heavy kind that settled into the lines around his mouth and stayed there, the face of a man encountering a thing he could not fit against the version of the world he had been carrying five minutes ago. "Millhaven's quiet tonight."

"Millhaven is empty," Sylvanas said.

The old man sat with that for a moment, his hands still on the reins, his eyes moving down the lane the way a man looked at a place he had visited before and found changed past recognition. The horse stamped once and blew through its nostrils.

"Empty," he repeated. Not a question. A word he was turning over, fitting against what he had expected to find. "I was here six weeks ago. Full market. Hanna---the baker---she bought two sacks of barley flour and complained about the price for twenty minutes." He shook his head, slow. "What happened?"

"Plague." The word was enough. Sylvanas did not elaborate; she did not owe a stranger on the road the details of what this village had become, and the less she said the more useful his own answers would be. "You trade grain on this road?"

"Thirty years, give or take." He pushed his hat back and rubbed his forehead with the heel of his hand, the gesture of a man readjusting to bad news. "Millhaven, Corin's Crossing, the farms between. I know every sack of flour and every bushel of seed barley between here and Andorhal." His gaze came back to Sylvanas, measuring the bow, the armor, the pointed ears. "You're Farstriders."

"We are." She did not confirm more than that. "The settlements south of here---what is their condition."

He thought about it, tilting his hat back to scratch the line it left across his forehead. Not the fast calculation of someone preparing a lie, but the slower work of a man pulling together road impressions that had not seemed important until right now. The kind of pause a local put between a question and an honest answer when the honest answer had to be assembled from weeks of small observations none of them had been labeled as important at the time. "Corin's Crossing had a cough going through when I came north, four days back. Nothing that shut the market. The farms between are trading normally---prices are high because some of the flour shipments have been short, but people are eating. I did not see anything like---" He gestured at the dark village with one thick hand. "Like this."

"Were you the only grain seller on this stretch?"

"I was for years. Lately there has been another---younger fellow, came up from the south, started making the same rounds maybe two months back." The old man shrugged. "I lost a few customers to him. Prices were lower. It happens."

One data point. It matched the girl's memory---a newer grain man on the road---and it came from a different source, which gave it weight without giving it shape. Sylvanas filed the information and moved on.

"The road south. Is it passable for mounted riders?"

"Clear to Corin's Crossing, last I saw. Bridge at the mill fork is sound. After that I could not say---I turned north at the fork." He gathered his reins. "If the plague did this, there are people south who do not know it is coming. You might tell them."

"We intend to."

He clicked his tongue at the horse, and the wagon creaked forward, angling past their horses in the narrow lane. As he passed, he tipped his hat---a road courtesy, automatic, the gesture of a man who had been tipping his hat at strangers for three decades and did not think about it anymore.

"Safe roads," he said. "What's left of them."

The words carried the weight of a man who had been saying the same farewell for thirty years and only now meant it differently.

Sylvanas watched the wagon roll north past the last houses. The grain dust in the slatted bed caught the fading light for a moment---fine, pale, the kind of residue that accumulated over years of hauling flour and seed in an open wagon. The horse leaned into the collar and the wheels found the ruts, and the whole assembly moved with the heavy, patient rhythm of a working animal that had made this trip a hundred times and would make it a hundred more.

She looked back once.

The old man sat the bench with his shoulders rounded against the evening chill, hat brim low, reins easy in his hands. He did not look back. He was already past the garden walls when the last of the light went, just a shape against the road---a grain seller heading north through a dead village he had not expected to find empty.

Nothing about it meant anything. A trader surprised by what happened to a village he knew. Road talk exchanged between strangers. His surprise had been the right shape, and his information about the road south matched what a local would carry. The newer grain seller was a thread---thin, and hanging loose, and not yet attached to anything heavy enough to pull.

Sylvanas let the glance go and turned south.

The horses were saddled and waiting. Cyndia had redistilled the saddlebags while Sylvanas dealt with the dispatch and the trader---water, grain, field rations split four ways instead of six, the math of a smaller party carrying the same distance. Lyriel had the rear position already, her bow cased but her eyes working the treeline south of the village with the automatic sweep of a ranger who did not need to be told the watch had changed.

Jaina mounted without being asked and without asking where they were going. She settled her staff across the saddle and looked south, toward the road that would carry them deeper into country where the plague was still spreading through villages that did not know it yet.

Four riders. The road south opening into darker farmland, fallow fields edged with bare hedgerows, the country flattening as it left the river valley behind and the last of Millhaven's cold chimneys disappeared into the trees. Somewhere ahead, Corin's Crossing was still trading, still sick and not yet dead, still misunderstanding the cough in its streets as ordinary winter illness. Behind them, Millhaven sat in its silence with empty houses and a granary full of contaminated grain and nobody left alive to eat it.

The dead had gone north. The living still had to go south.

Sylvanas put her heels to the horse and rode south.
