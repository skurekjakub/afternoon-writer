# Chapter 3: The Dead Village

From the ridge Sylvanas had read Millhaven as cold chimneys and silence, the kind of wrongness a field commander could price from a distance. Down here the price kept climbing. A door stood open on the first house past the mill, swinging on good hinges in the wind off the river. Inside, the stove was cold. A pot on the iron plate had gone to crust and pale mold, and flies worked the rim without hurry, the loudest sound on the street.

She held at the threshold and listened.

No dog behind a fence. No goat in a pen. No voice from a kitchen. No child in the dusk. The river slipped past the mill pilings with a thin secretive sound, water moving through a place that had stopped being worth passing through. Behind her the horses pinned their ears flat. Even the Thalassian mounts, steady past blight-sign and old death in the wards, wanted nothing from this ground. Jaina's mare sidled twice before Neral caught the bridle, and the crystal in Jaina's staff pulsed in the half-dark, unbidden and unwelcome.

"Sweep," Sylvanas said. "Pairs. Cyndia, take Lyriel. North side first, south after. Report what you find, not what it means."

Cyndia went at once, Lyriel half a step behind with her bow strung. They moved through the houses in Farstrider rhythm, one forward and one covering, angles crossing when the gaps between the buildings opened wide enough to make room for an arrow. The fourth rider held the road at the village entrance, horse turned broadside, ready to whistle if the approach changed.

"Neral, hold the mage's horse. She'll want to get down."

Neral caught the rein before Jaina could dismount the way she always did—too fast, one boot catching, the staff already flaring. The mage dropped to one knee in the road and pressed her free hand to the packed dirt without waiting for the perimeter to close, the same gesture she had made at every stop since the ward-stones fell behind them.

The habit still grated. Jaina knelt in exposed places because the reading mattered more to her than the angles, and two days of riding together had not changed that arithmetic. Useful and careful were separate facts.

Sylvanas took the first house alone.

The half-eaten loaf, the shoved-back chair, and the untouched dust fixed the timing at a glance. The loaf sat on a board near the stove, the cut side furred with mold thick enough for days of abandonment. Dust lay on surfaces that should have been touched that morning: the door handle, the water bucket's bail, the broom propped in its corner with grit still on the bristles.

She crossed into the sleeping room. A straw pallet. Blankets thrown aside. The body's impression still pressed into the ticking. A child's wooden horse lay on its side beside the bed, dropped from a hand that had never come back for it. The room held the stale chill of a hearth gone dark for days.

She came back into the lane as Cyndia's whistle carried from two streets north—the short rising note for all clear, nothing active. Lyriel answered a moment later from farther on: same note, same nothing.

"Every house is the same," Cyndia said when she returned. "Meals left out. Beds slept in. Work abandoned in the middle of it. The blacksmith's forge still has iron in the tongs, cold. The tanner's yard has hides pegged on the frames. I counted fourteen houses open and not one body in any of them."

The only living thing Sylvanas had found was a tabby cat on a windowsill, cleaning its paw while the village rotted around it. A dog's water dish sat full and scummed outside the cooper's workshop. The dog was gone. Whatever had emptied Millhaven had taken the people and left some of their animals behind. Some creatures had scattered before it finished; others had stayed to starve.

"Lyriel."

"Twenty-two dwellings on my side, including the mill house and the chandler's shop." Lyriel had slung her bow to report, which she only did when the threat had stepped back. "Same picture. Life interrupted. No bodies. No blood. No sign of a fight. The mill stores have grain sacked and tied for market, still waiting."

Thirty-six households and not a single corpse. The houses still held meals, tools, and beds; only the people were gone.

Jaina was standing now, dirt ground into her palm, the staff pulsing low in her other hand.

"The granary," she said. "That reads strongest."

"Show me."

The granary sat behind the mill on river-stone foundations, timber-framed, its heavy door propped open by a split grain sack. Inside, dust and dry rot sat in the air with the flat sweet smell of grain gone past its season. Sacks lined the walls in straight rows. Nothing had been torn open by looters or scattered by a fight. Everything sat where traders had left it for a market day that never came.

Jaina knelt in the spill by the door, set her staff against the wall, and laid both palms against the packed-earth floor. The crystal guttered once. She held there while Sylvanas cleared the room behind her—corners, loft, loading door over the river—then spoke.

"The whole village is soaked through. Well, soil, root cellar floors, the trough by the blacksmith's. This building reads three or four times stronger than anything else." Dust clung to her fingers when she lifted her hand. "It's been here a while."

"How long."

"Days. Maybe a week. Maybe more. The traces are stacked too tightly to split clean." She looked up. Jaina's answer came fast and clipped, stripped of the careful border-post phrasing. "The granary reads strongest. Grain stored here may be the thickest patch. It may be one of a dozen. Something brought this in. After that, people spread it through the whole village."

On the farms two days back, Sylvanas would have called that scholar's caution. Here, with rot in every house, it looked more like discipline. Jaina had evidence under her hands and still refused the easy answer.

"What else carries it in."

"Water. Runoff. Anything they drank, cooked with, or fed to livestock. The well is fouled. The river trough is worse. This was the whole village feeding it at once."

That was more than Jaina had been giving her on the road. It was harder to dismiss when it came from a woman kneeling in tainted grain and refusing her own easiest conclusion.

Sylvanas turned from the granary and walked back into the lane.

###

She found the rest behind the village, where the lane narrowed into a muddy track between garden plots. Too many feet had gone through a space built for one cart at a time. The earth was churned to paste. Sylvanas crouched and pressed her fingertips into the nearest print.

Shallow. Sliding. A foot dragged through soft mud instead of planting cleanly. The gait ran wrong—half walk, half lurch, legs moving by force of habit.

She followed the track north twenty paces, reading where mud met firmer ground. Crust had snapped along both sides where bodies wandered off the path and came back. Patches had frozen and broken again under the drag of too many heels. She tried to count and gave it up. The overlap buried the number, but Sylvanas could still read dozens in the churned prints.

Cyndia appeared beside her without being called and crouched lower over the same dirt.

"Sixty, maybe more," she said. "I can't separate them. Too many passes through the same ground, and scavengers have chewed the edges after. Rats or dogs, I think. Direction is mostly north, but past the garden wall the track fans out. I can't give you a line I'd stake a warning on."

Sixty dead, at the least, had walked out of Millhaven. North covered too much country for a bad dispatch.

She stayed crouched a moment longer with her hand in the mud. Cooling air and broken crust were already stripping the track away. A warning sent on bad direction was worse than none. It pulled riders to the wrong road and left the right one open.

She needed a witness or a cleaner spoor before she spent riders on a warning.

"Keep working the track," Sylvanas said. "Go as far as the road cut and mark anything fresh. We need someone alive who saw where they went."

###

The cellar was under the fourth house from the river, behind a trapdoor half-hidden by a fallen shelf.

Lyriel found it on the second pass through the houses, after the ground search had given them all it was going to give and the work had shifted from spoor to living bodies. She came back at a near-silent run and stopped beside Sylvanas with her voice pared down to what mattered.

"Someone alive. Fourth house. Under the floor."

Sylvanas took the stairs first.

Stone steps dropped into damp dark. The smell hit before the shapes did—old urine, stale fear, the sour reek of somebody too frightened to leave for days. She went down with bow in hand and an arrow nocked. Cellars in dead villages did not earn the benefit of doubt.

The girl was behind a barricade of flour sacks and a water barrel gone mostly dry, knees pulled to her chest, blanket scrap clenched in one fist. She made no sound at all. That was worse than screaming.

"Neral," Sylvanas said up the stairs. "Come down. Slowly."

She stayed back from the barricade. Armor and a drawn bow were the wrong first shape for a child already cornered. Sylvanas knew that from the same place she knew kill ranges and approach lines: long practice at reading what made living things hold still and what made them bolt.

Neral came down with her bow slung and her hands empty. She crouched at the foot of the steps and waited for the girl's breathing to come down from a raw rasp before she spoke.

"I know," Neral said. "It's dark and it stinks and you've been down here too long. My name is Neral. I'm a ranger. I have clean water and a horse with a terrible temper. What's your name?"

The girl's mouth worked once.

"Miri," she said.

Jaina stood at the top of the stairs with the staff dimmed low. Sylvanas gave her a short nod. The mage came down carefully and knelt beside Neral, no nearer to the girl than the ranger.

"Miri," Jaina said. "Can I look at you? I won't touch you."

The child measured the stairs, the trapdoor, the women between her and the way out. Then she held out her free hand, palm up, in the practiced motion of someone who had seen a village healer work.

Jaina held her own hand an inch above the skin. The crystal pulsed once.

"She's clean," Jaina said. "Dry, hungry, bruised. The plague never reached her."

"The cellar."

"Stone kept it below the taint, most likely. The water table may not have carried it this deep." Jaina drew her hand back. "She was lucky."

They brought Miri into the last of the light. Neral wrapped her in a ranger's cloak and put a waterskin in her hands, correcting her desperate gulping with the same firm touch she used on skittish horses. Sylvanas gave the girl a few minutes. Enough for the shaking in her hands to ease and for her breath to stop skittering after every shadow.

"Miri," she said. "What happened here."

Miri answered in scraps.

Sweetness first. That was what Miri remembered earliest—a sweetness on people's breath that turned up in the bread and the water and the morning porridge. Honey in the wrong place.

Then sickness. House by house. The baker first, then the miller's family, then the tanner and his wife and two of their boys, then more. People stopped eating. They stopped getting out of bed. The village healer made rounds until the healer stopped making them too.

"And then," Jaina said, low and steady.

"They died." The blanket scrap tightened in Miri's fist. "And then they got up."

No one in the lane moved.

"My mother told me to go to the cellar," Miri said. "She told me take water and bread and not come out until somebody came. She told me not to look." Her stare went north, to the dark gap between the garden walls. "I looked. Through the crack in the trapdoor. At night. They walked. The dead ones came out into the lane and walked toward the north road. Slow, like they had forgotten how legs worked. Some came back for the sick who hadn't died yet and carried them."

"North."

"The north road," Miri said again.

Miri's account and the churned ground pointed the same way. Jaina gave one short nod. Same conclusion.

"The grain man," Miri said then. "Mama said there was a newer grain man on the road before it started. A different one from the man who came every season. She said the bread tasted different after."

Sylvanas took it for a loose thread, nothing more yet.

"Neral. Stay with her."

She walked to the north road cut, Cyndia already there with cloth flags driven into the mud, and Jaina followed with her staff dimmed.

The road cut was worse than the lane behind the houses. Wagon ruts from weeks of trade lay over the dead's passage. Hoof prints. Rain damage. Fox, rat, crow. Wagon ruts, rain damage, and scavenger sign had buried what mattered under a season's traffic.

Cyndia's markers picked out the places where the dead could still be separated from the ordinary traffic: torn strips of cloth on twigs, the quick field notation of a ranger who had been reading ground since Jaina was learning to hold a pen.

"I can give you north-northeast past the second field wall," she said. "That's as clean as the ground gets."

"Jaina. Can you strip the tainted sign from the rest of it."

Jaina knelt at the verge where Cyndia's first marker stood. She planted the staff in the mud, spread both hands over the road, and the crystal went dark. Sweat broke along her hairline in the cold. The strain showed along her forearms, in the cords standing out under her skin as she held one layer of the road apart from another.

The light was nearly gone now. Beyond the cut, the land had flattened into dark fields and hedge lines, the kind of country where sixty walking dead could move for miles at night and leave nothing for anyone but a ranger to read in the morning.

"Here," she said without looking up. "This layer carries the taint. The rest doesn't. If I hold it, can you read only the ground that answers?"

Sylvanas crouched beside her and put her own fingers into the marked earth.

With the road's other scars pushed back, the drag marks stood out under the wagon ruts—the shuffle-step of bodies that did not bend their knees correctly, the places where feet had caught the crust and torn it instead of stepping over.

North-northeast. Parallel grooves. A rough column, shoulder to shoulder in a lane meant for carts, pressing toward the road that led to the Quel'Thalas border. With the ordinary traffic stripped back, the dead's passage lay clear in the earth. Jaina had cut the noise away for her.

Sylvanas stood.

"Sixty at least. North-northeast. Column, not rout. They had direction, and somebody was keeping them in it."

Cyndia's fingers closed around the nearest cloth marker.

"That is toward home," she said.

Toward the border. Toward the wards, the pass, the patrol lines. Cyndia did not say more. She did not have to. Sixty bodies moving in a column toward Quel'Thalas was warning enough.

Sylvanas looked once more at the broken crust and committed.

"Neral carries the warning."

## III

She wrote the dispatch in the miller's house on a bread board angled toward the last grey light. Ink from her field kit. Steel nib folded from its own cap. She kept the message short. Field dispatches were for coordinates, numbers, and threat. Anything else belonged to desks.

*Millhaven emptied. Sixty-plus reanimated, moving NNE in column toward Thalassian border. Contamination confirmed in village water, soil, grain stores. No living population remaining. One survivor recovered, child, uncontaminated. Dispatch rider Neral carries this warning and the survivor north. Recommend border patrol double on the northeastern approach and Farstrider command be notified at earliest relay. Investigation continues south. —Windrunner.*

She rolled the paper tight, sealed it with the wax stub from her kit, and pressed the seal flat with her thumb.

The flat oval of a field mark was enough to tell the next post this had come off the road instead of out of an office.

Outside, Miri sat wrapped in a ranger's cloak beside the well while Taelis held both northbound horses steady and Neral checked cinches.

Sylvanas handed over the dispatch tube. "Straight north. Do not stop at any settlement between here and the border. If you meet a patrol, hand this off and keep riding with the girl until you reach a garrison. If you do not meet a patrol, ride until you do."

Neral strapped the tube to the saddle ring. "And the girl?"

"She stays with you until you reach someone who can keep her safe. You were good with her. That's why you're carrying this and not Cyndia."

Neral nodded, turned to the girl, and held out a hand.

"Miri. We're riding north now, where there are walls and hot food and people who are still alive. It'll be a long ride and most of it will be boring. My horse pulls left when she's tired, so if we drift that's her being lazy, and I'm still on the road. Can you sit in front and hold the mane?"

Miri stared at the bay mare, then let Neral lift her into the saddle. Neral mounted behind her, settled the cloak around her shoulders, and gathered the reins. The child sat stiff at first, the way children did when they knew horses but not speed, and Neral covered her small fingers on the mane for one steadying beat before taking the reins again.

"Ride well," Sylvanas said.

"Captain." Neral touched two fingers to her brow and turned the mare north.

They rode north until the bend took them. Neral kept the mare to a working trot, pacing for distance instead of speed. Taelis stayed close on the off side. In two minutes the bend and the falling dark hid all three.

Four riders left. Sylvanas, Jaina, Cyndia, Lyriel. Two rangers and a mage heading south in failing light, with Corin's Crossing ahead and Millhaven at their backs.

She had just turned back toward the horses when a wagon came around the north bend at the slow creak of a draught horse with an empty load.

Open-bed farm wagon. Slatted sides. Canvas rolled back and lashed. Dust and chaff in the joints. An old man sat on the bench in a patched road coat and broad hat, broad-shouldered and sun-darkened, his nose broken once and set badly.

The horse saw them first and slowed. The trader pulled up and squinted at the armed women in the middle of a village that should have had people in it.

"Evening," he said. He looked past Sylvanas at the dark houses and the empty well. His mouth eased open a fraction. "Millhaven's quiet tonight."

"Millhaven is empty."

He sat with that, reins loose in his hands.

"Empty," he repeated. "I was here six weeks ago. Full market. Hanna the baker bought two sacks of barley flour and complained about the price for twenty minutes. What happened?"

His hands never left the reins. He kept searching the lane for the missing people.

"Plague." Sylvanas let the word stand between them. "You trade grain on this road?"

"Thirty years, give or take." He pushed his hat back with the heel of one hand. "Millhaven, Corin's Crossing, the farms between. I know every sack of flour and every bushel of seed barley between here and Andorhal." He took in her bow, her armor, her ears. "You're Farstriders."

That much rang true. A local grain man would know the baker by name, remember who haggled, know which road prices had pinched and which had not.

"We are. The settlements south of here—what condition are they in."

He took a moment over that, picking through what he'd seen on the road before he answered.

"Corin's Crossing had a cough going through when I came north four days back. Nothing that shut the market. The farms between are trading normally. Flour shipments have been short, so prices are high, but people are eating. I didn't see anything like this."

"Were you the only grain seller on this stretch?"

"For years. Lately there's been another. Younger fellow, came up from the south, started making the same rounds maybe two months back." One hand lifted off the reins in a brief, loose turn. "I lost a few customers to him. Prices were lower. It happens."

That matched Miri's memory closely enough to keep.

"The road south. Passable for mounted riders?"

"Clear to Corin's Crossing, last I saw. Bridge at the mill fork is sound. After that I couldn't say. I turned north at the fork." He gathered the reins. "If the plague did this, there are people south who don't know it's coming."

"We intend to tell them."

He clicked his tongue, and the wagon creaked forward through the lane.

"Safe roads," he said. "What's left of them."

The wagon rolled north past the last houses, pale grain dust in the bed catching the last light. The trader never looked back. The horse leaned into the collar with the patient rhythm of an animal that had made that road a hundred times and expected to make it a hundred more. On any other road, Sylvanas would have taken him for one more grain seller and kept riding.

She let the wagon go and turned south.

The horses were saddled and waiting. Cyndia had already broken the saddlebags down from six riders to four. Lyriel had the rear watch. Jaina mounted without asking where they were going and settled her staff across the saddle.

Four riders. Darker farmland ahead, fallow fields edged with bare hedgerows, the road flattening as it left the river valley. Corin's Crossing still trading, still sick and not yet dead. Behind them, Millhaven sat silent with empty houses and a granary full of tainted grain. Somewhere ahead, people in another market still took the cough in their streets for ordinary sickness and the grain on their tables for safe food.

Sylvanas put her heels to the horse and rode.
