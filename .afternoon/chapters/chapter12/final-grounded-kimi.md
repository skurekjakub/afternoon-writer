Chapter 12: The Necromancer
Kel'Thuzad's sanctum was an office buried at the bottom of a grave.

Jaina had braced herself for vulgarity. A throne of femurs. Black tallow candles. Some macabre theater. Instead, the room offered meticulously organized bookshelves crowded with leather-bound folios and glass jars. A broad oak desk sat under a slate chalkboard filmed with white dust. Bile-green fungi crept up the brass fittings of the shelves, washing the suspended organs and preserved humors in a drowned, sickly light. The air was frigid and dry, heavily salted to stave off decay, but underneath it lingered the rotting-sweet stench of embalming fluid that had followed her all the way down through Scholomance.

Archmage Antonidas's influence was on every surface.

Or rather, his academic habits were. Five-pointed margin stars denoting critical theorems. The cross-braced geometry of portal proofs. The distinct, hooked correction mark Antonidas used in red ink when a student reached the right mathematical answer using an inefficient spell matrix. Jaina had spent half her life arranging her thoughts to avoid that specific hook. Here, those same marks covered the deepest room in a necromancer's lair, drawn by the man who had mastered the Kirin Tor's lessons and decided they weren't enough.

At the threshold, Jandice Barov kept one hand on the iron door latch. Her posture was rigidly proud, and the flush of excitement still showed in the sharp angle of her jaw.

Kel'Thuzad turned a parchment page, pinned it down with a lead paperweight, and finally looked up.

He was gaunt, his skin the color of old parchment, and his grey hair was tied back severely. His robes still bore the violet violet-and-gold tailoring of the Kirin Tor, though the fabric had been dyed a suffocating black. If it weren't for the jars of human eyes behind him, he could have been any senior archmage reviewing a dissertation.

"Mistress Barov," he said, his voice crisp and entirely devoid of echo. "You have done enough."

Jandice's knuckles whitened on the latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore bypassed the upper abjuration sequences faster than I expected. She recognized the false ley-lines almost as soon as—"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad interrupted, locking his pale, unblinking eyes on Jaina. "I can see the frost burns on her cuffs. She bypassed the runes of misdirection entirely."

The eager flush drained from Jandice's face. She bowed stiffly, stepped back into the corridor, and pulled the door shut. The heavy stone clicked into place, and the violet wards etched into the floorboards hummed to life, sealing the room.

Kel'Thuzad folded his spindly hands on the desk. "You have tracked the plaguing mechanism a long way just to find the missing variable."

Jaina didn't move from the center of the room. She stood across from him at the long examination table that divided the space. A stick of chalk lay near her right hand. "I came because the infected corpses in Andorhal were completing a magical sequence my field diagnostics couldn't parse," she said, keeping her voice level. "Your plagued grain carries a necrotic signature. Every silo we tested flared with it. But the spell structure stalled before completion."

"Good." He exhaled the word like a sigh. "Then let us begin where your diagnostics failed."

He rose, his robes whispering against the stone, and stepped to the chalkboard. The chalk clicked sharply against the slate.

"The grain is a mere physical vector," he said. He drew a perfect circle, then nested a second one inside it. A containment rune. "You deduced that much."

"I deduced that the necrotic catalyst survives the heat of baking and the acid of the stomach because the enchantment is blunt and heavily shielded," Jaina countered. "I know repeated ingestion builds the toxicity. The host's nervous system is primed before their heart stops. What I couldn't isolate was the trigger."

Kel'Thuzad drew a second sequence of runes below the first—jagged, interconnected glyphs of binding and reanimation, each tucked precisely inside the last to form an infinite loop.

"The grain's outer shell is meant to be digested and discarded," he said, the chalk rasping loudly in the quiet room. "The true spell matrix doesn't begin until it reaches the bloodstream." He tapped the interlocking runes. "The body does not merely suffer from a toxin. The necrotic magic slowly rewrites the marrow. The stomach lining dissolves, forming a mana-receptive lattice. Repetition forces the victim's anatomy to adopt a new arcane architecture. Death does not interrupt the sequence, Jaina. The cessation of a heartbeat is what completes the circuit."

Jaina looked from the chalkboard to a jar on the shelf above it. A child's severed hand floated in yellow fluid, its fingertips black with frostbite.

"The corpse becomes the battery and the rune at the same time," she said softly.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head. "Precisely."

The missing pieces snapped into place. The localized outbreaks. The way the villagers' flesh had turned grey and sloughed off before the fever broke. Why her standard divination spells had only caught the surface-level poison, missing the deep structural rot.

She walked to the board. The cold radiating from the floor seeped through her boots. She picked up the chalk, feeling the dry dust coat her thumb, and drew a branching ley-line off his primary rune.

"The first resurrection is violent and uncoordinated," she said, tracing the chaotic output of the spell. "Because the body has absorbed enough ambient necromancy to animate, but the brain has rotted too much to retain the original consciousness."

"Just so."

"A village fed the grain dies within hours of each other," Kel'Thuzad added from behind her. "And a hundred corpses rising simultaneously do not form a mindless mob. They share the same arcane frequency. They rise with a singular, collective appetite. They form a rudimentary hive-mind—enough to obey a simple command from a local acolyte."

Jaina stopped drawing. She turned to face him, the chalk tight in her fist. "The acolytes in the farmlands aren't animating the dead from scratch."

"No."

"They're just turning the key."

"They are catching the output and giving it a target," he corrected gently.

She set the chalk down on the wooden ledge. "Then the people in those villages never had a chance. You weaponized their daily bread."

"I utilized the most efficient distribution network available to the crown."

The cold, bureaucratic detachment in his voice hit her harder than a physical blow. "You speak of slaughtering thousands as if it were a municipal logistics problem."

"Routine and infrastructure are the strongest forces available to any grand design. As a mage of Dalaran, you should respect the power of systemic replication." Kel'Thuzad left the chalkboard and returned to his desk, pulling a leather-bound folio toward him. The lecture was over; the defense of his thesis had begun. "But there is a distinction between the crude mechanics of the plague cauldrons and what I am perfecting here."

He opened the folio, revealing a sprawling, intricate lattice of spellwork. It wasn't the jagged, brute-force necromancy of the village grain. It was delicate. Hundreds of concentric circles bound around a single, anchor-point rune. It was a masterpiece of abjuration and soul-binding.

"The plagued villagers are cheap," Kel'Thuzad said, tracing the ink lines. "They retain basic motor function and base hunger. That is sufficient for shock troops. But true immortality requires a much finer structure."

Jaina leaned over the desk. She recognized the underlying theories—they were heavily modified versions of Dalaran's forbidden texts on soul-transference. "This is a phylactery matrix. You're building a stasis chamber for a consciousness."

"Preserved selfhood," Kel'Thuzad said, his eyes glittering. "Yes."

She straightened up, stepping back from the desk. "You engineered a kingdom-wide genocide to test the groundwork for your own ascension into undeath."

"Do not flatter my ego to the detriment of the facts," he said, steepling his fingers. "I built the plague apparatus because my patron required an army. My private interest—my reward—lies in taking the science of immortality further than the Kirin Tor's fragile morality permitted."

"Dalaran banned necromancy because it obliterates the boundary between a living soul and an object!" Jaina snapped, the anger finally breaking through her academic restraint. "Because ripping a spirit back into rotting meat is a violation of the natural order."

"The Kirin Tor banned it because they are cowards who fear the finality of the grave, yet lack the stomach to conquer it." Kel'Thuzad’s voice finally rose, carrying a sharp, biting edge. "They drew an arbitrary line at the soul and declared it sacred, ignoring the absolute truth that the soul is just another form of energy to be harnessed. Fear is not refutation, Jaina."

"Your 'truth' is currently dragging mothers out of their beds to be eaten by their own reanimated children!"

"A temporary and localized horror," he dismissed with a wave of his hand. "I did something certain. I proved the mechanics. You followed my plague all the way down into this crypt because you are a scholar, and you could not resist solving the equation."

"I crossed a continent to burn your equation to the ground."

"Direction matters, but the underlying compulsion is the same. We both outgrew Antonidas's timid curriculum."

Jaina’s hand dropped to the crystalline head of her staff. Frost began to aggregate in the air around the gemstone, dropping the room's temperature further. "Do not compare my pursuit of you to what you've done to Lordaeron."

"If you had brought another senior mage with you to Andorhal," Kel'Thuzad pressed, entirely ignoring her staff, "would you have uncovered the grain silos faster? Or would they have demanded you file a report with the Council of Six and wait three weeks for a committee to vote on whether to quarantine the crop?"

Jaina gritted her teeth. She thought of the frantic, encrypted sending-stone messages to Antonidas, the delays, the demands for further proof while the death toll climbed.

Kel'Thuzad saw the hesitation. "Human bureaucracy is always invoked the moment a disciplined mind threatens to upset the status quo."

"Humanity is the point," Jaina said, her voice dropping to a harsh whisper. "To anyone who has ever had to bury someone they loved."

The air in the room seemed to stagnate.

"Love," Kel'Thuzad said, the word sounding foreign in his mouth, "is an argument born of biological attachment. Attachment makes for terrible strategy."

He flipped to the final page of the folio. A massive, continent-spanning schematic. Lines of necrotic energy tracing down from the frozen north, converging on Lordaeron.

"You came looking for the mechanism of a local plague," he said. "Look at the scale."

Jaina stared at the sprawling map, her eyes tracking the ley-lines. They didn't originate in Scholomance. They flowed down from across the sea. "This is a conduit. You're drawing power from somewhere else."

"From a will in Northrend," Kel'Thuzad said softly. "Bound in ice. Vast. Capable of telepathic resonance that shatters the minds of lesser mages. I did not design the plague alone, Jaina. The patronage came first. The blueprint followed."

The word chilled her more than the frost on her staff. "Patronage."

"A power equal to the labor of dismantling a kingdom."

"Give me the name."

He met her eyes, utterly serene. "The Lich King."

Jaina swallowed hard. It wasn't a demon lord. It wasn't a rogue dragon. It was something new, something actively coordinating the slaughter from a throne of ice. "This is an invasion."

"The Scourge is merely the vanguard," Kel'Thuzad corrected. "The preparation. The true master of this design is older, and far less patient." He didn't wait for her to ask. "The Burning Legion."

Jaina froze. The demons of myth. The shadow that had nearly broken the world thousands of years ago. Plague villages, rotting grain, reanimated corpses—it was all just a localized distraction to soften the ground for a demonic landfall.

"Why are you telling me this?" she demanded, her knuckles aching as she gripped her staff. "Why hand me the architecture of your master's invasion?"

"Because you are out of time to stop it," Kel'Thuzad said flatly. "And because there is no strategic value in withholding the names once the threshold of inevitability has been crossed. Tell Antonidas. Tell the Council. Watch them drown in committees trying to classify the threat while the grain ships continue to sail."

Jaina looked down at the phylactery schematic on the desk. He had priced the death of Lordaeron into his calculations, and he had priced in his own death, too. He was going to let himself be killed, only to ride the arcane current back into a monstrous, immortal phylactery.

She hated the elegance of it. She hated that she understood the magical geometry of his treason perfectly.

Before she could form a response, the floor beneath her boots violently vibrated.

It wasn't a tremor. It was a sharp, localized disruption in the arcane wards.

Kel'Thuzad stopped mid-breath. His head snapped toward the heavy iron door.

Jaina felt the pulse a second later. A tripwire spell in the upper stairwell had just been shattered. Not bypassed—shattered by brute physical force. Three seconds later, a secondary abjuration alarm—a high-pitched whine meant to deafen intruders—was cut off with the distinct crack of splintering crystal.

Kel'Thuzad stood up, his robes settling around him. "Interesting."

Jaina tightened her grip on her staff. The rhythm of the ward-breaks was incredibly specific. It wasn't the slow, methodical dismantling of a Kirin Tor mage, nor the clumsy, thundering charge of a Paladin.

Crack. Snap. Thud. It was a hunter’s cadence. Arrow strikes shattering the glowing ward-stones on the walls. Light, rapid boot steps skipping entirely over the pressure plates on the floor. A half-second pause at the junction. Then the heavy, wet sound of an undead guard being pinned to the masonry by a black-fletched arrow.

Kel'Thuzad’s pale eyes narrowed. He looked away from the door and studied Jaina's face. "You recognize that approach."

She did. The sharp intake of breath gave her away.

She knew that terrifying, hyper-efficient aggression. She knew exactly what kind of mind was currently dissecting Scholomance’s defenses, pricing the angles, and leaving a trail of ruined wards and twice-dead corpses in her wake.

Above them, a heavy iron grate was kicked off its hinges, slamming onto the stone floor with a reverberating crash. Then, absolute silence.

Jaina felt a sudden, dizzying rush of relief, followed immediately by a cold spike of panic.

Ranger-General Sylvanas Windrunner had arrived.
