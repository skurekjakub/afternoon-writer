# Chapter 12: The Necromancer

Kel'Thuzad's sanctum was an office sunk under Caer Darrow's graves.

Jaina had expected vulgarity. A throne. Black candles. Some theater she could brace herself against and hate cleanly. Scholomance offered shelves instead, warped a little by crypt damp and crowded with folios, labeled jars, and brass instruments laid out with Dalaran neatness. A broad desk stood under chalk-filmed slate. Green luminescent fungi along the far wall washed brass and glass in drowned light. The air was cold and dry, salted with natron, embalmer's alkali, and the old preservative sting that had followed her down through the Barov crypt spine. Under it sat the sweeter rot that belonged only to necromancy.

Antonidas's hand was on every surface.

His habits, rather. Margin stars. Cross-braced proofs. The little hooked correction mark he used when a student reached the right answer by an ugly route. Jaina had spent half her life arranging thoughts for that red pen in the Violet Citadel. Here the same habits covered the deepest room in Scholomance, written by the man who had taken the same lessons and carried them past every point where Dalaran had told him to stop.

At the threshold, Jandice kept one reagent-marked hand on the iron door. Barov burgundy suited her badly. It made her look like a noblewoman at play until the excited tilt of her chin ruined the illusion.

Kel'Thuzad turned a page, set a bone-white paperweight on it, and raised his head.

Grey-haired, gaunt, scholar-thin. His robe still held the bones of Kirin Tor cut beneath cloth darkened almost to mourning. In another room he might have been any senior archmage working late over a troublesome proof.

"Mistress Barov," he said, "you have done enough."

Jandice's fingers tightened on the latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore followed the upper sequence farther than I expected. She caught the false rings almost as soon as the-"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad said, looking at Jaina at last. Pale eyes. Steady. Interested. "I can see that."

Warmth touched Jandice's face and vanished. She bowed and left.

The iron door shut with the thick sound of old vault metal seating into stone. The ward-lines under the flagstones hummed once, almost below hearing.

Kel'Thuzad folded his hands on the desk. "You have come a long way for the missing step."

Jaina stayed where Jandice had left her, seated across from him at the long table that cut the room in two. A stick of alchemical chalk lay near her right hand. White dust marked her thumb and the blue wool at her cuff.

"I came because the body was doing work my field reads could not finish," she said. "Your Barov grain carries the strongest signature because you want it to. Every site answered there first. Every site stalled there too."

"Good." Plain as breath. "Then begin there."

He rose and crossed to the nearest slate. Chalk clicked once.

"The grain is only the carrier," he said.

He drew a circle, then a second around it. Outer shell. False ring. He did not write the words. Antonidas had broken her of labeling what a competent reader could already see.

"You know that much," Jaina said.

"You know more than that much. You know the treated grain survives storage, ferrying, weather, even baking, because the preserving work is simple and brutal. You know repeated ingestion matters more than any single loaf. You know the host is altered before death. What you lacked was the final relation."

Jaina went still. Host. Altered. He used the words like fixtures in a room.

Kel'Thuzad laid a second chain below the first, deeper and tighter, each mark nested inside the next like a ward-line seeking its true lock.

"The outer shell teaches nothing," he said. "It only arrives intact. The lesson begins in the body."

Chalk rasped on slate. Prepared host. Repeated teaching. The body keeping the lesson. He said it all in the old Dalaran language of wards, sympathetic bindings, and prepared mediums, and Jaina could follow every step.

"Not poison," she said.

"No. Poison spends itself."

She answered before she could stop herself. "This settles. Waits. The body carries it farther than the bread can."

His mouth changed. Almost a smile.

"Exactly."

Heat climbed the back of her neck. She hated the flicker of pleasure his approval drew from her. Jandice had been fast. Kel'Thuzad was faster. He left half a theorem open and expected her to hate herself by finishing it.

He tapped the deeper chain with the chalk. "Your field work at the farmhouse was close. The body does not merely suffer contamination. It is taught by it. Repetition lays down an answerable shape. Death does not interrupt the sequence. Death releases it."

Jaina looked at the slate, then at the specimen jars on the shelf beyond him. A child's hand floated in clear preservative. Two eyes in another caught the fungus light like wet glass.

"The body becomes the answering chamber," she said quietly.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head.

The phrase completed too much at once. The bread. The villages south of Andorhal. Why the strongest readings had always led her to the grain and never yielded the whole thing.

She stood and crossed to the slate. Cold had settled into her knees during the descent through Scholomance. She took the chalk from the ledge and added a branching line under his.

"The first waking is crude," she said. "Because the body has been taught enough to answer, not enough to remain itself."

"Precisely."

She drew another branch.

"A village fed together dies together," Kel'Thuzad said behind her. "A village that dies together does not rise as a pile of accidents. It rises in relation. Appetite. Direction. Response. Enough to muster. Enough to become useful under a competent hand."

Her hand stayed raised over the slate.

"The acolytes in the villages," she said. "They do not create that from nothing."

"No."

"They catch it."

"And harden it."

She set the chalk down with great care. White dust clung in the cracks beside her nails.

"Then the dead in those villages never had a chance," she said.

Kel'Thuzad turned the chalk between his fingers. "The villages had many chances. They baked them."

The flatness of the answer struck harder than outrage. She turned back to him.

"You speak as though routine were consent."

"I speak because routine is the strongest force available to any large design. You of all people should respect repetition."

He set the chalk aside and moved back to the desk. The shift from slate to table changed the room with him. Proof done. Argument beginning.

"There is still a difference," she said. "What rises in a village and what you are doing here."

"At last."

He drew one folio nearer and opened it. Notes. Diagrams. A lattice of circles bound around a single central mark. Finer work than the village mechanisms. Costlier. Individualized. Her eye went at once to the preserving shell, the deeper brace, the place where memory would have to be held if anything crossing the threshold were meant to return as itself.

"Your village dead keep their function," he said. "Motion. Appetite. Rough answerability. Their pattern survives in outline because outline is enough for war. The finer work requires finer structure."

Jaina leaned over the page despite herself. Ink lines. Layered binding. Kel'Thuzad's hand over Antonidas's school.

"This is what the villages do not receive," she said.

"This is what the villages cannot afford."

The distinction opened under her feet. Low village dead. The rough answerables. And above them, a second aim.

"Preserved selfhood," she said.

Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened with pleasure. "There. Yes."

She stepped back from the desk.

"That is what you want for yourself."

"Of course."

Mild surprise that the point needed saying aloud.

"You built a kingdom-scale slaughter to prove a private theorem."

"No." He sat and steepled his fingers. "I built a kingdom-scale apparatus because the theorem was true whether anyone liked it or not. My private interest lies in carrying that truth farther than your teachers permitted."

"Dalaran banned necromancy," she said.

"Yes."

"Because it destroys the line between a body and a person."

"Yes."

"Because it collapses consent."

"Yes."

"Because it turns every hearth, every healer, every grave in Lordaeron into a point of violation."

Approval warmed his face again. "Much better. You have stopped objecting at the level of taste."

Anger hit clean and fast.

"Do not mistake clarity for agreement."

"I have never required agreement from a serious mind."

Jaina put both hands on the table. Cold wood. Smooth with use. The grain of it had been sealed against the damp that crept up from Darrowmere and the lower crypts.

"The fact that the theorem holds," she said, "does not absolve what it does."

"Absolve?" he repeated. "No. That is a priest's word. The work needs no absolution. It needs to be named."

"Naming it is not enough when the method consumes whole villages."

"And prohibition is not enough when the method remains sound."

He said it the way Antonidas might have opened a Council dispute, except Antonidas had always kept one hand on the room while Kel'Thuzad cared only for the line of proof.

"Your Council never disproved necromancy," he said. "It forbade it. There is a difference. The Council of Six found the place where valid method touched living society and became intolerable. Persons. Consent. Continuity of self. Civic trust. They were right to fear that line. Fear, however, is not refutation."

Jaina opened her mouth. Nothing useful arrived. Dalaran had not called the art nonsense. Dalaran had called it forbidden.

"That is your answer?" she said. "That because the logic survives the ban, the ban can be ignored?"

"No. My answer is that the ban names the cost more honestly than the scholars who still invoke it as proof the art itself was impossible."

"You think this makes you brave."

"No," Kel'Thuzad said. "Bravery is for men about to face the unknown. I did something certain and accepted the result."

"The result is famine, plague, and mothers dragged out of Eastweald cottages by what used to be their sons."

"Yes."

"You say that as though the word were sufficient."

"It is sufficient to name what occurred."

"You built an atrocity and then taught yourself to say it cleanly."

Kel'Thuzad tipped his head a fraction. "And your response has been what, exactly? You crossed ward-lines without sanction, pursued a plague no one had authorized you to pursue, and followed your private analysis into the Barovs' crypt school because you were certain the answer waited below."

"Do not compare us."

"I have no need to force the comparison. You made it on your own the moment you entered."

Jaina's hands curled at her sides. Chalk dust marked one cuff. Grain dust, road grit, Sylvanas's blood drying on her own fingers at the farmhouse ridge.

"I crossed a border to stop this."

"Yes."

"You crossed one to spread it."

"Direction matters. I have never denied that."

"Then what are you claiming?"

"Only this." He touched the desk once with two fingers, the way a lecturer marked the true line of a problem. "We were both made by the same institution. We were both taught to follow pattern where it led. We both found the world outside our studies too slow for the work at hand."

"You call your trespass duty because the object of your conviction flatters your ethics. I call mine scholarship because it flatters my honesty. The underlying habit is the same."

"No."

"You left a note in Dalaran and rode north alone because you trusted your reading over orders. Admirable. So did I, once."

"I did not build a machine for stripping personhood out of a kingdom."

"No. You are earlier in the chain."

She turned away from him before she could stop herself. The nearest shelf carried labeled boxes in a careful hand, each marked by subject and date. One still held Antonidas's old sigil for translational instability scratched into the corner and crossed out later, replaced by Kel'Thuzad's cleaner notation. Even here he had argued with Dalaran in the margins.

She put her palm against the shelf. Dry oak. Cool from the stone behind it. Solid.

"You are wrong."

"About your intentions? I do not doubt them. About your methods? I doubt them less with every minute."

"If I had had another trained mind with me in Andorhal," she said, "the work would have gone faster."

"Would it have gone differently?"

"Yes."

"Because that second mind would have introduced superior ethics? Or because it would have forced you to price time, risk, and consequence against your hunger to complete the pattern?"

The questions arrived in a voice that was not in the room. How long. How many. Which door. Who pays if you stay another minute.

When she opened her eyes, Kel'Thuzad was watching with fresh interest.

"There," he said. "Now you are asking the useful question."

"No," Jaina said. "Now I am remembering the human one."

"Human concerns are always invoked at the point where disciplined minds become inconvenient."

"Human concerns are the point."

"To governments. To priests. To frightened committees."

"To anyone who has ever buried someone they loved."

That reached him. The last warmth went out of him.

"Love is an argument from attachment," he said. "Attachment makes poor policy."

"And your policy is to make every body in Lordaeron answerable to an intelligence it never consented to serve."

"Your outrage remains ethical rather than analytical. I understand. It is the one ground on which the old ban can still stand."

"That ban stands because once your work touches actual bodies, the category changes. You stop moving dead flesh. You assault the self. You turn a person into an instrument without their leave."

"Only because you insist on treating the self as sacred at the point where it becomes worth studying."

"Worth studying." The phrase left iron in her mouth. "That is what villages are to you."

"The villages are proof," Kel'Thuzad said.

"Dalaran did not lose its nerve," she said. "It found the place where your method became a civic atrocity and named it correctly."

Kel'Thuzad nodded once, almost formal.

"That is the strongest version of the argument," he said. "Antonidas never put it so cleanly."

"Do not speak as though you knew him better than I do."

"I knew him before you had a staff to hold."

"He taught you the same discipline he taught me. The difference is that I accepted where it led."

"No." Jaina's voice came quieter now, but harder. "The difference is that he understood the cost where you chose not to."

Kel'Thuzad studied her. "Perhaps," he said. "And perhaps he understood the cost only because he lacked the will to bear it."

She went back to the table because standing still came too close to yielding. The chair scraped over flagstone when she pulled it. She sat.

Kel'Thuzad remained standing another beat, then took his own chair again.

He folded one page over another and exposed a new diagram. A wider structure. Multiple chains descending toward one marked point. Jaina saw the turn before he spoke.

"You came for mechanism," he said. "You have it. You came for scale as well, though you did not know that when you entered."

Jaina stared at the new diagram. The lines no longer described a village or a granary. They described supply, hierarchy, direction. Not plague as incident. Plague as campaign.

"Say what sits above you," she said.

"Not above me. Above the work."

He placed one fingertip on the uppermost mark in the chain.

"A will in Northrend," he said. "Bound. Vast. Learned in ways your libraries in the Violet Citadel only gesture toward. I did not derive this design alone, Jaina. No mortal mind in Lordaeron could have derived it alone. The patronage came first. The revisions after."

The word hit first.

"Patronage."

"What else should I call it? I was given a larger frame than Dalaran ever offered me. A mind capable of answering the questions your teachers banned at the point of discomfort. A power equal to the labor required."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the table edge.

"Its name."

He met her eyes.

"The Lich King."

The new name reordered the whole conversation. Patron. Northrend. A will in the ice farther north than the Kirin Tor had any business ignoring.

"King of what," she asked.

"Of the dead, eventually." The smallest shrug. "Titles arrive before they are fully earned."

Jaina did not smile.

"The plague is logistics," she said, more to herself than to him. "Preparation."

"Yes."

"For an invasion."

That earned another degree of warmth. "You are quick when you stop arguing for the room."

"The Scourge is preparation," he said. "The force above it is older and far less patient."

He did not wait for her to ask the second name.

"The Burning Legion."

Jaina sat very still.

Plague villages. Grain roads. The ferry to Caer Darrow. Scholomance. And behind them, farther north, a named intelligence feeding revisions down the chain into mortal hands.

"You expect me to accept that on your word."

"You have accepted harder things on my word in the last hour."

"Do not become careless enough to mistake me for your only source of truth," he said. "I offer you scale because scale is already bearing down on your province. Whether your institutions can digest the names in time is another matter."

Antonidas would hear the first name and understand too much. The Council would hear the second and slow to the crawl of classification. Either way, the grain roads would keep moving south.

"How much of this design is yours?" she asked.

"Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only honest one available. I built the mortal apparatus. Grain. Cult. Instruction. Deployment. I refined the host work because mortal scholarship is still useful when properly guided. Some edges belong to higher authorship."

Jaina thought of all the villages already buried under that answer.

"It interests anyone trying to stop you."

"Then stop the machine you can touch. Leave the cosmology for later."

She pushed back from the table and crossed to the shelves. Green bars from the fungus lay across the floor. At the far wall the stone carried a colder thrum. Old ward lines. Stronger. Deeper under the keep, Darrowmere pressing dark against the island's foundations.

"Why tell me any of this?" she asked.

"Because you came this far."

"That is vanity."

"Partly." He did not deny it. "Mostly economy. There is no point withholding names once pressure has reached the threshold where names become unavoidable."

"And if I carry them out?"

"You will carry out a truer understanding of your defeat."

He reached for the final folio on the desk and opened it with care that bordered on reverence. The diagram inside was smaller than the rest. Tighter. Personal. Its lines nested toward a single center and held there.

"There remains one distinction you have not fully accepted," he said.

Jaina came back to the desk in spite of herself.

"You mean the difference between your villagers and your own plans."

"Yes."

He touched the central mark.

"The village dead are rough continuities. Useful enough for the field. The self remains only in outline. For them, recurrence is low and cheap."

The word landed in her stomach with colder force than resurrection would have. Plainer. More worked through.

"For you," she said.

"For me, the preserving structure is made for one mind. Costly. Deliberate. Designed to hold memory, intellect, priority, will."

Jaina looked at the page and understood before she wanted to. He had already priced his own death.

"You think this solves mortality."

"It solves enough of it."

"Enough," she repeated.

"You still frame this as faith," he said. "It is not. I have no interest in crude village survival for myself. The rough answerables proved what they needed to prove. The finer question was always whether the self could be carried intact through a prepared threshold."

"And you intend to be your own proof."

"Every serious scholar should prefer first-hand verification where possible."

"You made villages into rehearsal for your private continuation."

"The villages proved one branch of the theorem. My own work proves another. Do not collapse scales that differ in purpose because outrage prefers the shorter line."

Jaina looked down at the folio and the understanding came over her in one clean, intolerable sweep.

Carrier. Host. Answering chamber. Low recurrence. High preservation.

For one heartbeat she followed it all the way through. The body taught to hold its own ending. The threshold approached and crossed. The self not extinguished, only transferred along a prepared line braced tighter than any ward she had ever laid in Dalaran.

The ward-line under the floor shivered with the chair's blow against stone.

Jaina jerked back from the desk so hard the chair struck behind her. Air hit her lungs too fast. The room swam once, sharply, then steadied.

"Do not," she said.

Kel'Thuzad did not move.

"I said no such thing."

"You did not need to."

Her voice sounded wrong in her own ears. Thin where she wanted steel.

"Understanding is not surrender," he said.

"No," Jaina said, and forced breath into a shape that held. "But it is enough to wound."

She understood the structure. She hated it. The hatred did not erase the understanding.

Jaina made herself sit because her legs had gone unreliable and she refused to let him see more than he already had.

"You said Antonidas lost his nerve," she said.

"I said he stopped where the work offended the room."

"He stopped where it touched bodies."

"As though bodies were not always where the work was heading."

"They were people."

"Yes." This time the answer came with unmistakable impatience, brief and real. "That is exactly what made the question worth asking."

There. At last. His composure slipped and showed appetite beneath.

"That is the line," she said.

"That is one line."

"It is the only one that matters."

Kel'Thuzad turned one page corner between his fingers. When he spoke again his voice had lost the last warmth entirely.

"You have a partner," he said. "A friction your mind did not produce. I had a Council. They preferred order to inquiry. The outcomes differ."

Jaina did not answer. No one here had cut through elegance to ask what it did in a village with ovens, barns, and actual dead.

"Do not romanticize your distinction from me," he said. "Understand it precisely. Intelligence answers whatever hand directs it. Method does the same. What governs either is relation. Attachment. The bonds that still pull at you. I cut mine elsewhere."

Jaina sat with that because refusing too quickly would have been cowardice. Acceptance never entered it. Her mind ran toward pattern by default. Mercy required someone else in the room.

Neither of them spoke. Wards cycled under stone. Beyond the wall, Darrowmere water pressed black against Caer Darrow's foundations.

Jaina looked at the unfinished notes near his elbow.

Her own copied pages from the farmhouse lay there too, where he had set them beside his work, her hand in the same stack as his. Oilcloth edges. Her cramped field script. The sight of them in this room fouled something in her more deeply than any specimen jar had.

"You kept those."

Kel'Thuzad followed her gaze. "They were worth keeping."

The compliment soured the air.

"If I leave here with this," she said, "and speak it exactly, no one in Dalaran will know what to do with the truth."

"That is very likely."

"Antonidas might."

"Antonidas will understand it. Understanding and action have never been identical."

She thought of the sending stone going thin and blue with distance. Antonidas's voice stripped of everything but urgency. The Council compressing horror into orderly speech. The roads south where Barov grain still moved whether any chamber believed in the names attached to it or not.

Then the thought turned toward the missing correction she had left outside this room. How long. How many. What does it do outside this room. Correction, not comfort.

She wiped the alchemical chalk from her fingers onto her sleeve. White dust against Dalaran blue.

Kel'Thuzad's head turned before hers did.

Small motion. Attention lifting.

The ward-lines answered a beat later.

The flagstone took the disturbance first. A ward-pulse. Then another, farther up the crypt spine. Scholomance's old protections took the descent in order. No fumbling at the locks. No clumsy arcane shove. Each runic threshold answered and handed the motion downward.

Kel'Thuzad stood.

"Interesting," he said.

Jaina was already on her feet.

The next ripple came clearer. Stone. Landing. Stair again. Whoever was coming knew how to clear hostile ground and kept their pace through it.

Kel'Thuzad looked not at the iron door but at her.

She understood why a moment later. The change had shown on her face before she could master it.

"Someone expected?" he asked.

Jaina did not answer.

Another ward-pulse. Closer now. The wards answered one after another to a body driving through them without waste. A pause at one choke point. Choice made. Then movement again, cleaner than before.

Kel'Thuzad's eyes narrowed. "You know the approach."

She did.

She knew that cadence. Pressure crossing defended space without waste, every angle priced on the way through. She knew what kind of mind had entered.

Above them, a heavy blow struck stone. Then silence. Then the next ward answered.

Relief nearly folded her knees. Dread came with it at once. Whoever entered next would see only the aftermath: the chair knocked back, chalk on her hands, Kel'Thuzad alive and calm at the center of the room like a lecturer interrupted between proofs.

Kel'Thuzad took one step toward the door and stopped. He was listening now in earnest.

"Remarkable," he said.

Jaina heard the next ripple and knew.

Sylvanas.
