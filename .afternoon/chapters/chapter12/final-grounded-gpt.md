# Chapter 12: The Necromancer

Kel'Thuzad's sanctum was an office at the bottom of Caer Darrow's grave.

Jaina had come below expecting vulgarity. A throne. Black candles guttering in skull sconces. Some obvious darkness a Dalaran-trained mind could brace against and reject on sight. The room gave her shelves instead, crowded with stitched folios and lead-sealed jars; a broad oak desk under chalk-filmed slate; brass instruments gone green at the edges where Scholomance's fungus-light never quite reached. Luminescent growth painted the stone in drowned green. The air had the old chemical sting of preserving salts worked into Barov crypt stone. Under it sat the sweeter rot that had followed her down through the academy.

Antonidas's hand was on every surface.

Not his hand. His schooling. Margin stars. Cross-braced proofs. The hooked correction mark he used when a student reached the right answer by an ugly route. The old Kirin Tor habit of tucking the refinement into the edge of the page instead of interrupting the main line. Jaina had spent half her life arranging thoughts for that red ink. Here the same habits crawled across the deepest room in Scholomance, written by the man who had taken Antonidas's lessons, kept the method, and discarded everything that had once made the method safe.

At the threshold Jandice Barov kept one reagent-marked hand on the latch. Barov burgundy at the sleeves. Dark hair pulled back too hard. Pride showing through the careful angle of her chin.

Kel'Thuzad turned a page, settled a paperweight over it, and raised his head.

Grey-haired. Gaunt. Scholar-thin. Pale eyes and a severe face cut by years spent at lecterns and worktables rather than war. His robe had been darkened almost past recognition, but the old Kirin Tor lines still showed in the collar and folded sleeves. In another room—some high archive chamber in the Violet Citadel, perhaps—he could have passed for any senior archmage working through supper.

"Mistress Barov," he said, "you have done enough."

Jandice's fingers tightened once on the latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore followed the upper sequence farther than I expected. She caught the false rings almost as soon as--"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad said, looking at Jaina at last.

Steady interest. No performance. No strain. Just the clear attention of a lecturer who had finally been given a student worth the chalk.

"I can see that."

Warmth brushed Jandice's face and vanished. She bowed and left.

The heavy door shut with a thick stone sound. Under the floor the sanctum wards answered once, almost below hearing, as if the whole buried school had acknowledged that the room now belonged to two people instead of three.

Kel'Thuzad folded his hands on the desk. "You have come a long way for the missing step."

Jaina stayed where Jandice had left her, seated across from him at the long table that divided the room. A stick of chalk lay near her right hand. White dust had already printed itself onto her thumb.

"I came because the body was doing work my field reads could not finish," she said. "Your grain carries the loudest signature because you built it to. Every site answered there first. Every site stalled there too."

"Good." Plain as breath. "Then begin there."

He rose and crossed to the nearest slate board. Chalk clicked once.

"The grain is only the carrier," he said.

He drew one circle, then another wrapped around it. Outer shell. False ring. He did not need to label either one. The structure said them cleanly enough.

"You know that much," Jaina said.

"You know more than that much. You know the treated flour survives wagon travel, silo storage, even the baker's oven, because the preserving work on the outer shell is simple and brutal. You know repeated ingestion matters more than any single loaf. You know the host is already being altered before death. What you lacked was the final relation."

Host. Altered. He set the words into the room as calmly as another mage might have said reagent or conduit.

Kel'Thuzad wrote a second chain below the first, deeper and tighter, each mark nested inside the next.

"The outer shell teaches nothing," he said. "It arrives intact. The lesson begins in the body."

Chalk rasped over slate. Prepared host. Repeated teaching. The body keeping the lesson inside itself until death gave the mechanism room to answer. He said it all in ward-language, in the grammar of bindings and prepared mediums and thresholds, and Jaina could follow every step because Antonidas had taught her to think in exactly those shapes.

"Not poison," she said.

"No. Poison spends itself."

She answered before she could stop herself. "This settles. Waits. The bread carries the shell. The body learns the rest."

His mouth changed. Not quite a smile. Something worse for how slight it was.

"Exactly."

Heat climbed the back of her neck. She hated the flicker of satisfaction his approval dragged out of her. Jandice had been fast. Kel'Thuzad was faster, and he knew exactly when she had reached the next line before he spoke it.

He tapped the deeper chain with the chalk. "Your farmhouse read was close. The host does not merely suffer contamination. It is taught by it. Repetition lays down an answerable shape. Death does not break the sequence. Death releases it."

Jaina looked past him to the shelf of specimen jars. A child's hand floating pale in preserving bath. Two eyes in another jar, turned cloudy as old pearls.

"The body becomes the answering chamber," she said.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head.

The phrase completed too much at once. The villages. The gut-first spread. The way the loudest grain signatures had kept failing to yield the whole lock. She stood and crossed to the board before the thought could do anything but move her. Cold had settled into her knees on the descent through the Barov crypts. She took the chalk from the ledge and added a branching line under his.

"The first waking is crude," she said. "Because the body has been taught enough to answer, not enough to remain itself."

"Precisely."

She drew the next branch.

"A village fed together dies together," Kel'Thuzad said behind her. "A village that dies together does not rise as a pile of accidents. It rises as a choir. Appetite. Direction. Rough response. Enough to muster. Enough to become useful under a competent hand."

Her hand stayed raised over the slate.

"The acolytes in the villages," she said. "They do not create that from nothing."

"No."

"They catch it."

"And harden it."

She set the chalk down with great care. White dust clung to the ridges of her fingertips.

"Then the dead in those villages never had a chance," she said.

Kel'Thuzad turned the chalk once between his fingers. "The villages had many chances. They baked them."

The flatness of the answer struck harder than outrage would have. She turned back to him.

"You speak as though routine were consent."

"I speak because routine is the strongest force available to any large design. You of all people should respect repetition."

He set the chalk aside and returned to the desk. The room changed with the movement. The proof had been laid down. What remained was argument.

"There is still a difference," she said. "Between what rises in a village and what you are doing here."

"At last."

He drew one folio closer and opened it. Notes. Diagrams. A lattice of circles cinched around one central mark. Finer work than the village mechanisms. Costlier. Individualized. Not the cheap broad teaching of the grain road, but a preserving structure made for one mind and one threshold.

"Your village dead keep function," he said. "Motion. Appetite. Rough answerability. Their pattern survives in outline because outline is enough for war. The finer work requires finer structure."

Jaina leaned over the page despite herself. Ink lines nested over corrections. Old Kirin Tor citation habits buried under newer, colder notation.

"This is what the villages do not receive," she said.

"This is what the villages cannot afford."

The distinction opened under her feet all at once. Low village dead. The rough answerables mustered by the thousand. And above them, another aim.

"Preserved selfhood," she said.

Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened with unmistakable pleasure. "There. Yes."

She stepped back from the desk.

"That is what you want for yourself."

"Of course."

Mild surprise that the point required speech.

"You built a kingdom-scale slaughter to prove a private theorem."

"No." He sat and steepled his fingers. "I built a kingdom-scale apparatus because the theorem was true whether anyone in Dalaran found it tasteful or not. My private interest lies in carrying that truth farther than your teachers permitted."

"Dalaran banned necromancy," she said.

"Yes."

"Because it destroys the line between a body and a person."

"Yes."

"Because it collapses consent."

"Yes."

"Because it turns every sickbed, healer's table, household oven, and grave into a point of violation."

Approval warmed his face again. "Much better. You have stopped objecting at the level of taste."

Anger hit clean and fast.

"Do not mistake clarity for agreement."

"I have never required agreement from a serious mind."

Jaina set both hands against the table. Cold wood. Smooth from use. The kind of desk that had held years of careful labor and exactly no hesitation.

"The fact that the theorem holds," she said, "does not absolve what it does."

"Absolve?" he repeated. "No. That is a priest's word. The work needs no absolution. It needs to be named."

"Naming it is not enough when the method eats whole villages."

"And prohibition is not enough when the method remains sound."

His voice never rose. That was part of the obscenity.

"Your Council never disproved necromancy. It forbade it. There is a difference. They found the place where valid method touched living society and became intolerable. Persons. Consent. Continuity of self. Civic trust. They were right to fear that line. Fear, however, is not refutation."

Jaina opened her mouth. Nothing useful came. Dalaran had never called the art nonsense. Dalaran had called it forbidden.

"That is your answer?" she said. "That because the logic survives the ban, the ban can be ignored?"

"No. My answer is that the ban names the cost more honestly than the scholars who still invoke it as proof the art itself was impossible."

"You think this makes you brave."

"No," Kel'Thuzad said. "Bravery is for men about to face the unknown. I did something certain and accepted the result."

"The result is famine, plague, and mothers dragged out of their own kitchens by what used to be their sons."

"Yes."

"You say that as though the word were sufficient."

"It is sufficient to name what occurred."

"You built an atrocity and then taught yourself to say it cleanly."

Kel'Thuzad tipped his head a fraction. "And your response has been what, exactly? You crossed borders without sanction, pursued a plague no one in the Kirin Tor had authorized you to pursue, and followed your private reading into this room because you were certain the answer waited below."

"Do not compare us."

"I have no need to force the comparison. You made it yourself the moment you entered."

Jaina's hands curled at her sides. Chalk dust marked one cuff. Roads. Village rooms. Flour on tables. Sylvanas's blood on her own fingers at the farmhouse ridge.

"I crossed a border to stop this."

"Yes."

"You crossed one to spread it."

"Direction matters. I have never denied that."

"Then what are you claiming?"

"Only this." He touched the desk once with two fingers, the way a lecturer marks the true line of a problem. "We were both made by the same institution. We were both taught to follow pattern where it led. We both found the world outside our studies too slow for the work at hand."

He held her with those pale, patient eyes.

"You call your trespass duty because the object of your conviction flatters your ethics. I call mine scholarship because it flatters my honesty. The underlying habit is the same."

"No."

"You left a note and rode north alone because you trusted your reading over orders. Admirable. So did I, once."

"I did not build a machine for stripping personhood out of a kingdom."

"No. You are earlier in the chain."

She turned away from him before she could stop herself. The nearest shelf carried labeled boxes in a careful working hand, subject and date on each spine. One still bore Antonidas's old sigil for translational instability scratched into the corner and crossed out later, replaced by a cleaner correction she recognized as Kel'Thuzad's.

She put her palm against the shelf. Dry oak. Cold through it. Solid enough to keep her body in the room.

"You are wrong."

"About your intentions? I do not doubt them. About your methods? I doubt them less with every minute."

"If I had had another trained mind with me in Andorhal," she said, "the work would have gone faster."

"Would it have gone differently?"

"Yes."

"Because that second mind would have supplied superior ethics? Or because it would have forced you to price time, risk, and consequence against your hunger to complete the pattern?"

The questions landed in a voice that was not in the room.

How long. How many. Which door. Who pays if you stay another minute.

When Jaina opened her eyes, Kel'Thuzad was watching with fresh interest.

"There," he said. "Now you are asking the useful question."

"No." Her voice came quiet and hard. "Now I am remembering the human one."

"Human concerns are always invoked at the point where disciplined minds become inconvenient."

"Human concerns are the point."

"To governments. To priests. To frightened committees."

"To anyone who has ever buried someone they loved."

That reached him. The last warmth went out of him.

"Love is an argument from attachment," he said. "Attachment makes poor policy."

"And your policy is to make every body in Lordaeron answerable to an intelligence it never consented to serve."

"Your outrage remains ethical rather than analytical. I understand. It is the one ground on which the old ban can still stand."

"That ban stands because once your work touches actual bodies, the category changes. You stop moving dead flesh. You assault the self. You turn a person into an instrument without their leave."

"Only because you insist on treating the self as sacred at the point where it becomes worth studying."

"Worth studying." Iron filled her mouth. "That is what villages are to you."

"The villages are proof," Kel'Thuzad said.

"Dalaran did not lose its nerve. It found the place where your method became a civic atrocity and named it correctly."

Kel'Thuzad nodded once, almost formal. "That is the strongest version of the argument. Antonidas never put it so cleanly."

"Do not speak as though you knew him better than I do."

"I knew him before you had a staff to hold."

"And he taught you the same discipline he taught me. The difference is that I accepted where it led."

"No." Jaina's voice was quieter now, but harder. "The difference is that he understood the cost where you chose not to."

Kel'Thuzad studied her. "Perhaps," he said. "And perhaps he understood the cost only because he lacked the will to bear it."

She returned to the table because standing still came too close to yielding. The chair scraped harshly across the stone when she pulled it back. She sat. Kel'Thuzad remained standing for one measured beat more, then took his own chair and turned a page.

A new diagram. Wider. Multiple chains descending toward one marked point. Jaina saw the turn before he spoke.

"You came for mechanism," he said. "You have it. You came for scale as well, though you did not know that when you entered."

Jaina stared at the page.

"Say what sits above you," she said.

"Not above me. Above the work."

He set one fingertip on the uppermost mark in the chain.

"A will in Northrend," he said. "Bound. Vast. Educated in ways your libraries only gesture toward. I did not derive this design alone, Jaina. No mortal mind in Lordaeron could have derived it alone. The patronage came first. The revisions after."

The word struck first.

"Patronage."

"What else should I call it? I was offered a larger frame than the Kirin Tor ever permitted me. A mind capable of answering the questions your teachers banned at the point of discomfort. A power equal to the labor required."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the table edge.

"Its name."

He met her eyes.

"The Lich King."

The name reordered the whole room. Patron. Northrend. The voice behind the grain roads and the hidden school beneath Caer Darrow.

"King of what?" she asked.

"Of the dead, eventually." The smallest shrug. "Titles have a habit of arriving before they are fully earned."

Jaina did not smile.

"The plague is logistics," she said, more to herself than to him. "Preparation."

"Yes."

"For an invasion."

That earned another dangerous degree of warmth. "You are quick when you stop arguing for the room."

"The Scourge is preparation," he said. "The force above it is older and far less patient."

He did not wait for her to ask the second name.

"The Burning Legion."

Jaina sat very still.

Plague villages. Grain roads. Scholomance. And behind them, farther north across the sea, a named intelligence feeding revisions into the work. Another chain above that. Scale stepping up faster than any council chamber would be able to swallow it.

"You expect me to accept that on your word."

"You have accepted harder things on my word in the last hour."

He leaned back a fraction.

"Do not become careless enough to mistake me for your only source of truth. I offer you scale because scale is already descending on your kingdom. Whether your institutions can digest the names in time is another matter."

Antonidas would hear the first name and understand too much at once. The Council would hear the second and slow to the crawl of classification. Either way the grain wagons would keep rolling.

"How much of this design is yours?" she asked.

"Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only honest one available. I built the mortal apparatus. Grain. Cult. Instruction. Deployment. I refined the host work because mortal scholarship remains useful when properly guided. Some edges belong to higher authorship."

Jaina thought of the villages already buried under that answer.

"It matters."

"It interests anyone trying to stop me?"

"It interests anyone trying to stop you."

"Then stop the machine you can touch. Leave the cosmology for later."

She pushed back from the table and crossed to the shelves again. Green bars from the fungi cut across the floor. At the far wall the stone carried a colder thrum where old ward lines ran through the foundation.

"Why tell me any of this?" she asked.

"Because you came this far."

"That is vanity."

"Partly." He did not insult them both by denying it. "Mostly economy. There is no point withholding names once pressure has reached the threshold where names become unavoidable."

"And if I carry them out?"

"You will carry out a truer understanding of your defeat."

He reached for the last folio on the desk and opened it with a care that bordered on reverence. The diagram inside was smaller than the others. Tighter. Personal. Its lines did not spread outward into village bodies or city networks. They wound inward around one preserved center and held there. Not a mass teaching. A mnemonic scaffold. A private recurrence built for one mind.

"There remains one distinction you have not fully accepted," he said.

Jaina came back to the desk in spite of herself.

"You mean the difference between your villagers and your own plans."

"Yes."

He touched the central mark.

"The village dead are rough continuities. Useful enough for the field. The self remains only in outline. For them, recurrence is low and cheap."

The word landed in her stomach with a colder force than resurrection would have. Plainer. More worked through. More honest.

"For you," she said.

"For me, the preserving structure is made for one mind. Costly. Deliberate. Designed to hold memory, intellect, priority, will."

Jaina looked at the page and understood before she wanted to. He had already priced his own death and found it acceptable.

"You think this solves mortality."

"It solves enough of it."

"Enough," she repeated.

"You still frame this as faith," he said. "It is not. I have no interest in crude village survival for myself. The rough answerables proved what they needed to prove. The finer question was always whether the self could be carried intact through a prepared threshold."

"And you intend to be your own proof."

"Every serious scholar should prefer first-hand verification where possible."

"You turned villages into rehearsal for your private continuation."

"The villages proved one branch of the theorem. My own work proves another. Do not collapse scales that differ in purpose because outrage prefers the shorter line."

Jaina looked down at the folio and the understanding came over her in one clean, unbearable sweep.

Outer shell. Prepared host. Answering chamber. Low recurrence. High preservation.

For one heartbeat she followed it all the way through. The body taught to hold its ending. The threshold approached and crossed. The self not extinguished, only transferred along a line laid down in advance by deliberate necromantic labor. Not village mustering. Not hunger with a handler at the other end. A mind built to wake and still call itself the same mind.

The chair struck stone behind her hard enough to jar the ward line under the floor.

Jaina jerked back from the desk. Air hit her lungs too fast. The room swam once, sharply, then steadied.

"Do not," she said.

Kel'Thuzad did not move. "I said no such thing."

"You did not need to."

Her voice sounded wrong in her own ears. Too thin. Too near breaking.

"Understanding is not surrender," he said.

"No," Jaina said, and forced breath into something that held. "But it is enough to wound."

She understood the structure. She hated it. Hatred did not erase the understanding.

Jaina made herself sit because her legs had gone unreliable and she refused to let him see more than he already had.

"You said Antonidas lost his nerve," she said.

"I said he stopped where the work offended the room."

"He stopped where it touched bodies."

"As though bodies were not always where the work was heading."

"They were people."

"Yes." This time the answer came with real impatience, brief and sharp. "That is exactly what made the question worth asking."

There. At last. The composure slipped just enough to show the appetite underneath.

"That is the line," she said.

"That is one line."

"It is the only one that matters."

Kel'Thuzad turned one page corner between his fingers. When he spoke again the last warmth had gone entirely.

"You have a partner," he said. "A friction your mind did not produce. I had a Council. They preferred order to inquiry. The outcomes differ."

Jaina did not answer. No one in this room had cut through elegance to ask what the theorem did in a village with ovens and actual dead. Sylvanas would have. Sylvanas had, without ever seeing this desk or these folios or the pale pleasure on Kel'Thuzad's face when somebody kept pace with him.

"Do not romanticize your distinction from me," he said. "Understand it precisely. Intelligence answers whatever hand directs it. Method does the same. What governs either is relation. Attachment. The bonds that still pull at you. I cut mine elsewhere."

Jaina sat with that because refusal, too fast, would have been cowardice. Acceptance never came near it. Her mind ran toward pattern by default. Mercy had never been its natural correction. Mercy had come in boots and leather, asking the questions she hated most at exactly the right time.

Neither of them spoke. Wards cycled under the stone. Beyond the wall, Darrowmere water pressed at the island foundations and withdrew again.

Jaina's gaze fell to the unfinished notes near his elbow.

Her own copied pages from the farmhouse lay there as well, where he had set them beside his work. Oilcloth edges. Her cramped field script. Her hand in the same stack as his.

That sight recoiled her harder than any specimen jar had.

"You kept those."

Kel'Thuzad followed her gaze. "They were worth keeping."

The compliment fouled the air.

"If I leave here with this," she said, "and speak it exactly, no one in Dalaran will know what to do with the truth."

"That is very likely."

"Antonidas might."

"Antonidas will understand it. Understanding and action have never been identical."

She thought of the sending stone in her pocket, thin and cold. Antonidas's voice stripped down to urgency. The Council of Six compressing horror into orderly speech. The safe high rooms of the Violet Citadel standing far above the problem while grain still moved through Andorhal and down every road that fed Lordaeron.

Then the thought turned, as it had to, toward the correction she had left outside this room.

How long. How many. Which door. Who pays if you stay another minute.

Correction, not comfort.

She wiped the chalk from her fingers onto her sleeve. White streaks across blue.

Kel'Thuzad's head turned before hers did.

A small motion. Attention lifting.

The wards answered a beat later.

The floor took the disturbance first. One pulse. Then another, farther out. Scholomance's old protections registered the descent in order. No fumbling at the locks. No brute-force shove of magic against stone. Threshold after threshold answered and handed the motion downward through the buried school.

Kel'Thuzad stood.

"Interesting," he said.

Jaina was already on her feet.

The next ripple came cleaner. Stair. Landing. Stair again. Whoever was coming knew how to move through hostile ground and kept their pace through it.

Kel'Thuzad looked not at the door but at her.

She understood why a heartbeat later. The recognition had shown on her face before she could master it.

"Someone expected?" he asked.

Jaina did not answer.

Another pulse. Closer now. The wards passed it on one after another to a body driving through them without waste. A pause at one choke point. Choice made. Then movement again, cleaner than before.

Kel'Thuzad's eyes narrowed. "You know the approach."

She did.

She knew that shape. Pressure crossing defended space without waste, every angle priced on the way through. Not Jandice's curated descent. Not a Cult acolyte blundering past locks she barely understood. This was fieldcraft forced underground. This was somebody turning a buried school into ground that could be cleared.

Above them, a heavy blow struck stone. Then silence. Then the next ward answered.

Relief nearly folded her knees. Dread came with it at once. Whoever came through that door next would see only the aftermath: the chair knocked back, chalk on Jaina's hands, Kel'Thuzad alive and calm at the center of the room.

Kel'Thuzad took one step toward the door and stopped. He was listening now in earnest.

"Remarkable," he said.

Jaina heard the next ripple and knew.

Sylvanas.
