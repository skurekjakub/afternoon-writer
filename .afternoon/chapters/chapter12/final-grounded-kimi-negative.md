Kel'Thuzad's sanctum was an office at the bottom of a grave.

Jaina had expected vulgarity. A bone throne. Black candles. Some theater to brace against. The crypt offered cedar shelves instead, crowded with folios and brine-filled jars, a broad mahogany desk under chalk-filmed slate, and crypt-moss washing brass and glass in drowned green light. The air was cold and dry, salted against decay. Under that sat the sweeter smell of embalming fluid that had followed her down through the Barov family vaults.

Antonidas's hand was on every surface.

His habits, rather. Margin stars. Cross-braced theorems. The little hooked correction mark he used when an apprentice reached the right answer by an ugly route. Jaina had spent half her life arranging thoughts for that red pen. Here those marks covered the deepest reliquary in Scholomance, written by the man who had taken the same Kirin Tor lessons and kept going.

At the threshold, Jandice kept one hand on the iron-banded oak. Proud. Careful. Her excitement still showed in the angle of her chin.

Kel'Thuzad turned a vellum page, set a skull-weight on it, and raised his head.

Grey-haired, gaunt, scholar-thin. His violet robe still carried the Violet Citadel cut under darker wool. In another tower he might have been any senior archmage at work.

"Mistress Barov," he said, "you have done enough."

Jandice's fingers tightened on the iron latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore followed the upper abjurations farther than I expected. She caught the false leylines almost as soon as--"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad said, looking at Jaina at last. Pale eyes. Steady. Interested. "I can see that."

Warmth touched Jandice's face and vanished. She bowed and left.

The door shut with a thick granite scrape. The blood-wards under the flagstones hummed once, almost below hearing.

Kel'Thuzad folded his hands on the mahogany. "You have come a long way for the missing step."

Jaina stayed where Jandice had left her, seated across from him at the long table that cut the crypt in two. A stick of chalk lay near her right hand. White dust marked her thumb.

"I came because the corpses were doing work my divinations could not finish," she said. "Your Andorhal wheat carries the strongest necrotic signature because you want it to. Every farm answered there first. Every farm stalled there too."

"Good." Plain as breath. "Then begin there."

He rose and crossed to the nearest board. Chalk clicked once against slate.

"The wheat is only the carrier," he said.

He drew a circle, then a second around it. Outer shell. False ring. No need to write the runes. The geometry said them.

"You know that much," Jaina said.

"You know more than that much. You know the blighted grain survives silos, wagon-ruts, even millstones, because the preserving spellcraft is simple and brutal. You know repeated meals matter more than any single loaf. You know the peasant is altered before death. What you lacked was the final binding."

Jaina went still. Peasant. Altered. He used the words like furniture.

Kel'Thuzad wrote a second rune-chain below the first, deeper and tighter, each sigil tucked inside the next.

"The outer shell teaches nothing," he said. "It only arrives intact. The lesson begins in the marrow."

Chalk rasped on slate. Prepared host. Repeated saturation. The marrow keeping the magic. He said it all in the syntax of soul-bindings and prepared phylacteries, and Jaina could follow every vector.

"Not poison," she said.

"No. Poison spends itself."

She answered before she could stop herself. "This anchors. Waits. The veins carry it farther than the flour can."

His mouth changed. Almost a smile.

"Exactly."

Heat climbed the back of her neck. She hated the flicker of pleasure his Kirin Tor approval drew from her. Jandice had been fast. Kel'Thuzad was faster.

He tapped the deeper runic-chain with the chalk. "Your field divinations at the farmhouse were close. The peasant does not merely suffer infection. He is attuned by it. Repetition lays down an answerable matrix. Death does not interrupt the anchoring. Death activates it."

Jaina looked at the slate, then at the specimen jars on the shelf beyond him. A child's hand floating in brine. Two preserved eyes in another.

"The corpse becomes the summoning circle," she said quietly.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head.

The phrase locked too many tumblers at once. The mass graves. The stomach-first rot. Why the strongest ley-line sweeps had never yielded the core spell.

She stood and crossed to the board. Crypt-chill had settled into her knees during the descent. She took the chalk from the ledge and added a branching vector under his.

"The first animation is crude," she said. "Because the peasant has been saturated enough to twitch, not enough to remember."

"Precisely."

She drew another branch.

"A hamlet fed together dies together," Kel'Thuzad said behind her. "A hamlet that dies together does not rise as a pile of ghouls. It rises in formation. Hunger. Direction. Obedience. Enough to march. Enough to hold a frontline under a competent necromancer."

Her hand stayed raised over the slate.

"The Cultists in the barns," she said. "They do not cast that from nothing."

"No."

"They channel it."

"And harden it."

She set the chalk down with great care. White dust clung to her fingertips.

"Then the farmers in those fields never had a chance," she said.

Kel'Thuzad turned the chalk in his fingers. "The farmers had many chances. They baked them."

The flatness of the answer struck harder than outrage. She turned back to him.

"You speak as though starvation were consent."

"I speak because starvation is the strongest lever available to any kingdom-scale design. You of all people should respect logistics."

He set the chalk aside and moved back to the desk. The shift from slate to mahogany changed the room with him. Proof done. Defense beginning.

"There is still a difference," she said. "What shambles in a field and what you are casting here."

"At last."

He drew one parchment nearer and opened it. Formulas. Schematics. A lattice of soul-bindings anchored around a single central diamond. Finer spellcraft than the ghoul mechanisms. Costlier. Individualized.

"Your field zombies keep their function," he said. "Bite. March. Basic obedience. Their matrix survives in outline because outline is enough for infantry. The finer binding requires finer architecture."

Jaina leaned over the vellum despite herself. Ink lines. Layered soul-traps. Different syntax, same laws underneath.

"This is what the ghouls do not receive," she said.

"This is what the ghouls cannot afford."

The distinction opened under her boots. Low field fodder. The shambling infantry. And above them, a second tier.

"Preserved intellect," she said.

Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened with pleasure. "There. Yes."

She stepped back from the mahogany.

"That is what you want for yourself."

"Of course."

Mild surprise that the point needed saying aloud.

"You built a kingdom-scale slaughterhouse to prove a private theorem."

"No." He sat and steepled his fingers. "I built a kingdom-scale slaughterhouse because the theorem was true whether Antonidas liked it or not. My private interest lies in carrying that truth farther than the Violet Citadel permitted."

"The Six banned necromancy," she said.

"Yes."

"Because it destroys the line between a corpse and a citizen."

"Yes."

"Because it collapses consent."

"Yes."

"Because it turns every hearth, every priest, every graveyard into a staging ground."

Approval warmed his face again. "Much better. You have stopped objecting at the level of aesthetics."

Anger hit clean and fast.

"Do not mistake revulsion for agreement."

"I have never required agreement from a serious magus."

Jaina put both hands on the table. Cold wood. Smooth from use.

"The fact that the math holds," she said, "does not absolve the mass graves."

"Absolve?" he repeated. "No. That is a paladin's word. The spellcraft needs no absolution. It needs to be categorized."

"Categorizing it is not enough when the ritual consumes whole counties."

"And prohibition is not enough when the ritual remains stable."

"The Six never disproved necromancy," he said. "They outlawed it. There is a difference. They found the mana-cost where valid arcane theory touched living peasants and became intolerable. Souls. Consent. Continuity of memory. Civic trust. They were right to fear that mana-cost. Fear, however, is not a counter-spell."

Jaina opened her mouth. No useful incantation arrived. The Six had not called the shadow magic flawed. The Six had called it treason.

"That is your answer?" she said. "That because the math survives the Kirin Tor ban, the ban can be ignored?"

"No. My answer is that the ban names the mana-cost more honestly than the archmages who still invoke it as proof the shadow magic itself was unstable."

"You think this makes you brave."

"No," Kel'Thuzad said. "Bravery is for footmen about to face an orc. I derived a certain formula and accepted the sum."

"The sum is famine, plague, and mothers dragged out of their own kitchens by what used to be their sons."

"Yes."

"You say that as though the noun were sufficient."

"It is sufficient to label the reagent."

"You engineered an atrocity and then trained yourself to say it clinically."

Kel'Thuzad tipped his head a fraction. "And your response has been what, exactly? You crossed the Alterac borders without King Terenas's sanction, pursued a plague no paladin had authorized you to pursue, and followed your private divinations into this crypt because you were certain the core spell waited below."

"Do not compare us."

"I have no need to force the comparison. You made it on your own the moment you bypassed the upper wards."

Jaina's hands curled at her sides. Chalk dust marked one blue cuff. King's Road ruts, Brill taverns, silo dust, Sylvanas's blood on her own fingers at the ridge.

"I crossed the mountains to stop this."

"Yes."

"You crossed them to spread it."

"Vector matters. I have never denied that."

"Then what are you claiming?"

"Only this." He touched the mahogany once with two fingers, the way Antonidas marked the true axis of a ley-line. "We were both made by the same spire. We were both taught to follow the arcane thread where it led. We both found the politics outside our libraries too slow for the spellcraft at hand."

"You call your treason duty because the object of your ambition flatters your logic. I call my trespass scholarship because it flatters my honesty. The underlying arrogance is the same."

"No."

"You left a resignation letter and rode to Northrend alone because you trusted your own translations over the Archmage's orders. Admirable. So did I, once."

"I did not build a plague-cauldron for stripping free will out of Lordaeron."

"No. You are earlier in the casting time."

She turned away from him before she could stop herself. The nearest shelf carried labeled cedar boxes in a careful hand, each marked by soul-weight and date. One had Antonidas's old Kirin Tor sigil for chronal instability scratched into the brass corner and crossed out later, replaced by a cleaner geometric notation she recognized as Kel'Thuzad's own.

She put her palm against the shelf. Dry wood. Cool. Solid.

"You are wrong."

"About your alignment? I do not doubt it. About your methodology? I doubt it less with every minute."

"If I had had another trained magus with me in Andorhal," she said, "the dispelling would have gone faster."

"Would it have gone differently?"

"Yes."

"Because that second magus would have introduced superior morality? Or because it would have forced you to price mana, risk, and Silver Hand politics against your hunger to solve the matrix?"

The questions arrived in a voice that was not in the crypt. How long. How many guards. Which tunnel. Who bleeds if you channel another minute.

When she opened them, Kel'Thuzad was watching with fresh interest.

"There," he said. "Now you are asking the Archmage's question."

"No," Jaina said. "Now I am remembering the mortal one."

"Mortal concerns are always invoked at the point where disciplined mana becomes inconvenient."

"Mortal concerns are the point."

"To kings. To priests. To frightened magistrates."

"To anyone who has ever buried someone they loved."

That reached him. The last scholastic warmth went out of him.

"Love is an argument from biology," he said. "Biology makes poor architecture."

"And your architecture is to make every corpse in Lordaeron bound to a Lich it never swore to serve."

"Your outrage remains moral rather than mathematical. I understand. It is the one pillar on which the Kirin Tor ban can still stand."

"That ban stands because once your math touches actual veins, the school changes. You stop animating dead flesh. You enslave the soul. You turn a citizen into a weapon without their breath."

"Only because you insist on treating the soul as Light-blessed at the exact point where it becomes quantifiable."

"Quantifiable." The syllables left iron in her mouth. "That is what farms are to you."

"The farms are proof," Kel'Thuzad said.

"The Six did not lose their nerve," she said. "They found the vector where your formula became mass murder and labeled it correctly."

Kel'Thuzad nodded once, almost formal.

"That is the strongest version of the thesis," he said. "Antonidas never put it so clinically."

"Do not speak as though you shared wine with him."

"I shared a laboratory with him before you had an apprentice-staff to hold."

"He taught you the same abjuration he taught me. The difference is that I respected where the spell stopped."

"No." Jaina's voice came quieter now, but harder. "The difference is that he calculated the blood-cost where you chose not to."

Kel'Thuzad studied her. "Perhaps," he said. "And perhaps he calculated the blood-cost only because he lacked the mana to pay it."

She went back to the table because standing still came too close to drawing steel. The chair-legs scraped when she pulled it. She sat.

Kel'Thuzad remained standing another beat, then took his own chair again.

He folded one vellum over another and exposed a new schematic. A wider ley-structure. Multiple runic chains descending toward one etched diamond. Jaina saw the geometry before he spoke.

"You came for the ritual," he said. "You have it. You came for the kingdom-scale as well, though you did not know that when you broke the door."

Jaina stared at the new map.

"Say what casts above you," she said.

"Not above me. Above the Scourge."

He placed one fingertip on the uppermost diamond in the ink.

"A psychic weight in Northrend," he said. "Bound. Vast. Fluent in shadow-weaves your Violet Citadel only gestures toward. I did not derive this plague alone, Jaina. No mortal intellect in Lordaeron could have derived it alone. The telepathy came first. The formulas after."

The word hit first.

"Telepathy."

"What else should I call it? I was given a larger chalk-board than Dalaran ever offered me. A mind capable of answering the void-equations your teachers banned at the point of madness. A mana-pool equal to the ritual required."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the mahogany edge.

"Its name."

He met her eyes.

"The Lich King."

The new title reordered the whole map. Telepath. Icecrown. Voice in the dark.

"King of what," she asked.

"Of the dead, eventually." The smallest shrug. "Crowns arrive before the kingdom falls."

Jaina did not smile.

"The blighted grain is logistics," she said, more to herself than to him. "Staging."

"Yes."

"For a vanguard."

That earned another degree of archmage warmth. "You are quick when you stop defending the Kirin Tor."

"The Scourge is the vanguard," he said. "The summoning-force above it is older and far less patient."

He did not wait for her to ask the second title.

"The Burning Legion."

Jaina sat very still.

Silo rot. Grain wagons. Scholomance. And behind them, across the frozen sea, a named telepath feeding shadow-math into the chalk.

"You expect me to accept demonology on your word."

"You have accepted harder alchemy on my word in the last hour."

"Do not become arrogant enough to mistake me for your only scrying-glass," he said. "I offer you the map because the vanguard is already crossing your borders. Whether your paladins can forge the armor in time is another matter."

Antonidas would hear the first title and lock the gates. Uther would hear the second and ride to war. Either way, the carts would keep hauling flour.

"How much of this spellcraft is yours?" she asked.

"Enough."

"That is not a metric."

"It is the only accurate one available. I built the mortal cauldrons. Wheat. Acolytes. Sermons. Wagons. I refined the flesh-binding because mortal alchemy is still potent when properly supplied. Some runic edges belong to higher architects."

Jaina thought of all the graves already packed tight under that answer.

"It matters to anyone trying to break your staff."

"Then break the cauldrons you can reach. Leave the Twisting Nether for later."

She pushed back from the table and crossed to the shelves. Green crypt-moss bars lay across the floor. At the far masonry the granite carried a colder hum. Old Kirin Tor ward-lines. Dense.

"Why hand me any of this?" she asked.

"Because you survived the descent."

"That is ego."

"Partly." He did not deny it. "Mostly efficiency. There is no point hiding the summoning-circles once the demonic footprint has reached the size where hoofprints become unavoidable."

"And if I portal out with them?"

"You will portal out with a clearer geometry of your defeat."

He reached for the final folio on the desk and opened it with care that bordered on prayer. The rune-circle inside was smaller than the rest. Compact. Solitary. Its vectors nested toward a single phylactery and held there.

"There remains one threshold you have not fully mapped," he said.

Jaina came back to the mahogany in spite of herself.

"You mean the gap between your zombies and your own phylactery."

"Yes."

He touched the phylactery diamond.

"The ghoul-ties are rough magic. Cheap enough for the mud. The soul remains only in muscle-memory. For them, the mana-cost is low and brute."

The syllables landed in her stomach with a colder rot than resurrection would have. Plainer. More calculated.

"For you," she said.

"For me, the frost-matrix is carved for one mind. Costly. Intricate. Woven to hold memory, spell-lists, ambition, ego."

Jaina looked at the ink and understood before she wanted to. He had already priced his own execution.

"You think this bypasses the grave."

"It bypasses the rot of it."

"The rot," she repeated.

"You still frame this as Holy Light theology," he said. "It is not. I have no interest in shambling farm-mud for myself. The brute ghouls proved what they needed to prove. The finer math was always whether the Archmage could be carried intact through a prepared phylactery."

"And you intend to be your own test-subject."

"Every serious Kirin Tor master should prefer first-hand scrying where possible."

"You made mass graves into scratch-paper for your private lichdom."

"The mass graves solved one side of the equation. My own phylactery solves the other. Do not collapse formulas that differ in complexity because morality prefers the shorter proof."

Jaina looked down at the vellum and the translation came over her in one clean, intolerable flash.

Carrier. Saturated marrow. Summoning circle. Low mud-magic. High frost-matrix.

For one heartbeat she read the runes all the way through. The corpse carved to hold its own ghost. The blade approached and welcomed. The intellect not extinguished, only poured along a frosted wire.

The blood-ward under the floor shivered with the chair's blow against granite.

Jaina jerked back from the mahogany so hard the oak struck behind her. Air hit her lungs too fast. The crypt swam once, sharply, then steadied.

"Do not," she said.

Kel'Thuzad did not blink.

"I said no such spell."

"You did not need to."

Her voice sounded thin in her own ears. Glass where she wanted steel.

"Deciphering is not corruption," he said.

"No," Jaina said, and forced breath into a rhythm that held. "But it is enough to stain."

She understood the rune-work. She hated it. The hatred did not erase the translation.

Jaina made herself sit because her boots had gone numb and she refused to let him map more of her aura than he already had.

"You said Antonidas lacked the mana," she said.

"I said he stopped where the chalk offended the Citadel."

"He stopped where it touched veins."

"As though veins were not always where the alchemy was heading."

"They were citizens."

"Yes." This time the bark came with unmistakable venom, brief and raw. "That is exactly what made the veins worth tapping."

There. At last. His Archmage mask slipped and showed the ghoul beneath.

"That is the line," she said.

"That is one law."

"It is the only one that keeps us human."

Kel'Thuzad turned one vellum corner between his fingers. When he spoke again his voice had lost the last Kirin Tor polish entirely.

"You have a Ranger," he said. "A blade your library did not supply. I had a Council of Six. They preferred politics to alchemy. The casualty lists differ."

Jaina did not cast back. No one here had cut through the arcane elegance to ask what it did in a barn with pitchforks and actual blood.

"Do not romanticize your robes," he said. "Map the weave precisely. Magic answers whatever staff directs it. Abjuration does the same. What anchors either is loyalty. Sentiment. The Silver Hand oaths that still weigh on you. I burned my oaths in Northrend."

Jaina sat with that because blinking too quickly would have been a rout. Agreement never entered it. Her mana ran toward abjuration by default. Mercy required someone else with a pulse.

Neither of them chanted. Runes cycled under granite. Beyond the masonry, lake water.

Jaina looked at the unfinished soul-math near his elbow.

Her own copied field-sketches from the farmhouse lay there too, where he had set them beside his vellum, her ink in the same stack as his. Oilcloth edges. Her cramped Dalaran script. The sight of them on this mahogany made her flinch harder than any floating hand had.

"You kept those."

Kel'Thuzad followed her gaze. "The geometry was sound."

The Kirin Tor praise fouled the crypt.

"If I teleport out with this," she said, "and hand it to the Six exactly, no one in the Violet Citadel will know what to cast."

"That is statistically certain."

"Antonidas might."

"Antonidas will translate it. Translation and war have never been identical."

She thought of the sending-crystal going dim and dead with interference. Antonidas's projection stripped of everything but static. Uther compressing the rot into Holy rhetoric. The King's Road south where wagons still rattled whether any throne room believed in the Lich attached to them or not.

Then the dread turned toward the missing Ranger she had left outside this iron door. How many arrows. How many ghouls. What does the shadow-weave do outside this mahogany. Death, not theory.

She wiped the chalk from her fingers onto her robe. White against blue.

Kel'Thuzad's head turned before hers did.

Small shift. Mana lifting.

The blood-wards flared a beat later.

The granite took the kinetic spike first. A tremor. Then another, farther up the stairwell. The reliquary's old abjurations took the assault in sequence. No lock-picking. No dispel magic. Each runic threshold shattered and handed the shockwave downward.

Kel'Thuzad stood.

"Violent," he said.

Jaina was already on her boots.

The next tremor came sharper. Stone. Bone. Stone again. Whoever was diving knew how to clear a kill-zone and kept their momentum through the choke.

Kel'Thuzad looked not at the iron but at her.

She understood why a breath later. The recognition had spiked her aura before she could shield it.

"An escort?" he asked.

Jaina did not cast an answer.

Another kinetic spike. Closer now. The glyphs ruptured one after another to leather boots driving over them without breaking stride. A pause at the ossuary gate. Angle checked. Then a sprint again, faster than before.

Kel'Thuzad's eyes narrowed. "You know the footfalls."

She did.

She knew that cadence. Light armor crossing flagstones without drag, every blind corner cleared on the draw. She knew what kind of hunter had breached the crypt.

Above them, a heavy shaft split obsidian. Then silence. Then the stairwell glyph burned out to ash.

Relief nearly dropped her staff. Terror came with it at once. Whoever breached next would see only the still life: the chair knocked back, chalk on her robes, the Necromancer alive and casting at the center of the mahogany.

Kel'Thuzad took one step toward the iron and stopped. He was tracking the mana now in earnest.

"Brutal," he said.

Jaina heard the bowstring snap and knew.
