# Chapter 12: The Necromancer

Kel'Thuzad's sanctum was an office at the bottom of a grave.

Jaina had expected vulgarity. A throne of bone. Black candles guttering in iron holders. Some theater the Cult of the Damned might have furnished its deepest room with, something to brace against. The room offered shelves instead, crowded with folios and labeled specimen jars, a broad mahogany desk under chalk-filmed slate, green bioluminescent fungi washing brass fittings and glass containers in drowned light. The air was cold and dry, salted with the same preservative compounds Dalaran used in its restricted archives. Under that sat the sweeter smell that had followed her down through Scholomance—embalming resin and something older, something the flagstone could not quite hold.

Antonidas's hand was on every surface.

His habits, rather. Margin stars. Cross-braced proofs. The little hooked correction mark he used when a student reached the right answer by an ugly route. Jaina had spent half her life arranging thoughts for that red pen in the Violet Citadel's tutorial rooms. Here those marks covered the deepest chamber in Scholomance, written by the man who had taken the same Kirin Tor lessons and kept going past the line where the Council of Six told him to stop.

At the threshold, Jandice Barov kept one hand on the iron-banded door. Proud. Careful. Her excitement still showed in the angle of her chin, the way it had when she first pulled Jaina's field reads apart on the row across Darrowmere Lake.

Kel'Thuzad turned a page, set a crystal paperweight on it, and raised his head.

Grey-haired, gaunt, scholar-thin. His robe still carried the bones of Kirin Tor cut under darker cloth—the folded sleeves that traditionally held chalk and notation tools, the high collar that once marked Council rank. In another room he might have been any senior archmage at work in the restricted stacks.

"Mistress Barov," he said, "you have done enough."

Jandice's fingers tightened on the latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore followed the upper sequence farther than I expected. She caught the false rings almost as soon as—"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad said, looking at Jaina at last. Pale eyes. Steady. Interested the way Antonidas was interested when a proof went somewhere ugly but correct. "I can see that."

Warmth touched Jandice's face and vanished. She bowed and left.

The door shut with a thick stone sound that Jaina felt in her sternum. The wards laid into the flagstone hummed once, almost below hearing, and the chamber sealed itself along ward lines older than the Barov estate above.

Kel'Thuzad folded his hands on the desk. His fingers were long, reagent-burned at the tips, callused in the wrong places for a combat mage—notation calluses, the same as hers. "You have come a long way for the missing step."

Jaina stayed where Jandice had left her, seated across from him at the long mahogany table that cut the room in two. A stick of alchemical chalk lay near her right hand. White dust marked her thumb and the ridge of one knuckle.

"I came because the body was doing work my field reads could not finish," she said. "Your Andorhal grain carries the strongest signature because you want it to. Every site answered there first. Every site stalled there too."

"Good." Plain as breath. "Then begin there."

He rose and crossed to the slate board mounted beside the shelves. Chalk clicked once against the surface.

"The grain is only the carrier," he said.

He drew a circle, then a second around it. Outer shell. False ring. No need to write the words. The pattern said them in the same notation Antonidas had drilled into both of them decades apart.

"You know that much," Jaina said.

"You know more than that much. You know the treated grain survives storage, transport, even baking, because the necrotic preserving layer is simple and brutal. You know repeated ingestion matters more than any single meal. You know the host tissue is altered before death. What you lacked was the final relation."

Jaina went still. Host. Altered. He used the words like furniture—the same way Antonidas said lattice or conduit, each term placed once and expected to hold.

Kel'Thuzad wrote a second chain below the first, deeper and tighter, each mark tucked inside the next. The notation was recognizably Kirin Tor arcane shorthand pushed into territory the Kirin Tor had never sanctioned.

"The outer shell teaches nothing," he said. "It only arrives intact. The lesson begins in the body."

Chalk rasped on slate. Prepared host. Repeated teaching. The body keeping the lesson. He said it all in the language of wards, bindings, and prepared mediums, and Jaina could follow every step the way she had followed Antonidas through the advanced conjuration sequences in the Violet Citadel—except that these sequences ended in mass death instead of examination marks.

"Not poison," she said.

"No. Poison spends itself."

She answered before she could stop herself. "This settles. Waits. The body carries it farther than the bread can."

His mouth changed. Almost a smile. The warmth in it was genuine, and she hated the flicker of pleasure his approval drew from her the way sunlight draws moisture from stone. Jandice had been fast. Kel'Thuzad was faster—he offered the completed formula and trusted her mind to reach for it.

"Exactly."

Heat climbed the back of her neck. She pressed her thumb against the chalk dust on the table edge until it hurt.

He tapped the deeper chain with the chalk. "Your field work at the farmhouse north of Andorhal was close. The body does not merely suffer contamination. It is taught by it. Repetition lays down an answerable shape within the nervous tissue. Death does not interrupt the sequence. Death releases it."

Jaina looked at the board, then at the specimen jars on the oak shelf beyond him. A child's hand in one, suspended in yellowish preservative. Two intact human eyes in another, the irises still holding color. Research materials, labeled in the same careful notation she had seen upstairs in Jandice's archive.

"The body becomes the answering chamber," she said quietly.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head. The gesture was so precisely Antonidas's that her stomach turned.

The phrase completed too much at once. The Eastweald villages. The gut-first spread through Andorhal's grain network. Why her strongest field readings had never yielded the whole mechanism—she had been reading the carrier, not the chamber.

She stood and crossed to the board. Cold from the flagstone had settled into her knees during the descent through Scholomance's lower levels. She took the alchemical chalk from the slate ledge and added a branching line under his.

"The first waking is crude," she said. "Because the body has been taught enough to answer, not enough to remain itself."

"Precisely."

She drew another branch. The chalk felt warm in her hand, the same chalk she had used for protective ward notation in Dalaran's conjuration halls.

"A village fed together dies together," Kel'Thuzad said behind her. "A village that dies together does not rise as a pile of accidents. It rises in relation. Appetite. Direction. Response. Enough to muster. Enough to become useful under a competent hand."

Her hand stayed raised over the slate.

"The acolytes in the villages," she said. "Your Cult's field necromancers. They do not create that from nothing."

"No."

"They catch it."

"And harden it."

She set the chalk down with great care. White dust clung to her fingertips, the same residue she had carried out of every laboratory in the Violet Citadel, except that these experiments had been conducted on the population of a kingdom.

"Then the dead in those villages never had a chance," she said.

Kel'Thuzad turned the chalk in his long, stained fingers. "The villages had many chances. They baked them."

---

The flatness of the answer struck harder than outrage. She turned back to him.

"You speak as though routine were consent."

"I speak because routine is the strongest force available to any large design. You of all people should respect repetition." He said it the way he might have addressed a seminar at the Violet Citadel—measured, collegial, as if Lordaeron's dead were a problem set rather than a kingdom.

He set the chalk aside and moved back to the desk. The shift from board to table changed the room with him. Proof done. Argument beginning.

"There is still a difference," she said. "What rises in a village and what you are doing here."

"At last."

He drew one folio nearer and opened it. The leather binding was cracked with age, the pages heavy Dalaran vellum. Notes. Diagrams. A lattice of arcane circles bound around a single central mark, each ring annotated in the same shorthand the Kirin Tor used for ward-chain analysis. Finer work than the village mechanisms. Costlier. Individualized.

"Your village dead keep their function," he said. "Motion. Appetite. Rough answerability. Their pattern survives in outline because outline is enough for war. The finer work requires finer structure."

Jaina leaned over the page despite herself. Ink lines in reagent-resistant iron-gall, the same formula Dalaran used for its restricted texts. Layered binding sequences. Different words, same arcane logic underneath.

"This is what the villages do not receive," she said.

"This is what the villages cannot afford."

The distinction opened under her feet. Low village dead—the rough answerables who rose from Andorhal's poisoned bread. And above them, a second aim.

"Preserved selfhood," she said.

Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened with pleasure. "There. Yes."

She stepped back from the desk. The flagstone was colder underfoot than it had been an hour ago; the wards cycled deeper as the conversation went on, as if Scholomance itself were paying attention.

"That is what you want for yourself."

"Of course."

Mild surprise that the point needed saying aloud.

"You built a kingdom-scale slaughter to prove a private theorem."

"No." He sat and steepled his fingers—reagent burns catching the green fungal light. "I built a kingdom-scale apparatus because the theorem was true whether anyone liked it or not. My private interest lies in carrying that truth farther than your teachers in the Violet Citadel permitted."

"Dalaran banned necromancy," she said.

"Yes."

"Because it destroys the line between a body and a person."

"Yes."

"Because it collapses consent."

"Yes."

"Because it turns every household, every healer, every grave into a point of violation."

Approval warmed his face again. "Much better. You have stopped objecting at the level of taste."

Anger hit clean and fast. The kind that sharpened her thinking instead of clouding it.

"Do not mistake clarity for agreement."

"I have never required agreement from a serious mind."

Jaina put both hands on the table. Cold mahogany. Smooth from decades of use, the same way Antonidas's tutorial desk in the upper Citadel was smooth—palms and elbows and years of scholars leaning into arguments that mattered.

"The fact that the theorem holds," she said, "does not absolve what it does."

"Absolve?" he repeated. "No. That is a priest's word. The work needs no absolution. It needs to be named."

"Naming it is not enough when the method consumes whole villages."

"And prohibition is not enough when the method remains sound."

"Your Council never disproved necromancy," he said. "The Council of Six forbade it. There is a difference. They found the line where valid arcane method touched living society and became intolerable. Persons. Consent. Continuity of self. Civic trust. They were right to fear that line. Fear, however, is not refutation."

Jaina opened her mouth. Nothing useful arrived. Dalaran had not called the art nonsense. The Kirin Tor had called it forbidden—had expelled Kel'Thuzad from the Council of Six and stripped his academic standing, but never published a formal proof that his underlying theory failed.

"That is your answer?" she said. "That because the logic survives the ban, the ban can be ignored?"

"No. My answer is that the ban names the cost more honestly than the scholars who still invoke it as proof the art itself was impossible."

"You think this makes you brave."

"No," Kel'Thuzad said. "Bravery is for men about to face the unknown. I did something certain and accepted the result."

"The result is famine, plague, and mothers dragged out of their own houses by what used to be their sons."

"Yes."

"You say that as though the word were sufficient."

"It is sufficient to name what occurred."

"You built an atrocity and then taught yourself to say it cleanly."

Kel'Thuzad tipped his head a fraction. "And your response has been what, exactly? You crossed the Thalassian border without sanction from Dalaran, pursued a plague no one in the Kirin Tor had authorized you to pursue, and followed your private analysis into this room because you were certain the answer waited below Caer Darrow."

"Do not compare us."

"I have no need to force the comparison. You made it on your own the moment you crossed the ward line into Scholomance."

Jaina's hands curled at her sides. Chalk dust marked one blue cuff. Roads, village rooms, grain dust in the Andorhal granaries, Sylvanas's blood on her own fingers at the ridge where the Cult ambush had caught them all too slow.

"I crossed a border to stop this."

"Yes."

"You crossed one to spread it."

"Direction matters. I have never denied that."

"Then what are you claiming?"

"Only this." He touched the desk once with two fingers, the way a lecturer marked the true line of a problem—the same gesture Antonidas used when he wanted the room to understand that everything before the gesture was preamble and everything after was the point. "We were both made by the same institution. We were both taught in the Kirin Tor's restricted stacks to follow pattern where it led. We both found the world outside our studies too slow for the work at hand."

"You call your trespass duty because the object of your conviction flatters your ethics. I call mine scholarship because it flatters my honesty. The underlying habit is the same."

"No."

"You left a note and rode north alone because you trusted your reading over the Council's assessment. Admirable. So did I, once."

---

"I did not build a machine for stripping personhood out of a kingdom."

"No. You are earlier in the chain."

She turned away from him before she could stop herself. The nearest shelf carried labeled boxes in a careful hand, each marked by subject and date in standard Kirin Tor archival script. One had Antonidas's old sigil for translational instability scratched into the corner—the looping mark she had learned to recognize in her first year at the Violet Citadel—and crossed out later, replaced by a cleaner notation she recognized as Kel'Thuzad's own revision.

She put her palm against the shelf. Dry oak. Cool. Solid as the restricted stacks in Dalaran, where the dangerous texts lived behind wards and she had spent three summers earning the right to read them.

"You are wrong."

"About your intentions? I do not doubt them. About your methods? I doubt them less with every minute."

"If I had had another trained mind with me in Andorhal," she said, "the work would have gone faster."

"Would it have gone differently?"

"Yes."

"Because that second mind would have introduced superior ethics? Or because it would have forced you to price time, risk, and consequence against your hunger to complete the pattern?"

The questions arrived in a voice that was not in the room. How long. How many. Which door. Who pays if you stay another minute. Sylvanas's voice, stripped down to its bluntest Thalassian efficiency—the voice that had kept interrupting elegant theory with operational cost since the Millhaven investigation.

When she opened her eyes, Kel'Thuzad was watching with fresh interest.

"There," he said. "Now you are asking the useful question."

"No," Jaina said. "Now I am remembering the human one."

"Human concerns are always invoked at the point where disciplined minds become inconvenient."

"Human concerns are the point."

"To governments. To priests. To frightened committees."

"To anyone who has ever buried someone they loved."

That reached him. The last warmth went out of him the way light went out of the green fungi when the wards cycled colder—not a dramatic shift, but an absence that changed what the room meant.

"Love is an argument from attachment," he said. "Attachment makes poor policy."

"And your policy is to make every body in Lordaeron answerable to an intelligence it never consented to serve."

"Your outrage remains ethical rather than analytical. I understand. It is the one ground on which the old Kirin Tor ban can still stand."

"That ban stands because once your work touches actual bodies, the category changes. You stop moving dead flesh. You assault the self. You turn a person into an instrument without their leave."

"Only because you insist on treating the self as sacred at the point where it becomes worth studying."

"Worth studying." The phrase left iron in her mouth. "That is what the Eastweald villages are to you."

"The villages are proof," Kel'Thuzad said.

"Dalaran did not lose its nerve," she said. "The Council of Six found the place where your method became a civic atrocity and named it correctly."

Kel'Thuzad nodded once, almost formal—the way a senior mage acknowledged a well-built argument before dismantling it.

"That is the strongest version of the argument," he said. "Antonidas never put it so cleanly."

"Do not speak as though you knew him better than I do."

"I knew him before you had a staff to hold. Before the Violet Citadel gave you your first notation set."

"He taught you the same discipline he taught me. The difference is that I accepted where it led."

"No." Jaina's voice came quieter now, but harder. "The difference is that he understood the cost where you chose not to."

Kel'Thuzad studied her. The green light from the fungi found the hollows of his face and made him look older than any human man had a right to look and still be breathing. "Perhaps," he said. "And perhaps he understood the cost only because he lacked the will to bear it."

She went back to the table because standing still came too close to yielding. The chair scraped against the flagstone when she pulled it—a sound too small and ordinary for the weight of what was being said in a room built beneath a lake. She sat.

Kel'Thuzad remained standing another beat, then took his own chair again. The mahogany creaked under him the way everything in Scholomance creaked—old materials holding weight they were never designed to carry.

He folded one page over another and exposed a new diagram. A wider structure. Multiple chains descending toward one marked point, the notation denser than anything she had seen in the Kirin Tor's restricted archives. Jaina saw the turn before he spoke.

"You came for mechanism," he said. "You have it. You came for scale as well, though you did not know that when you entered."

Jaina stared at the new diagram. The arcane shorthand was still recognizably Kirin Tor at its roots, but the upper layers used notation she had never been taught—symbols that reached into whatever theoretical framework lay north of anything Dalaran had mapped.

"Say what sits above you," she said.

"Not above me. Above the work."

He placed one fingertip on the uppermost mark in the chain.

"A will in Northrend," he said. "Bound. Vast. Learned in ways your Kirin Tor libraries only gesture toward. I did not derive this design alone, Jaina. No mortal mind in Lordaeron could have derived it alone. The patronage came first. The revisions after."

The word hit first. He said her name the way Antonidas said it—with intellectual intimacy, as if the use of it meant she had earned something.

"Patronage."

"What else should I call it? I was given a larger frame than Dalaran ever offered me. A mind capable of answering the questions your teachers banned at the point of discomfort. A power equal to the labor required."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the table edge. The mahogany grain pressed against her knuckles.

"Its name."

He met her eyes. The pale irises held no heat, no theater—only the certainty of a man delivering the next line of a well-ordered lecture.

"The Lich King."

The new name reordered the whole conversation. Patron. Northrend. Voice in the dark. A named intelligence feeding revisions into the work that had turned the Eastweald into a slaughter by grain.

"King of what," she asked.

"Of the dead, eventually." The smallest shrug. "Titles arrive before they are fully earned."

Jaina did not smile.

"The plague is logistics," she said, more to herself than to him. "Preparation for the army."

"Yes."

"For an invasion."

That earned another degree of warmth. "You are quick when you stop arguing for the room."

"The Scourge is preparation," he said. "The force above it is older and far less patient."

He did not wait for her to ask the second name.

"The Burning Legion."

Jaina sat very still. The ward-hum under the flagstone changed pitch—or she imagined it did.

Plague villages. The Andorhal grain roads. Scholomance beneath Caer Darrow. And behind them, farther north across the sea, a named intelligence feeding revisions into the work. And above that, something older.

"You expect me to accept that on your word."

"You have accepted harder things on my word in the last hour."

"Do not become careless enough to mistake me for your only source of truth," he said. "I offer you scale because scale is already bearing down on Lordaeron. Whether the Kirin Tor can digest the names in time is another matter."

Antonidas would hear the first name and understand too much. The Council of Six would hear the second and slow to the institutional crawl that had made the Violet Citadel useless at every crisis Jaina had ever watched unfold. Either way, the grain roads would keep moving poisoned stores through every Eastweald bakery.

"How much of this design is yours?" she asked.

"Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only honest one available. I built the mortal apparatus. The Andorhal network. The Cult's infrastructure. The instruction doctrine at Scholomance. The deployment sequences. I refined the host work because mortal scholarship is still useful when properly guided. Some edges belong to higher authorship."

Jaina thought of all the villages already buried under that answer. Millhaven's empty tables. The farmhouse north of Andorhal with its dead animals and its contaminated well.

"It interests anyone trying to stop you."

"Then stop the machine you can touch. Leave the cosmology for later."

She pushed back from the table and crossed to the shelves. Green bars of bioluminescent light lay across the flagstone floor. At the far wall the stone carried a colder thrum—older ward lines than the ones Kel'Thuzad had laid. The original Barov estate foundations, perhaps, or something deeper still, predating the island keep. Stronger.

"Why tell me any of this?" she asked.

"Because you came this far."

"That is vanity."

"Partly." He did not deny it. "Mostly economy. There is no point withholding names once pressure has reached the threshold where names become unavoidable."

"And if I carry them out?"

"You will carry out a truer understanding of your defeat."

He reached for the final folio on the desk and opened it with care that bordered on reverence. The diagram inside was smaller than the rest, drawn on finer vellum with a steadier hand. Tighter. Personal. Its lines nested toward a single center and held there—a preservation lattice that Jaina recognized as theoretically identical to the village mechanism, only built with the precision of a master jeweler instead of a field commander.

"There remains one distinction you have not fully accepted," he said.

Jaina came back to the desk in spite of herself. Her feet moved before her judgment could stop them, the way they had moved toward every locked problem Antonidas had set for her in the Citadel's restricted stacks.

"You mean the difference between your villagers and your own plans."

"Yes."

He touched the central mark.

"The village dead are rough continuities. Useful enough for the field. The self remains only in outline. For them, recurrence is low and cheap."

The word landed in her stomach with a colder force than resurrection would have. Plainer. More worked through. Recurrence. Not rebirth. Not salvation. A technical term for a solved problem.

"For you," she said.

"For me, the preserving structure is made for one mind. Costly. Deliberate. Designed to hold memory, intellect, priority, will."

Jaina looked at the page and understood before she wanted to. He had already priced his own death. Had built the preparation for it the way he had built everything else—cleanly, logically, with the Kirin Tor notation system applied to a question the Kirin Tor had never dared ask.

"You think this solves mortality."

"It solves enough of it."

"Enough," she repeated.

"You still frame this as faith," he said. "It is not. I have no interest in crude village survival for myself. The rough answerables proved what they needed to prove. The finer question was always whether the self could be carried intact through a prepared threshold."

"And you intend to be your own proof."

"Every serious scholar should prefer first-hand verification where possible."

"You made the Eastweald's villages into rehearsal for your private continuation."

"The villages proved one branch of the theorem. My own work proves another. Do not collapse scales that differ in purpose because outrage prefers the shorter line."

Jaina looked down at the folio and the understanding came over her in one clean, intolerable sweep.

Carrier. Host. Answering chamber. Low recurrence. High preservation. The same arcane logic running at two different fidelities, and she could see the elegance of it—could trace the line from Antonidas's lecture on sympathetic carryover all the way through Kel'Thuzad's forbidden extension to this final, monstrous proof.

For one heartbeat she followed it all the way through. The body taught to hold its ending. The threshold approached and crossed. The self not extinguished, only transferred along a prepared line. Beautiful. Complete. Built on sound arcane theory that Dalaran's ban had never disproved, only forbidden.

The ward-line under the flagstone shivered when the chair struck stone behind her.

Jaina jerked back from the desk so hard the chair hit the wall. Air hit her lungs too fast. The sanctum swam once, sharply, then steadied around its cold green light and its careful shelves and its jars of preserved human parts.

"Do not," she said.

Kel'Thuzad did not move. His hands stayed flat on the mahogany, exactly where they had been.

"I said no such thing."

"You did not need to."

Her voice sounded wrong in her own ears. Thin where she wanted steel. The chalk dust on her fingers was the same chalk dust on his.

"Understanding is not surrender," he said.

"No," Jaina said, and forced breath into a shape that held. "But it is enough to wound."

She understood the structure. She hated it. The hatred did not erase the understanding, and the understanding would not erase itself no matter how many miles she put between herself and this room beneath Caer Darrow.

Jaina made herself sit because her legs had gone unreliable and she refused to let him see more than he already had. The chair's back pressed cold against her shoulders. Stone through wood.

"You said Antonidas lost his nerve," she said.

"I said he stopped where the work offended the room."

"He stopped where it touched bodies."

"As though bodies were not always where the work was heading."

"They were people."

"Yes." This time the answer came with unmistakable impatience, brief and real—the first crack in the lecturer's composure since the conversation began. "That is exactly what made the question worth asking."

There. At last. His composure slipped and showed appetite beneath.

"That is the line," she said.

"That is one line."

"It is the only one that matters."

Kel'Thuzad turned one page corner between his fingers. The vellum creased softly. When he spoke again his voice had lost the last warmth entirely.

"You have a partner," he said. "A friction your mind did not produce. I had a Council. The Council of Six preferred order to inquiry. The outcomes differ."

Jaina did not answer. No one here in this sealed room beneath Darrowmere Lake had cut through elegance to ask what it did in a village with ovens and actual dead. Sylvanas would have asked in the first minute and forced the price into the open.

"Do not romanticize your distinction from me," he said. "Understand it precisely. Intelligence answers whatever hand directs it. Method does the same. What governs either is relation. Attachment. The bonds that still pull at you when the theorem is running. I cut mine when Dalaran cut me first."

Jaina sat with that because refusing too quickly would have been cowardice. Acceptance never entered it. Her mind ran toward pattern by default, the same way it had run toward every pattern Antonidas had ever set before her. Mercy required someone else in the room—someone whose mind did not work this way.

Neither of them spoke. Wards cycled under stone. Beyond the sanctum wall, the weight of Darrowmere Lake pressed against the island's foundations.

---

Jaina looked at the unfinished notes near his elbow. Arcane diagrams in progress, annotations in two different inks—the iron-gall notation she had seen all evening and a fainter violet wash that might have been corrections received from the patron-intelligence he had named.

Her own copied pages from the farmhouse north of Andorhal lay there too, where he had set them beside his work, her hand in the same stack as his. Oilcloth edges. Her cramped field script, written on the road with frozen fingers and inadequate light. The sight of them in this room—her work placed beside his work as if they were stages in the same process—made her recoil harder than any specimen jar had.

"You kept those."

Kel'Thuzad followed her gaze. "They were worth keeping."

The compliment fouled the air like the embalming compound beneath it.

"If I leave here with this," she said, "and speak it exactly as it is, no one in Dalaran will know what to do with the truth."

"That is very likely."

"Antonidas might."

"Antonidas will understand it. Understanding and action have never been identical in the Violet Citadel."

She thought of the Kirin Tor sending stone going thin and pale with distance. Antonidas's voice stripped of everything but urgency. The Council of Six compressing horror into orderly debate. The roads south along the King's Road where Andorhal grain still moved through Eastweald bakeries whether any deliberative chamber believed in the names attached to it or not.

Then the thought turned toward the missing correction she had left outside this room. The voice that would have asked the questions Kel'Thuzad's elegant theorem never paused to answer. How long. How many. What does it do outside this room. Correction, not comfort. Sylvanas's kind of question—operational, immediate, priced in bodies rather than in theorems.

She wiped the chalk from her fingers onto her sleeve. White alchemical dust against the blue Kirin Tor travel-cloth.

Kel'Thuzad's head turned before hers did.

Small motion. Attention lifting from the scholarly quiet the way a dog lifts its ears before the sound arrives.

The wards answered a beat later.

The flagstone took the disturbance first. A pulse through the sanctum's deepest protective layer. Then another, farther out, traveling up the crypt spine toward the surface. Scholomance's old defenses took the descent in order—each threshold identifying the intruder, answering, and handing the motion downward through the keep's layered architecture. No fumbling at the locks. No clumsy magical shove. Each ward threshold answered and yielded to a body driving through them without waste.

Kel'Thuzad stood. His chair scraped the flagstone.

"Interesting," he said.

Jaina was already on her feet. The chalk stick rolled off the table edge and broke against the floor.

The next ripple came clearer. Stair. Landing. Stair again. The old crypt spine beneath Scholomance's service levels, not the curated guest route Jandice had walked her down. Whoever was coming knew how to move through hostile ground and kept their pace through it—each ward cleared with the economy of someone who had been clearing defended positions for centuries.

Kel'Thuzad's eyes narrowed. "You know the approach."

She did.

She knew that shape. Pressure crossing defended space without waste, every angle priced on the way through. She knew what kind of mind moved like that—the mind that counted doors, timed pauses, read fortification geometry the way Jaina read arcane formula. The mind that had been absent from this room for the entire conversation and whose absence had made every step of the conversation possible.

Above them, a heavy blow struck stone. The sound carried through Scholomance's layers with the clarity of a bell. Then silence. Then the next ward answered.

Relief nearly folded her knees. Dread came with it at once. Whoever entered next would see only the aftermath: the chair knocked back from the mahogany desk, alchemical chalk on Jaina's hands, Kel'Thuzad alive and calm at the center of the scene. And no coercion anywhere in sight. Just a scholar's office at the bottom of a grave, and two people who understood each other too well.

Kel'Thuzad took one step toward the door and stopped. He was listening now in earnest, his pale eyes tracking the ward ripples the way a conductor follows an orchestra tuning.

"Remarkable," he said.

Jaina heard the next ripple and knew.

Sylvanas.
