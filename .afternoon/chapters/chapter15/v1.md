# Chapter 15: The Prince

The road south stopped pretending before Hearthglen did.

A child's shoe lay half sunk in the mud where the wheel-ruts deepened, leather dark with water, one lace worked down into the track by passing wagons. Nobody had come back for it. Nobody behind them had the spare hand.

Northbound traffic kept grinding past. Farm carts rode so low their axles whined. A hay wagon had been turned into a cradle for three old women and a goat tied by the horns to the rear rail. One man walked with both hands under the shaft of a handcart because the wheel on one side had split and he was too tired to fix it. Children sat on sacks and stared at the three riders going the wrong way.

Eastweald still wore its ordinary face badly. Orchard rows climbed the low rises in black-limbed lines, fruit shriveled on the branches where nobody had picked it. Hedges held their shape. Smoke still came out of farmhouse chimneys. Supper still mattered to the people going north.

The road ahead had run out of patience for all that.

Jaina rode in the middle, too straight in the saddle. Her gloves had gone pale across the knuckles. Once or twice her head turned a fraction, not toward any sound Sylvanas could hear, but toward some pressure riding under the ground and through the air both. Cyndia held the left as she had all day, bow unstrung for the speed of the road but never far from hand.

Sylvanas kept her mare half a length forward and to the right, where she could watch the road, the ditches, the wagons, the people, and Jaina's profile all without seeming to choose.

"They're still fleeing loaded," Jaina said over the creak and wheel-noise. "That means the town held."

"Long enough for them to fill a cart," Sylvanas said.

Jaina accepted that with a short nod and looked south again.

The last rise gave Hearthglen to them whole.

The town stood where it had stood yesterday in reports and maps: walls of pale stone, square towers at the approaches, chapel roof lifting over the inner line, the long backs of stables and smithy buildings tucked in against the defensive shape. It should have looked solid. Instead it looked worked over—gate stone black-scored, one western tower patched with fresh timber, smoke rising from three separate points inside the walls and one outside them where the pyres had been pushed clear of the houses.

The gate stood open to a choke of men, stretchers, priests, and carts stripped down for carrying bodies.

Bells were ringing again before the last peel had fully died.

They had nearly reached the gate when a woman on foot caught at Jaina's stirrup leather. Not hard enough to stop the mare. Hard enough to show desperation had burned courtesy out of her.

"My boy went in with the wall detail at dawn," she said. "If you're riding to the prince—"

Her voice broke there. Jaina looked down at her hand, then at the woman's face.

"Which wall?" Jaina asked.

"North. Near the chapel tower."

Sylvanas leaned from the saddle. "Name."

The woman blinked at her. "Tomas. Tomas Bell."

Sylvanas fixed it in memory. "If he's alive, somebody will have seen him. Clear the way."

The woman let go at once. Not comforted. Only answered. On this road that was as much as anyone had time to buy.

They rode through the gate into heat, noise, and the thick working smell of a town saving itself one body at a time.

Holy fire had burned lines into the inner stone. The nearest yard had become a triage ground: blankets thrown over mud, soldiers stripped to shirtsleeves despite the chill, priests on their knees with hands lit gold over men who had not yet decided whether they would stay men. Bodies lay on doors lifted from hinges and on ladders stripped of their use. Two boys dragged straw toward the pyres. A horse screamed from somewhere behind the stables and went silent all at once.

The first stretcher passed them with a corporal on it, jaw tied shut with bandage cloth, one arm hanging down and knocking weakly against the boards. He was dead. Sylvanas knew it before the priest beside him did. The stillness had gone wrong. The weight had settled wrong.

The body jerked before the stretcher reached the fire.

The priest on the right shouted. The bearers cursed and dropped their end. The corporal came up fast and ugly, all shoulders and teeth, bandaged jaw working wetly around the gag. One bearer stumbled backward into the mud. The other jammed a knee into the corpse's chest by instinct rather than courage and very nearly lost his hand for it.

Jaina was already turning in the saddle.

Sylvanas was faster. She had the bow half up before she was clear of the mare. The shot took the corpse through the eye and pinned the head to the mud. The priest on the right did the rest, both hands brightening in one hard burst that set hair, cloth, and ruined flesh alight together.

By the time the body stopped moving, nobody in the yard had even turned fully toward them. Another cry had gone up beyond the tents. Another stretcher. Another piece of work.

"This is what held looks like here?" Sylvanas asked.

Nobody answered. Nobody had time.

They left the horses with a stablehand who looked twelve and forty at once and moved deeper into the town on foot, taking the lane the nearest officer pointed out with a bloody hand. The closer they came to the southern field tents, the cleaner the motion became. Not calmer. Simply narrowed by people who had been at it too long to waste anything.

That was where they found Uther.

He was kneeling in the trampled strip between two treatment tents with a wounded soldier propped against his knee. Golden light ran from his scarred hands into the man's chest in slow steady pulses. It was warm enough to feel at ten paces even through the smoke and damp. The soldier's breath, which had been catching high and thin, eased under it. Not cured. Not made safe. Brought back under the body's own command.

Uther looked up before the healing was finished.

The first thing he saw was not Jaina's Kirin Tor blue or Cyndia's braid or Sylvanas's bow. It was the shape they made: Cyndia on the left, Jaina in the middle, Sylvanas half a pace forward-right, the line working and damaged at once.

His eyes rested there one beat too long.

Then the soldier under his hands breathed deep for the first time since they'd arrived, and Uther let the Light go. He rose in one motion, older hammer hanging at his side, plain-faced and heavy from long use. His shoulders made most men look decorative.

"Ranger-General," he said.

"Lord Uther."

His gaze shifted. "Lady Proudmoore."

Jaina dipped her head. "We need the prince."

"You and everyone else." His voice had gravel in it and no wasted softness. "Come."

He led them between the tents at a pace that said he had already chosen what to leave undone for the next five minutes and would pay for it later.

Up close the town's survival looked worse. Men with fresh burns were being laid beside men with old bites. A paladin in Silver Hand colors was asleep sitting upright against a barrel because there was nowhere else to put him. Two priests argued over oil, flame, and whether a family had been given enough time. Every few yards another stack of cut wood stood ready. Nobody pretended the stacks were for warmth.

"How often?" Sylvanas asked.

Uther knew which question she meant. "Often enough that we stopped using clocks. If a body lies unburned longer than a prayer and a half, someone stays with it."

"And the dead outside?"

"Worse." He kept walking. "When the line breaks them quickly enough, they stay down. When the line thins, the field feeds itself again."

Jaina pushed hair back from her face with the heel of one hand. "Kel'Thuzad is dead."

Uther glanced at her, then forward again. "Yes. So I was told."

"This is still answering."

"I had noticed."

Dry enough to be almost funny. Not meant that way.

They passed a wagon being unloaded into a supply lean-to: bandages, lamp oil, sacks of grain someone had the sense to leave untouched until a priest could bless or burn them. Men were looking at the sacks as if they expected them to bite.

"The town held because the defenders never stopped killing their own dead," Uther said. "Then Arthas arrived and the walls remembered how to stand for another hour. Then the dead remembered how many they were. We are still having that argument."

The command tents had been raised in the lee of the south wall where runners could get in and out fastest. Falric stood outside the central pavilion with two King's Guard men and a map slate under one arm. Mud climbed nearly to his knees. He had the look of a man who had not sat down since yesterday and intended to be praised for that later, if there was a later.

His eyes went from Sylvanas to Jaina to Uther and sharpened.

"My lord's in conference," he said.

"He can conference while hearing something useful," Uther said.

Falric stepped aside at once.

Before he pushed the flap open, Uther looked once at Sylvanas.

"If you have a brake for him," he said quietly, "use it fast."

There was no room in the sentence for hope.

Sylvanas felt the old warning thread pull tight under her ribs. The field chose him. He never stepped back.

"Does he still listen?" she asked.

Uther's mouth moved as if he had nearly smiled at the question's honesty.

"He hears," he said. "Listening is a slower act."

Then he opened the flap.

Arthas stood over the campaign table with one gauntlet planted beside Stratholme.

The plate on his left shoulder had been cracked and buckled, not repaired, only strapped down so it would keep riding. Dried blood sat black in the scrollwork along the vambrace. Somebody had wiped his face clean and stopped there. The room around him was map boards, chalk, knives, relay strips, two tired officers, and the hard smell of wet wool. None of it landed before he did.

His gaze cut to them, fast and total.

"Ranger-General," he said. Then, after the briefest turn of the head: "Lady Proudmoore."

Jaina's posture tightened by less than an inch. Sylvanas saw it anyway.

Arthas had already looked back to the map.

"Report," he said.

Sylvanas stepped to the table's edge. Daggers pinned the corners of the map. Chalk marks had turned the roads south of Hearthglen into a nest of cuts and guesses. One of the guesses had already been crossed out so hard the waxed surface showed through.

"The plague road is wider than you were told," she said. "Andorhal was the throat, not the end of it. The grain leaves there, moves on ordinary wagons, and keeps moving until somebody eats it. The dead it makes are crude on their own and worse when the Cult has live hands nearby to turn them. Hearthglen proves the field can run hot without waiting for a necromancer to stand over every corpse."

Arthas did not look at her while she spoke. He tracked the road from Andorhal with two fingers, then dragged them to Hearthglen, then farther southeast.

"How many days on the Stratholme routes?" he asked.

"Too many already."

"That is not a count."

"No," Sylvanas said. "It is the truth you have time for."

One of the officers looked at the table instead of at her. Falric did not.

Arthas's jaw shifted once. "Go on."

She gave him the route work cleanly: the dead villages, the grain sellers, the cult handling points, the way the countryside had been maintained to move plague and silence witnesses at once. She told him what they had seen on the road into town. Refugees. Bodies turning inside the line. Orchards blackening while the chimneys still smoked. Every sentence pointed toward one fact: the kingdom was feeding its own dead.

When she finished, Jaina stepped in without waiting to be invited.

"The grain is only the shell," she said. Her voice came out quick and built, every clause landing where she meant it. "It survives transport, storage, and baking. People keep eating it. The body does the rest of the work from there. By the time symptoms read clearly, death is only the last turn, not the first one. The wards cities trust are built for force coming in from outside. This reaches the table before it reaches the gate."

Arthas's eyes lifted from the map to her face for the first time.

"And Kel'Thuzad?" he said.

"Dead."

"You sound dissatisfied by the fact."

"I am dissatisfied by how little it improved."

That earned the nearest thing to approval Sylvanas had yet seen in him. Not warmth. Not agreement. Sharper attention.

"There is more," Jaina said. "He was not building this for Lordaeron alone. There's a chain above him. Northrend. The Lich King. A larger war behind the plague."

One of the officers swore under his breath. Falric went still in the way disciplined men did when they knew they would need the next order whole.

Arthas tapped Stratholme once with the back of his knuckle.

"Then the larger war can wait its turn," he said. "This city won't."

Uther had come in behind them and taken position near the rear pole with the older hammer in his hand. "That is exactly how a larger war arrives," he said.

Arthas did not turn. "And exactly how smaller men lose cities while naming it."

The words landed hard and flat, not raised. The officer nearest the flap looked suddenly interested in his own boots.

Jaina ignored the exchange. Her hand came down on the northeastern quarter of the map hard enough to shift one of the knives.

"There may still be a narrow answer," she said.

That changed the room.

Arthas's head came up. Falric leaned in despite himself. Even Uther's grip altered on the hammer haft.

"Speak," Arthas said.

Jaina flattened her palm over Stratholme and began cutting the city apart with one finger.

"Not the whole city. Specific feed points. Granaries if any stock is still held intact. Bakehouses. Warehouse lanes where flour is stacked before it moves. The shell is strongest there. If I can break enough of it before it disappears into bodies, I might blunt the next turn."

"Break how?" Arthas asked.

"Cold first. Fire if forced."

The room tightened around that.

"You would burn grain in a city already starving," the officer by the flap said before he could stop himself.

Jaina turned her head and gave him one clean look.

"I would choose sacks over citizens if the clock still gave me the choice."

Nobody in the tent spoke for a beat after that.

Arthas looked down at the map again. Sylvanas watched his gauntlet settle beside Stratholme's walls. He was already measuring distance, horseflesh, road width, which officers could move without sleep, how many bodies a city of that size could hide before it stopped hiding anything at all. Everything about him turned bad news straight into motion.

"How much of a head start?" he asked.

Jaina did not soften the answer to earn it.

"Hours matter. Not days. The city may already be too far in. I don't know until I see the stock."

"You want me to halt for a maybe."

"I want you to let one useful attempt leave this tent before you bury it under boots."

Falric's gaze flicked away from the map to Jaina, then to Arthas. He had probably heard men speak to the prince in many tones. That one had not been common among them.

Uther pushed off the pole and came forward a step. "She isn't asking for your halt. She's asking for a lane."

Arthas turned then, not to Jaina but to him.

"And if her lane fails?"

"Then you lose nothing you were not already willing to spend," Uther said. "If it succeeds, you arrive at a city with one door still open."

The silence after that had weight in it. Outside the tent somebody shouted for more lamp oil. Another voice answered from much farther off.

Arthas looked at Jaina again. The title he'd chosen for her was still in the room between them like a knife left out on purpose.

"Lady Proudmoore rides ahead," he said at last. "Captain Cyndia with her. Falric, clear the north-east lane and have quartermasters throw them fresh horses from the reserve tack. Route packet, store maps, signal priority on every relay between here and Stratholme."

Falric moved before the sentence had finished.

Arthas's eyes went to Sylvanas.

"Ranger-General, your speed is useful in either column," he said. "Ride with me and clear the road ahead of the main body. Ride with her and buy the attempt everything it can be bought. I do not care which, so long as neither lane slows for want of discipline."

He could have ordered her cleanly one way or the other. He spent the choice instead.

Sylvanas looked at the map, at Jaina's hand still braced over the quarter she had marked, at Uther standing behind the prince with fear locked down so hard it had become posture.

The room from Scholomance tried to rise at the edge of memory and found no purchase. Jaina was not shielding what she'd learned there. She was already trying to ruin it.

"If she can still cut the feed points," Sylvanas said, "she'll need something faster than faith behind her."

Jaina's eyes met hers for one beat and dropped back to the table. No thanks. No relief. Only a harder line to the mouth, as if she had taken the answer and put it to work immediately.

Arthas gave a single curt nod. "Then ride."

He turned away from them in the same motion and snapped three orders at once, one to the left side of the tent, one to Falric at the flap, one to no one Sylvanas could see. Officers began moving before all three were finished. Uther stayed where he was, watching the room become its own consequence.

As Sylvanas turned to leave, Arthas said, "One more thing."

She stopped.

He had not lifted his eyes from the map. "If the city is already gone, I want that answer brought to me cleanly. No hope added to it."

Jaina's mouth tightened.

"I don't dress failure for company," she said.

Something in Arthas's face moved. Not enough to name.

"Good," he said.

They stepped back out into the camp and were swallowed by motion.

The departure rush had already begun. Stable boys ran tack from chest to mounting line. Quartermasters shouted over one another with the fury of men who knew speed would be blamed on them if it failed. A clerk was pinning relay strips under a table knife because the wax would not hold in the damp. Fresh reins lay over an open tack box, leather still stiff where they had not yet been broken by a hand.

Uther came with them as far as the horse line.

"The outer lane is yours," he told the quartermaster nearest him. "If the main column blocks it, you move the main column."

The man started to answer, saw who had spoken, and only nodded.

Uther looked at Jaina then, properly, perhaps for the first time since the tent.

"If the door is there," he said, "take it before he reaches the wall and decides doors are for other people."

Jaina gave him the smallest bow Sylvanas had ever seen pass for gratitude. "I will."

His gaze shifted to Sylvanas. "And if the door isn't there."

"We'll know quickly," Sylvanas said.

"Quickly is all the day is giving anyone."

He left them then, broad back already angling toward the triage ground again, the hammer at his side and the Light waiting in his hands for the next wounded man. Watching him go, Sylvanas understood why the soldiers on the field had not broken yet. He made endurance look like an ordinary task someone had to do well.

Cyndia came in from the left carrying the packet case and three relay tabs.

"Horses are ready," she said. Then, lower: "Ranger-General."

Sylvanas turned. Cyndia's eyes had gone to one of the quartermaster tables.

An aide stood there in plain campaign wool, helping sort route strips by district and gate mark. Entirely ordinary. Dark hair damp at the temples. Mud on the hem. The kind of man nobody remembered because three hundred camps had raised him in the same shape.

He pressed a packet flat beneath the knife, said something to the boy beside him, then passed the boy a second strip.

When the boy took it, the aide said, clear enough to carry through the shouting:

"May the stars keep your road."

Cyndia's head had already turned before the words ended. Once right. Once right again, harder.

"Fourth time," she said.

Sylvanas looked at the aide's left hand as he reached for another strip. A ring sat there, dark as wet stone, swallowing lamplight instead of catching it.

Then someone slammed a tack chest shut between them, a horse bit at the air, Falric shouted for the lane to clear, and the aide became one more moving body in the crush of necessary work.

Not enough to stop for. Too much to ignore.

"Hold it," Sylvanas said.

Cyndia's mouth thinned. "Yes, ma'am."

Jaina was already at the horses.

They had been given the best of the reserve string: hard-chested field mounts with clean legs and the kind of disciplined eye Silvermoon bred for patrol lines. Foam had dried white at one gelding's bit from an earlier run, but he stood quiet under Cyndia's hand. Another mare danced once, read the rider at her shoulder, and settled.

Jaina had a foot in the stirrup when Sylvanas reached her. Up close the strain showed harder. Not fear. Use. She had already begun spending herself against the day.

"What do you need from me first?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina put her weight into the saddle and swung up. "Speed."

Reasonable.

"After that?"

Jaina gathered the reins, eyes already on the lane opening through the camp. "Anything between me and the stock."

No speech under it. No plea. Just work.

Sylvanas mounted.

By the time her mare settled under her, the main column had begun to form beyond the outer tents. Infantry standards were lifting through the smoke. Mounted officers were cutting across the churned ground toward their places. Dust had started rising behind the first lines already, though the army had scarcely moved. Arthas's pace reached ahead of him.

Cyndia took the left without asking.

Jaina looked once over her shoulder toward the forming column, once toward the road south-east, then snapped the reins and sent her horse through the lane Uther had forced open for them.

They broke from the camp in a three-rider line and hit the road at speed.

The wind took the first shouted words apart, but Sylvanas caught enough.

"North stores first if they still have them—"

"Service gate if the market's choking—" Cyndia said.

"And if the grain's already broken up," Jaina called back, "we go where the bread's moving."

That was all. Not a full plan. Only the first hard edges of one.

Behind them the main column gathered itself into thunder. Ahead, the road to Stratholme ran between fields built to feed a city that might already be eating its own death.

Jaina leaned lower over the horse's neck and drove for distance. Cyndia matched her on the left. Sylvanas settled on the right, where the ground opened and the trouble usually showed first.

Three horses stretched into a faster line. Dust rose behind the army. Smoke held low in the south.

They rode for the head start while there was still one to ride for.
