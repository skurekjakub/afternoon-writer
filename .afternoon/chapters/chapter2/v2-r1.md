# Chapter 2: The Mage

The ward-stones hummed in her teeth.

Jaina had been inside the circle for six hours. The hum lived behind her eyes and in her jaw, too deep to ignore. The bench was cold stone. The bucket was clean. The slit window had gone dark, replaced by ward-blue she could have dismantled in four minutes if they had not taken her staff.

She was going to sit with her hands in her lap, knuckles split where the frost had bitten back, and wait for someone worth talking to.

The officers had been polite, which was worse than hostile. They listened, took notes, and asked the sort of questions that ended in drawers. She had made the same warning three times: once for a border lieutenant, once for a senior scout, once for the dark-braided ranger who had taken her satchel with careful hands and enough sense to know what the instruments were. Cyndia had looked troubled. The others had looked patient.

Boots crossed the yard outside. One set was Cyndia's even stride. The other moved lighter, faster, flat-footed on guarded ground.

The ward-light climbed the threshold. The door opened.

The woman who stepped inside was taller than the frame suggested, silver-blonde hair pulled back for the field, Farstrider armor worn the way a second skin is worn: no settling tug, no show of rank, no wasted motion. Her eyes swept the room once. Jaina had the clear sense of being counted with the bench, the bucket, the window, and the distance to the door.

Ranger-General. It had to be. Cyndia had fallen half a step behind in the way subordinates do when authority is obvious and nobody needs to name it.

Sylvanas Windrunner waited until the wards were the loudest thing in the cell.

Then she said, "You crossed my ward line alone. Start there."

Jaina stood too fast. Stiff legs told on her. She steadied her voice.

"Your southern leylines are contaminated. Something necromantic has been moving through them for weeks. I came north because your ward-stones are carrying the damage, and if I can see it from the Dalaran side of the border, it's already too deep to ignore."

Sylvanas did not move. One hand rested near the hip where a quiver would sit in the field.

"Necromantic."

"Death-aligned. The signature is unmistakable once you've seen clean lines and these beside them." Jaina's hands wanted to map the pattern in the air. She let them. "The damage is in the existing channels under the ward lattice, moving fastest near settled roads. I tracked it south from Dalaran, then north when the trail turned, and your ward-stones picked it up before I crossed."

She heard herself doing it again: careful, pared down, built for someone she assumed needed the simpler version.

"You've said this to my officers."

"Three times."

"You left out the part where a Kirin Tor mage arrives alone instead of with credentials and a delegation."

Everyone in the cell went still around that question.

Jaina had rehearsed the answer on both roads and through the hours in the cell. The honest version was worse. It was also shorter.

"Because nobody believed me."

The ward-hum filled the pause. Cyndia stood at the door without moving. Somewhere out in the lean-to a horse stamped once and got an answer from another.

"I brought my readings to the senior council twice," Jaina said. "First they wanted more proof. Then they asked whether I'd mistaken leyline drift for contamination. I hadn't. Drift doesn't carry a death signature, and it doesn't gather along trade roads. Antonidas knows I left. I wrote him a note. He does not know I crossed the border. I came without Kirin Tor approval because this keeps spreading while people ask for permission slips."

Sylvanas touched the edge of the ward circle without quite touching it. "You left Antonidas a note."

"Yes."

"And crossed a sovereign magical border alone to inspect our ward-stones."

"Yes."

"Because you were right and nobody else could see it."

The way she said it made it sound like an illness.

"Because the contamination does not care who signed off on it."

Sylvanas's mouth thinned. She turned to Cyndia.

"Her things. Where."

"Locked room off the yard. Staff, satchel, field notes, two keyed crystals, one knife."

"Bring the satchel and the map. Leave the staff."

Cyndia left. Sylvanas looked back at Jaina.

"If I ride south and the lines read clean, you go back to your cell and your note to Antonidas becomes a diplomatic problem."

"They won't read clean."

"If they do."

"Then I'm wrong, and you can send me north under guard."

Sylvanas unfolded Jaina's map against the wall and read the blue-marked crossings in silence.

Jaina watched her eyes track the route without hesitation. This was no courtesy glance. Sylvanas was following the logic, weighing roads, villages, and crossings in the same sweep.

"The lines pinch here," Sylvanas said, tapping the village Jaina had circled twice. "And here. Millhaven is the first place you'd feel it from the surface."

While Jaina sat on the bench waiting for someone worth talking to, this woman had already been inside the map.

"Yes," Jaina said. "The wards are damping the signal. Past them, Millhaven should read strongest."

Cyndia returned with the satchel. Sylvanas took the map, folded it, and slid it inside her armor.

"Terms," she said. "You ride with my escort. Your hands stay where my rangers can see them. You do not leave the road without my word. You do not cast without warning. If this is what you say it is, we ride to Millhaven and I see it myself. If it is nothing, you ride north under guard and the diplomats can argue over the rest."

"I can read the lines. I can tell you what the contamination is doing. What I cannot do is keep myself alive on a road that may already be sick. I need your escort."

"Cyndia," Sylvanas called toward the yard. "Pull the anchor stone."

The barrier dropped.

The ward pressure vanished so suddenly Jaina staggered. Her palms hit the wall. Through the stone the southern lines roared up at her: deep, dragged, wrong, the contamination she had been tracing for weeks now uncovered where the wards had kept it muffled. Copper rose on the back of her tongue. Her teeth ached. The room even smelled different without the barrier, the clean ward-scent gone and something metallic waiting underneath.

Worse than eight hours ago. Worse enough to feel in the gap between one breath and the next.

She pushed off the wall and crossed into the yard. Evening air hit her sweat-damp skin. Cyndia stood with the anchor stone in her hand, watching Jaina the way someone watches a lid come off a pot that may boil over.

"My staff," Jaina said.

"Not until we're on the road," Sylvanas said, already moving toward the horses.

The contamination pulsed below the packed yard like a heartbeat in the wrong place. Every moment spent arguing was a moment it had to spread. Jaina bit back the protest and followed.

###

Cyndia brought the staff when the horses were saddled.

She offered it grip-first, careful as someone returning a weapon. The crystal head flared the moment Jaina took it, resonance snapping through her palm like a numb limb waking. The read hit harder than the last one: deeper, uglier, faster.

The escort formed around Sylvanas without discussion. Cyndia on the left. Lyriel on the right with a longbow already strung. Neral at the rear, broad-shouldered and steady, the sort who looked built to keep order when the road broke. Sylvanas spoke once, and the escort dropped into formation.

They brought Jaina a grey Thalassian mare, fine-boned and high-headed, with quiet buckles and well-kept tack. She mounted badly. The mare accepted this with patient contempt, shifted once under her, then settled as if she had carried worse riders in worse weather.

Sylvanas wheeled south, and the escort flowed after her. They left the border post at a canter that ate the last of the light.

The road ran through pine forest while the ward-stones thinned. Jaina felt each one as they passed: a brief clean pulse against the background rot, then a gap, then another pulse weaker than the last. She counted distances under her breath. Thirty yards. Fifty. A hundred. The stones stood along the road like lanterns running out of oil. Whatever silver had paid for this border, it had not stretched far enough south.

The pines thinned to birch. The birch gave way to open country. The last ward-stone fell behind them.

Jaina hauled the mare short.

The full signal slammed through the staff and up her arm. The crystal blazed white-blue. Her shoulders locked. Out here there was no filter left, no ward-pressure shaving the edges off what lay under the road. The contamination pressed at her sternum, copper and turned earth in her throat, wrongness humming under the packed dirt hard enough to make her hands want to shake. She had been reading it through glass. The glass was gone.

"How far," Sylvanas said, reining in beside her.

"Miles. Deep and everywhere south of us. Your ward-stones were holding it back. Past this point it's wide open."

Around them the escort changed shape without a word. Cyndia's eyes went to the tree line. Lyriel drifted wider. Neral settled farther back over the road behind them. The horses felt it too. Ears flicked. The grey mare tossed her head once and went taut under Jaina's knees.

They rode on.

The farmland should have been green. In the failing light it looked washed thin instead, wheat gone grey at the tips, pasture grass dull and tired. Jaina leaned over the staff and read the road through the crystal. The corruption was no longer only in the deep lines. It had climbed into the soil, the shallow water, the roots. If it had reached this far up, then soil and water already held the contamination itself.

"Stop," she called.

Sylvanas raised a hand. The party pulled up.

Jaina dismounted before the mare had fully stopped and pressed her palm into the road.

The wrongness came up through her skin like a pulse. Death-taint, copper, and beneath both a sick liveliness. The corruption had crossed from line to land. It was in the dirt, in the water feeding the fields, in whatever roots still dared drink here. It was not just passing through. It was taking hold.

She stood, hand filthy.

"It's in the ground," she said. "Soil and water both. It's in the soil and the water now as well as the leylines."

Sylvanas watched her from the saddle. "How it gets in."

"I don't know yet. Someone built this. I need the source, or the thickest knot of it."

"Millhaven."

Jaina's hands stopped halfway through the explanation she had been about to give.

Sylvanas had already done the work on the map. Already followed the convergence south.

"Yes," Jaina said, and now her voice came out in its natural cadence, quicker and cleaner. "Three channels knot there. If the contamination is following what already exists, Millhaven is where it bunches hard enough to show its hand. Source, worst damage, or both."

"Or bait."

That possibility hit late and hard. Jaina mounted in silence.

The farther south they rode, the worse the countryside looked. Cold chimneys. Untended fields. A trough green with scum. A cart left loaded in a yard. The staff crystal had begun pulsing on its own at the edges of her sight. Farm after farm sat with its doors shut and no smoke rising, the emptied stillness of places where people had stopped coming out long before sunset.

Rot had reached the crops. Jaina could see it from the road now: soft heads of grain bending too low, leaves gone dark at the edges, damp ground under rows that should have been dry. The corruption had gotten into the land itself, working through the crops and the soil.

Then the village showed through the last of the trees: cold hearths, no smoke, stillness where evening life should have been. Millhaven lay ahead under the dying light, still as a trap.

###

The deer burst from the undergrowth before Jaina understood what she was seeing.

A doe and a young buck, both running wrong. The doe lurched with one foreleg buckling under her. The buck's eyes were filmed over, but it came on with frantic speed. Something dark crusted its antlers.

Lyriel's bow sang. The doe took an arrow through the chest and crashed hard in the road, legs still churning.

The wolves hit from the right before the doe stopped moving.

Three of them. Grey, gaunt, fast. The mare shied so hard Jaina nearly lost the saddle. She grabbed horn and staff together, heart slamming at her ribs, the whole road narrowing to wolves, hooves, and the taste of copper in her mouth.

Sylvanas moved first.

"Neral, horses. Cyndia, left. Lyriel—"

Lyriel was already loosing again. Her second arrow sank into the nearest wolf's shoulder. It twisted and kept coming. Cyndia was moving left with her bow half up, making space for a clean shot around the horses.

The buck charged.

Jaina threw frost.

Fear made the choice before thought could. Ice climbed the buck's forelegs and locked them to the road. It screamed—a raw grinding sound from the wrong part of the throat—and tore at the dirt with its antlers while the frost bit back through Jaina's split knuckles. Blood welled fresh where old burns reopened.

Sylvanas put an arrow through its skull.

One shot. Through the eye. The buck dropped where Jaina's ice held it.

The doe was still trying to rise.

Cyndia's second arrow had gone through the chest a finger from the first. The animal kept scrabbling in the dirt, blind and driven.

"Again," Sylvanas said.

Cyndia's third shot ended it.

By then the wolves were down as well. Neral had three mounts bunched in her fists and held steady through blood, panic, and snapping teeth with the flat calm of long practice. One wolf lay with its jaws open, gums dark, body far too thin for the muscle still on it. The smell coming off the carcasses was not ordinary blood. It carried that same sweet-rot the wind had been dragging down the road.

Jaina slid from the saddle. Her right hand was bleeding where the frost had reopened old damage. She knelt beside the doe and laid her good hand on the body.

Warm.

Warm, and wrong clear through. The necromancy was in the blood and meat, not sitting on the hide like a curse and not waiting for death to start its work. These animals had taken it in while alive—from water, grass, air, whatever this land was feeding them—and the magic had pushed their bodies past the point where they should have stopped. The worst part was the magic in living flesh, keeping it going after sense and mercy had both run out.

She looked up.

"It's in the flesh," she said. "Blood too. They didn't die first and rise after. It got into them while they were alive and kept driving them when their bodies should have failed."

Sylvanas still had an arrow nocked. Her attention flicked from Jaina to the trees to the road ahead.

"How far."

"I don't know. Maybe the whole countryside. Anything drinking here, grazing here, living in this soil could be carrying it." Jaina wiped her bloody hand on her robe. "I can tell you what it's doing. I cannot clear a road or a village alone. If Millhaven is worse than the farms, you'll need rangers, quarantine, and a cordon. Yesterday, if you can manage it."

"First I need your read," Sylvanas said. "The rest I can get."

She turned to the escort.

"Mount. We close on the village before full dark."

They obeyed at once. Lyriel stripped arrows from the carcasses. Cyndia pulled her shafts from the doe. Neral steadied the grey mare for Jaina without being asked.

Jaina hauled herself back into the saddle. Her hands had stopped shaking.

They rode.

The formation had pulled tight now. Cyndia close enough that stirrups nearly brushed. Lyriel ahead with bow in hand. Neral near enough behind that Jaina could hear her horse breathe.

Nobody spoke. Hooves thudded on packed dirt. Leather creaked. The wind carried the same sweet-rotten smell rising from the doe's body. Jaina could feel the rangers listening to the dark on all sides of her, each one holding her own piece of the road.

The road narrowed. Trees thinned. Millhaven gathered out of the dusk by a river bend: low buildings, a still mill wheel, cold chimneys, no light anywhere. No dog barked. No lantern lit for the road. Even the river sounded wrong, too thin under the hush.

Jaina gripped the staff and watched the village come up at them. She had been right about the spread. Right about the signature. Right enough to cross a border alone and ride into this. The part of her that had always trusted being right to carry the day kept reaching for that certainty and finding nothing solid under it.
