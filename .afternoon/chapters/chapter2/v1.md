## Chapter 2: The Mage

"Start talking."

The command hit Jaina like cold water.

She had heard variations of it since dawn. Explain yourself. Start again. Slower. What were you doing at the stones. None of the men who had asked had listened past the shape of her answers. They had wanted a spy with a motive they recognized or a fool they could laugh at once the paperwork was done.

This felt different at once.

The ward-circle still pressed at her skin, a low blue weight that turned every thought of magic into pressure behind her eyes. Its hum filled her teeth. The room smelled of lamp oil, mountain stone, damp leather, elf-made polish. Jaina lifted her head from the confiscated papers on the table and saw the woman who had finally come to decide whether the morning would keep wasting itself.

Tall for an elf. Fair hair pulled back from a face too still to be called calm. Bow at her shoulder, no ornament that was not also a weapon, and a way of standing that made the little holding room seem to have arranged itself around her the instant she stepped inside. Ranger-General, then. It had to be. No one else would have been allowed to carry that much silence with her.

Jaina's throat hurt from repetition. She did not waste what voice remained on courtesy.

"The leylines south of your border are contaminated," she said. "Something necromantic is moving north through them, and your ward-stones have started carrying the same damage."

The elf did not blink. "How far south."

Not disbelief. Not mockery. A question that assumed the answer might matter.

Jaina leaned forward before she could stop herself. Stiff muscles pulled at her back. She had spent too many hours on stone. "I first picked it up north of Andorhal. Faint then. I thought it was residue from a botched working until I found the same wrongness at the next relay point, and the next after that. It strengthened along the road. By the time I reached the lower pass, your wards were answering it."

"Answering how."

Jaina spread her hands on the table and traced the lines she could still feel in memory, one current leaning into another. Ink stained two fingers. The burn-healed skin across her right hand tightened when she curled it. "Like a struck string answering a note. The stones are clean in themselves. The corruption is riding the lattice between them."

One of the rangers by the wall shifted. Cyndia, the auburn-haired officer who had taken her staff and notes, stayed at the door without moving at all. Jaina had spent enough of the morning with her to know that stillness meant the woman was paying attention.

The Ranger-General's eyes dropped once to Jaina's moving hand, then back to her face. "You crossed the ward line alone to tell us this."

"I crossed it to confirm it." Irritation made the words sharper. Good. Let them be sharp. "By the time your patrol found me, I already knew something was wrong. I needed to see whether the contamination survived contact with the stones."

"And did it."

"Yes."

The answer came too fast. Jaina forced herself to slow. Soldiers needed clean edges, not the full shape of the reasoning. "The lines are carrying death magic. Deliberate death magic. Not decay, not runoff from a battlefield, not some hedge-wizard poisoning a spring. Something is using the network of the land itself."

The Ranger-General's expression changed so little Jaina only noticed it because she was watching for disbelief and got concentration instead.

"Death magic," the elf said. "You name that quickly."

"Because it is quick to name." Jaina heard the snap in her own voice and fought it down. "Broadly, at least. I was not trained in necromancy. No one worth respecting is. But there are signatures common to the forbidden disciplines, and this one is plain. The field folds inward. The current does not dissipate. It clings."

She had slipped. Too much vocabulary at once. Jaina heard it a heartbeat late and corrected downward before anyone could ask her to.

"It behaves wrong," she said. "Like something dead refusing to stay dead."

"Better."

The word landed flat as a knife on wood.

Heat crawled up the back of Jaina's neck. She had been cut off before. She had not expected to be cut so exactly. "If you prefer I can give you the childish version in even fewer syllables."

Cyndia inhaled through her nose. One of the wall rangers looked hard at the floor.

The Ranger-General did not so much as glance aside. "If I preferred that, I would have sent for a child."

For one savage instant Jaina liked her.

It passed. Armed custody still ringed the room, her staff still sat on the rack in plain sight and out of reach, and the ward pressing on her magic still made every second feel stolen. But the irritation shifted. A smaller, cleaner thing now. Not the dull fury of being dismissed. The sharper sting of being answered by someone who had heard the shape of the mistake.

"Fine," Jaina said. "The contamination is necromantic. It is preserving itself through transit. The ward lattice is carrying the pressure instead of breaking it, which means whatever started this was built to survive movement through preexisting magical infrastructure. I followed the sequence north until the first of your stones answered it back."

The elf's gaze held her another moment, then moved to the satchel on the floor below the rack. "Your notes."

"Confiscated."

"Your map."

"Also confiscated."

"Your authority to cross my border."

There it was. The soft place in the argument, struck with no wasted motion.

Jaina set her jaw. "I did not have one."

Silence dropped into the room. The wards filled it, steady and indifferent.

"Who sent you?" the Ranger-General asked.

No use dodging. Dodging would smell like fear, and fear was what lesser men had been sniffing for all morning.

"No one." Jaina swallowed against a throat gone raw. "I left Dalaran because the pattern was worsening faster than anyone around me was willing to believe. I left Antonidas a note. By the time anyone read it, I meant to be back with proof."

The words sounded worse spoken aloud than they had in her head on the road north. Less like urgency. More like youthful arrogance with good boots.

The Ranger-General let the weight of that sit where it was. "So the Kirin Tor does not know where you are."

"Not precisely."

"Antonidas did not send you."

"No."

"You crossed into Quel'Thalas on your own judgment."

Jaina wanted to say On correct judgment. The need to say it flared and died. The sentence would have sounded like pleading for praise from a woman whose face suggested praise was rarer than snow in summer.

"Yes," she said instead.

Something in her chest went tight with the admission. She hated that it did. She had come because someone had to come. Because every patient explanation in every clean-lit chamber north of the pass had ended with caution, committee, one more day. Because wrongness moving through the bones of the world did not care what stamp a request form carried. None of that sounded better spoken here, under blue glass, with armed strangers listening.

The Ranger-General looked at her for so long Jaina felt every crease in her road-grimed sleeves.

"Cyndia," she said at last.

"My lady."

"Bring her staff."

Jaina's head came up.

Cyndia crossed to the rack, lifted the staff free, and held it midway instead of giving it outright. Wise. The wards were still up. The staff's crystal tightened like a held breath once it came within reach, familiar and frustrating all at once.

"You said the stones were answering the contamination," the Ranger-General said. "Can you still read it from inside the circle."

"Barely." Jaina kept her eyes on the staff. "Your wards are dampening everything. That was the problem once your people dragged me off the marker. I could still feel enough to know I wasn't mistaken. Not enough to track spread."

"Could you track it if I let you out."

The question struck harder than any accusation had.

Relief came first, hot and humiliating. Dread followed on its heels. If the road south proved her wrong, she would deserve every cold look she had earned in this room. If it proved her right, then hours had already been lost, and whatever moved beneath the land had been feeding while she argued.

"Yes," she said.

"Could you keep yourself alive on the road while doing it."

Jaina opened her mouth and closed it again.

The truthful answer stood there between them with nowhere to hide. She could hold a barrier. She could freeze a man where he stood. She could read a current through miles of soil and stone. She had ridden alone because waiting felt impossible. None of that changed the practical arithmetic of a hostile road in unfamiliar country with her notes in someone else's hand.

"Not as well as I could with an escort," she said, each word scraped clean. "I can read the lines. I cannot watch every tree while I do it."

The Ranger-General's mouth shifted by the smallest degree. Not a smile. Recognition, perhaps, of usefulness honestly named.

"Good," she said. "At least one of us is interested in surviving the experiment."

Jaina would have answered that if she had been given time. She was not.

"Terms," the elf went on. "You ride under escort. You keep your hands where my rangers can see them unless I tell you otherwise. You do not leave the road, and you do not touch another ward-stone without instruction. Your papers stay with me. Your map stays with me. If you run, I put an arrow through your spine and save us both the trouble of a second conversation."

The words were dry enough to make their own weather.

Jaina lifted her chin. "If I intended to run, I would not have spent the morning begging to be released in the opposite direction."

"That is almost reassuring."

There it was again, that dry edge so flat it took a heartbeat to recognize as humor.

Jaina exhaled through her nose. "Fine. Escort me. Watch my hands. Just stop holding me here while the contamination keeps moving."

The Ranger-General turned her head a fraction. "Cyndia. Drop the circle. Bring up six horses. You ride with us."

"Yes, my lady."

Cyndia moved at once. No repeated order, no surprise. The room changed around the command before Jaina had fully absorbed it, rangers peeling away from the walls, boots sounding on stone, the practical speed of people used to action following decision without a layer of ceremony in between.

The Ranger-General held out a hand.

Cyndia set Jaina's staff into it first.

For one wild second Jaina thought the woman meant to keep even that in her own grip. Then the staff turned, offered crosswise, not generous, not trusting, simply efficient.

Jaina stood too fast. Pins and needles lanced up both legs. She caught the table edge, hated the wobble, took the staff anyway. The crystal at its head flashed cold under her palm. Even muted by the ward circle, the contact drove a clean line through the headache behind her eyes.

"Can you walk," the Ranger-General asked, "or shall I have someone carry the great magical authority of Dalaran out by the elbows."

"I can walk."

"Good. Start there."

Jaina bit down on the answer that wanted out. Tiredness hit with ugly clarity. Not soft tired. The kind that lived in the lower back, in the dry blur behind the eyes, in the hollow place after too many hours of repeating a truth to people who preferred easier lies. She followed the Ranger-General out into the pass before the fatigue could show on her face.

Outside, the mountain air hit her like a basin of cold water.

Blue light ran along the ward-stones around the holding yard. Cyndia knelt by the anchor marker near the road, one hand braced to the cut face of the stone while she worked a sequence into the rune groove with the other. Jaina felt the barrier loosen before she saw it go. The pressure over her magic thinned, thinned again, and then dropped all at once.

The world slammed back in.

Jaina caught her breath against the surge. Currents flooded through her senses in overlapping strands, not clean and blue as they should have been, but streaked with that same deep wrong note she had been chasing for days. The nearest ward-stones still held their own pattern. Beyond them, southward, the contamination dragged at everything like rot under varnish.

"It's worse," she said before anyone asked.

She stepped past the circle's broken edge and the reading sharpened so abruptly her hand clenched on the staff. Not imagination. Not fatigue. Not a scholar's panic dressed up as certainty. Worse.

"How much worse," the Ranger-General said.

"Eight hours ago the spread was riding the lines. Now it's biting deeper into them." Jaina turned south, crystal angled toward the road. "If it keeps accelerating at this rate, Millhaven may already be inside the densest knot."

Six horses came up from the stable cut into the rock. Thalassian stock, lean-necked and bright-eyed, bred for distance and steep footing. They felt the edge in the air and tossed their heads, leather whispering, iron ticking on stone. Rangers took reins with the ease of old habit. The mounted detail settled into shape around the Ranger-General without discussion.

Two of the women Jaina had not seen inside the holding room swung up first. One narrow-faced and pale as ashwood, bow already unstrapped across her knees. The other broader through the shoulder, steady hands on a gray gelding that danced once and then obeyed. Lyriel and Neral, Cyndia said as she passed Jaina the reins of a chestnut mare with intelligent ears and too much awareness in her dark eyes. A fourth ranger mounted last, unnamed, already forgettable except for the ease of her seat.

"You ride between me and Cyndia," the Ranger-General said. "If you fall off, do it away from the horses."

"Your bedside manner is remarkable."

"So I've been told."

Jaina mounted with less grace than she wanted and more relief than she would ever admit aloud. Her thighs protested at once. Hours on a bench had left her stiffer than the morning ride north ever had. She settled the staff across her lap, found the mare's mouth, and turned south with the rest of them as the pass opened under the last of the ward-light.

The change came a few hundred yards later.

Inside the border grid the ward-hum had lived under everything, too constant to call sound, more like the shape of safety pressed into the land. Jaina had been annoyed by it when she first approached the pass. Then trapped by it. Now, once the party crossed beyond the last standing stones, she felt its absence like a door torn off its hinges.

The full force of the leylines hit her in one white surge.

She sucked air between her teeth and tightened on the reins hard enough that the mare flicked an ear back in protest. Raw current rushed up through the road, through the horse, through the bones of her wrists. Too bright. Too crowded. The necromantic pressure that had felt muted at the pass now stood exposed in the open land, a copper-rot taste under the tongue of the world.

"Mage," Cyndia said from her right. "Stay in the saddle."

"I am in the saddle."

The Ranger-General looked back once over her shoulder. "For the moment."

Jaina resented the accuracy of that.

Beyond the pass the road unwound south through harsher country, the clean geometry of elven stone giving way to rutted Lordaeron earth. Pines thinned. The wind lost its mountain bite and picked up the smell of damp soil, old thatch, distant standing water. The escort moved differently here. At the post the rangers had occupied space like people at home in it. Out on the road they narrowed, spacing themselves by sightline and flank, bows eased free, every head turning on a slower, meaner rhythm.

Useful. Jaina noticed it only because it contrasted so sharply with the way mages rode when they thought a staff excused them from watching the hedges.

The current beneath the ground tugged again, harder south and west.

"There," Jaina said, and pulled the mare down.

Before anyone could object she was on the ground, boots thudding into the packed road. She dropped to one knee and set the heel of her hand against the dirt.

The earth was cold, damp a finger's depth down. Pebbles bit her skin. She shut out the creak of tack and the wind in the grass and reached with the staff as a brace, letting the crystal open the reading instead of forcing it.

Wrongness answered at once.

Not from the lines alone. From the ground between them. From seep and root and the shallow run of water somewhere under the roadside weeds. The taste of it coated the back of her mouth, metallic, sweet in the sickening way spoiled meat could seem sweet if the nose caught it wrong. It moved through the land like stain through linen, deeper in some threads than others, gathering where water held and living things drank.

"Well," she said, because the word was all that fit for a heartbeat.

"Report," the Ranger-General said.

Only then did Jaina realize the silence around her had a shape.

Lyriel had turned her horse broadside to cover the left verge, arrow already nocked and held low. Neral had drawn the gray behind Jaina's mare, controlling both animals with one hand while the other rested near her own bow. Cyndia watched the trees on the right, not Jaina, not the road, the trees, already treating this as the moment something hungry would choose.

Jaina looked back to the dirt before embarrassment could settle too far into her ribs.

"It's in the ground," she said. "And in the runoff. The lines are carrying it, yes, but they aren't the whole problem. The contamination is taking hold inside living systems."

"Meaning?"

She nearly gave the careful answer again. The smaller answer. The one built to survive puzzled stares from men who heard magic and decided the details were decoration.

"Meaning the land is not simply tainted at the surface," she said, aiming for plain. "Water takes it. Root takes it. Anything drinking or feeding here takes some measure of it in."

"Anything," Cyndia repeated. "Game included."

"Yes."

The Ranger-General swung down from the saddle without flourish and came the last few steps on foot. Even that she did with a soldier's economy, landing light, already turned toward the widest stretch of view. "Can you tell how long."

"No. Not exactly." Jaina pushed up to standing, brushing grit from her palm. "The spread is uneven. Some places hold more of it. There are concentrations, almost knots, where several currents pinch together." She pointed south with the staff. "Millhaven is the first major convergence point on the route I tracked."

"Because."

Again the question struck the weak joint first, but this time Jaina had the answer ready. "Because three local lines cross the river branch there before splitting toward the farms east of the road."

She stopped.

The Ranger-General was already looking at her.

"Go on," the elf said.

Jaina blinked once. "That is the relevant point."

"I know." Dry as dust. "The interesting part is why you assumed I didn't."

The road held still around them. Leather creaked. One of the horses stamped.

Jaina felt the moment she had misstepped with humiliating clarity. She had heard herself simplifying and had not stopped in time. Three local lines, spoken like she was addressing a clerk who needed the map drawn in large bright chalk.

"You read it," she said.

"Your map." The Ranger-General's hand tapped the satchel at her saddle. "Closely, while you were occupying my holding room. Millhaven is where the pressure gathers first. If the spread has speed, we test there before we waste daylight on every empty ditch between here and the border."

No boast in it. No triumph. Only the bare statement of what she had done and what she had concluded from it.

Jaina's fingers, still lifted from the gesture toward the south, closed slowly around the staff.

"Right," she said.

The single word felt inadequate. So did the flush prickling under the road dust on her face. She was angry, mostly at herself. Angry that she had come into this country prepared to fight condescension and had brought her own with her, folded small and tidy beneath the argument.

The Ranger-General waited half a beat.

Jaina inhaled. Started over.

"The local lattice narrows at Millhaven," she said, her natural cadence returning before she could smooth it down. "If something was built to move by current, it would reinforce there. I still cannot tell whether the village is source or casualty, only that the pressure thickens around it. If the contamination has found a way into wells and irrigation channels, the concentration won't remain magical for long. It will become biological by simple contact."

There. Cleaner. Truer.

The Ranger-General gave one short nod. "Mount."

They moved.

Jaina kept her place between Sylvanas and Cyndia now, the formation no longer an insult but a practical fact. The mare's gait ate ground in long easy pulls. Thalassian breeding showed in the horse's balance over broken road, in the quick recovery after each dip and stone, in the animal's refusal to punish Jaina's inexpert tension.

Wind flattened the grass along the ditches. Crows rose from a field ahead, circled once, and settled again without sound.

The farther south they rode, the more the country looked interrupted rather than abandoned. A gate stood open with its latch hanging loose. A cart leaned at the edge of a lane, one wheel sunk into the mud where someone had left it in a hurry and never returned. Then the first farmhouse appeared beyond a stand of alder, low-roofed and colorless under the afternoon light.

No smoke.

Jaina's attention snagged on that before anything else. Hearths should have been going at this hour even in poor country. Bread ovens, wash water, some thin stew trying to become supper. The chimney stood black and bare against a clouded sky, not even the ghost of heat above it.

The garden below the house had gone ragged. Cabbage heads split in the rows. Onion tops had fallen over and rotted where they grew. Something larger than rabbits had been through the turnips and left torn leaves drying in the soil.

Jaina's crystal gave a soft pulse under her hand.

"Do you often pass empty farms without comment in Quel'Thalas," she asked before she could stop herself.

"No," Sylvanas said.

That was all. Still enough.

The next place was worse. A line of washing had been left on the rope until the wind twisted it into itself. A bucket lay on its side beside the well, its spilled water long since turned to mud and then to a dark stain dried into the yard. Chickens should have been there pecking through it. There were no chickens.

Jaina felt the reading before she saw the field behind the house. Grain bent there in an odd patchwork, green in one square, yellowing too soon in another, a third already collapsed in a flattened ring around some invisible center. She looked at it only a moment and then away. No answer in it yet. Only evidence. The lines beneath the road tugged harder and harder south.

"How far to Millhaven," she asked.

"Less than an hour if nothing delays us," Cyndia said.

"Then something will," Sylvanas said.

Jaina turned that over. Cynicism, perhaps. Experience, more likely. She had spent too much of her life in libraries and councils to hear the difference quickly. Out here the land itself supplied it.

Another farmhouse. Another dead chimney. A child's hoop half buried in a ditch where it had rolled and been left. The road narrowed between hedges gone shaggy with neglect. Jaina's shoulders tightened without meaning to. The crystal at the head of her staff reacted in small involuntary flashes now, catching on spikes in the current even when she tried to damp it.

She wished, abruptly and fiercely, that Antonidas were here.

Not for rescue. For confirmation. For one of his maddeningly patient looks that meant yes, you are seeing it correctly, now go one step farther. She had left him a note and ridden north believing brilliance could stand in for witness. On the open road, with cold hearths watching them from the fields, the choice felt lonelier than it had in the stables at dawn.

Ahead, Sylvanas raised one hand.

The whole line checked at once.

Jaina saw the reason a heartbeat later. The mare scented it before she did, ears pinning, muscles bunching under the saddle. Something moved in the trees to the left of the road. Fast. Too heavy for a hare, too ragged in sound for healthy deer.

Lyriel's bow came up.

"Hold," Sylvanas said quietly.

The brush burst open.

The first deer hit the road in a spray of mud and dead leaves, ribs showing sharp under a hide gone dull and patchy. Its eyes were clouded over with a bluish film. Foam flecked its lips. A second crashed after it, one antler broken to a bloody stump, head lolling at an angle that should have sent it to the ground long before now.

For one disbelieving instant Jaina thought sick. Then the first animal saw the horses and charged straight at them.

"Left one," Sylvanas snapped.

Lyriel loosed.

The arrow took the deer high in the chest and drove through. It stumbled, recovered with a convulsive jerk, and kept coming. Jaina's mare screamed and sidestepped hard enough to wrench the reins through her fingers.

Wolves came in from the right.

Not one. Three. Gray shapes low and fast through the ditch grass, hitting the flank of the party with the deer still breaking the line. The first slammed for the unnamed ranger's mount, teeth flashing for the foreleg. Neral hauled the gray across with brutal timing, interposing horse and shoulder, while Cyndia's bow bent in one smooth savage line.

An arrow punched into the lead wolf's side.

It spun, snarling, and Jaina was already moving.

No careful sequence. No measured setup. Fear burned the hesitation out of her so completely that the spell came clean. She drove the butt of the staff into the road and threw frost low and wide, not at the wolves themselves but under them, a skin of ice flashing over churned mud. One beast lost its footing at once, crashed hip-first, rolled screaming into the ditch. The second leaped the slick and kept coming.

"Down," Sylvanas said.

The order was for Jaina. She obeyed before pride had time to interfere, ducking along the mare's neck as an arrow hissed past her ear. Lyriel's shot took the second wolf through the open mouth. It hit the road already dead and slid on Jaina's fresh ice until it struck a stone with a sound like dropped wet cloth.

The third reached the deer instead of the horses. Its jaws closed on the wounded animal's throat. For a heartbeat the two creatures locked together in a blur of blood, fur, and ruined momentum.

Then the deer tore free with half its neck open and kept moving.

Jaina stared.

The first shot had gone through the chest. The second beast was bleeding down both forelegs from brush and stone. A living animal should have folded by now, lungs wrecked, wind gone, body already deciding what it could not survive.

This one came on anyway.

Sylvanas had seen it too. Jaina knew that only from the angle of her horse and the speed with which the elf changed the field. "Eyes," she said. "Or skull. Cyndia."

Cyndia was already drawing.

The broken-antlered deer lunged past Jaina's stirrup close enough that she smelled rot under the hot copper reek of blood. Its jaw worked crookedly. Its breath came in bubbling snorts. Jaina turned in the saddle, staff half-raised, and saw the pulse in it.

Not heartbeat. Wronger.

A hot, stuttering push inside the body where the body's own life should already have been giving way.

"Move," Sylvanas said, and her horse drove forward past Jaina's knee with terrifying precision.

She loosed at such short range Jaina barely saw the draw. The arrow struck the deer's ruined shoulder and knocked it sideways, opening the line.

Cyndia's second shot went through the eye and deep into the skull.

The animal collapsed in an instant, its hindquarters sliding under it. Antlers scored two furrows into the road. Mud sprayed Jaina's boots. The body twitched once, twice, then lay still at last.

Silence came back in torn pieces.

One horse blowing hard. A wolf kicking in the ditch. Leather creak. Neral swearing under her breath while she fought the gray back from panic. Jaina realized she was half out of the saddle, braced on one stirrup and gripping the staff so hard her fingers ached.

Sylvanas looked across the road at each ranger in turn. Counting. Verifying. "Report."

"Lyriel clear."

"Neral clear. Horse nicked, nothing worse."

"Escort clear," the unnamed ranger said, shaken but upright.

"Cyndia clear."

Sylvanas's gaze cut to Jaina last. "Mage."

"Alive," Jaina said.

"Useful."

Then the elf was off her horse and standing over the deer before Jaina could frame a reply.

The wolves mattered less now. One had died in the road. One thrashed once more in the ditch and did not rise. The third dragged itself into the grass on a broken hind leg, leaving a red smear. Lyriel dismounted to finish it without ceremony. Neral soothed the horses in a low steady stream of Thalassian Jaina could not follow except by tone.

Jaina slid from the saddle on legs that felt abruptly unreliable.

The deer on the road looked worse close up. Flies had already found the wet around the eye wound. The hide along its flank showed lesions under the fur, dark crusted patches where the skin had cracked and tried to heal wrong. Jaina crouched and put her hand to the carcass before the body's remaining heat could leach away.

The reading hit her at once.

Heat, yes, but wrong heat, trapped in tissue that had stopped serving life cleanly long before death. Blood thickening in the vessels. Organs soaked with the same pressure she had felt in the soil and water, only stronger here, gathered and answered by flesh itself. Necromantic current threaded through the animal not as an afterthought laid on a corpse, but as something already active before the body had finished dying.

Jaina closed her eyes a moment. Not to shut it out. To feel the pattern without distraction.

The pulse was still there, faint now, fading under the damage Cyndia's final shot had done. Hot wrong beat in dead matter. A thing trying to go on past the point where going on meant anything natural.

"Well," Sylvanas said above her, "that is unpleasant."

The understatement nearly made Jaina laugh. It came out instead as a short frayed breath.

She opened her eyes. Sylvanas had not crowded her, had not spoken over the reading, had not made the common mistake of filling silence because silence felt like ignorance. She was simply waiting. Bow in hand, expression cut from the same hard material as before, waiting for the answer before she decided what the road meant.

Jaina noticed that. She wished she had not, because once noticed it demanded honesty.

"It's in the tissue," she said.

Sylvanas's face did not change. "Plainly."

"No. Listen." Jaina wiped her bloody palm on the dead grass and stood. This time she did not soften a word. "The contamination isn't merely killing what touches it. It's teaching living flesh to answer necromancy before death has fully taken hold. The deer was dying when Lyriel hit it. It should have dropped. Instead the pressure in the body bridged the failing parts and kept driving it forward."

Cyndia had come back within earshot. Lyriel rose from the ditch bank, arrow wiped clean. Neral still held the horses but had turned enough to listen. Jaina heard herself and kept going, faster now, because the shape of it had locked.

"Ground and water are carrying the agent. Animals take it in. Once enough accumulates in blood and organ tissue, the body stops obeying ordinary limits cleanly. I still don't know what first seeded the land, but whatever did this is already active in living creatures. We are past the point of a strange magical disturbance. This is a regional lethal event."

Silence followed. Not blank silence. Working silence, each person fitting the answer into her own craft.

Sylvanas broke it first. "How much farther to the village."

"Close," Cyndia said. "Fifteen minutes at this pace."

"Then we keep moving." Sylvanas's eyes held on Jaina one beat longer. "Can you still read the pressure."

Jaina looked down at the blood drying dark in the lines of her hand. At the deer's half-open eye filmed over in death and something worse that had almost kept it from being death at all.

"Yes," she said.

"Good. On your horse."

No praise. No reassurance. No false comfort offered for the thing on the road behind them.

Good.

Jaina mounted with the answer still hot in her skin. The party re-formed faster now, every motion tighter. Even the unnamed ranger had shed her shaken look. Wolves and diseased deer had a way of clarifying the afternoon. Sylvanas took the lead again without announcement. Jaina fell into place beside her because that was where the work now sat.

The road narrowed between fields gone ragged with neglect. Evening lowered itself over the countryside in a bruised wash of violet and iron. Birds had gone quiet. No farm dogs barked as they passed. No smoke rose anywhere.

Jaina read the pull in the land with each stride of the mare. The currents that had tangled north of the pass now drew into one another with ugly certainty. South, yes. Inward too. Toward a point ahead where the pressure thickened until even without the staff she could have turned toward it blindfolded and named the direction.

Millhaven.

They topped a low rise and saw the village.

It lay in the fold beyond the road, roofs dark against the failing light, clustered close around a narrow center lane and the black cut of the stream. From this distance it might have been peaceful, a poor settlement settling into evening. Then the details sharpened.

No cooksmoke.

No lamp at any window.

No movement in the yards.

The whole place sat under the sky like a held breath gone too long. Jaina felt the knot of contamination there so strongly the crystal in her staff brightened of its own accord, blue light crawling over her hand and the mare's mane.

Beside her, Sylvanas went still in that particular way that meant attention honed to a point.

"That it," she asked.

Jaina could not take her eyes off the village. The road into it looked narrower than any road had a right to look with this much silence laid over it.

"Yes," she said. Her voice came out lower than she intended. "That's where it gathers."

No one spoke after that.

They rode down toward Millhaven together, six horses and six shadows stretching long before them on the empty road, while the last light drained from the fields and the dead village waited without a single hearth burning.
