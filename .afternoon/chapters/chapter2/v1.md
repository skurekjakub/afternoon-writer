# Chapter 2: The Mage

The ward-stones hummed in her teeth.

Jaina had been inside the circle for six hours, and the hum had settled into her bones the way a headache settles behind the eyes — not sharp enough to scream about, too deep to forget. The bench was cold stone. The bucket was clean. The slit window had stopped giving useful light an hour ago, replaced by the blue glow of wards she could have dismantled in four minutes if they hadn't confiscated her staff.

She was not going to dismantle the wards. She was going to sit on the bench with her hands in her lap, knuckles split and throbbing where the frost had bitten back, and wait for someone worth talking to.

The officers had been polite. They had listened, taken notes, asked the kind of questions that told her they were going to file the notes in a drawer. She had explained the contamination three times in three different registers — once for the border lieutenant, once for the senior scout, once for the ranger with the dark braid and the sharp green eyes who had confiscated her satchel with the efficient gentleness of someone who understood what the instruments meant. That one, Cyndia, had at least looked troubled. The others had looked patient, which was worse.

Now someone was coming across the yard. Two sets of boots: one she recognized as Cyndia's even stride, and one she did not — lighter, faster, the flat-footed placement of someone who moved through guarded ground without thinking about it.

The ward-light climbed the threshold. The door opened.

The woman who stepped inside was taller than the doorframe suggested, silver-blonde hair pulled back for the field, Farstrider armor worn the way a second skin is worn — without adjustment, without display. Her eyes swept the room once. Jaina had the clear, immediate sense of being included in an inventory that also contained the bench, the bucket, the window, and the distance to the door.

Ranger-General. It had to be. Cyndia had fallen in half a step behind the way subordinates fall in behind someone whose rank lives in the way she enters a room, not in anything she needs to announce.

Sylvanas Windrunner let the silence pull tight until the ward-light was the loudest thing in the cell.

Then she said, "You crossed my ward line alone. Start there."

Jaina stood. Too fast — she could feel it, the eagerness reading as desperation instead of urgency, stiff legs betraying the hours on stone. She steadied her voice and aimed it at the one thing that mattered.

"Your southern leylines are contaminated. Something necromantic is moving through them, and it's been moving for weeks. I came north because your ward-stones are carrying the damage, and if I can see it from the Dalaran side of the border, it's already too deep to ignore."

Sylvanas did not move. Her weight stayed forward, one hand resting near the hip where a quiver would sit in the field, and the silence stretched long enough for Jaina to hear her own heartbeat against the hum.

"Necromantic," Sylvanas said. One word, flat as a table. Not a question. Not quite a dismissal. A word laid out to see what Jaina would build on top of it.

"Death-aligned. The signature is unmistakable once you know what clean leylines look like and what these look like instead." Jaina's hands wanted to trace the patterns in the air — she let them, because the alternative was standing with fists clenched and ink-stained fingers curled against her palms. "The corruption isn't surface damage. It's traveling through existing magical channels, deeper than the ward lattice, and it's moving faster near population routes. I tracked it south from the Dalaran observatories, then north when the trail turned cold, and your ward-stones picked it up at the second ring before I crossed."

She was talking too carefully. She could hear it in her own voice — the downshifted, over-translated version she used when she expected to be misunderstood. The lines are carrying the damage. That's why I came north. Plain words for a soldier's ears, stripped of the precision that would have made the argument land faster if the person listening could keep up.

"You've said this to my officers."

"Three times. In three different versions, each one simpler than the last."

"I know." Sylvanas's gaze had not left her face. It was not the blank assessment of the officers who had listened and filed; it was the focused, stripping attention of someone who was judging the argument and the person making it in the same breath. "You left out the part where you explain why a Kirin Tor mage is alone at my border instead of arriving with credentials and a delegation."

The room got smaller. Not the walls — the air between Sylvanas's question and the answer Jaina did not want to give.

She had rehearsed this. She had rehearsed it on the road south from Dalaran, and again on the road north into Quel'Thalas, and again in the three hours of silence after the officers stopped asking questions. The honest version was worse than the rehearsed one, and they both ended in the same place.

"Because nobody believed me."

The ward-hum filled the gap where a response should have been. Cyndia stood at the door, expression unreadable, and Jaina could hear a horse stamp once in the lean-to and get an answer from another.

"I brought the readings to the senior research council," Jaina said. "Twice. The first time they asked for more data. The second time they asked whether I had considered natural leyline drift. I had. It isn't drift. Drift doesn't carry a death signature. Drift doesn't accelerate near trade roads." Her voice had roughened. Six hours of making the same case to faces that wanted a smaller problem. "Antonidas knows I left. I wrote him a note explaining where I was going and why. He does not know I crossed the border. The Kirin Tor did not send me. Nobody sent me. I came because the contamination is spreading and every day I spend asking permission is a day it gets worse."

The silence that followed was not the patient silence of the officers. It was the hard silence of someone weighing what she had heard against what she intended to do about it.

Sylvanas's hand moved to the edge of the ward circle — not quite touching the barrier, fingers testing the air where the ward pressure lived. "You left Antonidas a note."

"Yes."

"And crossed a sovereign magical border alone, without authorization, to inspect our ward-stones."

"Yes."

"Because you were right and nobody else could see it."

The way she said it made it sound like a disease. Jaina's jaw tightened. "Because the contamination doesn't care who authorized me to look at it."

Something shifted in Sylvanas's posture — not softening, nothing so generous. A decision moving from assessment into action, the way a hand moves from the bowstring to the release. She turned to Cyndia.

"Her things. Where."

"Locked room off the yard. Staff, satchel, field notes, two keyed crystals, one knife."

"Bring the satchel and the map. Leave the staff."

Cyndia left without a word. Sylvanas turned back to Jaina, and the quality of her attention had changed — still sharp, still hard, but directed now. The interrogation had become something else.

"If I ride south and the leylines read clean, you go back to your cell and your note to Antonidas becomes a diplomatic incident."

"They won't read clean."

"If they do."

"Then I'm wrong, and you can file me with the officers who already decided that."

Sylvanas's mouth moved — not quite a smile, closer to the expression of someone who had heard an answer she expected and found it adequate rather than impressive. She pulled Jaina's folded map from inside her armor where she must have carried it from Cyndia's briefing, unfolded it against the wall, and studied the ley crossings Jaina had marked in blue ink along the southern approaches.

Jaina watched her read. The Ranger-General's eyes tracked the route without hesitation — not the squinting scan of someone deciphering unfamiliar notation, but the focused sweep of someone who already understood where the trouble gathered and was checking whether the map confirmed it.

She had read it before. Closely. While Jaina sat on her bench and waited for someone worth talking to, Sylvanas had been reading her map and following the route logic on her own.

The realization landed like a correction: a sharp, small adjustment to a model Jaina had been running since the door opened. She had been talking to this woman the way she talked to the border officers — careful, plain, translated down for a military mind that needed the mage version stripped to its simplest parts. And the military mind had been ahead of her the whole time, following the contamination spread with the same structural logic Jaina used, just mapped onto terrain and patrol routes instead of arcane theory.

It was an uncomfortable revision. Jaina was used to being the fastest person in a room. She was used to simplifying, to waiting, to the particular loneliness of understanding a problem that no one around her could see. She was not used to discovering that someone had seen it first and simply not mentioned it.

"The lines pinch here." Sylvanas tapped the map where three ley crossings converged south of the border, near a village Jaina had circled twice. "And here. Millhaven is the first place where you would feel it from the surface."

"Yes." Jaina's voice had changed without her deciding to change it — the careful, thinned-down register falling away, replaced by the sharper cadence she used when someone could actually keep up. "The wards dampen the signal. Once you're past them, the contamination should read at full strength. If it's still progressing at the rate I measured three days ago, Millhaven's leylines will be saturated."

"And the village."

"I don't know. The contamination in the lines is necromantic. What it does when it reaches living ground — living water, living soil, living people — I don't have enough data to say. That's why I crossed your border. I needed to get closer."

Sylvanas folded the map and put it back inside her armor. Jaina's map, in Sylvanas's keeping, the route test hers to verify rather than a blind act of faith in a human mage who had arrived without credentials.

"Terms," Sylvanas said. "You ride with my escort. Your hands stay where my rangers can see them. You do not leave the road without my word. You do not cast without warning. If this proves to be what you say it is, we ride to Millhaven and I see it for myself. If it proves to be nothing, you ride north under guard and the diplomats can argue about it."

"I can read the leylines," Jaina said. "I can find the contamination and I can tell you what it's doing. What I cannot do is keep myself alive on a road that might already be sick. I need your escort and I know it."

Sylvanas heard that. Jaina could see it land — not as weakness, but as the admission of a specialist who understood her own operational limits. Usefulness, weighed and accepted.

"Cyndia," Sylvanas said, raising her voice toward the yard. "Pull the anchor stone."

The barrier dropped.

It happened in a breath: the ward pressure that had been leaning on Jaina's senses for six hours simply vanished, and in the gap where it had been, the raw leyline noise of the southern border came flooding back like light after a blindfold. Jaina staggered. Her hands found the wall, and through the stone she could feel it — the deep, dragged wrongness in the lines below, the necromantic contamination she had been tracking for weeks, now screaming where the wards had kept it muffled.

Worse. It was worse than eight hours ago. The dragged-hum she had felt through the wards was a whisper under glass; this was the thing itself, and the thing was moving, and the thing was hungry, and the thing was in the ground beneath her feet.

The room smelled different without the ward — a faint copper undertone that had been there the whole time, masked by the barrier's filtration. Jaina breathed it in and tasted the contamination on the back of her tongue, and her body knew before her mind did that the problem was not abstract anymore.

She pushed off the wall, legs stiff, knees protesting the stone, and crossed the threshold into the yard where the evening air hit her sweat-damp skin and Cyndia stood with the anchor stone in her hand, watching her with the expression of someone who had just let a problem out of its box.

"My staff," Jaina said.

"No." Sylvanas was already moving, crossing the yard toward the lean-to where the horses stamped. "Not until we're on the road."

The contamination pulsed in the lines below Jaina's feet — a slow, sick throb like a heartbeat in the wrong place. Every minute she stood here was a minute it spread further. She bit back the argument and followed.

###

Cyndia brought the staff when the horses were saddled.

She held it out grip-first with the care of someone returning a weapon, and when Jaina's fingers closed around the shaft the crystal head flared once, resonance snapping back through her palm like a nerve waking up. The leyline read hit her immediately — deeper, uglier, and faster than the last time she had held the staff four hours and six explanations ago.

The party formed around Sylvanas without discussion. Cyndia mounted and took the left side. Two rangers Jaina hadn't seen before fell in on the flanks — one dark-haired with a longbow already strung, the other broader and steadier, handling the horses with the unhurried competence of someone who could ride hard and still keep formation in the dark.

"Lyriel, right," Sylvanas said, and the dark-haired ranger shifted without a word. "Neral, trail. If I need a dispatch north, you ride."

Neral nodded once. She looked like someone who had heard that order before and understood what it meant: if things went wrong enough to need a warning sent home, she was the one fast enough to carry it.

They brought Jaina a horse — a grey Thalassian mare, fine-boned and high-headed, with light tack and quiet buckles. The mare stood steady while Jaina mounted badly, one hand on the staff, the other on the saddle, and settled with a patient sidestep that said she had carried worse riders in worse conditions. Good leather, no jangle. Farstrider gear was expensive because it was maintained, not because it was showy, and the horse knew the difference between a ranger and a passenger and had decided to tolerate the latter.

Sylvanas wheeled south without looking back, and the escort fell in around her like water finding its course, and they left the border post at a canter that ate the last of the evening light.

###

The road ran south through pine forest, and the ward-stones thinned as they rode.

Jaina could feel each one as they passed it — a brief pulse of ordered magic against the contamination noise, then a gap, then another pulse weaker than the last. The network was strung along the road like lanterns, and the intervals were getting longer. Whatever the ward budget allocated for the southern border, it ran out well before the border itself did.

She counted the gaps. Thirty yards between the first two stones. Fifty between the next. A hundred. The contamination signal rose in each gap like water filling a hole, and each time the next ward pulled it back down, and each time it pulled less. Her staff crystal flickered with each transition — blue-white to amber-tinged and back, the color shift tracking the ratio of clean to corrupted ley energy in the ground beneath them.

The pines gave way to birch, and the birch to open country, and the last ward-stone passed behind them.

The wards ended like a door closing behind them.

One stride the hum was there — the deep bass vibration of the Thalassian ward network, familiar now after hours of sitting inside it, a pressure she had stopped noticing the way you stop noticing the weight of water until you surface. The next stride it was gone, and the air changed, and the road changed, and everything south of the border hit Jaina at full volume.

She pulled the mare up short and sat very still in the saddle while the leyline signal slammed through her staff and into her grip and up through her arm like putting her hand on a hot stove. The crystal head blazed white-blue in the dusk. Her teeth ached. Her shoulders had gone rigid without her permission, every muscle bracing against the noise.

The wards had been dampening what she felt. All those hours in the cell, the contamination had been there beneath her — and the ward network had been cutting it in half, in a third, filtering it down to the dragged hum she had been able to bear. Out here, past the last stone, the full signal was a physical pressure against her sternum, a taste in the back of her throat like copper and turned earth, and a wrongness in the lines below that made her hands want to shake. She had been reading the contamination through glass. The glass was gone.

Worse. Measurably, viscerally worse than eight hours ago. The spread rate she had calculated on the road from Dalaran was wrong — too conservative, too hopeful, the kind of estimate you produce when you want the numbers to be manageable. These numbers were not manageable.

"How far." Sylvanas had pulled up beside her. The Ranger-General's face was unreadable in the failing light, but her voice carried the focused calm of someone who had just watched a specialist confirm the worst version of the briefing.

"Miles," Jaina said. "The contamination is deep and it's everywhere south of us. Your ward-stones were holding it back. Everything past this point is wide open."

The escort had changed around them. Cyndia's posture had tightened; her eyes swept the tree line instead of the road. Lyriel had drifted wider on the right, longbow resting across her thigh, covering the angle where the forest pressed closest. Neral held the trail position with the settled stillness of someone whose job had just shifted from road duty to rearguard.

They were out of their home ground, and they knew it.

Sylvanas said nothing for a moment. The horses shifted beneath them — the grey mare and Sylvanas's tall bay both uneasy, ears flicking, reacting to something in the air or the ground that training couldn't quite override. Then the Ranger-General touched her heels to the bay's flanks and the party moved south again, faster now, the canter stretching into a rhythm that covered ground.

The road ran through farmland that should have been green. In the last of the light, Jaina could see the fields on either side — wheat that should have been heading, grass that should have been thick with summer — and something was wrong with all of it. Not dead, not yet, but dulled, the color leached to a grey-green that made the landscape look like a painting left in the rain.

She gripped the staff and let the crystal do the work, reading the leylines through the road itself. The corruption was in the ground. Not just in the deep magical channels where she had first tracked it, but in the topsoil, the water table, the root systems of the crops wilting in the fields. Whatever was moving through the land was taking hold inside living systems — soaking in, not just passing through.

She needed to stop and put her hands in the dirt. She needed to confirm whether the corruption had crossed from the leylines into the physical world, because if it had, this was not a magical disturbance. This was an invasion.

"I need to check the ground," she called to Sylvanas.

The Ranger-General raised a hand and the party pulled up. Jaina dismounted before the mare had fully stopped, dropped to one knee in the packed earth of the road, and pressed her palm flat to the dirt.

The wrongness came up through her skin like a pulse. Copper-taste, death-taint, and underneath it a sickly vitality — the contamination was not merely poisoning the soil. It was changing it. The corruption was entering living tissue and living root systems and rewriting them from the inside, and the necromantic signature she had been tracking in the leylines was here, in the dirt under her hand, in the water that fed the wheat, in everything that grew and drank and lived in this ground.

Lyriel and Neral had split to cover opposite angles while Cyndia watched the tree line, and Jaina had a brief, distant awareness that she was kneeling alone in a darkening road in hostile country without looking up, and that the three rangers were covering her because she would have stayed kneeling until something bit her.

She stood. Her hand was dirty and her knees were wet and the reading was as bad as she had feared.

"It's in the ground," she said. "Not just the leylines. The contamination has crossed into the soil and the water. Whatever is moving through this land isn't sitting on the surface — it's taking root inside living systems. The earth, the crops, the water table. Everything here that grows is already carrying it."

Sylvanas was watching her from the saddle, and for the first time since the holding room, the quality of her attention had a thread of something other than judgment. Calculation, maybe. The fast, cold arithmetic of a field commander hearing a specialist confirm that the problem is bigger than the original briefing suggested.

"How it gets in," Sylvanas said.

"I don't know yet. The signature is necromantic — death magic, and not the accidental kind. Someone built this. But I can't tell you the delivery path from a road sample. I need the source point, or at least the thickest concentration. And based on my map—"

"Millhaven," Sylvanas said.

Jaina's hands stopped in mid-gesture. She had been about to explain the ley convergence — the way the three southern channels pinched together at the village, the way contamination traveling through existing infrastructure would pool at the junctions the way water pools at a low point in a field. She had been about to explain it carefully, the way she explained things to people who needed the plain version.

Sylvanas had not needed the plain version. She had read the map, followed the route logic, and arrived at the same conclusion before Jaina opened her mouth.

"Yes," Jaina said. "Millhaven." And her voice was different now — not the careful, downshifted register she had used in the cell and again on the road. Her natural cadence, sharper, faster, more exact: the way she talked to people who could keep up, because this woman could keep up and Jaina had been too busy assuming otherwise to see it. "The ley channels converge there. If the contamination is following existing infrastructure — and it is, the spread pattern is too clean for natural drift — then Millhaven is where it knots tightest. Whatever is happening to the land, the village is either the source, the worst casualty, or both."

"Or bait," Sylvanas said.

Jaina had not considered bait. The word sat in her chest like a stone, because it was a field-judgment word, a tactical-assessment word, and it came from a kind of intelligence she had been discounting since the moment the holding-room door opened.

She mounted without answering. The horse tack creaked; the road stretched south into deepening dusk; and the party closed ranks and rode.

The farther south they went, the worse it got.

Cold chimneys first. A farmstead set back from the road with no smoke, no light, no sound of livestock. Then another, half a mile on, the same dead quiet — doors closed, fields untended, a water trough green with scum that should have been cleaned a week ago. The staff crystal had begun reacting without Jaina's command, pulsing a faint blue-white at the edges of her vision, keying on the contamination density the way a compass needle keys on iron.

Rotting crops in the fields. Not the slow rot of neglect — the grey-green leaching she had seen from the road, but worse here, closer to the village, the plants soft and foul-smelling and the ground underneath damp with something that was not rain. A cart stood in a dooryard with its load still on, sacks of something gone to ruin. Jaina did not need to open them to know what the contamination would read.

Households already failing. Families gone before the party reached the village center. Whatever was happening in Millhaven, it had been happening long enough to empty the farms around it, and the emptying had been quiet — no burned roofs, no broken doors, just the slow, terrible absence of people who had stopped coming outside.

The road narrowed and the silence closed in and the staff blazed in Jaina's hand, and ahead through the last of the trees the village sat against the darkening sky with cold hearths and no smoke and the stillness of a place that had stopped being lived in.

###

The deer came out of the trees before Jaina had time to understand what she was seeing.

Two of them, bursting from the undergrowth on the left side of the road — a doe and a young buck, running wild-eyed and wrong. The doe's gait was lurching, one foreleg buckling with each stride, and the buck had a film across its eyes like cataracts but moved with a desperate, driven speed that had nothing to do with the ruined look of the rest of it. Its antlers were caked with something dark.

Lyriel's bow sang. The first arrow took the doe through the chest and she went down hard, sliding in the dirt, legs still working.

The wolves hit from the right before the doe stopped moving.

Three of them, grey and gaunt and fast, coming out of the tree line at the horses' flanks with the silent coordination of pack hunters who had done this before. The grey mare shied hard and Jaina grabbed the saddle horn with one hand and the staff with the other and her heart was hammering against her ribs, the world narrowing to the wolves and the horses and the taste of copper in her mouth.

Sylvanas moved. Not the slow, considered movement of someone weighing her options — the instant, whole-body response of a commander who saw the entire mess at once. Where the wolves were, where the horses were, who had a clear shot and who was tangled in reins. She saw all of it in the time it took Jaina to get her staff up. "Neral, horses. Cyndia, left. Lyriel—"

Lyriel was already loosing again, drawing and releasing in a motion so fast the arrow seemed to appear in the nearest wolf's shoulder. It yelped and twisted and kept coming.

The buck charged.

Jaina threw frost.

It left her hand before she made the decision — fear burning through the hesitation she had been carrying since the holding room, since the careful explanations, since the hours of sitting on stone and proving she was not a threat. The ice hit the buck's legs and locked them to the road, white crystal climbing the forelegs and freezing them to the packed earth. The buck screamed — a sound no deer should make, a raw, grinding shriek that came from the wrong part of the throat — and its antlers scored the dirt as it fought the ice, filmed eyes rolling, mouth working around sounds that had no business coming from a living animal.

The cold bit back through Jaina's hand. Her split knuckles cracked fresh and a line of blood welled across her index finger, the old frost burns reopening where the magic drew too hard.

Sylvanas put an arrow through the buck's skull. Clean, fast, exactly placed — one shot, through the eye, in the three-inch window between the antler base and the ear. The buck dropped and lay still and the ice held it there, Jaina's frost and Sylvanas's arrow finishing what the contamination had started.

But the doe was still moving.

Cyndia's second arrow had gone through the doe's chest a full inch from the first, and the animal was still trying to stand, forelegs scrabbling in the dirt, and the look in its filmed eyes was not the wild panic of a wounded deer. It was the blind, driven insistence of a body that did not know how to stop.

"Again," Sylvanas said, and Cyndia drew and put the third arrow through the doe's skull, and the legs stopped working, and the body lay still in the road in the thickening dusk.

Jaina's hands were shaking.

The wolves were down — Lyriel and the unnamed fourth ranger had finished them while Jaina watched the deer die, and the road was quiet again except for the horses blowing hard and sidestepping the smell of blood and worse. Neral had the reins of three mounts bunched in her fists, holding them steady through main force and the kind of nerve that only long field practice could build. One of the wolves lay with its jaws still open, and even in death the wrongness showed — the gums grey-black, the tongue swollen, the body thinner than any healthy wolf should be and yet still carrying muscle tone that suggested it had been hunting hard until the moment the arrow stopped it.

Jaina dismounted. Her legs were unsteady and her right hand was bleeding where the frost had split the knuckle fresh. She walked to the doe's carcass and knelt beside it and put her good hand on the body.

Warm. Still warm, and underneath the warmth a pulse of something that was not life. The necromantic signature was in the tissue — in the blood, the organs, the deep structures of the body. Not laid on from outside, not a surface curse or a post-mortem animation. The contamination had entered this animal through the environment — through the water, the grass, the air — and it had taken root in living flesh while the deer was still alive.

The violence had begun before clean death. The buck's driven charge, the doe's inability to stop moving — those were not reanimation. Those were the behaviors of living bodies already under necromantic pressure, flesh that had been conditioned until the magic had a foothold deep enough to keep the muscles firing past the point where the animal should have dropped.

"It's in the tissue," Jaina said. She looked up at Sylvanas, who stood over her with an arrow still nocked, watching the tree line. "Blood, organs, everything. This isn't a surface taint — the necromancy is inside the body. It's entering through the environment. The water, the soil, the food chain. These animals didn't die and get raised. They were alive when the contamination took hold, and it kept them moving past the point of death."

She had stopped translating downward. The diagnosis came out in her natural cadence, fast and precise, because Sylvanas was waiting for the answer the way a field commander waits for a scout's report — to use it, not to admire it — and Jaina could see that the full version was exactly what she needed. No careful version. No simpler words for a soldier's ears. The soldier's ears were sharper than she had given them credit for, and the lost time she had spent simplifying was time the contamination had used to spread.

Sylvanas lowered the bow. Her eyes moved from Jaina to the tree line to the road south and back to Jaina, the calculation visible in the way her weight shifted — already thinking about the next mile, the next problem, the village waiting in the dark.

"How far."

"I don't know. The whole countryside could be carrying it. Everything that drinks the water, eats the grass — anything living in this soil is a host waiting to turn." Jaina pulled her hand from the carcass and wiped her fingers on the hem of her robe. The blood mixed with the wet and the dirt, and she tried not to think about what she had just put her hands inside. "I can read the contamination and I can tell you what it's doing. I cannot clear a road or a village on my own. If Millhaven is as bad as the farms around it, you'll need more than my diagnosis. You'll need rangers and quarantine and a cordon, and you'll need them yesterday."

"I'll need your diagnosis first," Sylvanas said. "The rest I can get."

She turned to the escort. The rangers were already resetting — Lyriel collecting arrows from wolf carcasses, Neral steadying the horses, Cyndia pulling her shafts from the doe with the methodical calm of someone cleaning up after a drill. They moved around Sylvanas the way water moves around a stone: naturally, without discussion, each one knowing where to be.

"Mount," Sylvanas said. "We close on the village before full dark."

Jaina wiped her hand on her robe, took the grey mare's reins from Neral, and pulled herself back into the saddle. Her hands had stopped shaking. The staff crystal blazed steady blue-white, reading the contamination density, pointing south toward Millhaven like a finger pointing at a wound.

They rode.

The formation had changed. The loose, wide spread of the first hour — rangers covering the flanks at comfortable distance, Neral trailing far enough back to watch the whole road — had pulled tight. Cyndia rode close enough that her stirrup nearly brushed Jaina's. Lyriel had moved ahead, bow in hand, no longer slung. The ranger rode the way Jaina's hands moved over a ley-reading: scanning, testing, not committing to rest. Neral had closed the gap behind until Jaina could hear the breathing of her horse.

Nobody spoke. The hooves hit the packed road in uneven rhythm, and the leather creaked, and the wind carried a faint sweetness that Jaina recognized from the doe's carcass — organic matter going wrong, decay accelerated past its natural pace.

The road narrowed. The trees thinned. The last farmsteads fell behind them, and ahead the village resolved through the dusk — low buildings huddled against a river bend, a mill wheel standing still, chimneys cold, no lights in any window. Smoke absence. The kind of silence that made the horses' hoofbeats sound too loud and the creak of leather too sharp and the staff's blue-white pulse the brightest thing in the fading world.

Millhaven sat in the gathering dark the way a body sits in a sickbed — still, and wrong, and waiting.

Jaina gripped the staff and watched the village grow closer and could not shake the thought that had been building since the farmsteads, since the deer, since Sylvanas said the word bait and made her see the shape of something she had been too focused on the leylines to consider.

She had come to Quel'Thalas because she was the only person who believed the contamination was real. She had crossed a sovereign border alone because the Kirin Tor would not move and the contamination would not wait. She had been right about all of it — the spread, the signature, the necromantic design — and being right had brought her here, to a dead village on a dying road, with a woman who did not trust her and a contamination she could diagnose but not cure.

She was right about the contamination. She had always been right about the contamination.

She was beginning to understand that being right was not going to be enough.
