# Chapter 2: The Mage

The shadow of the elven commander cut across the ward circle, long and sharp against the blue light of the containment lattice.

"You crossed my ward line alone," the elf said. The voice was smooth, carrying the low rasp of someone accustomed to having orders followed before the sentence ended. "Start there."

Jaina didn't need an introduction. She had never met Sylvanas Windrunner, but the Ranger-General's reputation reached all the way to the Violet Citadel, and the absolute, rigid deference of the cell guards confirmed it. The rangers had stopped leaning against the walls. They stood at perfect attention. This was the woman with the power to either listen to the warning, or forget Jaina in a deeper hole until the world burned.

Jaina's throat felt like old paper after hours of repeating the same answers to officers who had only heard the noise of her arrival and cared nothing for the substance of it. She swallowed hard, trying to summon saliva that wasn't there, and kept her hands perfectly still over her knees. The cold of the stone floor had seeped through her breeches long ago, settling deep into her joints until her hips ached. The perimeter pressure of the elven ward-stone pushed against her natural reserves, an unyielding, continuous bass note that made the roots of her teeth hurt. It tasted faintly of ozone, old dust, and the heavy copper tang of foreign wards.

"I crossed because the lines are carrying damage from the south," Jaina said. Her voice cracked slightly, rougher than she intended after hours of silence, but she didn't apologize. Apologies were for diplomats playing games in Silvermoon. "It's spreading along the major ley corridors. It's reached your border stones."

Sylvanas watched her. The Ranger-General's attention felt entirely different from the border captain who had arrested her. It was a predator's geometry—narrow, physical, and perfectly still. The kind of stillness that meant every exit, angle, and weapon in the room had already been catalogued and dismissed as insufficient to save the prisoner. Her pale eyes flicked down to Jaina's travel-stained boots, up to the ink stains and frost-burns on her knuckles, and finally back to her face.

"Show me where it breaks," Sylvanas said.

"It doesn't break." Jaina shifted, ignoring the scream of stiff muscles, and lifted her bruised hands to trace the invisible structure of a leyline in the damp air between them. "It's not a strike against the lattice. That would be loud, and your wards would repel it. This is contamination. It's inside the conduit itself, moving with the flow of the earth. A necromantic signature."

Cyndia, standing half a step behind Sylvanas's right shoulder, shifted her weight. Leather creaked. She had heard the explanation before, down in the dirt by the ward-stone, but the word *necromantic* landed differently now that her commander was in the room to weigh it.

"You're a Kirin Tor apprentice," Sylvanas said, her voice entirely flat, devoid of either curiosity or belief. "You study arcane theory in a tower. Necromancy is a banned discipline."

"I know the broad signature," Jaina said, her voice sharpening instinctively into the exact, clipped cadence she used with Antonidas when he was being deliberately obtuse with her research. "I don't need to practice a banned school to recognize when a death-tainted current is bleeding into the preserving shell of the ward network. It feels like rotting ice. I followed that wrongness north for a hundred miles. Your stones are echoing it because the earth south of here is already soaked in it."

She realized her mistake a second too late. Sylvanas's eyes narrowed imperceptibly. Jaina had let her tone slip into the lecture hall; she was talking to a frontline soldier, not a peer in the Violet Citadel who needed a debate.

Sylvanas didn't blink. "And yet you came alone. No Kirin Tor delegation. No diplomatic seal. You crossed a closed border on a theory."

The stone room felt suddenly colder. Outside the narrow slit window, the wind shifted, and the ward-hum rushed in to fill the silence between them.

"I came alone because nobody in Dalaran believed me," Jaina said, hating how small, how incredibly inadequate the truth sounded out loud. "The council requires proof before they mobilize. They want soil samples, documented decay rates, and three signed affidavits from local magistrates before they'll admit the sky is falling. This—" She lifted her bruised hands again, the ink stains dark and desperate against the raw, split skin. "This is the proof. The leylines are failing. I left a note for Antonidas so he would know what direction I took. That's all."

She waited for the dismissal. She waited for Sylvanas's lip to curl. In Dalaran, they had called her obsessed. In Quel'Thalas, she fully expected Sylvanas to see an isolated, unhinged student chasing phantoms, a political nuisance to be shipped back across the border with a stern letter.

Instead, Sylvanas stepped closer. Her soft leather boots made almost no sound on the grit-covered stone. Her hand brushed the very edge of the blue ward-light, long fingers relaxed but ready. "So you have no authority," Sylvanas said softly. "No backing, and no escort. Only a theory about our wards."

"I have the reading," Jaina said. Her heart hammered against her ribs, but she forced herself to meet the elf's gaze without flinching. "If you let me show you the road south, I can prove it."

The silence stretched, thick and heavy with the hum of the stones. It wasn't the silence of a magistrate deciding whether to punish a trespasser. It was the silence of a commander assessing a new variable. Sylvanas was calculating distance and time, deciding whether the claim outweighed the trespass.

"Cyndia," Sylvanas said, not looking back, her eyes never leaving Jaina's. "Pull the anchor stone."

Cyndia moved without a single question.

The barrier dropped with an audible snap, like a glass rod breaking in a quiet room. Raw, unmanaged leyline noise flooded back into Jaina's senses, thick and chaotic after the filtered, heavily suppressed quiet of the cell. She stood up immediately, her legs stiff and protesting the movement. The rush of relief was physical, a breath she hadn't realized she had been holding for the last six hours.

It was immediately undercut by Sylvanas's next words.

"You ride south under escort," Sylvanas told her, the terms laid out cleanly. "Your hands stay where we can see them. You do not leave the road. If the staff comes up for anything other than a reading, you die before the spell finishes."

Jaina nodded once, accepting the terms because pride was useless here. "I can't survive the road alone anyway. I just need to read the lines."

Sylvanas reached under her dark green cloak and produced the weathered leather satchel and the tightly folded map Cyndia had confiscated at the border. She didn't hold them out. She tucked them purposefully into her own belt. "I'll hold these. We check the route together."

The conditional release followed so fast Jaina barely had time to process it. Sylvanas turned on her heel and walked out, her stride eating up the distance to the heavy oak door. Cyndia motioned silently for Jaina to follow.

They stepped out of the stone box and into the open yard of the Farstrider outpost. Late afternoon light was failing fast over the amber canopy, the air already holding the sharp, damp chill of the coming night. A ranger stepped forward from the shadows of the armory, holding out Jaina's pale wooden staff. The crystal head felt cold and dead against her palm until her fingers fully wrapped around the wood. Then, the resonance snapped back through her skin—a familiar, grounding shock of alignment that brought the physical world into sharp, stinging focus.

And the focus was terrible.

The contamination was louder now than it had been at dawn. It didn't just sing along the ley-lines anymore; it thrummed upward through the packed dirt of the courtyard, an ugly, discordant vibration that made Jaina's stomach turn.

Sylvanas's escort was already mounting. It happened without barked orders or chaotic scrambling. Three rangers—Cyndia, a sharp-eyed archer named Lyriel who moved with liquid grace, and another named Neral, heavily built for an elf and settling his massive chestnut horse with profound calm. A fourth unnamed ranger secured saddlebags and checked cinches. They moved as a single organism around their commander, a silent machine of leather, steel, and muscle.

"We need to move," Jaina said. The urgency tasted like ash in her mouth. "It's accelerating."

"Mount up," Sylvanas ordered.

Jaina swung gracefully enough into the unfamiliar, light elven tack, her staff balanced carefully at the pommel. The escort fell in around Sylvanas without a word of discussion. Lyriel took the right flank, Neral took the rear. Cyndia rode just behind Sylvanas's left shoulder. The formation settled with fluid, rehearsed efficiency. They wheeled the horses outward, leaving the stone outpost and its protective glow behind.

The moment they rode past the final Thalassian monolith, the curated, eternal-spring climate of the elven woods sheared off, entirely absent. The air turned biting and raw, smelling of pine needles and cold mud. With the dampening effect of the ward network gone, the corrupted current in the earth hit Jaina like staring directly into a midday sun. The magnitude of the rot was staggering.

The escort tightened their formation immediately. They felt the change in the road, the sudden hostility of the shadow beneath the trees, even if they couldn't read the magic itself.

Jaina reined her horse to an abrupt halt a mile south of the border. She didn't ask permission. She swung down from the saddle, her boots hitting an uneven root in the track, and dropped heavily to her knees in the packed dirt of the road. Yanking off her glove, she pressed her bare palm entirely flat against the ground. The soil was damp, gritty, and shockingly cold against her frost-burned skin.

Instantly, Lyriel and Neral wheeled their mounts outward, covering the opposite tree lines with bows half-drawn. The creak of stretching wood and saddle leather was sudden and loud in the quiet air. Cyndia watched the northern flank, an arrow already nocked. Sylvanas simply sat upon her mare, watching Jaina. The rangers were protecting her not out of trust, but because she was entirely exposed, reading the dirt like a blind woman while ignoring the forest around her.

The magic hit her hand like a physical blow. The earth tasted of old copper and rotting ice, a sick, heavy resonance that vibrated upward through her wrist and up her forearm as a deep, structural rot. It felt like sticking a hand inside a dying animal.

"It's the ground," Jaina said, yanking her hand back as if she'd been burned, wiping the dirt frantically on her trousers. "It's not just running along the leylines. The lines are overflowing. It's bleeding upward into the soil itself, into the water table."

"Why here?" Sylvanas asked, her horse stepping lightly sideways, tossing its head to avoid the stinging smell of Jaina's panicked magic.

"Because the lines are shallower here." Jaina stood, her knees trembling slightly. She tried to translate the vast, ugly architecture of the spell into something actionable, trying to find the simplest metaphor. "Think of it like an aquifer. The deeper the line, the safer the surface. But where the lines rise toward the crust, the spillage reaches the roots—"

"Millhaven," Sylvanas interrupted.

Jaina stopped mid-gesture.

"The map," Sylvanas said, her voice dry and infinitely practical. "I read it while you were locked in the cell. Three of your primary lines pinch together directly under the Millhaven settlement. We're on the main route perfectly positioned above that intersection."

Jaina stared at her, the breath knocked out of her. She had spent the last ten miles carefully planning how to dilute her explanation, assuming she'd have to walk a bow-wielding soldier through the complex geography of arcane convergence. Sylvanas had simply read the topographic overlay, measured the threat, and deduced the choke point before they had even saddled the horses.

"Yes," Jaina said, dropping the simplified translation entirely. "Millhaven is the epicenter. If this soil is carrying the resonance this strongly, then the village isn't just sitting on a corrupted conduit. It's soaking in it."

"Remount," Sylvanas ordered, her eyes fixed on the southern road.

They rode faster. The light began to fail in earnest, the shadows stretching long, grey, and distorted across Lordaeron's northern hills. The land itself began to look sick. This wasn't just a magical sensation anymore; the decay was forcing itself into the physical world. Jaina saw entire stands of crops sagging black and wet on the stalk, drowning in their own putrefaction. The chimneys of the isolated farmsteads they passed stood utterly cold, no welcoming woodsmoke softening the sharp evening air. Windows were dark. Doors were shut tight or left swinging loosely in the wind.

The crystal of Jaina's staff sparked fitfully against her saddlebow, shedding erratic pulses of violet light as it reacted to ambient necrotic spikes without her command.

"Empty," Cyndia noted quietly, nodding toward a dark farmhouse half-hidden by dying oaks.

"For at least two days," Sylvanas said, tracking the unbroken sweep of mud in the yard. No footprints. No wagon tracks. No livestock.

Jaina gripped her reins tighter, her knuckles standing out white against the leather. She had known the magic was wrong. She had seen the diagrams in her head. But she hadn't been ready for what the wrongness looked like when it reached human kitchens and children's bedrooms.

They rode on, the silence deepening around them as the tree line crowded close against the packed dirt road. The wind carried nothing but the smell of damp earth, slow decay, and stagnant water. Whatever had emptied these farms had left a vacuum that the forest was too quiet to fill. No birds called. No insects chirped.

They were less than two miles from Millhaven when the underbrush on the right flank simply exploded.

Two deer burst from the tree line with tremendous, shattering force. They didn't run with the panicked grace of hunted prey; they moved in violent, broken, agonizing lunges. Their eyes were filmed over with thick milky cataracts, blind and unseeing, and their flanks were ruined by fresh, tearing bites that should have hamstrung them.

Before Jaina could process the grotesque sight, the wolves hit.

They struck from the brush in a gray blur, totally silent, snapping viciously at the horses' legs. Jaina's mount screamed—a high, terrified equine shriek—and reared up, front hooves thrashing the air, nearly throwing her backward onto the hard ground.

"Hold the line!" Sylvanas's voice cut through the chaos, absolute, commanding, and unpanicked.

Jaina cast on pure reactive instinct. Fear burned the hesitation out of her. Leaving the staff balanced on the pommel, she thrust her left hand out, pulled the ambient moisture straight from the freezing air, and drove a wedge of solid ice directly into the nearest wolf's ribs. The impact shattered the beast's momentum with a sickening crunch, throwing it backward into the brush where it lay twitching.

Sylvanas had already drawn and fired twice, the thrum of her bowstring vibrating through the air. She didn't yell; she didn't curse. She mapped the geometry of the ambush in a fraction of a second and dismantled it. "Lyriel, the deer. Neral, control the mounts."

Cyndia fired over Jaina's shoulder, the shaft hissing past Jaina's ear. The arrow took the second wolf perfectly through the eye, dropping it instantly into the mud.

Lyriel's shaft punched cleanly through the leading deer's neck, severing the spine. An animal hit there should have dropped immediately, folding into dead weight. Instead, the deer stumbled, dragged its broken knees through the dirt, and horrifyingly lunged forward again, its massive antlers scoring a trench into the earth as it tried to reach Neral's horse.

Cyndia didn't hesitate. Her second arrow took the crawling deer straight through the skull, bursting bone with a wet crack. The animal finally collapsed, twitching violently and aimlessly for several seconds before finally going still.

The fight was over in less than ten seconds.

The horses stamped, snorted, and threw their heads, eyes wide and rolling, their flanks lathered with sudden, terrified sweat. Neral backed his mount firmly into the center of the formation, keeping an expertly steady hand on the reins of the unnamed ranger's panicking horse, calming it with low, steady clicks of his tongue. Jaina's pulse hammered, loud enough to interfere with her hearing. The fear was useful; it meant she was finally forced completely into her physical body.

Sylvanas lowered her bow but didn't put it away. The string hummed faintly for a long moment as the tension settled. She looked down at the deer Lyriel had shot through the neck. The wound was black and gangrenous. It leaked a sluggish, dark fluid down the matted fur instead of bright, steaming arterial blood.

"Proudmoore," Sylvanas said. It was an order to investigate.

Jaina felt the adrenaline crash, leaving a cold, clinical focus in its wake. She slid from the saddle and walked to the deer. The smell was suffocating—rotting leaves, old blood, and the sharp, metallic reek of active necromancy. Her stomach turned, a biological warning she cataloged and dismissed, forcing herself to kneel. She pressed her bare hand against the animal's ruined flank, her fingers sinking into the coarse hair.

The pulse was there, but it was completely wrong. It wasn't the frantic, fluttery heartbeat of a dying, terrified animal fading into nothing. It was a hot, slow, artificial throb. It pushed back against Jaina's palm with rhythmic, unnatural, mechanical pressure. That heartbeat belonged to the earth they had been riding over. It was an animating force welling up from below to push the torn muscles along long after the brain had died.

*If the carrier were the whole design, it would have failed on the road,* Jaina thought, the hypothesis forming unbidden, terrifyingly clear in the back of her mind as the pieces snapped into place. *The magic isn't a residue. It's an engine. Something deeper is waiting for the body to die to finish the work.*

"It's in the tissue," Jaina said, looking up at Sylvanas. The Ranger-General was waiting for the answer, not doubting her, not dismissing her. Just demanding the data. "The necromancy. It's entering living systems from the environment. The necrotic sequence started before the deer was fully dead. The magic is animating the biological material while the flesh is still failing."

She paused, waiting for Sylvanas to argue. She waited for the commander to demand a simpler explanation, to call it a disease or a localized curse.

Sylvanas surveyed the dark, winding road ahead. "So whatever is happening in Millhaven," she said, her voice deadpan and flat, "it won't be contained to the leylines."

"No," Jaina said. "It's in the water. It's in the soil."

"Remount." Sylvanas turned her horse south without another word.

They pulled back into formation, leaving the ruined carcasses bleeding black fluid into the dirt. Cyndia fell in quietly behind Jaina, her bow resting easily across her saddle. Sylvanas took the lead. The decision to proceed into the nightmare had been made without a single unnecessary word of debate, and Jaina realized with a cold, sharp jolt that she was no longer dragging a skeptical, obstructive soldier toward the truth. Instead, she found herself riding with a cold-blooded commander who had already factored the absolute worst-case scenario into her logistics. This was far more terrifying.

The road widened slightly, deeply rutted by heavy wagon wheels that hadn't seen use in days. The ancient trees began to thin, giving way to the cleared ground, stone walls, and wooden fences of a settlement perimeter.

Ahead, the jagged outline of Millhaven emerged against the deep purple of the fading sky.

No smoke rose from the chimneys. No lanterns burned in the windows. Not a single dog barked to announce their arrival. The road abruptly narrowed into the village thoroughfare, and the suffocating silence waiting for them was absolute, heavy, and undeniably dead.
