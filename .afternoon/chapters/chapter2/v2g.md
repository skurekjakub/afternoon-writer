# Chapter 2: The Mage

The ward-stones hummed in her teeth.

Jaina had been inside the circle for six hours. The sound lived below hearing, a bass note sunk into the old stone, and it had worked its way into her jaw, behind her eyes, down the roots of her molars. The bench was cut from the same warm white block as the floor. The bucket was clean. The slit window had gone dark a long while ago, leaving only ward-blue along the carved seam where the holding room met the border wall. Runes old enough to look inevitable burned there without flicker. She could have dismantled the whole lock in four minutes if they had not taken her staff and if dismantling a sovereign border defense had struck her as a productive way to begin the conversation.

She sat with her hands in her lap, knuckles split where the frost had bitten back, and waited for someone worth talking to.

The officers had been polite, which was worse than hostile. They listened, took notes, and asked the sort of questions officers filed away in drawers: where she had crossed, who had sent her, whether she understood she was standing inside Quel'Thalas without leave. She had made the same warning three times. Once to a border lieutenant with rain still on his cloak, once to a senior scout who smelled faintly of bow wax and stable hay, once to the dark-braided ranger who had taken her satchel with careful hands and enough sense to know what the instruments were. Cyndia had looked troubled. The others had looked patient.

Boots crossed the yard outside. One set was Cyndia's even stride on fitted stone. The other came lighter and faster, flat-footed in the way of someone so sure of the ground she did not need to announce each step to it. Leather gave one quiet creak. Then nothing at all.

Ward-light spread across the threshold. The door opened.

The woman who stepped inside was taller than the frame suggested, silver-blonde hair pulled back for the field, Farstrider armor fitted close enough to show where the pieces had learned her body. Nothing on her jingled. The room took her in stages: the bench, the bucket, the slit window, Jaina's stance, the distance to the door, the chalk-pale line of the ward circle underfoot. Jaina had the clear sense of being counted with the rest of the furnishings and found, for the moment, adequate.

Ranger-General. It had to be. Cyndia had fallen half a step behind without being told.

Sylvanas Windrunner waited until the wards were the loudest thing in the cell. One hand rested near the hip where a quiver would sit in the field; the other hung loose at her side, still in the way of someone holding herself a fraction back from motion.

Then she said, "You crossed my ward line alone. Start there."

Jaina stood too fast, and the hitch in her legs showed. The stone had leached the feeling from her calves hours ago. She straightened anyway.

"Your southern leylines are contaminated," she said. "Something necromantic has been moving through them for weeks. I came north because your ward-stones are carrying the damage, and if I can see it from the Dalaran side of the border, it is already too deep to ignore."

Sylvanas did not move. The ward-blue caught along the pale curve of one cheekbone and the metal at her throat.

"Necromantic."

"Death-aligned, if you prefer the plainer term. The signature is unmistakable once you've seen clean lines and these beside them." Jaina wanted to map the pattern in the air and let her hands do the work, but the circle kept them at her sides. "The damage is in the existing channels under the ward lattice, moving fastest near settled roads. I tracked it south from Dalaran, then north when the trail turned, and your ward-stones picked it up before I crossed."

She heard herself doing it again: careful, pared down, built for someone she assumed needed the simpler version.

Sylvanas's gaze did not soften, but it sharpened. "You've said this to my officers."

"Three times."

"You left out the part where a Kirin Tor mage arrives alone instead of with credentials and a delegation."

Everyone in the cell went still around that question. Even the wards drew tighter, the blue lines along the wall burning a shade brighter.

Jaina had rehearsed the answer on both roads and through the hours in the circle. The honest version was worse. It was also shorter.

"Because nobody believed me."

The ward-hum filled the pause. Cyndia stood at the door with one hand braced against the frame. Somewhere outside, under the lean-to where the tack was kept dry, a horse stamped once on stone and got an irritated answer from another.

"I brought my readings to the senior council twice," Jaina said. "First they wanted more proof. Then they asked whether I'd mistaken leyline drift for contamination. I had not. Drift does not carry a death signature, and it does not gather along trade roads. Antonidas knows I left. I wrote him a note. He does not know I crossed the border. I came without Kirin Tor approval because this keeps spreading while people ask for permission slips."

Sylvanas stepped closer to the line of the circle. Not across it. Close enough for the wards to brighten where her shadow crossed the runes.

"You left Antonidas a note."

"Yes."

"And crossed a sovereign magical border alone to inspect our ward-stones."

"Yes."

"Because you were right and nobody else could see it."

Jaina bit back the first answer and gave the truer one. "Because the contamination keeps spreading regardless of permission."

Sylvanas's mouth thinned by a degree. She turned her head, no farther than that.

"Her things," she said to Cyndia. "Where."

"Locked room off the yard. Staff, satchel, field notes, two keyed crystals, one knife."

"Bring the satchel and the map. Leave the staff."

Cyndia went at once. The door shut. The wards pressed the room flat again.

Sylvanas looked back at Jaina. "If I ride south and the lines read clean, you go back to your circle and your note to Antonidas becomes a diplomatic problem."

"They will not read clean."

"If they do."

"Then I'm wrong, and you can send me north under guard."

That earned the faintest shift. Not approval. Revision, perhaps.

Cyndia returned with the satchel and the folded map tube. Sylvanas took both, slid the map free, and braced it against the wall below the ward seam. White stone, blue runes, Jaina's own inked routes over both. The Ranger-General's hand flattened the vellum with field-practical impatience. She read in the same economical sweep she had used on the room, following roads, crossings, hamlets, the places Jaina had marked twice when the readings worsened.

She tracked the route without hesitation. Lordaeron farms. Southbound roads. River forks. Distances a mounted patrol would care about even if a mage did not.

"The lines pinch here," Sylvanas said, tapping the village Jaina had circled twice. "And here. Millhaven is the first place you'd feel it from the surface."

While Jaina had waited in the cell, the other woman had already done the work.

"Yes," Jaina said, and this time she heard the unnecessary restraint in her own voice. "The wards are damping the signal. Past them, the lattice opens and the corruption stops behaving like a boundary problem. Millhaven should read strongest because three channels narrow there before they split toward the river road."

Sylvanas's eyes lifted from the map to her face. No blank patience. No courteous tolerance. She had followed every word and wanted the next one cleaner.

Jaina gave it to her.

"If the taint has climbed from the deep lines into surface systems, the road south of the pass will tell us that before the village does. If it has not, then I have wasted your evening and earned my cell."

The corner of Sylvanas's mouth moved once. It might have been a smile. It might have been the rejection of one.

She folded the map with quick, practiced hands and slid it inside her armor rather than giving it back.

"Terms," she said. "You ride with my escort. Your hands stay where my rangers can see them. You do not leave the road without my word. You do not cast without warning. If this is what you say it is, we ride to Millhaven and I see it myself. If it is nothing, you ride north under guard and the diplomats can choke on the rest."

"I can read the lines," Jaina said. "I can tell you what the contamination is doing. What I cannot do is keep myself alive on a road that may already be sick. I need your escort."

"Useful answer."

It should not have irritated her that the word did. It did anyway.

Sylvanas turned toward the yard. "Cyndia. Pull the anchor stone."

The command went through the wall before the sound of motion did. Metal brushed stone outside. A socket gave with a dull click. Then the barrier dropped.

The ward pressure vanished at once and Jaina staggered. Her palms hit the wall below the runes. Through the stone the southern lines roared up at her: deep, dragged, wrong, the contamination she had been tracing for weeks now uncovered where the border system had kept it muffled. Copper climbed the back of her tongue. Her teeth ached. Even the room smelled different without the barrier, the clean Sunwell-bright ward-scent stripped away so something metallic and wet could breathe underneath.

Worse than eight hours ago. Worse enough to feel in the space between one heartbeat and the next.

She pushed off the wall and crossed the dead line where the circle had been. The yard beyond opened larger than it had looked through the slit window. White border stone underfoot, dark pines beyond, the low lean-to with saddles hanging in disciplined order, a rack of spare spears along the wall, and four horses already being brought up under lantern light. Cyndia stood with the anchor stone in her hand, watching Jaina the way someone watches a lid come off a pot that may boil over.

"My staff," Jaina said.

"Not until we're on the road," Sylvanas said. She was already moving toward the tack shelter. "I prefer my proofs outside the gate."

The contamination pulsed below the packed yard like a heartbeat in the wrong place. Every moment spent arguing was a moment it had to spread. Jaina bit back the protest and followed, boots slipping once on smooth stone before she found the rougher seam cut for drainage.

By the time she reached the horses, the yard had narrowed around Sylvanas's decision. Cyndia was tightening girths. Another ranger checked a bridle and passed it over without looking up. Somewhere to the right, a stableboy vanished the instant he was no longer needed. Authority moved fastest when the room had been waiting for it.

###

Cyndia brought the staff when the horses were saddled.

She offered it grip-first, careful as someone returning a weapon whose temper she respected. The crystal head flared the moment Jaina took it, resonance snapping through her palm like a numb limb waking. The read hit harder than the last one, deeper and uglier and faster, as if the lines had spent her captivity gathering strength for the pleasure of proving her right.

The escort closed around Sylvanas without a word. Cyndia dropped left. Lyriel took the right with her longbow already strung, pale hair tied so tight it barely moved. Neral settled at the rear, reins loose in practiced hands, the posture of someone who could turn a fleeing horse with one wrist and still keep the rest of the column straight. The fourth ranger remained what Jaina had first taken her for: another quiet shape in Farstrider green, present enough to matter and disciplined enough not to announce herself.

They brought Jaina a grey Thalassian mare, fine-boned and high-headed, with quiet buckles, clean leather, and the steady offense of a creature asked to carry an amateur. She mounted badly. The mare accepted this with patient contempt, sidestepped once at the staff's flare, then settled under her as if deciding not to embarrass them both on the same evening.

Sylvanas wheeled south, and the escort flowed after her. They left the border post at a canter that ate the last of the light.

For the first stretch the road still belonged to Quel'Thalas. Pine closed over them in dark ordered ranks, the trunks set back from the road as if the forest itself had agreed to military use. Ward-stones stood at intervals along the verge, shoulder-high and old, their runes giving off that same low bass vibration Jaina had been swallowing in the cell. The horses knew them. Each mount gave the nearest stone a minute sidelong ear and then went on. Patrol rhythm. Home ground.

Every marker struck Jaina as they passed: a brief clean pulse against the background rot, then a gap, then another pulse weaker than the last. She counted distances under her breath. Thirty yards. Fifty. A hundred. The stones stood along the road like lanterns running out of oil. South of the pass, the eased Thalassian air gave up on them by degrees. The managed warmth bled away. The night sharpened. Even the wind changed, losing the soft finished feel of elven country for something rougher that had crossed open fields and animal pens to get here.

The pines thinned to birch. The birch gave way to open country. The last ward-stone fell behind them.

Jaina hauled the mare short.

The full signal slammed through the staff and up her arm. The crystal blazed white-blue. Her shoulders locked. Out here there was no filter left, no ward-pressure shaving the edges off what lay under the road. The contamination pressed at her sternum, copper and turned earth in her throat, wrongness humming under the packed dirt hard enough to make her shake. She had been reading it through glass. This was the thing itself, broad and patient and sunk into the land as if it had always expected to get this far.

"How far," Sylvanas said, reining in beside her.

"Miles. Deep and everywhere south of us." Jaina swallowed against the copper. "Your ward-stones were damping it. Past this point the line opens and the whole field answers."

Around them the escort changed shape without needing the explanation. Cyndia's mare angled toward the tree line. Lyriel widened until her horse ran near the ditch lip. Neral let another length of road open behind them. The unnamed ranger drifted closer to Sylvanas's right knee. The grey mare under Jaina went taut as the rest of the column tightened with her.

They rode on.

The farmland should have been green. In the failing light it looked washed thin instead, wheat gone grey at the tips, pasture grass dull and tired, irrigation ditches carrying water too still for a healthy evening. Jaina leaned over the staff and read the road through the crystal. The corruption was no longer only in the deep lines. It had climbed into the soil, the shallow water, the root-web beneath the roadside grass. If it had reached this far up, then the land itself had stopped being only a carrier of signs and become part of the mechanism.

"Stop," she called.

Sylvanas raised a hand. The party pulled up at once.

Jaina dismounted before the mare had fully stopped and pressed her palm into the road. Packed dirt. Small stones. Day warmth still held under the surface. Beneath all of it, that wrong answering pulse.

Death-taint, copper, and under both a sick liveliness. Not surface blight. Not residue. The contamination had gotten into the dirt, the seep water, whatever the roots were taking up and passing on.

She stood with soil ground into her handline.

"It's in the ground," she said. "Soil and water both. Not just the leylines. Whatever is doing this has crossed from the deep channels into living systems."

Sylvanas watched her from the saddle. One hand had settled on the bow hanging at her side. "How it gets in."

"I don't know yet." The old habit rose up again, the urge to flatten the thought into soldier-sized pieces. She heard it coming and despised it. "No—that is not the useful answer. I do know this much: the contamination is not waiting for death to matter. It is entering the land first and teaching living tissue to answer before the body fails. I need the source or the thickest knot of it."

"Millhaven."

Jaina cut herself off halfway through the explanation she had been about to offer with her hands and a patient tone and words like if and likely.

Sylvanas had already done the work on the map. Already followed the convergence south.

"Yes," Jaina said, and let herself speak in her own register at last. "Three channels narrow there before they split toward the river. If the field is amplifying along what already exists, Millhaven is where the contamination bunches hard enough to become visible. Source, worst damage, or both."

"Or bait."

Horse tack creaked around the silence that followed. Jaina's hand, still dirty, tightened on the staff until grit bit her palm.

The road answered before she could. Cold chimneys. Untended fields. A trough green with scum and filmed over at the edges. A cart left loaded in one yard with sacks gone dark from dew, no lantern hung near it, no dog barking at six armed riders coming fast along the road. Farm after farm sat with its shutters closed and no smoke rising. The staff crystal had begun pulsing on its own at the edge of her sight.

Soft heads of grain bent too low at the road's edge, heavy in a way that read wrong after the rest. Damp ground blackened under rows that should have been drier this close to dusk. A gate stood open on one hinge, tapping against its post in the wind with a small patient knock nobody came to answer.

The escort tightened further as the evidence worsened. Cyndia rode nearer, not crowding Jaina's horse but near enough to lay a hand on the rein if the mare lost her mind. Lyriel kept her bow half-raised. Neral's position behind them never slipped. Jaina, belatedly, understood that every time she had knelt in a road or leaned too far over the saddle, someone else had been carrying the shapes she failed to see.

Then the village showed through the last of the trees.

Millhaven sat by a bend in the river with low houses, a mill and a squat granary shape beyond it, though Jaina gave the last only a passing glance before the rest claimed her. The wheel on the mill race stood still. No smoke rose from the clustered chimneys. No window held lamplight. The whole place had the wrong quiet of a chapel after the dead were taken out.

###

The deer burst from the undergrowth before Jaina understood what she was seeing.

A doe and a young buck, both running wrong. The doe lurched with one foreleg folding under her and catching again. The buck's eyes were filmed over milk-pale, but it came on with frantic speed, antlers crusted dark as if it had already been putting its head through things that should have stopped it.

Lyriel's bow sang. The doe took an arrow through the chest and crashed hard in the road, legs still churning against the ruts.

The wolves hit from the right before the doe stopped moving.

Three of them. Grey, gaunt, fast. They came low out of the ditch grass, using the horses' panic as cover, exactly the kind of thing Jaina would have failed to plan for and Sylvanas had clearly expected from the moment the countryside went quiet. The mare shied so hard Jaina nearly lost the saddle. She grabbed mane and staff together, heart slamming against her ribs, wolves and hooves and the copper taste in her mouth crowding out every elegant thought she had ever had.

Sylvanas moved first.

"Neral, horses. Cyndia, left. Lyriel—"

Lyriel was already loosing again. Her second arrow struck the nearest wolf in the shoulder. It twisted, stumbled, and kept coming with one leg half-dead under it. Cyndia was moving left with her bow half up, opening a lane past Jaina's mare, eyes on the angle instead of the teeth.

The buck charged.

Jaina threw frost.

She cast before she had time to think. Ice climbed the buck's forelegs and locked them to the road, rime racing over packed dirt and old hoof dents alike. The spell bit back through her split knuckles so hard skin gave. The animal screamed—a raw grinding sound from the wrong part of the throat—and tore at the frozen earth with its antlers while the mare fought the reins under her.

Sylvanas put an arrow through its skull.

One shot. Through the eye. The buck dropped where Jaina's ice held it, all that frantic force gone out of it at once.

The doe was still trying to rise.

Cyndia's second arrow had gone through the chest a finger from the first. The animal kept scrabbling in the dirt, blind and driven, front legs cutting grooves in the road as if it could claw itself back into life by force.

"Again," Sylvanas said.

Cyndia's third shot ended it.

By then the wolves were down as well. One lay half in the ditch water, ribs laddering its hide. Another had made it three more strides with Lyriel's shaft through the lung before Sylvanas cut it off with a second arrow so fast the draw vanished inside the release. Neral had three mounts bunched in her fists and held them steady through blood, panic, and snapping teeth with the flat calm of long practice. The fourth ranger turned her horse broadside to the road without flourish, making a living wall where Jaina's left flank had been open a breath earlier.

Jaina slid from the saddle. Her right hand was bleeding where the frost had reopened old damage. She knelt beside the doe and laid her good hand on the body.

Warm.

Warm, and wrong clear through. The hide still held the day's heat. Under it the flesh answered her like bad instrument wire, a live necromantic buzz running blood to muscle to organ with no clean boundary anywhere in the chain. Not a curse laid on from outside. Not something waiting politely for death to finish the work. The body had already been taught.

These animals had taken it in while alive—from water, grass, soil, something common enough that deer and wolves could carry the same lesson—and the magic had kept driving them past the point where nerves should have failed and blood loss should have won.

"It's in the flesh," she said. Her hand was still on the carcass when she looked up. "Blood too. It got into them while they were alive and kept teaching the body after damage that should have stopped it. This starts before death."

Sylvanas still had an arrow nocked. Her attention flicked from Jaina to the trees to the road ahead and back again, weighing threat, answer, and distance in a single economy of movement.

"How far."

"I don't know. Maybe the whole countryside. Anything drinking here, grazing here, living in this soil could be carrying it." Jaina wiped her bloody hand against the side of her robe and hated the uselessness of the gesture at once. "I can tell you what it is doing. I cannot clear a road or a village alone. If Millhaven is worse than the farms, you'll need rangers, quarantine, and a cordon. Yesterday, if you can manage it."

"First I need your read," Sylvanas said. "The rest I can get."

That was the second correction of the night, and this one landed deeper than the first. Not because Sylvanas had dismissed her. Because she had not. She had taken the diagnosis for what it was and the field for what it still required. Jaina, belatedly and against her own preference, understood the distinction.

Sylvanas turned in the saddle.

"Mount," she said to the escort. "We close on the village before full dark."

They obeyed at once. Lyriel stripped arrows from the carcasses with efficient jerks and wiped the shafts on dead grass. Cyndia bent for her own without wasting a glance. Neral steadied the grey mare for Jaina without being asked; when Jaina's boot missed the stirrup the first time, Neral pretended not to notice the mistake.

Jaina hauled herself back into the saddle. Her hands had stopped shaking.

They rode.

The party pulled in tight now. Cyndia's stirrup rode near enough to brush hers if either horse drifted. Lyriel went ahead with bow in hand, her silhouette breaking and re-forming against the last of the dusk. Neral stayed close enough behind that Jaina could hear her mount breathe between hoofbeats. Leather creaked. Iron rang once off a buckle and was gone. The wind still carried the sweet-rotten smell off the doe's body long after the road bent away from it.

Nobody spoke. They did not need to. The escort's spacing said enough: left covered, right covered, rear covered, the road ahead already belonging to Sylvanas's decision.

Millhaven appeared out of the dusk by the river bend, closer now and worse for it. Low houses crouched with their shutters shut. The mill wheel sat black and motionless over the water. No lantern burned for the road. No dog barked. No voices leaked from the houses or the yards. Even the river sounded wrong—too thin under the hush, slipping past the pilings with the small secretive noise of water moving through a place that had stopped being lived in.

Jaina gripped the staff as the village came up at them, and this time when she looked at Sylvanas she did not see a soldier she needed to drag toward the truth. She saw the other half of a road she could not have survived alone.
