# Chapter 5: The Grain Road

The first depot sat an hour south of Millhaven where the King's Road split cleanly: the broader line holding east toward Andorhal, the lesser road slanting west through the mill villages with its ruts full of old straw and black water. A hitch rail ran the length of the counting room wall. Cyndia and Lyriel had already tied the Thalassian horses there, light tack quiet, reins looped short. The geldings stood with their heads low and their ears working at every wagon on the road, then settled again when nothing came of it.

Inside, the counting room smelled of cold ash, lamp oil, and grain dust worked into old wood. Ledger shelves climbed one wall, their upper boards smoked black by years of candles burned too near the spines. The clerk behind the desk was round through the middle, with ink dried in the webs of his fingers and oat chaff caught in one sleeve. He spent five minutes insisting his books were in perfect order. Sylvanas let him spend them. When she laid the Millhaven manifest flat on the desk, weighting one corner with two fingers, his mouth closed all at once.

"This batch," she said. "Who brought it in."

His eyes went to the mark before they went to the entry. He knew it, then. That mattered.

He licked a thumb, turned pages in the autumn book, and found the line in the third week. Two carts. Supplier marked with the same symbol she had seen elsewhere now often enough to hate it on sight: a wheel inside a wheel, neat as a wagonmaker's stamp and twice as forgettable. He called it a road-factor mark, one of the men who bought grain out of estate storehouses and pushed it farther south under his own tally.

"Name."

He gave her one and an address two days down the road. Sylvanas wrote both in the margin of her notes. At the far window the mage made a small sound, not surprise exactly, more the click of a piece dropping into place. Her staff leaned against the sill within reach. Frost had silvered one split knuckle and gone no farther.

Sylvanas drew the next manifest from her satchel. "What else moved through here that week."

That took longer. Cyndia stayed in the yard with the horses. Lyriel stood just inside the door with one shoulder against the frame, giving the road half her attention and the clerk the rest. The man worked by fits, first too fast to be useful, then slower when Sylvanas made him start over. By the end of the hour the desk was buried in open books, tally slips, and one cracked wax board used for temporary counts before the fair copies were made.

Four more routes touched the same mark.

Three fed towns already reporting the kind of autumn stomach sickness no one sent a rider over. One man vomiting in a village was weather. Ten was still weather if the right people lived far enough from the capital to die without offending anyone important. The fourth line fed a bakehouse on the Andorhal road and a mill east of the Sorrow Hill relay.

"The other two," Sylvanas said.

The clerk found them with more reluctance than skill. She copied the names. The mage came over at her shoulder, close enough for grain dust shaken from the books to catch on the damp hem of her blue sleeve.

"The bakehouse first," she said. "The mill is three days farther, and flour puts the grain one step closer to the oven."

Sylvanas tapped the page with the butt of her charcoal. "No. Fenwick Crossing first. The bakehouse buys flour. That is already downstream. I want the road above it."

The mage took the sheet from her, checked the marks, then the road distances written in Sylvanas's hand. She adjusted fast; that, more than the answer itself, kept proving useful.

"Fenwick, then," she said, and folded her own notes to match.

---

Fenwick Crossing had four clerks, deeper shelves, and a yard master who wore his authority like a coat borrowed for the day and not yet trusted to fit. He asked for warrants. Seals. Approval out of Tarren Mill. Sylvanas showed him her commission and let the silence after it do the rest. He stepped aside with the expression of a man already drafting his complaint.

The clerks were better than he was. By midday the counting-room table had vanished under three autumns of records. Wax drips. Cord ties. A broken tally weight rolling every time someone jostled the boards. The mage had her contamination notes open beside the depot books, matching grain tallies to the sickness reports she had copied from village infirmaries and captain's ledgers farther north. Her fingers moved between both stacks without hesitation now. Less translating. More joining.

"Here," she said, flattening two sheets with her palm. "And here. The worst places are the ones that kept buying. The places that ordered once and lived on their own harvest after that are sick, but not nearly as sick."

Sylvanas leaned over the table. Ink columns, wagon dates, place names. Fenwick. Thornfield. A bakehouse north of Andorhal. A relay east of Sorrow Hill. The same road-factor mark kept touching the sickest towns, and the same grain kept moving south by feeder roads and side roads, farther than any village captain could see from his own gate.

She took the regional map from her satchel and spread it across one corner of the table, pinning one edge with her knife before the draft from the open shutters could roll it back on itself. "Show me where the orders start."

The mage traced them with one frost-roughened fingertip.

South and west. Not toward one spoiled storehouse, but into the grain roads laid in her grandmother's day to drag harvest out of the Andorhal basin and deeper into Lordaeron. Sylvanas had ridden those roads on summer exercises and thought of them only in terms of march time, choke points, and where you could water horses without losing an hour. Now every clean curve of road on the map looked like a supply line offered up for someone else's hands.

"The same mark is on three of these," the mage said. "The wheel inside the wheel. It doesn't match any estate I know."

"Road-factor," Sylvanas said. "Middlemen. They buy from the estate and sell onward."

"So the estate may not know where its grain is ending up."

"Or it chose the factor because a factor's mark draws less notice than a house seal."

The mage's eyes moved once over the place names again. She said nothing. Either she agreed or she was building a cleaner objection. Sylvanas could use either.

The yard master appeared long enough to mutter about a midday meal. The room's air had gone stale from too many bodies and too much paper. Sylvanas kept one hand on the map while he spoke. He went away with his grievance intact.

By the time they left, the horses had cooled and fed and gone impatient again. Lyriel had already checked the girths. Cyndia passed Sylvanas the reins without needing the order. The leather was warm from the mare's neck.

---

The road from Fenwick to Thornfield ran three hours through late-autumn fields the color of old rope. Harvest stubble stood in the open ground like cut bristles. Here and there a wagon had shed grain enough for sparrows to work the ruts. Cyndia rode ahead. Lyriel held the back trail. The mage kept beside Sylvanas, her copied map folded badly over the saddle horn and trying to lift in the wind each time the road opened.

"It's spread wider than that," she said. "If it were one fouled storehouse or one bad shipment, we'd see a spike. This is different. The same low dose scattered through dozens of roads, and the places that stay on shipped grain longest are the ones hit worst."

She talked faster in the saddle than at a desk. The cold and motion shaved the softness from her phrasing.

"Someone is sowing it," Sylvanas said. "Many placements."

"Placed so one loss does not kill the whole design. If a single wagon gets caught, the rest still land."

Sylvanas had built ambushes on the same rule. One strike was warning. Three laid at once meant the attack had started before the first man hit the ground.

"The road-factor mark," she said.

"Yes. They're using the kingdom's own grain roads, slipping bad grain into loads already moving, and letting the wagons do the work for them."

That was the ugliness of it. No siege train. No cult banners. Just harness bells, axle grease, tallies, and ordinary bread arriving on time. By the time any clerk thought to look twice, people had already eaten the proof.

Sylvanas kept her mind on distance, stables, horseflesh, the number of depots she could still strip before even Thalassian mounts needed a day to breathe. Four days more and they would be deep in the basin where the roads ran closer and the records thicker. Four days more and any hand ahead of them would know exactly what they were following.

"How many villages," she said.

"Sick now, or sitting in the chain."

"Both."

The mage considered with the road moving under them, one hand braced flat against the folded map to stop its snapping. "Eleven I can name with certainty. Forty, maybe fifty more tied to the same trade. If the sowing was thorough, the worst places are still ahead of us."

"They are," Sylvanas said.

A rider came north out of the south with courier posture written through his spine. She let him pass after one read. No grain dust on the cloak. No factor's habits in the hands. Just haste and cold.

Thornfield's sign appeared ahead, fresh yellow paint over older wood. Beyond it, granary roofs and a church spire showed above bare poplars.

"Two more depots today," Sylvanas said. "Before dusk."

The mage refolded the map without being told and tucked it inside her coat. Better.

Thornfield gave them two more supplier marks and a ledger that changed hands halfway through the autumn entries. The old scribe had left useful filth in the margins: late wagon, damp grain, a driver who cheated weights if you let him, complaints about axle breakage on the west lane. The new hand wrote only dates and amounts, clean as a prayer card and twice as dead.

Sylvanas fixed the change in memory. Old hand to new hand. Late Elul. A month before the first sickness reports worth naming.

The last stop of the day was a mill-side depot called the Crossbar, its name painted fresh across a barn wall. Good road junction. Good place to matter without standing out. The attached storehouse smelled of ordinary grain, old burlap, and mouse droppings in the corners. Too ordinary. The books were balanced. The tallies exact. No margin notes. No scraped figures. No places where a clerk had blotted ink trying to repair a lie in haste.

Somebody had been ahead of them.

"This was arranged," Sylvanas said.

Lyriel held the office door with one hand near her knife and looked out over the yard while the light thinned westward. "Too neat?"

"Too dead."

The mage made a slow circuit with both hands in her coat pockets, reading what the room held and what it did not. "No magical cover. Preservation wards on the grain, nothing more."

"Then the cover is paper."

"Or the bad load never reached this stop. If they knew this road was hot, they could pull it off farther north and send clean grain through instead."

Sylvanas checked the current tally against last year's. Nearly half the load for the same month. "They pulled it back," she said. "Either they know we're behind them or they guessed someone would come."

"Or they're finished here," the mage said. She had one hand on the ledger spine now, eyes narrowed on the date columns. "If the grain reached the places it was meant for before we started cutting through the records, then they may be scrubbing the road behind themselves."

Worse.

An enemy still moving could be caught. One already done had only to close doors and leave clean shelves.

"We're reading a road after the wagons passed," Sylvanas said.

Outside, Cyndia let the horses drink from a trough ringed with thin ice. The north road had gone under shadow. South still held the light.

"South," Sylvanas said.

---

Millbrook Spur had been built for freight, not comfort. The depot yard sat where the King's Road bent east before straightening for Andorhal, with merchant bunks on one side, the factor's office on the other, and a barn-roofed storehouse squatting behind both. On any autumn morning the place should have been all racket—teams blowing steam, wheel rims on stone, men shouting weights across the yard. Instead the cold held it flat and empty.

No carts.
No smoke.
No voices.

The chimney above the bunkhouse was dead. The water barrel by the office door still wore its skim of ice, unbroken except where one ladle had been dipped and left on the rim. A handcart sat in the yard with a rope coil on it, half the rope hanging in the mud as if somebody had meant to come back within the minute.

Sylvanas drew up and read the place. Lyriel was already off her horse and onto the low roofline by the grain shed, bow canted down over the yard. Cyndia had turned her mare toward the far fence, taking the blind angle at the back of the barn before Sylvanas needed to point.

Nothing moved.

Interrupted, then.

She swung down, gave Cyndia the reins without looking, and crossed to the office. The door was unlocked. Inside, the room kept the shape of work without the work itself: writing kit on the desk, sand pot capped, invoices stacked where they could be seen from the road through the glass. Ledger shelves along the wall. Stool tucked in. Too proper. The kind of order a place wore when it expected inspection and wanted the inspection to stop at the threshold.

She pulled one of the bottom ledgers free.

Blank.

The second was blank as well. Fresh paper. Fresh stitching. Cheap calfskin still stiff at the corners. She went to the older books and counted four days missing from the run that mattered.

"Left in a hurry," Lyriel said from the doorway.

"No." Sylvanas turned a page and found the dates picked clean without tearing the binding. "They had time."

She checked the desk, the sill, the shelf behind the door. The invoices stacked in the window were blank too, good thick paper set where a rider coming up the road would see business at a glance and go away satisfied. Whoever had built the false face understood clerks well enough to counterfeit them.

"If they cleaned it," the mage said from behind her, "they were watching the road."

Sylvanas crossed to the front window. From the high side of the yard the southern approach lay open for nearly a mile, road running pale between stripped hedges and winter fields. Long enough to see riders coming. Long enough to turn a room inside out and leave only its skin behind.

"They can see a mile south from here," she said. "Enough to leave a clean face and be gone."

"Then the real books are gone."

"Or never sat here in the first place."

She made herself think it through as she would an enemy supply line. If the people running this stop expected scrutiny, the true record would travel with the men who mattered. What sat in the office was decoy work: blank books, window paper, the right smell of ink and dust. Enough to hold a shallow search.

Cyndia appeared in the doorway, one glove dark with old soot from where she had braced herself in the passage beyond. "Back room."

The rear office held shelves, spare sacks, and a dead man on the floor.

He lay on his back with his hands at his sides, too neat by half. Factor's ring on one hand. Heavy man, grey working through his beard. The boards beneath him had been swept after he fell; dust gathered at the wall, not around the body. Someone had cleaned the room and left the corpse because the corpse looked ordinary from the door.

Sylvanas crouched and ran a field read over him. Hands first. No defensive bruising. Face. No burst vessels in the eyes. Wrist. Chest. Clothes. No wound, no blood, no knife-cut in the linen, only that wrong inward color she had started to recognize now: a body gone dead from within while the skin still tried to pass for natural.

"How long."

The mage stepped past her, took the crystal from her satchel, and went to work without asking for room. Four slow passes over the chest, throat, and mouth. Then she stilled.

The expression on her face was the same one she had worn over Davan. Not fear. Precision narrowed hard enough to cut with.

"Three days," she said. "Four at most. Same touch."

Sylvanas rose. "Necromancy."

"Quiet necromancy." The mage turned the crystal once in her hand, watching the trapped light gutter blue against the dead man's collar. "Small. Precise. Enough to stop a heart and leave a body that looks ordinary unless you know where to look. I've seen this hand before."

"The granary watcher."

"Yes."

Same hand, then. Or someone trained cleanly enough to imitate it.

That meant a mage moving ahead of the search. Cleaners behind. And someone feeding them the road quickly enough to keep pace.

"They know we're coming," Lyriel said.

"They knew before we did," Sylvanas said.

She left the corpse and went back into the front office. Fast cleaners always missed one thing. Take everything and you advertised fear. Leave enough and you risked a seam. The trick was finding the thing too ordinary to notice until it mattered.

Under the desk a pale rectangle showed where a document box had sat for years. Gone now. On the far wall one bent nail marked the place where a route map had hung. Also gone. The proper ledgers were missing, as they should have been. But not everything in a place like this lived in a bound spine.

Beside the yard door hung a slate chalk tablet for wagon turns and last-minute marks.

No one had taken it because it looked like weather and routine.

Sylvanas lifted it down. Chalk dust came away on her glove. Most of it was nothing—wagon counts, abbreviations, dates rubbed half dead by the heel of a hand. Near the bottom three entries crowded together in a different script. Smaller. More cramped. And beside them the mark she had been chasing since the first depot.

Wheel inside wheel.

One line below it, written tighter still, sat a property name. The last word stopped her harder than the mark had.

Barov.

Not proof. A house name in Lordaeron could mean rent, tenant grain, a storehouse ledger nobody noble had touched in person for years. But the name had weight, and it kept turning up in the same strip of road beside the same road-factor sign.

She tilted the slate toward the light. Something had been trapped between the frame and the wood backing. A torn strip of route paper, missed in the hurry. She worked it free with her knife point. Most of the writing had gone with the tear. What remained was a district marker, half a delivery line, and a clot of red wax pressed flat by folding. The seal had broken badly, but enough survived for a house device and the same name beneath it.

Barov again.

The mage crossed to her side, reading over the slate, then the torn strip in Sylvanas's hand. "That's a noble house."

"With storehouses on half the useful roads between here and Andorhal."

"It's still only a lead."

"I know." Sylvanas copied the chalk entries and the surviving route scrap into her notes, then folded the paper into her coat. "It's a road to follow."

---

They found a waystation two miles east of Millbrook Spur, one of the old road houses built for relay riders and factors who preferred stone walls to sleeping in their wagons. Four rope bunks. A narrow hearth. One table scarred by knives and ink both. Cyndia and Lyriel split the first watch without discussion, one taking the yard, the other the lane. Outside, the horses stood rubbed down and blanketed under the lean-to, occasional breath pushing white through the slats. Their tack hung in orderly loops from pegs driven into the beam.

The room smelled of woodsmoke, damp wool, and the stale sweetness of old grain worked into travellers' coats over a hundred autumns. Sylvanas spread the road map on the table, pinned one edge with her knife, and let the candle stub throw its weak light over the basin roads. Jaina laid her notes beside it, then a second page, then the copied chalk entries from Millbrook Spur. She no longer waited to be asked for the missing half.

"What you said," Sylvanas said, "about this stretch being finished."

Jaina sat across from her and put both palms on the table to flatten the papers against the draft creeping through the shutters. "Possible. If the grain reached the towns it was meant for before we started cutting through the records, then this part of the work is over. What we're following is what they left behind."

"They're still moving."

"To cover themselves." Her fingers shifted from the copied chalk entries to the route scrap with its broken wax. "The man at Millbrook Spur, the blank books, the missing shipment—those read like cleaners scrubbing the road behind them, not fresh loads still pushing through this exact line."

"Unless the sowing shifted somewhere else."

"Unless that."

The answer came back to her without the cushion she had used in the pass and the border villages. Jaina, then, not the mage. Somewhere over the last days the change had happened and left no ceremony behind, only less wasted motion.

Sylvanas studied the map. South of Millbrook Spur the King's Road widened into the main grain line, the one that ran toward Andorhal before branching farther into the kingdom. For three days they had been stripping the quieter feeder roads instead, following the wheel-in-wheel mark from counting room to counting room while the broader road waited like a river too obvious to read from the bank.

"They used the feeder roads," she said. "Too much traffic rides the King's Road. Too many patrols. Too many clerks who know the common seals. A factor on the feeder lines disappears into other factors."

Jaina bent over the map, braid slipping farther loose at the nape. "Maybe. But the King's Road is also faster. If they were racing bad grain into the larger trade before anyone understood it, they may have used both."

"Then we need the central books at Andorhal."

"Or copies at Hearthglen."

Sylvanas did not look up. "We are not riding to Hearthglen first."

"I didn't say we were." Jaina slid one note half over another and recalculated aloud. "I'm saying if the main Andorhal books are gone by the time we reach them, the duplicate routes have to live somewhere. Large buyers keep copies. Quartermasters keep copies. Priories do if they're feeding enough mouths. But the Barov entries don't sit on the direct line anyway."

That was the useful part. Not theory made pretty, but the clean drop from theory into doors, desks, and who would have a duplicate tucked in a coffer.

Sylvanas traced the southwest branch where the feeder road cut away before bending back toward the basin and the Alterac foothills beyond. Strahnbrad sat off that line. So, according to the last reports she remembered, did Arthas with a slice of his campaign still loose in the field.

"This road," she said.

Jaina leaned in until the candle laid gold along the side of her face. "That passes near Strahnbrad."

"Yes."

"Arthas is operating there."

"Yes."

The flame bent in a seam of draft. Somewhere outside a horse stamped once against the plank floor of the lean-to, then settled.

Jaina looked from the route to her notes and back again. "It also skirts the thickest contamination line. If the worst roads are the direct approach to Andorhal, the southwest branch should leave us cleaner records and a lighter trail. We lose two days."

"We gain the Barov properties on that side of the basin."

Jaina bent lower over the papers, thinking with both hands flat on the table the way she did when the pattern stood on the edge of locking. "Three of the four wheel-in-wheel entries I flagged sit in the southwest district," she said. "The basin-side ones look like second-stage stops, not where it started."

"Origin," Sylvanas said.

"Possibly. If the grain entered the chain there, then the southwest road isn't a detour. It's the road."

Sylvanas kept one finger on the map and let the old trader come back into her head at last.

The north-road grain seller.

The empty wagon with grain dust silvering the slat corners. The patched coat. The broad hat. The broken nose set crooked long ago and left that way. A plain road voice that knew bridge conditions, flour shortages, and which baker bought heavy before the weather turned. He had met Millhaven with what looked like honest surprise. She had given him one hard read and let him pass because at the time he had been one more trader on one more road and she had not yet known what grain could carry.

Still.

He had known more than he should have for a harmless man with an empty wagon.

She had not yet known grain was the carrier. Had not yet known watchers stood on the roads with poison in their teeth. Had not yet known the whole thing reached wider than one dead village. It had been the best read she had. That did not make the memory less irritating.

He had been riding south.

Into this same web of roads.

One man with a wagon silvered by old grain and enough local knowledge to pass for useful. She could not pull a face from the memory now sharp enough to hunt. Coat. Nose. Voice. The white caught in the slat corners. Not enough.

She had let him pass.

Jaina did not interrupt. She had gone still across the table in the way she did when she knew better than to fill a silence that was working.

"The southwest route," Sylvanas said.

Jaina nodded once. "We stay on the feeder roads. We cut in from the west. If the Barov books vanish ahead of us, at least we reach the places where they vanished from."

"And we reach Andorhal from that side." Sylvanas lifted her finger and set it down again near Strahnbrad. "If Arthas is there, we do not stop."

"I had no intention of stopping," Jaina said, dry as old paper.

Sylvanas let the line stand. It was close enough to wit.

Outside, Lyriel's tread crossed the yard gravel and faded again. Cyndia murmured to one of the horses. The sounds held the room to the road even with the shutters closed.

Sylvanas studied the map one last time. Knife pinning parchment. Candle guttering low. Route line angling south toward Strahnbrad, then back toward Andorhal like a bow drawn on the basin.

"Sleep," she said. "We ride before first light."

Jaina gathered her papers into neat stacks, capped the ink, and lay down without ceremony on the bunk by the window, one hand still resting on the top page as if she might wake and need it at once.

Sylvanas let the candle burn a little longer, long enough to hold the southwest road in view once more. Then she pinched the flame out.

The hearth settled. The room went dark. Outside the feeder road kept running south.
