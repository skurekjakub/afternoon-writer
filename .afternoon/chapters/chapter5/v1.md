# Chapter 5: The Grain Road

The first depot was an hour's ride south of Millhaven, a way-stop where the King's Road split and a smaller track veered west toward the mill villages. It had a covered yard, a counting room, and a ledger shelf that held three years of tallow candle marks and grain tallies. The clerk was a round man with ink-stained fingers who spent the first five minutes explaining that all his records were in perfect order before Sylvanas set the Millhaven manifest on his desk and watched him go quiet.

"This batch," she said. "Who brought it in."

He found it in the autumn ledger. Third week, two carts, a supplier marked with a symbol she did not recognize—a wheel inside a wheel, which he identified as a road-factor mark, one of the registered middlemen who moved grain between estate storehouses and the King's distribution network.

"Name," she said.

He gave her a name and an address in a town two days south. Sylvanas wrote it down. The mage—standing at the far window, not blocking the light, saying nothing—made a small sound that was not agreement but was not disagreement either.

Sylvanas pulled out the next manifest. "What else moved through here the same week."

It took an hour. Cyndia kept the horses in the yard and Lyriel stood at the door with her arms loose at her sides, watching the road. The clerk found four more supply lines that had touched the same road-factor. Three of them served towns she had already flagged as reporting unusual sickness, the kind described as a bad autumn stomach, the kind no one had sent word about yet because it had not killed anyone with enough rank to generate a messenger.

"The other two," she said.

He found them. A bake-house on the Andorhal road and a mill that served four villages east of the Sorrow Hill relay.

She copied the addresses. The mage came to her shoulder, looked at the list, and said, "The bake-house first. The mill is three days farther."

"Not the bake-house," Sylvanas said. "The relay depot at Fenwick Crossing. The bake-house buys finished grain, which means we are looking at downstream. We want to go upstream."

The mage looked at her. Not surprised, exactly—it was more like she was adjusting a figure she had already been running.

"Fenwick, then," she said.

---

Fenwick Crossing had a bigger operation: four clerks, a yard master who wore his authority like a borrowed coat, and ledgers going back seven years. The yard master wanted to see authorization documents. Sylvanas showed him her commission seal and said nothing else until he had made two attempts to redirect her to the outpost commander in Tarren Mill and failed.

The clerks were more useful. By midday the table in the counting room was covered in three autumn's worth of supply records, and Jaina—the mage had been Jaina for long enough in Sylvanas's head that she had stopped noticing the distinction—had her contamination notes out beside them, comparing what the grain tallies said against the pattern of sickness reports she had copied from four villages' worth of infirmary ledgers.

"Here," Jaina said, setting two sheets side by side. "And here. The exposure depth matches the delivery interval. Places that bought in volume and bought repeatedly are the worst cases. Places that made one order in high summer and then ran local harvest are barely touched."

Sylvanas looked at the sheets. The pattern was right there in the numbers, as plain as troop density.

She pulled the regional supply map from her saddlebag and spread it on the corner of the table. "Show me where those orders originate."

Jaina traced the lines. They did not go north, toward the borderland estates and the farms Sylvanas had been thinking about. They went south and west, toward the heavy distribution network that fed the kingdom's interior, the roads built in her grandmother's time to carry grain from the Andorhal basin to the northern settlements. Sylvanas had ridden those roads during summer border exercises, never once thinking about what moved along them.

"The same supplier mark is in three of these," Jaina said. "The wheel-in-wheel. It does not match any estate I know."

"Road-factor," Sylvanas said. "Registered middlemen. They buy from estate storehouses and sell forward to the distribution network."

"Which means the estate itself may not know what it is selling into."

"Or the estate arranged the factor precisely because an estate's name on a shipping manifest is more visible than a factor's mark."

Jaina took that in without arguing, which meant she either agreed or had decided to hold her objection until she had a better version of it. Both outcomes suited Sylvanas.

The yard master appeared in the doorway and said something about the midday meal. Sylvanas kept her eyes on the map and said nothing. He left.

---

The road between Fenwick Crossing and the Thornfield relay was three hours on horses that had already been ridden since dawn, through late-autumn farmland going the color of old rope. Cyndia and Lyriel rode ahead and behind, not close enough to hear conversation but close enough to be useful. The arrangement had established itself without discussion somewhere around the second day out of Millhaven. Sylvanas had not had to give the order.

"It is not one bad chain," Jaina said, riding alongside. She was looking at the copy she had made of the supply map, which was folded impractically on her saddle. "If it were one bad storehouse or one contaminated shipment, the pattern would be different. Isolated. A spike and a drop. What I am seeing is a consistent low level across dozens of supply lines, with severity tracking exposure time rather than proximity to any single source."

"Someone is seeding it," Sylvanas said. "Not one accident. Multiple placements."

"Deliberately distributed to avoid looking like a single outbreak. If any one site fails—if the grain is identified, if a supplier is caught—the rest of the network continues."

Sylvanas had built ambushes on this principle. A strike from one direction is information. A strike from three directions simultaneously is a problem that has already made its own decisions for you.

"The road-factor mark," she said.

"Yes. I think whoever is moving this is using the legitimate supply network as infrastructure. They are not building their own roads. They are placing their product inside the kingdom's existing logistics and letting it ride."

This was the part that changed the shape of the hunt. They were not looking for a rogue supply line. They were looking for something woven into the supply lines that existed, invisible as long as it moved in the right direction on the right roads. Any attempt to investigate would look, from the outside, like inspecting commerce. By the time the records flagged an anomaly, the grain had already been eaten.

She watched the road ahead, calculating distance, stable availability, how many more depots she could push through before the horses needed a rest day. Four days south and they would be in the basin proper, where the distribution network grew thick enough to have records for everything.

"How many villages," she said.

"Affected now, or potentially in the supply chain."

"Both."

Jaina thought about it with the road moving under them. "Affected in ways I can confirm: eleven. Affected in ways that match the pattern but have not yet progressed to visible illness: difficult to say. The network touches forty or fifty settlements between here and Andorhal. If the seeding was thorough, and if the exposure-depth model holds—"

"It holds," Sylvanas said.

"Then the worst cases are ahead of us. Not behind."

There was a rider coming from the south. She watched him move, read his posture and his horse's rhythm, and decided he was a courier and no concern. He passed them without slowing.

The road bent west. The first of the Thornfield landmarks came into view, a grain merchant's sign on a post that someone had recently painted over in bright yellow.

"We need the next two depots today," Sylvanas said. "Before they close for the evening."

Jaina folded the map without being told.

---

Thornfield gave them two more supplier marks and a ledger that had been started in a different hand halfway through the autumn entries, which meant someone had changed scribes mid-season. The new scribe wrote in a cleaner but slightly impersonal style that Sylvanas found less useful—the old entries had the kind of marginal notes that came from someone who knew the local suppliers personally, small annotations about late delivery or grain quality that told her something. The new hand noted only quantities and dates.

She photographed the discrepancy in her memory—old scribe to new scribe, late Elul, a month before the first unusual sickness reports—and kept moving.

The last depot of the day was a mill-side operation called the Crossbar, a name painted on the side of a barn in letters that had been recently touched up. The barn sat at the junction of two trade roads, which was useful for commerce and useful for not being particularly notable to anyone traveling either road independently.

They had been there less than ten minutes when Sylvanas understood that whoever was moving contaminated grain through this region had enough awareness to be cautious. The records at the Crossbar were clean. Not absent, not visibly tampered with—clean, the way a room looks when someone has had an hour's warning before an inspection. The tallies balanced. The supplier marks were unfamiliar, with none of the wheel-in-wheel she had been following. The grain in the attached storehouse smelled normal.

She walked the room once, looking at what was there, then looked at what was not there. No marginal annotations. No erasure marks or bleed-through from altered figures. No inconsistency in the ink weight or the pressure of the pen.

"Someone organized this," she said quietly.

Lyriel, near the door, did not move.

The mage came into the room and made a circuit, not touching anything, hands in her coat pockets, reading the air. When she finished she said, "There is nothing magical here that shouldn't be. Standard preservation wards on the grain, which is normal. Nothing that says taint and nothing that says concealment."

"Then they changed the records by hand," Sylvanas said.

"Or replaced the supplier entirely. If the distribution line that runs through here was already clean, they could have switched out the contaminated load before it arrived, left the clean paper trail, and routed the bad grain elsewhere."

Sylvanas looked at the tally for the current month. It showed a forty percent drop in incoming volume compared to the same month the previous year.

"They pulled the shipment back," she said. "They knew we were coming or they assumed someone would come, and they pulled it back and sent clean grain forward."

"Or the route was already winding down," Jaina said. "If the seeding phase is finished—if the grain has already been delivered to where it needs to go—they may be cleaning their own supply chain to avoid exactly this kind of audit."

This was worse than the first option. A cautious enemy that pulled back when observed could be outmaneuvered. An enemy that had already finished its work before the investigation reached them had nothing left to protect.

"We are not ahead of this," Sylvanas said. "We are following grain that has already landed."

"Yes," Jaina said.

Outside, in the settling evening, Cyndia had found a water trough and was letting the horses drink. The road north was already in shadow. The road south was still lit, angling down toward the basin where the distribution network grew too thick to untangle from a single depot's records.

She did not stop moving by assuming she had already missed the target. She stopped moving by deciding she had no more useful information in this direction. Neither was true yet.

"We continue south," she said.

---

The next morning the enemy showed her what she had been working toward.

The depot at Millbrook Spur sat two days' ride south of Fenwick, where the King's Road bent east before straightening again toward Andorhal. It was a significant stop—large enough to have its own factor's office, a lodging room for itinerant merchants, and a yard that could hold a dozen loaded wagons at once. On a normal autumn morning it would have been busy, the kind of place where a stranger asking questions would be unremarkable against the noise.

The yard was quiet when they rode in. Too quiet; no carts in the yard, no voices from the factor's office, no smoke from the chimney that should have had a fire in it on a morning this cold.

Sylvanas pulled her horse up and sat still, reading the yard. Lyriel was already scanning the roofline. Cyndia had angled her horse toward the far corner of the fence, covering the approach from behind the barn.

Nothing moved. The silence was not the silence of a place that had been abandoned—there was a water barrel still full by the door, a handcart with a rope coil on it in the middle of the yard, a stack of invoices on the factor's window sill visible through the glass.

She dismounted and handed her reins to Cyndia without looking, walking toward the factor's office door.

The office was empty. Not cleared out—the desk still had its writing kit, the ledger shelf still had ledgers—but the person who should have been sitting at the desk was not sitting at the desk. Two of the ledger spines on the bottom shelf were different. Newer. She reached for the leftmost one and found it blank inside, every page unmarked.

Blank ledger on a working shelf. The other new one was blank too.

She pulled the old ledgers. The last entries in the main book were four days ago. After that, nothing.

"Someone left in a hurry," Lyriel said from the doorway.

"Someone did not leave," Sylvanas said. "Someone came and cleaned."

She moved through the office, looking at the backs of the shelves, the floor under the desk, the window sill. The invoices she had seen through the glass were blank too, she discovered—a short stack of fresh paper, not filed correspondence. Someone had put it there to be visible at a distance.

"If they were cleaning," Jaina said from just inside the door, "they were also watching to see if anyone was coming."

"The road south is visible for a mile from the high end of the yard," Sylvanas said. "They had enough time to leave clean paper and go."

"Then the real records are gone."

"Or the real records were never here. If you know an audit is possible, you keep your records elsewhere and maintain a clean face at the depot."

She stood in the middle of the empty office and made herself think about it as a logistical problem. A supply network that anticipated inspection would need a separate record system—something mobile, something that stayed with the network's controllers rather than sitting in buildings that could be searched. What she was walking through was not the real operation. It was the window-dressing.

Cyndia appeared in the doorway. "There's a back room."

---

The back room was a storeroom with four shelves and a man lying on the floor between the third and fourth shelf.

He was on his back, fully clothed, with his hands at his sides in a way that looked peaceful and was not. The factor's seal ring was on his right hand. He had been a heavyset man in his middle years, with grey in his beard and the kind of deliberate stillness about him that was the stillness of death and nothing else.

Sylvanas crouched beside him, ran a ranger's eye over the hands, the face, the pulse points, the inside of the nearest wrist. No obvious injury. No blood. His color was wrong in the way people looked wrong when something had stopped from the inside, rather than from a wound.

"How long," she said, not looking up.

Jaina came past her, set her satchel on the empty bottom shelf, and took out the crystal. She did not ask Sylvanas to move. The assessment was quick, four or five passes of the crystal over the body, and when she finished the expression on her face was the same one she had worn over Davan.

"Three days," she said. "Maybe four. And it is the same touch."

Sylvanas stood. "Necromancy."

"Not dramatic necromancy. Something small and precise—the kind of thing that would look like a heart failure to anyone examining the body without the crystal. The disruption is deep inside the chest structure. It would not announce itself." A beat. "I have seen this signature before."

"The village," Sylvanas said. "The granary watcher."

"The method is the same. The same practitioner, or someone trained by the same practitioner, or someone using the same approach with the same taught technique. The signature is—" Jaina stopped, adjusted herself midway through the thought. "It does not vary much. The same hand, I think."

Someone had deployed a mage capable of quiet, targeted magical killing, moved ahead of the investigation, silenced the local witness, stripped the records, and left the window-dressing in place. That was three separate actions from three separate people minimum—the mage, the cleaners, and whoever had intelligence on where the investigation was heading.

"They know we're coming," Lyriel said. She was standing against the far wall, not looking at the body.

"They knew before we did," Sylvanas said.

She looked at the room again with what was left in it. The stripped shelves, the blank-paged ledgers stacked in the main office, the man on the floor who had known something and was no longer in a position to share it. They had done this methodically—not panicked erasure, but clean procedure.

She went back to the main office and looked at what the cleaners had missed.

There was always something. A cleaning done quickly enough to stay ahead of an investigation was a cleaning done with limits—they had to prioritize what was dangerous and leave what looked innocuous, because taking everything would look suspicious in its own way.

The floor under the desk had a faint rectangle of cleaner wood where something had sat for a long time and then been removed. Whatever had been there—a strongbox, a locked document case—had gone with the cleaners. The far wall had a nail at the height where a large map would hang, and nothing on the nail.

The supply ledgers were gone, as expected. But the grain tallies were not ledger items; they were scratched into a chalk tablet that hung on a peg beside the yard door, and no one had thought to take the chalk tablet, because chalk tablets were ordinary and the kind of thing an empty depot might still have.

She took it down and looked at it.

Most of it was ordinary—quantities, dates, standard shorthand for wagon loads. Near the bottom, three entries made in a different hand, smaller and cramped, with a mark she had seen twice now. The wheel-in-wheel.

Beside it, a name. Not a supplier name—a property name. Two words, one of which she recognized as a geographical reference to a district south of here, and one of which she recognized from somewhere else entirely.

She went through her memory of the last three days of ledger work. The Fenwick records, the Thornfield entries, the names she had noted against that particular supplier mark.

The second word was a surname. A large one.

Barov.

She had not been certain until the chalk tablet, and she was still not certain now—a property name beside a supplier mark was not a proof of anything, only a link in what might be a much longer chain. The Barov holdings were extensive, the kind of landholding that spread its name across half a dozen districts through tenant farms and leased storehouses and mills that bore the family name because a Barov ancestor had paid for them. Finding that name in a supply chain in Lordaeron was not surprising. Finding it repeatedly, and finding it connected to the specific route mark she had been following—

She copied the tablet onto her notes, replaced it on the peg, and considered the room one more time.

"We have a name," she said.

Jaina came to look at the notes. She was quiet for a moment. "That's a noble house, not a road-factor."

"A noble house with enough landholding to own storehouses on every major supply road between here and Andorhal."

"That is not proof of—"

"I know what it is," Sylvanas said. "I know what it is not." She folded her notes and put them in the breast of her coat. "It is a direction."

---

They found accommodation in a waystation two miles east of Millbrook Spur, a stone-walled room with a fire that the stablehand had already lit and four rope-strung bunks that were adequate. Cyndia and Lyriel took the first watch split between them, and Sylvanas set her road map on the central table, weighted the near edge with her knife and the far edge with a candle stub, and let Jaina set her contamination notes alongside.

The room smelled of woodsmoke and old wool. Outside, the horses were in the covered stall, fed and blanketed. The road south was invisible in the dark. Somewhere past it, a long day's ride beyond where her information currently reached, Andorhal was loading grain by lantern light or had finished loading it already.

"What you said about the seeding phase being finished," Sylvanas said.

"It is a possibility." Jaina was working through her notes with a methodical focus, not looking up. "If the contaminated grain reached its target destinations before we began tracing the supply chain—which, given what we found today, seems likely—then the network has already accomplished its primary objective. The evidence we are following is aftermath evidence."

"They are still moving."

"They are moving to cover. The man at Millbrook Spur, the blank ledgers, the pulled shipments—that is a network cleaning up, not a network actively seeding." A pause. "Unless the seeding in this area is finished and the active operation has shifted elsewhere."

Sylvanas looked at the supply map. The road south of Millbrook Spur was a thicker line than any of the roads she had been riding. The King's Road proper, the main artery of Lordaeron's grain trade, fed down from the eastern highlands and ran straight to Andorhal, where the distribution network branched out to every corner of the kingdom. She had been working the smaller roads, the feeder lines that carried regional supply. She had found the wheel-in-wheel mark on those feeder lines, following it from depot to depot, watching the pattern widen.

"The feeder roads are what they used," she said. "The King's Road is too observed—merchant traffic, outpost patrols, too many eyes to move contaminated grain on it without someone noticing the supplier mark. But the feeder roads are quieter, and if you distribute through a road-factor, you are just one of a dozen factors using the same roads."

"Yes," Jaina said. "Which means the King's Road might have cleaner records for exactly that reason—they avoided it. But—" She stopped, looked up. "The King's Road also means faster investigation. If the point was speed, getting grain into the distribution system as quickly as possible before anyone understood what was happening, the feeder roads would be slower. They might have used both."

"The King's Road records for the same period," Sylvanas said.

"We would need access to the central distribution records at Andorhal to know for certain. The outpost at Hearthglen may have copies."

She looked at the map. Hearthglen was north of Andorhal, and getting there without passing through the Andorhal basin itself would mean a detour.

"We are not going to Hearthglen first," she said.

"I did not say we should."

Sylvanas looked at the road map more carefully, tracing the lines south. The cleanest route to Andorhal ran straight down the King's Road, but the man at Millbrook Spur had been on a lateral road that the King's Road did not touch directly. The Barov name had come from the feeder-road supply chain. The active cleanup was happening on those smaller roads.

And somewhere in this basin, she was aware, Arthas had military forces. The winter campaign against the southern bandits had been ongoing since early autumn, and the reports she had seen in the final weeks before she had left Silvermoon had placed a significant column moving through the Alterac foothills. Strahnbrad was mentioned. A holding action, or a pursuit—she had not read closely, because it had not been her concern.

It was nearly her concern now.

She set her finger on the map at the junction where the southern feeder road split—one branch going straight down to Andorhal, the other angling southwest through the Alterac approaches and then bending back east toward the basin from the other side.

"This road," she said.

Jaina looked at where her finger was pointing.

"That routes past Strahnbrad," Jaina said.

"It does."

"Prince Arthas is operating in that area."

"He is."

A pause. The candle flame moved sideways in a draft from the window.

"That road also avoids the contamination corridor," Jaina said. "If the heaviest density of affected supply lines is along the direct King's Road approach—which the evidence suggests—then the southwest approach would take us through territory that has lighter contamination and more surviving clean records. We would lose two days, possibly three."

"We would gain access to the Barov properties that border the Alterac approach, which are not on the direct route."

Jaina looked at the map, then at her own notes, then at the map again in a way that meant she was running something.

"The supplier mark we followed," she said. "The wheel-in-wheel. Three of the four instances I flagged connect to properties in the southwest district, not the direct basin properties. The direct basin connections were secondary—redistributors, not origins."

"Origin," Sylvanas said.

"Possibly. If the southwest properties are where the contamination entered the supply chain, then the southwest route is the investigation route, not the detour."

Sylvanas did not move her finger from the map. She was thinking about Arthas—not the political calculation of encountering a prince on his campaign, but the simpler tactical fact that military operations changed roads. Arthas's column would have commandeered certain routes, placed guards at certain bridges, established resupply points that would affect what traffic was available.

She was also thinking about the grain seller.

The thought had been surfacing and submerging since Millbrook Spur. In the counting room at Fenwick, when the wheel-in-wheel mark had first appeared on multiple lines. In the long afternoon riding between depots, when Jaina had been describing the distribution pattern. The old man on the north road, the empty wagon with grain dust still in the slat corners, the too-specific knowledge about baker routes and flour shortages, the way he had studied the empty village with what looked like honest surprise.

It had not looked like honest surprise. She had given it one second's assessment and moved on because she had not yet known what she was looking at. That was not a failure of attention—it was accurate calibration for the information available at the time. She had not known grain was the vector. She had not known there were watchers. She had not known the network was regional.

She knew now.

The old man's route had been heading south. Away from Millhaven, into the same road network she had been tracing for three days. A single traveler with local road knowledge and an empty grain wagon, credible enough to pass, positioned exactly where the northern edge of the distribution network brushed against the border routes.

She could not identify him. She had not written down enough detail, and what she had written down—coat, nose, voice—was not enough to find a specific person in a supply chain that touched forty settlements. The road had offered him and she had let him go, because she had not yet understood the road.

That was the part that sat uncomfortably, not the fact of the error but the fact of its scale: if she had known then what she knew now, she would not have let him go. There was not enough to act on at the time, and certainty was not the same thing as the information to produce it. Still. She filed it.

"The southwest route," Sylvanas said.

Jaina had been watching her, patient, not pushing.

"We take the southwest route, we stay on the feeder roads instead of the King's Road approach, we reach the Barov properties with cleaner evidence—assuming they have not already been cleaned—and we come into the Andorhal basin from the west instead of the north." She paused. "We will pass near Strahnbrad."

"Yes."

"If Arthas is there we do not stop."

"I had no intention of stopping," Jaina said, with a dryness that was appropriate to the statement and nothing more.

Sylvanas looked at the road map one last time. The candle stub was burning low, the flame pulling sideways in the draft. Outside the waystation, somewhere in the dark, the road south was already the choice they were going to make; the map was only showing her she was right about it.

"Sleep," she said. "We ride before first light."

Jaina gathered her notes, set them in order, and said nothing about the time they were losing to sleep or gaining to clear heads. She took the bunk nearest the window and lay down without ceremony.

Sylvanas let the candle burn another minute, long enough to look at the road one last time, then pinched it out.

The dark settled in, the fire banked to coals, the road south waiting.
