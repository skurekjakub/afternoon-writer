# Chapter 5: The Grain Road

The first depot sat an hour south of Millhaven, where the King's Road forked and a smaller track ran west toward the mill villages. Covered yard. Counting room. Ledger shelf blackened by three years of candle smoke. The clerk was a round man with ink on his fingers who spent five minutes insisting his books were in perfect order before Sylvanas laid the Millhaven manifest on the desk and his mouth closed.

"This batch," she said. "Who brought it in."

He found the entry in the autumn ledger. Third week. Two carts. Supplier marked with a symbol she did not know—a wheel inside a wheel. He called it a road-factor mark, one of the middlemen who bought grain from estate storehouses and pushed it farther south.

"Name."

He gave her one, and an address two days down the road. Sylvanas wrote it down. At the far window the mage made a small noise.

Sylvanas drew out the next manifest. "What else moved through here that week."

It took an hour. Cyndia kept the horses in the yard. Lyriel stood by the door with her arms loose, keeping half an eye on the road. The clerk found four more routes touched by the same mark. Three served towns already reporting the sort of autumn stomach sickness nobody bothered to send a rider over. One man vomiting in a village was weather. Ten was still weather, if the right people lived far enough away.

"The other two," Sylvanas said.

He found them. A bake-house on the Andorhal road. A mill east of the Sorrow Hill relay.

She copied both. Jaina came to her shoulder, read the list, and said, "The bake-house first. The mill is three days farther."

"No. Fenwick Crossing first." Sylvanas tapped the page. "The bake-house buys flour. That is already downstream. I want the road above it."

Jaina checked the list again. "Fenwick, then."

---

Fenwick Crossing was bigger—four clerks, deeper shelves, a yard master who wore his authority like a coat borrowed for the day. He wanted warrants. Seals. Approval from Tarren Mill. Sylvanas showed him her commission and waited until he stepped aside.

The clerks were better. By midday the counting-room table had vanished under three autumns of records. Jaina had her contamination notes open beside them, matching grain tallies to the sickness reports she had copied from village infirmaries.

"Here," she said, laying two sheets together. "And here. The worst places are the ones that kept buying. The places that ordered once and lived on their own harvest after that are sick, but not nearly as sick."

The numbers showed the same mark feeding the sickest towns, and the same trouble kept moving south on wagon routes, farther than any one village could see.

Sylvanas spread the regional map across one corner of the table. "Show me where the orders start."

Jaina traced them.

South and west instead, into the heavy grain roads that fed the kingdom's interior, the ones laid in her grandmother's day to drag harvest out of the Andorhal basin. She had ridden those roads on summer exercises and never once cared what moved along them.

"The same mark is on three of these," Jaina said. "The wheel inside the wheel. It doesn't match any estate I know."

"Road-factor," Sylvanas said. "Middlemen. They buy from the estate and sell onward."

"So the estate may not know where its grain is ending up."

"Or it chose the factor because a factor's mark draws less notice than a house name."

Jaina did not argue. Either she agreed or she had a better objection brewing. Sylvanas could work with either.

The yard master appeared long enough to mutter about a midday meal. Sylvanas kept her hand on the map. He went away.

---

The road from Fenwick to Thornfield ran three hours through late-autumn fields the color of old rope. Cyndia rode ahead. Lyriel behind. Close enough to matter. Without naming it, they had settled into marching order.

"It isn't one bad chain," Jaina said. She was riding beside Sylvanas with the copied map folded badly across her saddle. "If it were one fouled storehouse or one bad shipment, we'd see a spike. This is different. The same low dose scattered through dozens of roads, and the places that stay on shipped grain longest are the ones hit worst."

"Someone is sowing it," Sylvanas said. "Many placements."

"Placed so no single failure kills the whole thing. One shipment gets caught, the rest keep moving."

Sylvanas had built ambushes on the same rule. One strike was warning. Three at once was an answer already in motion.

"The road-factor mark," she said.

"Yes. They're using the kingdom's own grain roads, slipping bad grain into the one already there and letting the wagons do the rest."

They were no longer chasing one crooked route; they were hunting shipments slipped into routes that already existed, hidden as long as they moved at the right pace on the right roads. By the time a clerk noticed anything wrong, people had already eaten it.

Sylvanas kept her mind on distance, stables, horseflesh, how many more depots she could strip before the mounts needed a day. Four days more and they would be deep in the basin, where every road thickened.

"How many villages," she said.

"Sick now, or sitting in the chain."

"Both."

Jaina thought with the road moving under them. "Eleven I can name with certainty. Forty, maybe fifty more tied to the same trade. If the sowing was thorough, the worst places are still ahead of us."

"They are," Sylvanas said.

A rider came north out of the south. She read courier in the set of his back and let him pass.

The road bent west. Thornfield's sign came up ahead, fresh yellow paint laid over older wood.

"Two more depots today," Sylvanas said. "Before dusk."

Jaina folded the map without being told.

---

Thornfield gave them two more supplier marks and a ledger that changed hands halfway through the autumn entries. The old scribe had left useful notes in the margins: late wagon, damp grain, a driver who cheated weights if you let him. The new one wrote only dates and amounts, clean as a prayer card and twice as empty.

Sylvanas fixed the change in memory. Old hand to new hand. Late Elul. A month before the first sickness reports.

The last stop of the day was a mill-side depot called the Crossbar, its name painted fresh across a barn wall. Good road junction. Good place to matter without standing out.

The books were too clean.

Clean. Tallies balanced. Supplier marks she had not seen before. The attached storehouse smelled of ordinary grain. Somebody had been ahead of them.

She walked it once. Missing margin notes. No erasures. No blotched ink where figures had been changed in haste. Nothing careless.

"This was arranged," she said.

Lyriel, near the door, did not move.

Jaina made a slow circuit with her hands in her coat pockets, reading what the room held and what it did not. "No magical cover. Preservation wards on the grain, nothing more."

"Then the cover is paper."

"Or the bad load never reached this stop. If they knew this road was hot, they could pull it off farther north and send clean grain through instead."

The current tally had dropped nearly half from the same month last year.

"They pulled it back," she said. "Either they know we're behind them or they guessed someone would come."

"Or they're finished here," Jaina said. "If the grain is already where it needed to go, they may be scrubbing the road behind themselves."

Worse.

An enemy still moving could be caught. One already done had only to close doors.

"We're not ahead of this," Sylvanas said. "We're reading a road after the wagons passed."

Outside, Cyndia let the horses drink from a trough. The north road was already under shadow. South still held the light.

She stopped because there was nothing else in this room worth the next hour.

"South," she said.

---

Millbrook Spur sat dead quiet when they rode in.

The depot sat two days below Fenwick, where the King's Road bent east before straightening toward Andorhal. It had its own factor's office and merchant bunks.

On any autumn morning the yard should have been loud.

It wasn't.

No carts. No smoke. No voices. The cold had teeth in it and the chimney was dead.

Sylvanas drew up and read the place. Lyriel was already on the roofline. Cyndia had turned her horse toward the far corner of the fence, covering the back side of the barn.

Nothing moved.

Interrupted.

The water barrel by the door was still full. A handcart sat in the yard with a rope coil on it. Through the office glass she could see a stack of invoices on the sill.

She swung down, gave Cyndia the reins without looking, and crossed to the office.

The room was empty. Not stripped, not wholly. Writing kit on the desk. Ledgers on the shelf. But the man who should have been at the desk was gone, and two of the ledgers on the bottom shelf were too new.

She pulled one free.

Blank.

The second was blank too.

She went to the older books. Four days of entries missing.

"Left in a hurry," Lyriel said from the doorway.

"No," Sylvanas said. "They had time."

She checked the shelves, the desk, the sill. The invoices in the window were blank as well—fresh paper stacked where it would be seen from outside.

"If they cleaned it," Jaina said from behind her, "they were watching the road."

"They can see a mile south from the high end of the yard," Sylvanas said. "Long enough to leave a clean face and be gone."

"Then the real books are gone."

"Or never sat here in the first place."

She made herself think it through as she would an enemy supply line. If the people running this stop expected inspection, the true record would travel with the people who mattered. What sat in this office was a front. Decoy books, left where a fast search would stop.

Cyndia appeared at the door. "Back room."

---

The back room held shelves, spare sacks, and a dead man on the floor.

He lay on his back with his hands at his sides, too neat by half. Factor's ring on one hand. Heavy man, grey in his beard, dead long enough to be still all the way through.

Sylvanas crouched and ran a field read over him: hands, face, wrist, chest, clothes. No wound. No blood. Wrong color. The look of a body gone dead from within.

"How long."

Jaina stepped past her, took out the crystal, and went to work without asking for room. Four slow passes. Then she stilled.

The expression on her face was the same one she had worn over Davan.

"Three days. Four at most. Same touch."

Sylvanas rose. "Necromancy."

"Quiet necromancy," Jaina said. "Small. Precise. Enough to stop a heart and leave a body that looks ordinary unless you know where to look. I've seen this hand before."

"The granary watcher."

"Yes."

Same hand, then. Or someone taught by it.

That meant a mage moving ahead of the search. Cleaners behind. And someone feeding them the road.

"They know we're coming," Lyriel said.

"They knew before we did," Sylvanas said.

She went back into the front office and made herself search for what had been left behind.

Fast cleaners always missed a seam. Take everything and you advertised fear. Leave enough and you risked one.

Under the desk a clean rectangle showed where a box or document case had sat for years. Gone now. On the far wall a nail marked the place where a large map had hung. Also gone.

The ledgers were missing, as they should have been. But not everything in a place like this lived in a ledger.

Beside the yard door hung a chalk tablet.

No one had taken it because it was ordinary.

Sylvanas lifted it down.

Most of it was nothing—wagon loads, dates, shorthand. Near the bottom three entries crowded together in a different hand. Smaller script. Cramped. And beside them the mark she had been chasing since the first depot.

Wheel inside wheel.

Beside it sat a name.

A property.

Two words. One local district marker. One word that struck somewhere older in memory.

She ran back through three days of books. Fenwick. Thornfield. Repeated marks. Repeated roads.

Barov.

Still not proof. One house name in Lordaeron could mean rent rolls, storehouses, tenant land, old claims hanging on from a dead grandfather's bargain. But the name kept showing up in the same strip of road, beside the same mark, on the same line she had been following south.

She copied the tablet into her notes, rehung it, and read the room once more.

"We have a name," she said.

Jaina crossed to her and read over the page. "That's a noble house."

"With storehouses on half the useful roads between here and Andorhal."

"That isn't proof."

"I know." Sylvanas folded the page and slid it into her coat. "It's a road to follow."

---

They found a waystation two miles east of Millbrook Spur. Stone walls. Four rope bunks. Fire already lit. Cyndia and Lyriel split the first watch without comment. Sylvanas spread the road map on the table, pinned one edge with her knife, and let Jaina lay her notes beside it.

The room smelled of woodsmoke and old wool. Outside, the horses stood fed and blanketed. South lay somewhere beyond the dark.

"What you said," Sylvanas said, "about this stretch being finished."

"Possible," Jaina said. She had gone back to her notes, methodical now that they were still. "If the grain reached the towns it was meant for before we started cutting through the records, then this part of the work is over. What we're following is what they left behind."

"They're still moving."

"To cover themselves. The man at Millbrook Spur, the blank books, the missing shipment—that feels like people scrubbing the route behind themselves, not moving fresh loads through it." She paused. "Unless the sowing has shifted somewhere else."

Sylvanas studied the map. South of Millbrook Spur the King's Road thickened into the main grain road, the one that fed straight into Andorhal and then branched to half the kingdom. For three days they had been working the quieter feeder lines, following the wheel-in-wheel mark from smaller stop to smaller stop.

"They used the feeder roads," she said. "Too much traffic uses the King's Road. Too many patrols. Too many clerks who know the common marks. A factor on the feeder lines disappears into other factors."

"Maybe," Jaina said. "But the King's Road is also faster. If they were racing to get bad grain into the larger trade before anyone understood it, they may have used both."

"Then we would need the central books at Andorhal."

"Or copies at Hearthglen."

Sylvanas did not look up. "We are not riding to Hearthglen first."

"I didn't say we were."

She traced the roads again. Straight south on the King's Road was the fast line. Also the obvious one. But the name they had found at Millbrook Spur sat off that main run. Barov roads. Smaller roads. The same smaller roads where the cleanup was happening now.

And somewhere out in that basin, near the Alterac foothills, Arthas had troops in the field. She remembered the reports only in pieces: winter campaign, southern bandits, Strahnbrad mentioned once and not worth her time then.

Nearly worth it now.

She put one finger on the southwest branch where the feeder road cut away before bending back toward the basin.

"This road."

Jaina leaned in. "That passes near Strahnbrad."

"Yes."

"Arthas is operating there."

"Yes."

The candle flame bent in the draft from the window.

"It also skirts the thickest contamination line," Jaina said. "If the worst roads are the direct approach to Andorhal, the southwest branch should leave us cleaner records and a lighter trail. We lose two days."

"We gain the Barov properties on that side of the basin."

Jaina bent over her notes, then the map, then her notes again. Thinking with both hands flat on the table.

"Three of the four wheel-in-wheel entries I flagged sit in the southwest district," she said. "The basin-side ones look like second-stage stops, not where it started."

"Origin," Sylvanas said.

"Possibly. If the grain entered the chain there, then the southwest road isn't a detour. It's the road."

Sylvanas kept her finger on the map and let another thought rise behind the first.

The old grain seller from the north road.

The one with the empty wagon dusted white in the slat corners. The one who knew too much about flour shortages and baker routes. The one who had met Millhaven with what she had taken for honest surprise.

Not honest surprise.

Or not only that.

She had given him one hard read and let him pass because at the time he had been one more traveler on one more road. It had been the best read available. She had not yet known grain was the carrier, or that watchers were on the roads, or that the whole thing spread wider than one dead village.

Still.

He had been riding south.

Into this same net of roads.

One man with an empty grain wagon and enough local knowledge to pass for harmless. She could not pull a face from memory now, not one good enough to hunt. Coat. Nose. Voice. Not enough.

She had let him pass.

She kept it.

"The southwest route," she said.

Jaina had been watching her without crowding the silence.

"We stay on the feeder roads. We cut in from the west. We get the Barov roads before they vanish, and we reach Andorhal from that side." Sylvanas lifted her finger, then set it down once more near Strahnbrad. "If Arthas is there, we do not stop."

"I had no intention of stopping," Jaina said, dry as old paper.

Sylvanas let herself study the map one last moment. Outside, in the dark beyond the waystation wall, the road south already existed. She had only chosen the road they were already riding toward.

"Sleep," she said. "We ride before first light."

Jaina gathered her papers into order and lay down without ceremony on the bunk by the window.

Sylvanas let the candle burn a little longer, enough to hold the road in view once more, then pinched it out.

The fire settled. The room went dark.
