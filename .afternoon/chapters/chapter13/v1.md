# Chapter 13: The Arrival

The lake announced itself before the shore did.

Cold changed first. Not mountain cold, not road-night cold. A wet mineral chill that got under leather and sat there. Then the ground flattened beneath the horses and the trees thinned, trunks standing apart in the mist like men who had already decided not to help.

Sylvanas raised one hand. Cyndia checked her mare without a sound.

Ahead, Darrowmere spread out under a skin of low white haze. The water showed through it in green-black strips where the mist tore thin, and farther out the island keep rose piece by piece: seawall, tower, the shoulder of old stone made darker by age and newer by use. Caer Darrow. The real seat. The manor behind them had only been the front gate dressed as a household.

No birds.

That absence landed first. No reeds shivering with small life. No wings lifting off the shore at the horses' approach. Only water lapping softly against mud and the creak of leather when Cyndia shifted in the saddle.

Sylvanas slid down and crossed the last stretch on foot. Mud took her boots shallowly. Reed stubble rasped at her greaves. Three rowboats lay half-pulled above the waterline, their hulls dark with damp. One had rotted through at the bow. One still held a length of line wrapped twice around a post. The third sat free, shoved up onto the mud in a hurry and left there.

She crouched by it.

Fresh scrape under the keel. Wet still clung to the planks below the gunwale. One set of drag marks out. None back.

Jaina had crossed. No need to guess at that anymore.

Beyond the boats, a green shimmer passed over the lake and vanished, so slight another eye might have missed it. Sylvanas bared her teeth at it. The ward sat low over the water, a skin laid over the approach. Not meant to stop a boat. Meant to feel it.

Cyndia came up on her left, leading both horses now.

"One out," she said.

"I saw."

Cyndia's gaze moved over the three boats, the post, the mist. Then, by reflex older than the last four days, she checked the right side as though a second ranger should have been there to take the farther angle. Her head turned once, then back again to the same empty strip of shore before she caught herself.

Lyriel's place. Still living in the motion.

Sylvanas straightened. "Any sign of a watch on the public landing?"

"Two posts on the west face when the mist shifts. Lantern hooded. They want to be seen from the proper approach." Cyndia looked out toward the island. "East wall is worse stone, less traffic. One upper window open a hand's width."

Sylvanas found it on the third pass. Narrow. Dark. High enough to keep the casual visitor honest. Good.

"We take that."

"And the ward?"

She watched the green skin ghost over the water again and measured the distance from the mud to the east face in her head. "If it was built for boats using the proper landing, it will be stronger where boats are supposed to go. We hug the wall. Keep low."

Cyndia nodded once. No argument. Just the quick acceptance of a ranger who had already been running the same angles.

They pushed the smallest boat down together. The hull sucked free of the mud with a wet noise that seemed too loud, though the lake swallowed it. Cyndia set wrapped cloth over the oarlocks before either oar went in. The care in the gesture was automatic, practiced, one more place where the missing fourth hand ought to have been.

Sylvanas held the boat while Cyndia shoved off. Then she stepped in, taking the bow. Water rocked under her boots. The mist closed around them almost at once, turning shore and island into guesses.

Only the oars remained sure.

Wood dipped. Lifted. Dipped again. The wrapped locks gave nothing back but a muffled thud. Water touched the hull in small black sounds. Somewhere ahead, stone waited.

Halfway over, the ward found them.

Not a blow. A pressure at the back of the teeth. A brief, searching drag along the skin, like cold fingers trying to decide what she was.

Sylvanas lowered herself further in the boat and kept her bow flat across her thighs. The pressure slid by, caught on the keep's stronger lines farther west, and thinned.

"Still there?" she murmured.

"Barely." Cyndia did not stop rowing. "East wall's deadening it."

Good. Not blind. Just thinner. Enough.

The stone loomed out of the mist at arm's length, slick and dark where the lake licked it. Cyndia brought the boat in without scraping. Sylvanas rose in one motion, caught the seam between stones, and climbed.

Wet mortar. Cold stone under her hands. Her boots found purchase where age had bitten chunks from the wall. She moved up three body lengths and flattened herself beneath the narrow window Cyndia had marked.

No voices. No steps.

She eased up, peered in, and saw shelving first. Crates. A bench. Not a corridor. Good enough.

Sylvanas swung through and landed in a crouch on stone damp with blown lake water.

Storage room, or what had once been one. The room smelled of dust until the deeper stink got through it: preserving salts, old blood, a sweet patient undertone beneath both. Crates lined one wall. On the other sat a row of narrow tables under linen cloths darkened at the hems.

One of the cloths twitched.

She crossed the space and whipped it back.

A peasant hand lay on the table by itself, greying at the wrist, the tendons in it still working. The fingers scratched slowly at the wood grain.

Sylvanas let the cloth fall again.

Invited scholars came through the front door. This was what the side of the building was for.

She went to the inner door, opened it a finger's width, and listened.

Silence. Then, farther off, the faint organized life of the place: a door closing, footsteps overhead, a low ward-hum in the walls. Not a ruin. A school at work.

The passage beyond ran narrow and clean. Service corridor. No tapestries. No grand floor mosaics. Shelving cut into the walls instead, holding ledgers, stoppered jars, boxes marked in a fast academic hand. A stair at the far end led up toward the respectable route. Another, half hidden behind an iron gate left ajar, turned down.

Jaina's trail did not go up.

It showed in small ways once Sylvanas learned the room's habits. A damp print on stone where lake water had dripped from a hem. Chalk dust on a rail too low for servants. The wrong kind of scuff in a place that expected softer shoes than Jaina wore in the field. Not much. Enough.

She moved deeper.

Service rooms opened one after another off the corridor. The estate still showed through some of them: a pantry converted to reagent storage, a family archive turned records room, a sitting room stripped to stone and tables under white cloth. Other rooms had been remade too thoroughly to guess what they once were.

One held six bodies laid out in numbered order, not dead enough for the room's comfort. Chests moved under linen in shallow, synchronized lifts. A seventh table stood empty except for iron restraints and a shallow basin gone pink at the drain.

Another room had drawers sorted by part.

Hands together. Eyes together. Jaws in a crate lined with straw, several still working minutely against one another with a dry clack like teeth in winter.

Scholomance had not built a school under a crypt. It had taken a school's habits and laid them over a butcher's work until the cuts looked methodical enough to grade.

She cleared the next corner and a spell snapped past her ear.

Sylvanas dropped, rolled, and came up inside the second cast before it could finish. A young woman in Barov burgundy hit the wall hard enough to lose the words. Scholar's hands. Reagent burns. Dark hair torn loose at the temple where Sylvanas drove her forearm across the throat.

"Quiet," Sylvanas said.

The woman bared her teeth and clawed for the focus ring on her left hand. Sylvanas caught the wrist, twisted, and pinned it to the stone. The ring bit the floor.

"Try it again and I break the hand."

That got stillness.

The woman's breath came fast but angry, not afraid. Her eyes went over Sylvanas's leathers, the bow, the wet stone mark on one sleeve, and found the answer she hated.

"You came through the side," she said.

"Talk."

"You are late."

Sylvanas leaned harder until the wrist bones shifted.

Pain finally sharpened the scholar's face. "The room is below," she said. "You can hear that much if you have ears."

"Who is with him?"

"Your mage."

The answer came too quickly to be a lie made on the spot. Sylvanas kept her weight where it was.

"What shape is the room?"

"A study. A board room. Whatever makes you angriest after the fact." Bitterness roughened her voice. "He sent me out because she could keep up and he wanted the pleasure of it alone."

There it was. Not fear. Wounded pride.

"She asked him questions?" Sylvanas said.

The woman's mouth tightened. "Real ones."

"And he answered."

"In full."

No need to ask whether the speaker meant to poison that answer. She did. The injury sat plain in the sound. That made the facts less clean, not less useful.

"Way down."

"Lower stair," the woman spat. "Past the old crypt spine. You can take the guest route if you want the whole building to know your name."

Sylvanas studied her a beat longer, then released the wrist only to drag both arms behind her back and bind them with the cord from a nearby curtain tie. She shoved the scholar down against the wall beside a cabinet of sealed jars.

"Stay there."

The woman's laugh came thin and furious. "If he'd wanted her gone, she'd be gone. He kept talking."

Sylvanas left her with that and took the lower stair.

It narrowed after twenty steps and lost the last pretense of a manor. The stone changed under her boots—older blocks, rougher joints, dust so dry it lifted in soft puffs with each step. The smell shifted too. Less lamp oil now. More preservative, more old rot, more of the sweet undertone that had lived under the whole island and grown stronger the deeper she went.

Discard rooms opened off the crypt run at uneven intervals.

No scholars here. No curated shelves.

A vat stood in one chamber with three shoulders rising from the fluid and no heads above the line. In another, failed stock hung from hooks by wire stitched through wrists and ankles, some dead, some moving after a fashion that showed memory had not survived with the meat. One jaw worked steadily in a bucket at the foot of a table, patient as a hinge.

The clean room below all this would be the lie told on top of it. Sylvanas knew that before the voices reached her.

Kel'Thuzad's came first, low and measured through stone. Not the bark of a cornered man. The cadence of a lecturer concluding a point.

Then Jaina answered.

Lower than usual. Not frightened. Thinking.

Sylvanas went still in the corridor, one hand on the bow, and listened.

Not pleading. Not stalling for time. The give and take ran too clean for that. One voice laying out structure. The other testing it and finding the weak place. A real exchange, carried at full speed between trained minds.

The scholar above her had not lied. She had only told it through envy.

The last turn opened on a short approach with the sanctum door standing wide.

No guard on it. No hurried barricade. Whoever had taken the room for granted had done so completely.

Cold tightened around Sylvanas as she crossed the final stretch. The corridor dust gave way to cleaner stone. Green fungi-light spilled across the threshold and flattened everything it touched.

She stepped through and took the room in one sweep.

Desk at center-left under chalk-filmed slate. Shelves and specimen jars along the walls. One side table with a cup gone cold beside it. Boards covered in two hands' work. Kel'Thuzad seated behind the desk, gaunt and pale-eyed, one hand still resting near an open folio, interrupted in the middle of ordinary work. Jaina three strides off the boards, not by the door, not with staff raised, not in any posture that called itself captive. Chalk dust marked the fingers of her left hand. More white streaked one sleeve.

The room was an office at the bottom of a grave.

That normalcy did the worst of it. No chains. No circle. No obvious force.

Only evidence.

Kel'Thuzad looked at her as one professional might acknowledge another entering late to an unpleasant meeting.

"Ranger-General," he said. "You have excellent timing. We were at the useful part."

His voice carried no strain at all. No false charm. No theatrical goading. Simple fact, offered with the same calm he might have used to name a theorem.

Sylvanas did not answer him. Her eyes went once more to Jaina.

Cold tea close to hand. Boards worked over for hours. Two sets of notation. Chalk on skin. Jaina's shoulders still and square, not caved inward. No sign of struggle on the room, the robe, the body. The deeper route's filth under all this only sharpened the contrast. She had come down through butchered waste to find refinement built on top of it, and Jaina standing in the middle as though she had been permitted all of it.

Worse: as though she had stayed.

Jaina saw the read land. That showed too, though everything else in her face locked down hard. One beat of stillness. One small failure to speak quickly enough. She knew what the room said.

Sylvanas filed it before anger had a chance to get theatrical.

Not betrayal. Not that. Something colder and more useful.

A flaw.

Kel'Thuzad rose without hurry. "Lady Proudmoore has an exceptionally disciplined mind," he said. "The first in years worth speaking to without simplification."

Honest. That was the wound in it.

No sneer. No bait.

He meant every word.

The file closed inside her with a clean, mechanical certainty. Jaina remained Jaina—useful, dangerous, the partner who had kept pace with her from road to city to trap ground. But the room had supplied a new line item: if the enemy spoke to the part of her that loved structure more than consequence, she could be held in place longer than was safe.

There was no good time to say that aloud. There might never be one.

Jaina's hand moved a fraction toward the staff propped near the board, then stopped, waiting.

Waiting for what?

For Sylvanas to read the room. For Sylvanas to decide whether she was still part of it.

Kel'Thuzad saw both women see it.

"You needn't look so wounded," he said to Jaina, though his eyes were on Sylvanas. "Comprehension is not capitulation. Serious minds deserve to be met seriously."

Jaina's mouth tightened.

That was enough.

Sylvanas moved.

Kel'Thuzad's hand flashed to the desk and a ward line struck for the far door. Jaina snapped first, ice racing white across the stone and sealing the exit in a pane thick as a shield. The spell left one northeast lane clear where the desk and board made awkward angles.

On any other day Sylvanas would have trusted the opening the instant she saw it.

This time she checked.

One heartbeat only. Long enough to see Jaina's eyes cut to the lane and hold it for her. Deliberate. Not a mistake. Then Sylvanas was through it, sliding past the desk edge as Kel'Thuzad turned with more speed than his scholar's body promised.

The first cast hit the stone where her shoulder had been.

She came up inside the second and loosed at close range. Kel'Thuzad twisted; the arrow took robe and flesh high in the arm instead of the throat. He did not cry out. Merely stepped back, one hand already sketching a new line in the air.

Jaina broke it with frost before the shape finished. White lattice climbed from floor to shelf, turning two walls into sealed planes and hemming him toward the center.

Good. Fast. Still the same language between them.

But even that carried the new gap in it. Jaina left space around Sylvanas she had not needed to leave before, as though confirming lane and body both. Sylvanas saw it and altered half a stride wider than the best shot required.

Functional. Damaging.

Kel'Thuzad backed into the narrowing geometry and laid one hand against the desk, steady as a lecturer at his podium. "Observe," he said, and drove power into the floor.

The sanctum answered with a pulse sharp enough to shake the jars on the shelves. One toppled and burst on the stone. Preservative spread under Sylvanas's boots in a cold slick. She braced on the desk edge, used it to turn, and saw Jaina already reading the pattern.

"Don't meet it," Jaina said.

Sylvanas didn't ask what it was. She trusted the order and went low. The ward surge passed over her shoulder in a skinning wave of green light and smashed into Jaina's ice instead, cracking the lattice across both walls.

Jaina did not flinch from the backlash. She cut her staff across the air and the broken lines folded inward, not stronger now but narrower, turning the room into a closing cage.

"Northeast," she said.

There it was again. The lane.

Sylvanas took it.

This time the confirmation came from Kel'Thuzad, not Jaina. His eyes tracked the same opening a fraction too late, scholar's calm finally asked to move at a soldier's speed.

Sylvanas reached the mark where Jaina's barrier left him nowhere clean to turn.

Bowstring. Breath. Release.

The arrow hit him in the throat.

It landed with almost no drama. A period, not a flourish. Kel'Thuzad's head rocked back once. Blood darkened the front of his robe in a narrow line.

His hands stayed on the desk.

For one strange instant he looked less like a dying man than a lecturer deciding whether to finish the sentence anyway.

He did.

"Northeastern corridor," he said through blood and ruin, each word pared down to cost. "Highest concentration."

Then the strength went out of his knees.

He folded behind the desk without taking his gaze from the room until the last possible moment. No plea. No curse. No final revelation offered for grandeur's sake. Only one more useful fact, priced and delivered before the body failed.

The fungi-light pulsed once and settled lower.

Silence came down over the sanctum hard enough to feel.

Jaina lowered her staff first. Not much. Enough to show the fight was over.

Sylvanas did not move to her.

She crossed to the desk instead, looked down at Kel'Thuzad where he had collapsed against the chair, and read what remained: no panic, no surprise, no true fear. The calm stayed on him even at the end. He had expected death the way another man expected weather. That, too, went into the file.

Then she looked at the desk.

Open folios. Nested diagrams. Jaina's copied farmhouse pages set beside his own work, claimed by the room now that they lay on his desk. Chalk ground into the grain of the wood. A cup gone cold near the place where Jaina had been standing. Too much time spent here. Too much proof of what shape that time had taken.

She heard Jaina breathe once through her nose. Quiet. Controlled.

"The grain isn't the plague," Jaina said.

Work voice. Too even.

"It's the carrier. The body does the real work after enough exposure. Death completes it."

Straight to the point. Useful first. No defense.

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the desk a moment longer, then turned.

Jaina still had chalk on one hand.

That detail would have meant nothing outside this room. Here it meant too much.

"Can you move?" Sylvanas asked.

"Yes."

No stumble in the answer. No attempt to explain the room around it.

Good. Better, perhaps. An explanation offered here would only have sounded like pleading to be read differently. Jaina knew that too. The knowledge sat between them and kept both women efficient.

Sylvanas went to the doorway. "We leave now."

Jaina nodded once, retrieved her staff, and fell in a half step behind.

The old partnership still knew how to move through danger.

Only the spacing had changed.

The building knew it too.

On the climb back up, Sylvanas found herself ranging closer to Jaina than the corridor strictly required. Not touch-close. Escort-close. Near enough to see the set of the mage's shoulders at every turn, near enough to answer if the floor, the wall, or the woman herself gave way without warning.

Jaina noticed. She said nothing.

Good.

Scholomance had changed with Kel'Thuzad dead beneath it. Not empty. Not clean. Pressure still lived in the stones. But the ordering mind had gone out of the place, leaving the wards to cycle on habit and old instructions. The fungi-light dimmed and brightened at the wrong intervals. Doors stood partway open where they had been shut on the descent. Somewhere far above, a bell gave one uncertain note and stopped.

They reached the crypt level without resistance.

The discard rooms remained what they had been. Hooks. vats. Parts laid out by category. The work outlived the worker. Jaina kept her eyes ahead and her mouth shut, which was wiser than turning those rooms into argument. Sylvanas approved of the choice and hated that she had to file the approval instead of letting it happen in the old easy way.

At the service corridor she lifted one hand.

Jaina stopped at once.

Ahead, the cabinet where Sylvanas had left the young scholar against the wall stood open. The curtain cord lay on the floor in two cut lengths.

Gone.

Of course.

Jandice Barov had enough pride to talk and enough sense to leave once the knife above her head went elsewhere. That made her a future problem. Not tonight's.

"She got out," Jaina said.

Sylvanas looked once at the severed cord, then down the branch hall where no one now stood. "Keep moving."

They took the breach route back, not the guest path. Service stairs. Window room. Wet stone underfoot where lake air reached in. Jaina kept pace easily enough, though the silence around her had a different weight than fatigue. She carried the new knowledge whole. Sylvanas could see that much in the stripped economy of her answers and the way her gaze touched each hazard only once before moving on. Nothing wasted. Nothing volunteered either.

At the window, dusk had gone thin and grey over the lake.

Cyndia was there exactly where Sylvanas had left her, one knee braced in the boat, bow across both hands. Her head turned right before the sound of them fully reached her, then right again, a second check into the absence she still carried, and only after that to the window where they emerged.

Her eyes went first to Sylvanas's face. Then to Jaina. Then back to the corridor behind them, measuring whether anything followed.

"Clear?" she asked.

"For now."

Cyndia looked at Jaina one beat longer. Chalk on the sleeve. Dust on the hem. Alive.

No comment.

She brought the boat in under the sill and held it while they dropped down. Jaina landed light but with the room's careful stiffness back in every joint. Sylvanas came after and took the stern. Cyndia pushed them off without another word.

The return crossing took less time. Perhaps because there was no question left in it about whether they were reaching the island or leaving it.

Mist gathered again as soon as the wall fell behind them. The ward touched the boat once, thinner now, a searching pressure that found three living bodies and let them pass. Cyndia rowed. Jaina sat in the middle with the staff across her knees. Sylvanas took the bow and watched the shore grow out of haze.

No one spoke until mud found the keel.

Then Cyndia said, "Relay found me on the south track an hour before full dark. Hearthglen's overwhelmed. Arthas is already moving toward Stratholme."

There it was. The next fire, waiting with its mouth open.

Sylvanas hauled the boat up just far enough to keep the lake from taking it and turned toward the horses.

"We ride now."

Cyndia was already cutting the tether loose.

Jaina rose more slowly. Not weakly. Just with the measured care of a body that had spent too long standing still in the wrong room.

"I have the full mechanism," she said.

Sylvanas gathered the reins. "Give me the road part."

Jaina mounted. "The grain only carries the outer shell. Repeated exposure teaches the body the pattern. Death releases it."

Short. Clean. No chalkboard in the voice now. Only information.

Sylvanas swung into the saddle and turned south.

"Anything that changes Stratholme?"

"If the grain reached the city weeks ago, then yes."

That was all. Enough for this stretch of road. The rest could wait until the horses settled into a rough rhythm and the night gave them room to hear themselves think.

They formed by habit and damage both.

Cyndia took the left, where she had always belonged.

Jaina rode center.

Sylvanas set herself forward-right by half a step and kept there, close enough to check the mage without seeming to, far enough to make the change plain to anyone who knew the old line.

Behind them, Darrowmere disappeared into mist.

Ahead, the south road took them through contaminated dark toward a city that might already be eating its own dead.

Jaina's next words came into the hoofbeat without preamble.

"There's more," she said.

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the road.

"Tell me while we ride."
