# Chapter 13: The Arrival

Cold reached them before the shoreline did.

Cold changed first. A wet mineral chill got under leather and stayed. Then the trees thinned and the ground leveled beneath the horses.

Sylvanas raised a hand. Cyndia checked her mare at once.

Darrowmere lay under low white haze. Green-black water showed where the mist tore thin, and farther out Caer Darrow rose in pieces: seawall, tower, old stone dark with damp. The manor behind them had been the front door. The real seat sat on the island.

No birds.

No reeds alive with motion, no wings lifting from shore. Only water against mud and leather creaking when Cyndia shifted.

Sylvanas dismounted and crossed the last stretch on foot. Three rowboats lay above the waterline. One had rotted through at the bow. One was tied off. The third had been shoved up in a hurry and left free.

She crouched beside it. Fresh scrape under the keel. Wet still clung to the planks.

Jaina had crossed.

A green shimmer moved over the lake and vanished. The ward sat low across the approach, not to stop a boat but to feel one.

Cyndia came up leading both horses. "One out."

"I saw."

Cyndia studied the boats, the post, the mist. Then her head turned right before she caught herself and brought it back.

Lyriel's place. Still living in the motion.

"Watch on the public landing?" Sylvanas asked.

"Two posts on the west face when the mist shifts. Hooded lantern. They want visitors to find them. East wall's worse stone. Less traffic. One upper window open a hand's width."

Sylvanas found it: narrow, high, good.

"We take that."

"And the ward?"

"If it was built for the proper landing, it will bite harder there. We hug the wall."

Cyndia put both hands on the gunwale and shoved with her shoulder. The hull came free of the mud with a wet suck. Cyndia wrapped cloth over the oarlocks before she set the oars. Automatic. Practiced. One more place a fourth hand should have been.

Sylvanas steadied the boat while Cyndia shoved off, then stepped in and took the bow. Mist closed around them at once.

Only the oars stayed certain.

Wood dipped. Lifted. Water touched the hull in small black sounds.

Halfway over the ward brushed them. A pressure touched the back of her teeth and searched along her skin. Sylvanas lowered herself and kept the bow across her thighs. The pressure slid by and thinned against stronger lines farther west.

"Still there?" she murmured.

"Barely. East wall's muting it."

The stone loomed out of the mist at arm's length, slick and black where the lake touched it. Cyndia brought the boat in without scraping. Sylvanas rose, caught a seam between stones, and climbed.

Wet mortar. Cold stone. Her boots found holds where age had bitten chunks out of the wall. She flattened herself under the window Cyndia had pointed out, listened, then eased up.

Shelves, crates, a bench. Storage room.

Good enough.

She swung through and landed in a crouch on damp stone.

Storage room, or what had once been one. Dust over preserving salts, old blood, and a sweeter undertone beneath both. Crates lined one wall. Along the other stood narrow tables under linen cloths dark at the hems.

One cloth twitched.

Sylvanas crossed the room and snapped it back.

A peasant hand lay on the table by itself, greying at the wrist, fingers scratching slowly at the wood.

She dropped the cloth.

Invited scholars came through the front door. This was what the side of the building was for.

She cracked the inner door and listened. Silence, then the place beneath it: a door closing somewhere overhead, steps, ward-hum in the walls. School.

Service corridor beyond. Clean, narrow, shelves sunk into the walls with ledgers, jars, boxes marked in a quick scholarly hand. One stair rose toward the respectable route. Another, half-hidden behind an iron gate left ajar, went down.

Jaina's trail did not go up.

A damp print where lake water had dripped from a hem. Chalk dust on a low rail. Scuffs from harder field boots than usually came through this corridor.

Enough.

She moved deeper.

The corridor had been broken into workrooms: a pantry turned reagent store, a family archive turned records room, a sitting room stripped to stone and worktables. In one chamber six bodies lay under linen, not dead enough for comfort. Chests moved in shallow, matched lifts. An empty seventh table stood beside iron restraints and a basin gone pink at the drain.

Another room had drawers sorted by part.

Hands together. Eyes together. Jaws in a straw-lined crate, several still working with a dry clack.

Scholarly order sat over butcher's work, making the cuts look methodical.

A spell snapped past her ear.

Sylvanas dropped, rolled, and came up inside the second cast before it finished. A young woman in Barov burgundy hit the wall hard enough to lose the words. Scholar's hands. Reagent burns. Dark hair torn loose where Sylvanas drove her forearm across the throat.

"Quiet."

The woman clawed for the focus ring on her left hand. Sylvanas caught the wrist and pinned it to the floor. "Try it again and I break the hand."

That bought stillness.

The scholar's breath came fast but angry. Her gaze ran over Sylvanas's leathers, the bow, the wet mark on one sleeve, and found the answer she hated.

"You came through the side."

"Talk."

"You are late."

Sylvanas leaned harder until the wrist bones shifted.

Pain sharpened the woman's face. "The room is below. Even you should hear that."

"Who is with him?"

"Your mage."

Too fast to be invented. Sylvanas kept her weight where it was.

"What shape is the room?"

"A study. Chalkboards. Whatever will anger you most after the fact." Bitterness roughened the words. "He sent me out because she could keep up and he wanted the pleasure alone."

"She asked him questions?"

The woman's answer came through clenched teeth. "Real ones."

"And he answered."

"In full."

Poison in the phrasing, yes. Not enough poison to erase the fact.

"Way down."

"Lower stair. Past the old crypt spine. Take the guest route if you want the whole building awake."

Sylvanas bound the woman's wrists with a curtain cord and shoved her down beside a cabinet of sealed jars.

"Stay there."

The laugh that followed was thin and furious. "If he'd wanted her gone, she'd be gone. He kept talking."

Sylvanas took the lower stair.

After twenty steps, the manor disguise gave way to the crypt. The stone changed under her boots—older blocks, rougher joints, dust dry enough to rise in soft puffs. Lamp oil thinned. Preservative, old rot, and sweet-rot grew stronger.

Discard rooms opened off the crypt run at uneven intervals.

No scholars here. No curated shelves.

In one chamber a vat held three shoulders above black fluid and no heads above the line. In another, failed stock hung from hooks by wire through wrists and ankles, some dead, some moving after a fashion that showed memory had not stayed with the meat. A jaw worked in a bucket at the foot of a table, patient as a hinge.

The clean room below would sit over the same butcher's work.

Then the voices reached her.

Kel'Thuzad first, low and measured through stone. A lecturer finishing a point.

Then Jaina, lower than usual and thinking.

Sylvanas stopped in the corridor, one hand on the bow.

They were laying out structure and testing it for weak places.

The scholar above had not lied. She had only told it through envy.

The last turn opened on a short approach with the sanctum door standing wide.

No guard. No barricade. Whoever owned the room had taken his safety for granted.

Cold tightened around Sylvanas as she crossed the final stretch. Corridor dust gave way to cleaner stone. Green fungi-light spilled across the threshold and flattened everything it touched.

She stepped through and read the room in one sweep.

A chalk-filmed slate hung over the desk. Shelves and specimen jars lined the walls. A cold cup sat on a side table. Boards bore two hands' work. Kel'Thuzad stood behind the desk, gaunt and pale-eyed, one hand near an open folio, caught mid-task. Jaina stood three strides off the boards, not near the door, not with her staff raised, not standing like a captive. Chalk dust marked her left hand and streaked one sleeve.

A scholar's office buried under stone and bones.

Nothing visible held her there: no chains, no circle, no force.

Kel'Thuzad raised his head as another professional might mark a late arrival to an unpleasant meeting.

"Ranger-General," he said. "Good timing. We had reached the useful part."

Sylvanas did not answer. Her eyes went to Jaina again.

A cold cup sat within reach beside boards worked over for hours, chalk on skin, shoulders still square, no sign of struggle anywhere in the room. She had come down through butcher's waste to find refinement laid over it and Jaina standing in the middle with the settled posture of someone already inside the work.

Worse: as if she had stayed.

Jaina caught the read land. One beat of stillness.

Colder than betrayal: a weakness she could not ignore.

Kel'Thuzad rose without hurry. "Lady Proudmoore has a disciplined mind," he said. "The first in years worth speaking to without softening anything."

Honest. The plainness of it hit harder than a sneer.

No sneer. No bait.

He meant it.

Sylvanas had her answer: Jaina was still useful and dangerous, but structure could hold her still longer than safety allowed.

No good time to say that aloud. Never.

Jaina's hand moved a fraction toward the staff propped near the board, then stopped, waiting.

Waiting for Sylvanas to read the room. Waiting to learn whether she still belonged on the same side of it.

Kel'Thuzad caught that too. "No need to look so wounded," he said to Jaina, though his eyes stayed on Sylvanas. "Understanding me is not yielding. Minds like yours deserve honest company."

Jaina's mouth drew flat.

Enough.

Sylvanas moved.

Kel'Thuzad's hand flashed to the desk and a ward line struck for the far door. Jaina snapped first, ice racing white across the stone and sealing the exit in a pane thick as a shield. The spell left one northeast lane clear where desk and board made bad angles.

On any other day Sylvanas would have trusted the opening at once.

This time she checked.

One heartbeat. Long enough to see Jaina's eyes cut to the lane and hold it there for her. Deliberate.

Then Sylvanas was through, sliding past the desk as Kel'Thuzad turned faster than his frame had suggested. The first cast hit the stone where her shoulder had been. She came up inside the second and loosed at close range. Kel'Thuzad twisted; the arrow took robe and flesh high in the arm instead of the throat. He did not cry out. One hand was already sketching the next line.

Jaina broke it with frost before the shape finished. White lattice climbed floor to shelf, sealing two walls and hemming him toward the center.

Good. Fast. Still the same language between them.

But Jaina still left extra room in it. She left more room around Sylvanas than the shot required, confirming lane and body both. Sylvanas took that in and went half a stride wider than the cleanest line demanded.

It still worked, and Sylvanas felt the cost.

Kel'Thuzad backed into the narrowing space and braced one hand on the desk. "Watch," he said, and drove power into the floor.

The sanctum answered with a pulse sharp enough to shake the jars. One toppled and burst. Preservative spread cold under Sylvanas's boots. She caught the desk edge, turned, and found Jaina reading the pattern already.

"Don't meet it," Jaina said.

Sylvanas went low without asking why. Green light skinned over her shoulder and smashed into Jaina's ice instead, cracking the lattice across both walls.

Jaina did not flinch from the backlash. She cut her staff through the air and the broken lines folded inward, narrower now, turning the room into a closing cage.

"Northeast."

There it was again.

Sylvanas took it.

This time Kel'Thuzad confirmed the opening for her. His eyes tracked the same lane a fraction too late, scholar's calm finally made to move at a soldier's speed.

Sylvanas reached the mark where Jaina's barriers left him nowhere clean to turn.

Bowstring. Breath. Release.

The arrow hit him in the throat.

His head rocked back once. Blood ran dark down the front of his robe.

His hands stayed on the desk.

For one strange instant he looked more annoyed at being interrupted than afraid of dying.

He did.

"Northeast corridor," he said through blood. "Strongest there."

Then the strength went out of his knees.

He folded behind the desk without plea or curse. One last useful fact, given before the body failed.

The fungi-light pulsed once and settled lower.

Silence came down over the sanctum.

Jaina lowered her staff first. Enough.

Sylvanas did not go to her.

She crossed to the desk instead and stood over Kel'Thuzad collapsed against the chair. He showed neither panic nor surprise; the calm had held to the end. He looked at it without surprise.

Then the desk.

Open folios lay beside nested diagrams. Jaina's copied farmhouse pages lay beside his own work on the desk. Chalk ground into the grain. A cup gone cold beside the place where Jaina had stood.

The cup had gone cold beside pages worked over long enough to grind chalk into the grain.

Jaina breathed once through her nose. Quiet. Controlled.

"The grain isn't the plague," she said. Work voice. Too even. "It's the carrier. The body does the real work after enough exposure. Death completes it."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the desk another moment, then turned.

Jaina still had chalk on one hand.

That detail would have meant nothing outside this room. Here it meant too much.

"Can you move?" Sylvanas asked.

"Yes."

Good. Any explanation here would sound like a plea, and Jaina left it unsaid.

"We leave now."

Jaina took up the staff and fell in a half step behind.

They cleared the stair without speaking and without colliding once.

On the climb back up Sylvanas kept a ranger's half-step too close to Jaina. Not touch-close. Escort-close. Near enough to answer if the floor, the wall, or the woman herself gave way.

She said nothing.

Good.

With Kel'Thuzad dead, the wards and routines kept cycling on their own. Still foul. Still active. But the ordering mind was gone. Fungi-light dimmed at the wrong moments. Doors stood partway open where they had been shut on the descent. Somewhere above, a bell gave one uncertain note and stopped.

They reached the service corridor without resistance.

The discard rooms had not changed. Hooks. Vats. Parts laid out by category. Kel'Thuzad was dead, but the hooks and vats were still there. Jaina kept her eyes ahead and her mouth shut. Wise.

At the cabinet where Sylvanas had left the young scholar, the door stood open. The curtain cord lay on the floor in two cut lengths.

Gone.

Of course.

Jandice Barov had enough pride to talk and enough sense to leave when the knife above her head went elsewhere. Jandice Barov could wait.

"She got out," Jaina said.

Sylvanas gave the severed cord one glance. "Keep moving."

They took the breach route back. Service stairs. Window room. Wet stone where lake air reached in. Jaina kept pace easily enough, but the silence around her carried a different weight now. Jaina wasted no motion and offered nothing she had not been asked for.

At the window dusk had gone thin and grey over the lake.

Cyndia waited exactly where Sylvanas had left her, one knee braced in the boat, bow across both hands. Her head turned right before the sound of them fully reached her, then right again, a second check into the absence she still carried, and only after that to the window.

Her eyes went first to Sylvanas. Then to Jaina. Then to the corridor behind them.

"Clear?"

"For now."

Cyndia gave Jaina one beat longer. Chalk on the sleeve. Dust on the hem. Alive.

She said nothing.

She brought the boat under the sill and held it steady while they dropped down. Jaina landed light, but every joint had gone careful and rigid again. Sylvanas came after and took the stern. Cyndia pushed them off without a word.

The return crossing took less time. Everyone faced shore.

Mist gathered as soon as the wall fell behind them. The ward touched the boat once, thinner now, a searching pressure that found three living bodies and let them go. Cyndia rowed. Jaina sat in the middle with the staff across her knees. Sylvanas took the bow and let shore grow out of haze.

No one spoke until mud found the keel.

Then Cyndia said, "Relay found me on the south track an hour before full dark. Hearthglen's overwhelmed. Arthas is already moving toward Stratholme."

Hearthglen was overwhelmed, and Stratholme was next.

Sylvanas hauled the boat up far enough to keep the lake from taking it and turned for the horses. "We ride now."

Cyndia was already cutting the tether loose.

Jaina rose more slowly. With the measured care of a body that had spent too long standing still in the wrong room.

"I have the full mechanism," she said.

"Give me the road part."

Jaina mounted. "The grain only carries the outer shell. Repeated exposure teaches the body the pattern. Death releases it."

Sylvanas swung into the saddle. "Anything that changes Stratholme?"

"If the grain reached the city weeks ago, yes."

The rest could wait until the horses found rhythm.

They fell back into line by habit, but the spacing stayed wrong.

Cyndia took the left, where she had always belonged.

Jaina rode center.

Sylvanas set herself forward-right by half a step and kept there, close enough to check the mage without seeming to, far enough that anyone who knew the old line would see the change.

Behind them Darrowmere disappeared into mist.

Ahead the south road ran through contaminated dark toward a city whose people might already be eating their own dead.

Jaina spoke into the hoofbeats.

"There's more."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the road. "Tell me while we ride."
