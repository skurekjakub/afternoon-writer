# Chapter 13: The Arrival

Cold reached them before the shoreline.

The chill turned wet and mineral beneath the leather. Trees thinned. Ground leveled under the horses.

Sylvanas raised a hand. Cyndia checked her mare at once.

Darrowmere lay under low white haze. Green-black water showed where the mist tore thin, and farther out Caer Darrow rose in pieces: seawall, tower, old stone. The manor had been the front door. The real seat sat on the island.

No birds. No reeds stirring.

Sylvanas dismounted and walked the last stretch. Three rowboats lay above the waterline: one rotted through, one tied off, one shoved up and left free. Fresh scrape under the keel. Wet still clung to the planks.

Jaina had crossed.

A green shimmer moved over the lake and vanished. The ward sat low across the approach, built less to stop a boat than feel one.

Cyndia came up leading both horses. "One out."

"I saw."

Cyndia studied the boats and the mist. Then her head turned right before she caught herself and brought it back. Lyriel's place. Still living in the motion.

"Watch on the public landing?" Sylvanas asked.

"Two posts on the west face when the mist shifts. Hooded lantern. East wall's worse stone. Less traffic. One upper window open a hand's width."

Sylvanas found it.

"We take that."

"And the ward?"

"If it was built for the proper landing, it will bite harder there. We hug the wall."

Cyndia shoved the boat free and wrapped cloth over the oarlocks before she set the oars.

Sylvanas stepped in and took the bow. Mist closed around them at once.

Only the oars stayed certain. Water touched the hull in small black sounds.

Halfway over the ward brushed them. Pressure touched the back of her teeth and searched along her skin. Sylvanas lowered herself and kept the bow across her thighs until it slid by.

"Still there?" she murmured.

"Barely. East wall's muting it."

The stone loomed out of the mist at arm's length, slick and black where the lake touched it. Cyndia brought the boat in without scraping. Sylvanas caught a seam between stones and climbed.

Wet mortar. Cold stone.

She flattened herself under the window, listened, then eased up. Shelves, crates, a bench. Storage room.

She swung through and landed in a crouch.

Dust over preserving salts and old blood. Narrow tables stood under linen cloths dark at the hems.

One cloth twitched.

Sylvanas snapped it back.

A peasant hand lay on the table by itself, greying at the wrist, fingers scratching slowly at the wood.

She dropped the cloth.

The side route led straight into the work.

She cracked the inner door and listened. Silence, then a door overhead, steps, ward-hum in the walls. A school still at work.

Service corridor beyond. Shelves with ledgers, jars, boxes marked in a quick scholarly hand. One stair rose toward the respectable route. Another, half-hidden behind an iron gate left ajar, went down.

Wet print and chalk dust both led that way.

The corridor had been broken into workrooms. In one chamber six bodies lay under linen, chests lifting in shallow matched breaths, with an empty seventh table beside restraints and a basin gone pink at the drain.

Another room held drawers sorted by part: hands together, eyes together, jaws in a straw-lined crate, several still working with a dry clack.

A spell snapped past her ear.

Sylvanas dropped, rolled, and came up inside the second cast before it finished. A young woman in Barov burgundy hit the wall hard enough to lose the words. Scholar's hands. Reagent burns.

"Quiet."

The woman clawed for the focus ring on her left hand. Sylvanas caught the wrist and pinned it. "Try it again and I break the hand."

The woman went still. Her gaze ran over Sylvanas's leathers, the bow, the wet mark on one sleeve.

"You came through the side."

"Talk."

"You are late."

Sylvanas leaned harder until the wrist bones shifted.

Pain sharpened the woman's face. "The room is below."

"Who is with him?"

"Your mage."

"What room?"

"A study. Chalkboards. Whatever will anger you most after the fact." Bitterness roughened the words. "He sent me out because she could keep up and he wanted the pleasure alone."

"She asked him questions?"

"Real ones. He answered in full."

"Way down."

"Lower stair. Past the old crypt spine."

Sylvanas bound the woman's wrists with a curtain cord and shoved her beside a cabinet of sealed jars.

"Stay there."

The laugh that followed was thin and furious. "If he'd wanted her gone, she'd be gone. He kept talking."

Sylvanas took the lower stair.

After twenty steps the manor disguise gave way to the crypt: older blocks, rougher joints, dust dry enough to lift in soft puffs. Lamp oil thinned. Preservative and sweet-rot grew stronger.

Discard rooms opened off the run at uneven intervals. In one chamber a vat held three shoulders above black fluid and no heads above the line. In another, failed stock hung from hooks by wire through wrists and ankles. A jaw worked in a bucket at the foot of a table, patient as a hinge.

The clean room below sat over the same butcher's work.

Then the voices reached her.

Kel'Thuzad first, low and measured through stone.

Then Jaina, lower than usual and thinking.

Sylvanas stopped in the corridor, one hand on the bow.

The scholar above had not lied. She had only told it through envy.

The last turn opened on a short approach with the sanctum door standing wide. No guard. No bar.

Cold tightened around Sylvanas as she stepped through.

A chalk-filmed slate over the desk. Shelves and specimen jars. A cold cup on the side table. Boards bearing two hands' work. Kel'Thuzad behind the desk, gaunt and pale-eyed. Jaina three strides off the boards, away from the door, staff down. Chalk dust on her left hand and one sleeve.

Nothing visible held her there.

Kel'Thuzad raised his head. "Ranger-General," he said. "Good timing. We had reached the useful part."

Sylvanas did not answer. Her eyes went to Jaina again.

Cold cup. Boards worked over. Chalk on skin. No sign of struggle.

She had come down through butcher's waste to find Jaina standing in the middle with the settled posture of someone who had stayed.

Jaina caught the read land. One beat of stillness. Her hand moved a fraction toward the staff propped near the board, then stopped.

Kel'Thuzad rose without hurry. "Lady Proudmoore has a disciplined mind," he said. "The first in years worth speaking to without softening anything."

The words landed clean. Worse for that.

Enough.

Sylvanas moved.

Kel'Thuzad's hand flashed to the desk and a ward line struck for the far door. Jaina snapped first, ice racing white across the stone and sealing the exit in a pane thick as a shield. The spell left one northeast opening clear where desk and board made bad angles.

On any other day Sylvanas would have trusted it at once.

This time she checked.

One heartbeat. Long enough to see Jaina's eyes cut to the opening and hold there for her.

Then Sylvanas was through, sliding past the desk as Kel'Thuzad turned faster than his frame had suggested. The first cast hit stone where her shoulder had been. She came up inside the second and loosed at close range. He twisted; the arrow took robe and flesh high in the arm instead of the throat.

Jaina broke the next cast with frost before the shape finished. White lattice climbed floor to shelf, sealing two walls and hemming him toward the center.

Kel'Thuzad braced one hand on the desk. "Watch," he said, and drove power into the floor.

A pulse jumped through the stone hard enough to shake the jars. One toppled and burst. Preservative spread cold under Sylvanas's boots.

"Don't meet it," Jaina said.

Sylvanas went low without asking why. Green light skinned over her shoulder and smashed into Jaina's ice instead, cracking the lattice across both walls.

Jaina cut her staff through the air and the broken lines folded inward, narrower now, turning the room into a closing cage.

"Northeast."

There it was again.

Sylvanas took it.

This time Kel'Thuzad confirmed it for her. His eyes tracked the same opening a fraction too late, scholar's calm dragged into a soldier's speed.

Bowstring. Breath. Release.

The arrow hit him in the throat.

His head rocked back once. Blood ran dark down the front of his robe. His hands stayed on the desk.

For one strange instant he looked more annoyed at being interrupted than afraid of dying.

"Northeast corridor," he said through blood. "Strongest there."

Then the strength went out of his knees.

He folded behind the desk without plea or curse. Even dying, he gave them a direction.

The sanctum went silent.

Jaina lowered her staff first.

Sylvanas did not go to her.

She crossed to the desk instead and stood over Kel'Thuzad collapsed against the chair. Open folios lay beside nested diagrams, Jaina's copied farmhouse pages beside his own work, chalk ground into the wood.

Jaina drew one measured breath through her nose.

"The grain isn't the plague," she said. "It carries it. The body does the rest after enough of it. Death finishes it."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the desk another moment, then turned.

Jaina still had chalk on one hand.

"Can you move?" Sylvanas asked.

"Yes."

"We leave now."

Jaina took up the staff and fell in a half step behind.

They climbed back in silence, without colliding once. Sylvanas kept a ranger's half-step too close to Jaina. Near enough to answer if the floor, the wall, or the woman herself gave way.

The wards still cycled after Kel'Thuzad, foul and active. Without him at the center they had begun to slip. Fungi-light dimmed at the wrong moments. Doors stood partway open where they had been shut on the way down. Somewhere above, a bell gave one note and stopped.

At the cabinet where Sylvanas had left the young scholar, the door stood open. The curtain cord lay on the floor in two cut lengths.

Gone.

Of course.

Jandice Barov had enough pride to talk and enough sense to leave when the knife above her head went elsewhere.

"She got out," Jaina said.

Sylvanas gave the severed cord one glance. "Keep moving."

They took the breach route back: service stairs, window room, wet stone where lake air reached in. Jaina kept pace and offered nothing beyond it.

At the window dusk had gone thin and grey over the lake.

Cyndia waited where Sylvanas had left her, one knee braced in the boat, bow across both hands. Her head turned right before the sound of them reached her, then right again, a second check into the absence she still carried, and only after that to the window.

Her eyes went first to Sylvanas. Then to Jaina. Then to the corridor behind them.

"Clear?"

"For now."

Cyndia gave Jaina one beat longer. Chalk on the sleeve. Dust on the hem.

She brought the boat under the sill and held it steady while they dropped down. Jaina landed light, but every joint had gone careful and rigid again. Sylvanas came after and took the stern. Cyndia pushed them off.

The return crossing took less time. Everyone faced shore.

Mist gathered as soon as the wall fell behind them. The ward touched the boat once, thinner now, a searching pressure that found three living bodies and let them go. Cyndia rowed.

No one spoke until mud found the keel.

Then Cyndia said, "Relay found me on the south track an hour before full dark. Hearthglen's overwhelmed. Arthas is already moving toward Stratholme."

If Hearthglen had broken, Stratholme came next.

Sylvanas hauled the boat up far enough to keep the lake from taking it and turned for the horses. "We ride now."

Cyndia was already cutting the tether loose.

Jaina rose with the measured care of a body that had spent too long standing still in the wrong room.

"I know how it wakes," she said.

"Give me the road part."

Jaina mounted. "The grain carries the outer shell. The body learns the rest from living with it. Death lets it answer."

"Anything that changes Stratholme?"

"If the grain reached the city weeks ago, yes."

They fell back into line by habit. Cyndia took the left, where she had always belonged. Jaina rode center. Sylvanas set herself forward-right by half a step, close enough to check the mage without seeming to, far enough that anyone who knew the old line would see the change.

Behind them Darrowmere disappeared into mist. Ahead, the south road cut through contaminated dark toward a city whose people might already be eating their own dead.

Jaina spoke into the hoofbeats.

"There's more."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the road. "Tell me while we ride."
