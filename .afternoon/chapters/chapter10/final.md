# Chapter 10: The Estate

Trimmed ditches and fresh labor kept Eastweald too neat for famine country.

Jaina rode through ruts in the old road, her Thalassian mare sweating in the cold while orchard walls and trimmed ditches showed fresh labor and money. Frost silvered the low grass. Barov grain fed half a province, and the estate meant to keep it moving.

The wrongness reached her through the reins first, a live tingle under the skin, then thickened as the road bent southeast. The estate gave her grain, stone walls, labor. The wards beneath it rose in layers, old work under newer, and the heaviest pressure sat beyond the house itself, out toward the lake.

She should have measured the gate, the treeline, the way back out if the servants smiled and the doors closed behind her. Instead she had a horse, copied route scraps, Cyndia's notes in her sleeve, and enough Kirin Tor authority to make a noble family hesitate before lying. No escort. No patience left for pretending she could take a wall the way a ranger would.

So she rode straight at the front gate.

The Barov crest hung in iron above the gate, dark against pale stone. Two men in estate colors stopped talking when they saw her. One glance went to her staff, then the horse, then the cut of her robes, and calculation pinched the nearer man's mouth before he smoothed it away.


The nearer man stepped into the road and bowed just enough to be respectful without pretending humility.

"My lady?"

"Jaina Proudmoore," she said. "Protégée of Archmage Antonidas. I require an audience with Lord Barov."

He blinked once at the name, once at the tone. "Is Lord Barov expecting—"

"No," Jaina said. "If he had been, we could both have spared ourselves that sentence."

The driver's eyes dropped at once. The guard's did not. Servants here were trained to take pressure without folding.

"The estate is occupied with harvest concerns," he said.

"Then he will understand why a Kirin Tor mage has come to ask about his grain."

He stood aside. "Of course, my lady."

The mare tossed her head under the gate, steady even with ward hum under her hooves. Inside the walls the wind fell off. Men crossed the yard with baskets, ledgers, harness, sacks. They were busy, fed, and sure the estate ran as it should.

Jaina dismounted before the main house and handed the reins to a stable boy whose sleeves were patched twice at the wrists and clean besides. The front doors opened before she reached them.

The great hall beyond was too still for a room this warm. A dark hearth yawned at one end, yet the air stayed mild against her face. Enchanted stones warmed the walls where a fire should have been.

Lord Alexei Barov rose from a chair near a low table where tea waited for two.

Silver-haired, narrow-shouldered, dressed in dark wool that sat too well to be local tailoring.

"Lady Proudmoore," he said in a tone polished for expected guests. "House Barov is honored."

"I doubt that."

His smile held, but thinned. "Honored, then, by your frankness."

Jaina remained standing. "Your grain routes brought me here."

"A regrettable subject." He gestured toward the tea. "Please."

"No."

"As you wish." He folded one hand over the other. "I assume this concerns Andorhal. We have suffered losses there ourselves. Rumors of contamination travel faster than wagons. Panic travels faster still. Estates our size attract blame because scale attracts blame."

"I came to ask about more than rumor," she said.

"Then ask."

"How long has Caer Darrow been doing scholarly work under your protection?"

"You are direct," Alexei said.

"You keep saying things I already know."

"Caer Darrow houses a private academy. Patronizing scholarship is not a crime, though the larger cities often behave as if it is."

"What field?"

"Comparative magical theory, among others."

"Necromancy."

He held still.

"A vulgar word," he said mildly. "The sort used by people who believe coarse naming is the same as understanding."

"Then educate me," she said.

"Gladly," a woman's voice said from the doorway.

Jaina turned.

Jandice Barov wore burgundy robes cut on Dalaran lines, made from Barov cloth. The sleeves would not catch at the wrist while working. Her dark hair was pulled back hard. Ink marked one thumb. Pale reagent scars crossed the skin between the fingers of her right hand.

Recognition hit fast enough to feel rude. Her mouth twitched, then stilled.

"Lady Proudmoore," she said. "I am Jandice Barov. My father was about to describe our embarrassment with less precision than it deserves. May I rescue him?"

Jandice crossed the room with measured ease. Ink. Chalk ground flat at the thumb. Small burns from bench work instead of lectures.

"We have students," Jandice said. "Researchers. Archivists. Some traditionalists think banning a thing counts as discipline. Let them."

"And the grain?" Jaina asked.

"The grain," Alexei said, retaking the conversation with practiced smoothness, "is my burden. Theft, spoilage, panic pricing, opportunists along the road. Put the same noble name in enough frightened mouths and it starts to sound monstrous."

"You expected scrutiny," Jaina said.

"I expected famine to make everyone ugly."

"You seem very prepared for ugliness."

Jandice's eyes sharpened. Alexei's hands stayed still.

"We can spend the morning fencing politely," Jandice said, "or I can show you the work people keep misnaming."

Jaina looked at her. Young. About her own age. The kind of woman she would have argued with for hours in a Dalaran archive.

"You would permit that?" Jaina asked.

"Permit?" Jandice checked her father and found no objection. "If the Kirin Tor's brightest apprentice has come all this way to inspect our provincial little school, I'd rather watch the inspection."

Alexei touched his daughter with one weary look. "Jandice. Mind the presentable route."

"Father." Amusement flickered there; this had happened before.

She took the offered cup. It was excellent, which figured.

"You keep a lake crossing for your academy," she said.

"A fortress does better on an island," Jandice said. "Even a scholarly one."

"Scholars expecting inspection usually keep records closer to the road."

"Scholars expecting theft keep them behind water."

Jaina set the cup down. "Then let's go look at them."

The road to the landing cut through ordered ground, then ended at a lake dark under mist.

"You came alone," Jandice said.
"Yes."
"Bold."
"Efficient."

A long narrow boat waited at the landing, tied to iron rings sunk into old stone. Jandice untied the rope and stepped in with easy practice.

"You row yourself?" Jaina asked.

"When I want to arrive."

The lake carried a chemical cold, mineral-heavy and stale. The outer ward touched the back of Jaina's teeth before the boat reached it, light but deliberate. Enough to mark her.

She stepped into the boat anyway.

Jandice pushed them off. Mist dragged low over the surface, swallowing the shore behind them faster than it should have.

"My father believes most crises can be endured if the room is expensive enough," Jandice said.

The ward line took them with a brief green shimmer under the hull. The pressure shifted and passed the message inward through the lattice.

Caer Darrow rose out of the mist a piece at a time—the seawall first, then towers, then the keep itself. Old stone converted rather than rebuilt, every line still meant for defense. Narrow windows above. Broad doors below.

A single porter in dark clothes waited with a lantern hooded against daylight.

Jaina stepped onto the island and the deep pull of the place settled into her bones.

The strongest signature came from under it.

The porter bowed them in through a side entry broad enough for carts and too clean for a ruin. The stone floors had been scrubbed. Lamps burned behind fitted glass. The air smelled of limewash, ink, lamp oil, and preserving compounds worked into the mortar over years.

"Disappointed?" Jandice asked.

"No."

"We have lecture schedules."

Two students in dark robes moved ahead of them carrying slates. They glanced at Jaina's face, then the staff, and stepped aside with the brisk irritation of people delayed on the way to actual work.

Jaina walked beside Jandice through a hall where anatomical sketches hung between alcoves of labeled jars. A preserved human hand lay on one table as casually as a pointer.

"You teach openly," Jaina said.
"Within the building? Of course."
"I meant the work itself."
"So did I."

They entered a lecture room with tiered benches and a board still chalked from the previous class. Someone had drawn the stages of tissue breakdown in a fine decisive hand, then crossed part of the sequence out and replaced it with a better one. The notation in the margin hit Jaina in the chest: Antonidas's seminar marks, his school carried forward in another hand.

She moved closer before she could stop herself.

"You know the system," Jandice said quietly.

"Yes."

The corrections were recent. Chalk dust still clung to the ledge. Jaina read three lines and had to make herself keep breathing evenly. Whoever had written them had taken Kirin Tor rigor into the place the Council had nailed shut and kept working.

"You were trained in Dalaran?"

"Briefly adjacent to it." Irritation flashed. "Enough to know what it refuses. Come. The archive is more interesting than our classroom scraps."

They crossed two more corridors and a stair. By the second turn Jaina knew Jandice was walking her through chosen rooms. Doors stood open on tidy workrooms where assistants copied notes and checked labeled vials against ledgers.

Dry dark paneling boxed the archive around three lamp-weighted tables. Cabinets stood along one wall, locked cases along the other. A man with thin spectacles looked up, saw Jandice, and looked back down without question.

Jandice opened one cabinet with a key from inside her sleeve and drew out a leather-bound ledger. She set it before Jaina with care close to reverence.

"Recent circulation from the lower research rooms," she said. "Restricted, like most things worth reading."

The first page might as well have been her own under a bad light: the same notational spine, the same crowded lower margin, the same clipped symbols for failed branches.

Kel'Thuzad's work, or a student shaped by him so completely the forms had merged.

What sat in the ledger ran straight at the hole in the farmhouse read—host response, survival through storage, some taught answer held in living tissue. The final waking step was still missing. Even so, it pointed at the same gap she had seen in the farmhouse.

Old leather, metal gall, dust warmed by lamp glass, ink and reagent, Dalaran with rot laid over it.

"You see why I thought the word necromancy was too blunt," Jandice said.

Jaina kept her eyes on the ledger. "I see disciplined work."

"Disciplined," she repeated.

"You wanted a better word?"

"I wanted an honest one."

"Honest is harder to offer without a fuller sample."

Jandice rested her fingertips on the table edge. "Most visitors stop at disgust."

"Most visitors cannot read it."

Pleasure showed openly now, quick and naked enough to make her younger.

Jaina turned another page and found a column of marginal symbols Antonidas had made her memorize at sixteen because Kel'Thuzad had once insisted the older system wasted less time than the fashionable one. She should have recognized the lineage sooner.

Repeated references to inward transfers and pressure readings pointed below the archive. One title repeated often enough to matter: master.

Jaina closed the book.

"You keep saying we," she said.

"We do the work here."

"How far down does your we travel?"

Jandice's mouth flattened, and the warmth went out of her eyes.

"Far enough."

"Convenient answer."

"A practical one." Jandice tilted her head. "You came in breathing accusation and are now asking real questions. That's progress."

"I came in following plague grain through noble storehouses."

"And found an academy."

"I found Kirin Tor method under all of this, holding up rot."

Jandice went still, then laughed softly. "That is much closer."

She took the ledger back and slid it under her hand with proprietary ease.

"Come," Jandice said. "You've seen the shell. You should at least see how honestly it's built."

Bodies lay on numbered tables under linen in the preparation chamber. In the cataloguing room beyond, two copyists bent over reports. Past them, stripped joints and sinew samples sat arranged by stage, every tray numbered, every instrument cleaned and put back in its place.

Clean instruments and straightened tags were worse than rot would have been.

At each threshold the pressure below bit harder. The shown rooms rode over deeper work. Cracks in the masonry, wards drawn too tight, the faint drag under her skin: all of it pointed down, and every turn Jandice chose bent away from it.

"You've been reading down all afternoon," Jandice said.
"You've been expecting me to."
"I was hoping you would."
"Because you want me impressed."
"Because I prefer being understood."

Jaina stopped beside a narrow window looking across an inner court to another tower. Two robed researchers crossed below carrying a locked box between them. The ward seals on it were plain and strong. Above, on the opposite wall, a gallery door stood shut under three sigils feeding the same pull downward. Someone had a private way through, and rank enough to use it.

"Who teaches beneath us?" Jaina asked.

"Beneath us?" Jandice repeated. "How dramatic."

"The wards are louder there."

"Only to you."

"No." Jaina touched the stone beside the window. Cold. Dry. Alive with the drag of contained force. "To the masonry too."

Jandice clenched her jaw, then let it go.

"There are private rooms," she said. "Every serious institution has them."

"Private scholarship. Private storage. Private authority. You don't have access to all of it."

"Do you?"

"No."

"Then we are evenly placed for once."

They returned her to an upper guest chamber overlooking Darrowmere's black water. Thick rug. Carved chair. A writing desk already supplied with paper and ink.

"Write it down before you send it home," she said.

"Will I see the library again?" Jaina asked.

"If you ask interestingly." Jandice's gaze dropped to the ledger in Jaina's hand, and only then did Jaina realize she had carried it out of the archive without thinking. Jandice laughed under her breath. "Keep it for now. I'd rather not pretend you didn't mean to."

"You trust me with it?"

"I trust your appetite. Do not mistake the distinction."

She left.

The door closed softly. Wards in the room listened for voices all the same.

Jaina stood still for a count of ten and read what the wards were doing. Window. Desk. Ward-thread in the lintel. Another in the lamp base. A finer web hidden under the plaster around the doorframe, tuned to vibration and voice.

She sat at the desk, opened the ledger, and read until the light outside thinned.

The same answer kept turning up. Repeated entries showed tissue conditioned until it held the pattern and answered to it again. The outer preparation survived grain, travel, baking, gut passage. After that the work moved inward. What turned conditioned tissue into obedience after death?

By the time the sky over the lake went iron-grey she had three pages of notes and one word penciled twice in the margin.

Resonance.

Antonidas would tell her to leave with what she had. He would be right, and still too slow.

The sending stone in her satchel grew heavier the moment she considered it.

She set a muting line round the desk with the smallest ward she knew, one meant for privacy between scholars in crowded archive rooms, not for this depth of interference. It would not defeat the chamber. It might blur the edges enough to cost an eavesdropper certainty.

Then she laid the stone in her palm and spoke Antonidas's name.

The reply took too long. When it came, the light was weak and blue and crowded with watery distortion.

"Jaina?"

Broken fragments of Dalaran came through behind his voice. Ward hum. A door. Someone speaking too far away to make out.

"I'm alive," she said at once.

"Where are you?"

"Inside the Barov line," she said. "The route evidence held. There is real work here. The scale is worse than Andorhal."

"Can you leave now?"

"With material, yes."

"Then leave with material."

The stone crackled. Distance did part of it. Wards did more.

"The archive uses our notation," she said. "Whoever built this place built it out of Kirin Tor method."

"I know what that implies," he said.

"The outpost model was right," Jaina said. "The preparation teaches living tissue to hold the pattern. The grain is only the outer carrier. I still do not have the waking step."

"Then you do not have enough to stay."

She looked at her notes, at the penciled word in the margin. "I have enough to know the answer is lower."

"Jaina."

The stone dimmed and flared again.

"Record everything you can carry," Antonidas said. "Do not trust memory to be kinder tomorrow than it is tonight. If the place is what you think it is, its wards have already told the master you are there."

"I am documenting everything," she said. "I'll leave safely."

"Send what you have the moment the line clears," he said. "And if it does not clear, keep breathing until it does."

"I know."

The blue light shook once.

"I should have moved faster," Antonidas said.

Then the connection tore.

The room went back to warm walls, dark water, and hidden wards in the plaster. Jaina kept the dead stone in her hand until it cooled.

She was still reading the margin when the knock came: light and precise.

"Come in," Jaina said.

Jandice entered carrying a second lamp and no mask.

"You stayed with the notes," she said. Her voice sharpened on notes.

"You left me the notes."

"A calculated extravagance." She set the lamp down and crossed to the desk. Her gaze found the penciled word in Jaina's margin at once. Her eyes lifted, bright as struck metal. "Did you write that?"

"Twice."

"Good."

"Then stop pretending you're only here to accuse me," Jandice said quietly.

"I'm not pretending anything. The design is elegant. The preparation survives storage and baking. After that the body keeps it alive. What I still can't see is what calls it back after death."

"So either something outside it keeps calling it back, crude and loud, or your method teaches the body to answer on its own."

Jandice's hands came up before she caught them. "Exactly."

"You're treating death as the answering point," Jaina said. "As the place where it answers."

"Closer."

"Force from outside is wasteful," Jandice said. "Everybody in Dalaran still thinks in that old brutal direction. Shove enough power in, the corpse jerks, and men call that mastery. It's childish."

"A struck string answers the note it knows. A room answers a sung pitch if the walls are built for it. Why should flesh be less teachable than wood or plaster if you have months instead of minutes?"

Resonance. There it was.

"Resonance," Jaina said.

"Yes." Jandice's mouth pulled into a quick fierce smile. "Yes, exactly."

"And the body keeps enough of the taught line that death strips resistance instead of the lesson."

"You see it."

"I see the shape," she said. "I don't yet see the whole answer."

"Neither did Dalaran," Jandice said. "They saw the gate and barred the field."

"Or Dalaran saw what the field did to the people inside it."

Jandice set her mouth hard.

"Moral disgust is a poor substitute for looking closely."

"So is elegance."

For a heartbeat, neither of them moved. Then Jandice laughed under her breath.

"Good," she said. "I'd wondered whether you were actually capable of being interesting."

"The full model is lower," Jaina said.

"Yes."

"Lower than your access."

"Partly below it."

"Partly won't do."

"It's honest."

Jandice straightened, folded her hands into her sleeves, then unfolded them again.

"The master knows you're here," she said.

"I assumed that when the outer ward flagged me."

"He was always going to know. The question was whether he would permit you farther than the route I was allowed to show."

"And?"

Jandice held her gaze long enough for the polish to slip.

"I asked," she said. "Sending you away now would be a waste."

"He agreed."

Her stomach tightened instead.

"Why?"

"Because he is curious," Jandice said. "And curiosity does not always oppose confidence."

"Then let's not keep his curiosity waiting," Jaina said."

The way down opened behind paneling at the end of a service hall Jaina had not seen earlier. Jandice touched a brass key to the seam and spoke a syllable. The ward in the frame folded aside.

Cold dry air moved up from below carrying stone dust, preserving salts, and the faint sweetness of long-kept decay.

The stair was older than the upper keep. Its walls narrowed, and the treads had been worn smooth by centuries of feet. Lamps had been fixed at intervals down the curve, and between their pools of light pale fungi clung to the mortar in thin crescent shelves. When Jaina stepped onto the first turning, the nearest growth gave off a dim blue-green bloom in answer to the magic on her skin.

Jandice glanced back at it. "They like practitioners."

Below them the ward pressure thickened until it pressed like weather against the face. Jaina kept one hand on the ledger until her fingers cramped around the leather spine. She had carried it out of the guest room and down the hidden stair on instinct, refusing to lose the only proof in hand.

She did not set it down.

The first landing opened on a short corridor lined with old burial niches, most sealed, some repurposed behind new iron grilles for storage. The second held a pair of shut doors labeled in the same clean hand she had seen in the archive, only more abbreviated here. Somewhere deeper, metal rang softly against metal.

"You understand," Jandice said without turning, "that permission is not the same as protection."

"I had not mistaken them."

They reached another turn. The stair bent out of sight again, down toward the pressure at the heart of the place. She tightened her grip on the ledger and followed Jandice lower.
