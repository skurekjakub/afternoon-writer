They stabled the horses at a smallholding two roads east of the gate, where the farmer's wife took the reins without questions and led the animals into a low stone barn that smelled of old hay and lamp oil. Lyriel handled the coin. The woman's hands stayed steady. Indifferent. She'd handled Thalassian stock before and knew not to fuss. Good enough.

The walk to the gate took ten minutes along a packed-earth track that met the main road where wagon ruts had chewed the frost into brown slush. Cyndia moved thirty paces ahead, close enough to read and far enough to seem unattached. Lyriel hung back the same distance. Old habit. Farstrider spacing for an urban approach. Neither of them needed telling.

Jaina walked beside her. Her shoulders had changed over the last mile, drawing tighter with every block, jaw clenched hard enough to show the muscle. Sylvanas read the effort in her stride. Shorter steps now. She said nothing. If Jaina needed to stop, she would say so. If she could not, Sylvanas would see it before it became a problem.

The gate opened onto a loading yard that stank of flour dust and animal sweat. Wagons backed up three deep, drivers standing beside them or sitting on the boards with the patience of men who had waited before and expected to wait again. Two guards flanked the arch, one leaning on a pike that had seen more ceremony than combat, the other marking something on a board. Neither looked dangerous. Both looked tired.

The important post sat just inside the gate: a clerk's table with manifests weighted by a river stone and an ink bottle that had stained a permanent ring into the wood. The clerk was a thin man with bruised eyes and sleeves rolled clear of the wrist, checking every driver's seal against a list. New procedure, or new to Sylvanas.

She counted as they approached. Two guards visible; one more in the arch's shadow on a stool with a crossbow across his knees. The arch itself was the choke point, ten feet wide and deep enough to trap anyone caught in it. The yard beyond opened wide. First cross street left toward warehouse roofs, right toward lower housing. Three exits she could see. More once they were inside.

"Papers," the clerk said without looking up.

Jaina stepped forward with the documents she had drawn up the night before: Dalaran inspection authority, route authorization, grain-quality survey credentials stamped with a seal that looked genuine because it was. The clerk took them, turned the top page, and held it to the light. His fingers left a faint ink mark on the corner.

"Dalaran," he said.

"Grain-quality survey," Jaina said. "We're expected at the inspection office."

The clerk finally looked at her, then past her at Sylvanas, then back to the papers. He ran the same line twice because the first pass had not stuck. Overworked, not suspicious. Sylvanas kept her hands visible and her posture plain. Hired escort. Nothing worth a second thought.

"The inspection office moved last month," the clerk said. "East side of the granary square. Ask for Alderman Rennic." He stamped both documents and reached for the next driver's manifest before Jaina had taken them back.

They walked through.

The arch smelled of damp stone and old grain, sweetness gone stale in the walls. Boot-packed mud gave way to cobbles, and the noise of a working city hit them before the light did.

Andorhal was working.

That changed everything the road had taught them. The villages had been dying or dead or halfway between. The depots had been empty or stripped or manned by people too frightened to speak plainly. Here the granary towers stood full against the sky, their caps dark with weather. Streets between them carried traffic: carts, porters, a woman with a flour sack on one hip arguing with a man holding a tally board. Bakers' smoke rose from a dozen chimneys farther in and thinned east on the wind. A dog trotted across the next intersection with the easy purpose of an animal that knew where it was going.

Sylvanas took in the district the way she took in new ground: exits first, then cover, then the flow of bodies. The main street ran straight from the gate toward a market square she could not yet see but could feel in the way traffic thickened ahead. Side lanes branched every forty paces, too narrow for two carts abreast. Stone ground floors, timber above, two storeys mostly, three at the corners where the rooflines rose. Good sightlines from upper windows. Bad from the street.

Grain dust lay in the cobble joints like pale sand. It powdered carts, porters' sleeves, the hem of a clerk's robe as he hurried past with an armful of scrolls. The city breathed it. Every surface carried the thing that kept the place alive and, if the road had taught them anything true, the thing that was killing it.

She marked the guard pattern as they walked. Two at the gate. One foot patrol on the main street moving south. Another pair at the mouth of a lane leading toward the granary towers, not guarding exactly, but set where they could see who went in and out. The militia here was not large, but it was awake. Someone had told them to watch the grain traffic. They were doing it without knowing what they were watching for.

Jaina had dropped half a step back. Strain came off her in waves. Jaina's hands hung loose at her sides, but her fingers kept flexing, the motion of someone holding a ward under pressure and needing somewhere for the tension to go.

"How bad?" Sylvanas asked, low enough for the street noise to bury it.

"Worse than any of the villages." Jaina flexed her fingers once. "Not worse than the last mile of the road."

"Can you hold it?"

"I'm holding it." A beat. "The whole district is saturated. Not a pocket. It's worked into the grain storage itself, floor to rafter. Whoever is doing this has been at it for months."

Cold turned in Sylvanas's stomach. Months. Every cart through those gates. Every sack on every porter's shoulder. Every bakehouse chimney trailing smoke while the city ground poison into bread. She let the cold sit for a breath, then locked it down and kept walking.

The market square opened ahead, wide and cobbled, ringed by taller, better-kept buildings than the streets behind it. A fountain stood dry at the center, its basin water-marked from older seasons. Vendor stalls lined the east side, half shuttered, canvas drooping where poles still stood. The inspection office sat where the clerk had said it would, stone-fronted on the east edge of the granary square with a brass plate beside the door that someone had polished recently.

Cyndia was already there near a water trough, resting her feet well enough to fool anyone who did not know her. She caught Sylvanas's eye and tipped her chin once toward the upper windows. No trouble. Lyriel arrived from the south lane two minutes later and drifted past without stopping.

"Inside," Sylvanas said.

The inspection office was one long room with a plank floor, a desk at the far end, and shelves along both walls stacked with ledgers, sample bags, and paperwork nobody had cleared in weeks. Grey afternoon light came through a rear window. The room smelled of ink, grain samples, and cheap lamp oil.

Two men waited.

The first stood by the window with his back to the light, broad-shouldered in militia leather ground dark with street dust, a truncheon on his belt polished by daily use. Scarred knuckles. Left hand near the truncheon without touching it. Captain Havel. The name had reached them two days before Andorhal through a chain Sylvanas trusted only because the links had been chosen by people she had already tested.

The second sat at the desk over a stack of ledgers, hunched so hard over the pages he seemed to have forgotten the room around him. A quill behind his ear had drifted sideways. Younger, mid-twenties perhaps, with ink-stained fingers and the bruised, sunken look of a man sleeping badly and no longer pretending otherwise. Half-hidden on his collar: a Kirin Tor pin. Corin. The junior researcher Jaina's contacts had pointed them toward.

Havel spoke first. "You're late."

"We're here," Sylvanas said.

His eyes went over her the way a street officer's would: hands, belt, stance, face. She let him find exactly what she meant him to find. Armed. Competent. Not inclined to explain herself twice.

"The inspection cover will hold for two days," Havel said. "Maybe three. After that the alderman's office starts asking why Dalaran sent people nobody requested."

"Then we have two days." She pulled a chair from the wall and sat without invitation. "What do you have?"

Havel glanced at Corin. The mage straightened and turned one of the ledgers outward.

"Three months," Corin said. "That's when the pattern changed." His voice was quiet and precise, the voice of a man who had explained this before to people who had not listened. "Before that the grain chain was normal. Predictable origins, consistent volume, the same carriers on the same routes. Then new suppliers appeared. Three, maybe four, feeding the central granary. Their documentation was clean. Too clean."

"How so?" Jaina asked. She had come to the desk and was already reading the ledger upside down, her eyes running the columns faster than Corin turned pages.

"Perfect seals, perfect weights, perfect scheduling. No delays, no shortages, no complaints." He flattened his palm on the page. "Every other supplier in the district shows variance. Late shipment, disputed weight, missing seal. These never miss." He turned a page and pointed to a column of figures. "I pulled their records against contamination reports from the downstream villages. Every tainted lot that traced back to Andorhal matched one of the new suppliers."

"You reported this," Sylvanas said.

Corin's jaw tightened. "Three times to Dalaran. Summary, evidence, lot-number cross-reference. The first got a receipt notice and nothing else. The second came back asking for cleaner samples and more controlled methodology." Bitterness crossed his face and stayed. "The third went by courier two weeks ago. No answer."

Havel shifted against the window frame. "While he wrote reports, I watched the district."

"Tell me."

"Workers," Havel said. "New ones. A dozen, maybe fifteen across the district. Loading docks, packaging sheds, overnight storage. On paper they're hires. The foremen signed them in. They don't move like laborers."

She touched the bowstring at her hip. "How do they move?"

"Still. Quiet. They don't drink with the others after shift. They don't argue over wages. They work in pairs, and when one moves the other is ready before it happens. I've seen soldiers in garrison who don't coordinate that well." He folded his arms. "And they watch the approaches. Not the way a dock hand watches for the next cart. The way a sentry watches for someone he doesn't want coming through."

Outside, a cart rolled past the window and someone shouted a price in the square.

"His reports and my streets say the same thing," Havel said. "What comes through the gate is clean. I've watched it unloaded. Good sacks, dry seals, grain smells right. What comes out the other end is not." He spread a hand flat on the desk. "The work happens here. Between intake dock and dispatch cart. Someone handles the grain after it arrives and before it leaves."

Sylvanas looked at Jaina.

Jaina had gone very still beside the desk. One hand lay flat across a column of lot numbers. Whatever she was seeing in those figures had caught her whole body and held it there.

"The grain is the carrier," Jaina said. One finger traced down the page. "Not before shipment, not at the farms, not on the road. The contamination enters inside Andorhal's chain, after intake and before dispatch. The village spread is too irregular for anything upstream."

"That's what the samples told me," Corin said. "I knew the source was local. I couldn't narrow the point."

"Which puts the hands on the grain somewhere inside the district," Sylvanas said.

"Some of them," Havel said. "The ones I can see. But they're not running themselves. Someone is directing traffic in that district, and I haven't found the room where the orders come from." He paused, choosing. "I've been mapping the movement for weeks. Every time I get close to the center, the schedule shifts. Whoever runs this knows how to read a patrol."

"You think they've made you."

"I think they know the militia watches the district and built their timing around it. Not me specifically. But I'm not walking into the granary quarter and kicking doors until something falls out." He straightened. "This needs time. We watch shift changes, learn which new men matter and which are just hires, follow the carts back to whatever shed they're loading from, then hit every door at once with enough bodies to hold every lane out."

The pull in it was obvious at once. Not caution. Control. Every recommendation came back to his ground, his routes, his timing.

"How long?" she asked.

"A week. Maybe less if the schedule holds."

"We don't have a week."

His jaw worked once. "Then you risk tipping them off. They shift the timing once and everything I've built disappears. You'll be chasing ghosts in a grain district that goes quiet the minute it smells trouble."

"Then we split the work," Sylvanas said.

His hand moved to the truncheon. Reflex, not threat. "Meaning?"

"Jaina confirms where the chain changes. Corin takes her through the intake records and every space your office can reach without noise. You walk me through the district, the shift timing, and the workers you've marked."

"To what end?"

"To find out whether your map survives contact with the ground."

The answer landed cleanly. His shoulders squared. Chin up. A man bracing against having his ground tested by someone who had arrived that afternoon.

"He's right that they'll shift if we're clumsy," Jaina said, stepping into the silence to redirect it, not soften it. "And you're right that we can't wait a week for a map that might never finish. If I can confirm where the contamination enters the chain, we stop arguing in the abstract."

Corin looked between them and pushed the drifting quill back behind his ear. "I can get her into the records room and onto the intake floor. The ledgers are already here. Nobody questions me carrying them."

Havel turned toward him. His face barely changed, but the change was there.

"I still want the lanes watched," Havel said. "No arrests. No overt move. Not until we know what we're standing on."

"Watch them," Sylvanas said. "Quietly."

Havel held her eyes for a long moment while the grey light thinned toward dusk.

Then she nodded once and turned to Jaina. "Two hours in the granary. Find me the point where the chain changes."

"Give me access and two hours with the intake records. If there's residue in the workspace, in the grain dust, on the tools, I'll find it."

"Corin goes with you."

Corin nodded and started stacking ledgers with the automatic speed of a man who had put these pages in order too many times to think about it. Some of the set went out of him. Not hope exactly. Direction.

Havel watched without comment, one hand still near the truncheon.

Sylvanas stood. Through the window, Andorhal kept working: carts in the lanes, smoke from bakehouses, a clerk crossing the square with his arms full of scrolls, grain dust turning in the late light.

"Two hours," she said. "Then we compare findings."

Jaina was already at the door. Corin gathered his ledgers and followed, eagerness in his step for the first time all day. Havel stayed by the window.

"Captain," Sylvanas said.

He looked at her.

"Walk me through the district. Show me the lanes, the shift timing, and the workers you've marked. I want to see it before dark."

He straightened, some of the resistance easing. He picked up a battered leather cap from the sill, set it on his head, and moved for the door.

"Stay close," he said. "The district knows my face. They don't know yours, and I'd rather keep it that way."

The granary district was louder than the main road and denser in every way that mattered: bodies, carts, blind corners, the heavy air pouring out of open loading doors. Havel walked half a pace ahead with the leather cap low, drawing the casual nod every labourer gave a militia face he saw every day. He moved through the district with a householder's certainty, turning corners before she could read the lane beyond, steering with his body instead of his words.

Somewhere east, through lanes she had not walked, Jaina was in the records with Corin's lot numbers spread in front of her and two hours on the clock. Sylvanas had sent her where the numbers led and taken the side that walked on feet. If it was the wrong call, she would know when the clock ran out.

Cyndia was already in the district. Sylvanas had caught the edge of her silhouette two rooftops back, a shadow that moved when the street below moved and went still when it went still. Lyriel had the south approaches. Neither needed instructions. The old Farstrider spacing held: scouts high and wide, commander on the ground, flanks covered by people who understood the work without being told.

Havel turned into a lane between warehouse walls, stone below, timber above, loading doors every thirty paces at ground level. Most were padlocked. Heavy iron. Hasps newer than the wood they bit into. Someone had spent money on security in a district where old locks had once been rope and habit.

"Third tower." He pointed with two flat fingers, the gesture of a man used to directing patrols without drawing eyes. "Changed suppliers three months back. New intake documentation. Clean weights. Clean seals."

"Same window Corin found."

"Same window everyone found who bothered to look." The bitterness in his voice was old and compressed. "Difference is, I watched what happened on the ground after the paperwork changed."

"Show me."

She caught the loading yard by smell before sight. Two carts stood empty, their beds stained dark with grain dust and a faint oily residue that caught the late light. Two workers sat on the tailgate of the nearer cart eating bread from a shared cloth. One laughed at something low and unhurried. Ordinary. Entirely ordinary.

Neither looked at Havel.

People always looked at the militia captain. Reflex. Truncheon, posture, authority. These two sat half-turned away, not noticing him because either he no longer mattered or they had been told not to notice him.

Sylvanas read the yard without turning her head. Loading bay left, empty. Narrow passage between granary walls right, shadowed deep enough to lose the back end entirely. One man at the mouth of that passage with folded arms and a flour sack at his feet that he was not loading. He was not watching the yard. He was watching the lane behind her.

A sentry watched approaches. A labourer watched his hands.

"How many?" she asked.

"Eight I'm certain of. Probably more. They rotate, but the positions hold. Same angles every shift."

Eight trained watchers buried in a grain operation. In a military camp that could cover a garrison of forty. In a covert cell it could guard ten or fifteen. She kept walking.

The cart workers returned Havel's nod with the comfortable indifference of men used to seeing him. The man by the passage did not nod. He shifted his weight a fraction, opening his stance toward the lane and closing it toward the passage behind him. Covering his exit.

The next three blocks told the same story at different volumes.

Workers who went quiet when someone passed too close to the wrong door. Loading lanes where sightlines had been broken by casually placed carts nobody was filling. Two women carrying sealed crates down a side lane at a pace that said urgency and a posture that said routine, neither quite matching the other. Havel named what he could: shift times, routes, which face belonged to which granary. The intelligence was good. Detailed. Honest as far as it went.

But every lane he chose curved back toward ground he already knew. Every vantage used a rooftop or window belonging to someone he had already spoken with. He was showing her the district through his map, and she could feel the edges of it.

A grey cat sat on a windowsill above a loading bay, washing its face with the solemn devotion of an animal never once disturbed there. Behind it, shutters were closed from inside. A building that should have been open for ventilation in working hours, grain storage needing airflow the way a forge needed draft. New hinges gleamed against weathered wood. The glass beyond them was dark.

"That one," she said.

"Storage annex. Supposed to be overflow." Havel spoke carefully now. "I've never seen anyone go in or out during a working shift."

She memorised the face of the building: door, upper loading hatch, roof gap to the adjacent wall, three fast routes to open street. Then she kept walking without breaking stride.

Afternoon thickened toward amber. Grain dust drifted through the light in slow turns. She touched the bowstring without thinking. Then the handler stepped out.

He stepped out of a plain door at the end of the block. Plain wood in plain stone, the sort of entrance every city grew by the dozen and forgot at once. Medium height, medium build, labourer's wool coat, sleeves rolled to the forearm. His hands were clean.

That was the first wrong thing. Every loading worker in the district carried grain dust in the knuckles, the nail beds, the dry creases of the hand. This man's hands were clean the way a man gets clean when he washes with purpose.

The second was the walk. He moved with purpose and looked at nothing. No casual street-reading. No glance for traffic. The route lived in his body. He never turned his head toward the two people standing forty paces behind him on the same lane.

Sylvanas steadied. Not calm. Readiness. The same evening-out that came when a target broke cover at range and the world narrowed to the distance between hand and string.

"Who—" Havel started.

"Stay here."

"That is not how—"

"Stay."

She crossed to the far side of the lane, folding herself into the district's rhythm: purposeful, uninteresting, a woman with somewhere to be. The handler pulled ahead and turned right at the end of the block.

Sylvanas hit the nearest wall.

Rough stone, old mortar, timber beam above. Enough. She pulled herself onto the roof in three breaths, grit and chimney soot grinding into her palms. The roofline ran thirty paces unbroken: flat clay tiles, low parapet, washing lines between chimneys with shirts and bedding nobody had taken down before dark. She moved low along the parapet and kept the wool coat in sight below.

He did not look up. People in cities never did. It was the one gift urban ground gave a ranger.

Second street. He crossed at an angle between a cart and a stack of barrels that screened him from the main approach without advertising that he wanted screening. Discipline. Someone had trained him to move through crowds without drawing attention, and the training sat in him deep.

Third street. Narrower, darker, buildings leaning close overhead. She had to jump the roof gap. The tiles shifted under her landing; she froze until they settled. Below, the handler slowed for the first time, checked left and right, and stepped toward the wall.

The dead drop took one motion. Hand into coat. Something folded out. Into a crack in the mortar at waist height. No pause. No backward glance. Hand back down. Pace unchanged.

She marked it: third building on the left, twelve courses above street level, between a shuttered window and a drainpipe. Then she went still and watched.

Sixty heartbeats. Ninety.

A heavy-set woman came up the lane from the opposite direction, apron still on, all the shape of a kitchen worker between shifts. She stopped at the wall and pressed one hand flat to the stone in the posture of someone catching her breath. The other hand took the drop. From the street it was nothing but a tired woman leaning on a wall. From the roof the fingers closed and tucked the folded paper into the apron pocket in a single practiced movement.

The woman turned into a side lane and moved with the pace of someone who knew exactly how long this errand was meant to take.

Sylvanas followed.

Two more rooftops, a gutter crossing that grabbed at her boot, then a chimney she went around because the soot cap was crumbling and one bad tile would sing to the whole lane below. The woman never deviated.

She went straight to a building at the far edge of the granary district, low and one-storey, loading dock on one side and a single door on the other. From the street it should have had windows. It did not. They had been bricked over from the inside; from above Sylvanas could see the new mortar, paler than the wall around it. The loading dock was chained shut. Only the door worked. The woman knocked twice, paused, knocked once, and was let in.

Sylvanas flattened herself on the roof opposite and counted.

Three minutes. The door opened. A different figure stepped out, shorter, cloth mask tied over nose and mouth, gloves past the elbows. He scanned the lane with quick nervous cuts, the look of someone who knew what was inside and did not want to be seen near it. He crossed to the dock, unchained it, hauled up a sack, and carried it back inside with both arms wrapped around it.

Through the gap where the dock doors did not quite meet, the view gave her enough.

Dirty lamplight on stone. Grain sacks in rows, some sealed, some open. Two masked figures moved between them with a rhythm that told its own story, pressing and kneading something thick into the loose grain with the patience of hands that had done it a hundred times. They handled the sacks the way soldiers handled plague dead: carefully, steadily, with gloves and cloth masks and no bare skin touching what they were touching.

Her stomach turned. Not nausea. Recognition. This was the weapon stripped of distance. Not plague on a countryside road. Not a girl coughing blood in a roadside hut. Gloved hands in lamplight working poison into grain that would be loaded before dawn and hauled to bakeries, market stalls, tables where people would eat without knowing what sat in them.

She counted four figures she could confirm: two at the sacks, one at the door, the woman from the dead drop somewhere deeper past the edge of the light. The building could hold more.

She stayed on the roof ten minutes more. Fixed the exits: one door, one loading dock, no other ground-level opening. Roof access from the north building where the gap could be jumped hard. Three approach lanes and the traffic rhythm on each. Light shift through the dock gap. How often the masked figures came near the door.

Then she withdrew, backing along the roofline, dropping down a drainpipe that groaned but held, landing two blocks north of where she had started. Her hands were black with soot and grit. She wiped them on her coat and walked back toward the market square at the pace of a woman with nowhere in particular to be.

Cyndia found her at the intersection, coming out of a doorway with the silent ease that meant she had been in place before Sylvanas arrived. She fell in beside her without a word.

"Secondary granary, far edge of the district," Sylvanas said, low enough for the street noise to swallow it. "Bricked windows. Chained dock. At least four inside working the grain by hand. Dead-drop chain feeding instructions from the handler network to the site."

Cyndia's eyes narrowed. That was all. She tipped her chin toward the south lane. Lyriel stood there by a water trough with the patient look of a woman who had watched approaches for the better part of an hour and found nothing that required movement.

The three of them walked back toward the inspection office. Light had dropped below the granary towers, and the first lamplighters were at work on the main street, long poles lifting fire into the gathering dark. Traffic had thinned enough that Sylvanas could hear their own boots on the cobbles.

The inspection office was half-dark when they reached it. One lamp burned low on the table. Shutters were drawn. Jaina already stood over a spread of paper that covered the desk edge to edge. Corin sat at her right with intake records open to pages flagged by strips of torn linen, his face pale and stiff over the ledgers.

Sylvanas shut the door behind her and felt the day settle into her shoulders: rooftops, gutter crossings, soot in her palms, a faint ache in her knees from the drainpipe drop. The room smelled of tallow, old paper, and the sour trace of grain dust that clung to everything in this city.

Havel arrived a full minute later with the easy pace of a man reestablishing himself in his own room. He took his place by the window, set the battered cap back on the sill, and kept one hand near his belt.

Cyndia took the chair nearest the door. Lyriel stayed against the far wall covering the angle Cyndia could not see. Neither of them discussed it. The arrangement did not need discussion.

Sylvanas sat, wiped soot from her palms onto her coat, and met Jaina's eyes across the table.

"The granary district," she said. "Start."

Jaina did not look at her notes. She had already built the picture and was delivering it clean.

"The grain is being treated during packaging. Not in the field, not in transit. Here, inside the granary complex, by people trained enough to work necromantic poison into loose grain without killing themselves."

She tapped a ward signature in the margin of her notes, lines hand-drawn and too angular for Dalaran schoolwork, asymmetry there by design rather than damage.

"The poison is layered." Her finger moved from diagram to intake columns, tapping contaminated lots as she spoke. "Outer shell mimicking natural grain oil. It survives milling, survives baking, survives anything a kitchen does to flour. The design accounts for every step between sack and table." She paused only long enough for the answer to turn uglier. "Under that, a necromantic signature I have never seen outside theoretical texts. This is not crude. This is not hedge-work. Someone with high-arcanum training built this and hid it inside grain. Inside flour. Inside bread people eat every morning without thinking."

The room went still.

"The poison alone is not the whole weapon," Jaina said. Her voice sharpened. "The signature activates after repeated ingestion. It needs the living body to finish the work. Warmth, blood, the slow labour of a living gut breaking down what it has been fed. One meal will not do it. A week of bread from a contaminated sack will."

Corin leaned forward, knuckles white on the table edge. "I found the lot numbers. Fourteen contaminated shipments in the last three months, all marked clean, all processed through the same intake window. Perfect documentation. If you only read the paper, you would never know."

Fourteen hit below Sylvanas's ribs. Her fingers found the bowstring before she told them to. Fourteen shipments through one intake window in one city, and the country beyond was already sick.

"I walked the district," she said, voice flat and steady because flat and steady was useful. "Dead-drop chain running from a handler network through at least two relays to a treatment site at the far edge of the granary complex. Bricked windows. Chained loading dock. One working door. Four handlers inside working the grain by hand, gloves and cloth masks, no skin touching the product. Eight trained sentries embedded in labourer positions across the district, covering approaches with rotation discipline better than most city militias."

She put her route marks down beside Jaina's ward diagrams. Jaina reached across at once, tracing a line from contamination marker to sentry position at the third tower, drawing from the opposite direction the same route Sylvanas had walked over the roofs. Apart, each half had been warning. Together, they were a map.

Outside the shutters, the city was still finishing its day: last carts rattling home, a lamplighter calling to someone two lanes over. Inside, nobody moved.

"It is designed," Jaina said. Finished certainty, no room for debate. "This is not contamination. It is not an accident. Someone with high-arcanum training built a weapon that uses the grain trade as the road in and the human body as the furnace, and built it well enough to hide it inside ordinary commerce."

Havel broke the quiet. "How many more cities?"

Nobody answered. Not because the question was wrong. Because nobody here had the right to guess.

Corin moved first. He pulled a folded sheet from inside his coat and spread it beside Jaina's notes: access schedules, door codes, the internal layout of the secondary granary drawn from memory in a hand steady enough to trust. Chalk marks along the routes noted ventilation gaps and load-bearing walls.

"The treatment shift runs between the fourth and sixth bell," he said. "Two handlers inside, one at the door, at least one relay runner outside. They feel safe. They've been doing this for months and nothing has touched them." His finger traced the north entry. "The service door has not been barred from inside since they bricked the windows. They need the ventilation channel. It runs through the door frame."

"You," Havel said, careful weight in every word, "are proposing to walk into a building where people handle a plague weapon."

"I am proposing to walk the people who can stop it through the door that opens."

"Through a door you found. On information you gathered. While posted to an office that has no authority over—"

"The authority question," Jaina said quietly, "stopped mattering when fourteen shipments passed through your inspection process unchecked."

Havel's jaw tightened. He pulled a creased district map from his coat and spread it over Corin's notes, weighting the corners with a knife and a ring of heavy keys.

"If you move on the site, you move through here." His finger traced a lane from the militia post to the granary's southern approach: open sightlines, better lit than the rest of the district. "Dawn patrol. My people. Inspection authority already in place. We go in with eight men, control the exits, do it in daylight where my watch can see what they're walking into."

"Dawn gives them eight hours to finish and scatter," Sylvanas said.

"Dawn gives us control."

His hand stayed on the map. Same lane. Same route. Same center as before. His routes, his timing, his people. Maybe caution. Maybe habit. Maybe something else.

When she caught his eyes across the lamplight, they were not on the door. They were on who was about to go through it.

She stored that and kept her voice level. A suspicion without proof was rope without anchor.

"Corin. When the north service door is unbarred, how many breaths from the opening to the first handler position?"

He did not hesitate. "Six, if they're working at the far table. Four, if they've moved the station closer to the ventilation channel since last week."

Breaths. Not paces. Not seconds. Breaths. The measure of a man who meant to go through the door himself.

"You are with us," she said.

Nobody argued. Not Jaina. Not Cyndia. Not Lyriel. Havel drew breath and kept whatever followed behind his teeth. His hand stayed on his map.

"Tonight," Sylvanas said.

The word went into the room point first.

Havel straightened. "Tonight is not—"

"The shift runs between the fourth and sixth bell. They will be inside, masked, hands occupied. That is the window where they cannot run and cannot fight without abandoning the weapon they are building." She looked at Jaina. "Can you hold one?"

"If we take one alive, I can tell you what he knows before he decides whether to share it." No boast. Only certainty. She pushed loose hair from her face, and her hand shook once before it steadied.

"Corin. The north service door."

"I can have it open by the third bell. Between the second and third the relay runners change over. There's a gap in coverage."

Sylvanas turned to her own.

"Cyndia. South approach, rooftop position. You saw the loading dock. Nobody leaves through it."

Cyndia inclined her head.

"Lyriel. North lane, ground level. Cover the service door after Corin opens it. If anyone comes out, they come into you."

Lyriel's hand rested on the short blade at her hip in a brief check. Enough.

"Jaina and I go through the door."

She said it plainly. The mage and the ranger, together, into the place where the poison was made.

Havel leaned forward, candlelight throwing the keys long across Corin's chalk marks. "I can have four men at the southern approach by the second bell. We contain the district, we close the—"

"No."

"This is my city. My jurisdiction. My—"

"Your routes." She met his eyes without raising her voice. "Every lane you've drawn tonight runs through your ground. Every approach uses your people. Every timing suggestion gives the cell more hours to finish and disperse." She let that sit in the same air as the grain dust and lamp smoke. "Captain. I am not accusing you of anything. But I am not running this operation through your lanes and your doors."

The muscle in his jaw worked once. He held the map flat under both hands. The fold lines looked old enough to remember the shape of his thinking.

"If it goes wrong in there—"

"Then it goes wrong with people who chose to be inside."

She stood. The chair scraped across the planks. Outside, evening kept moving: wheels on cobblestone, a dog barking twice and stopping, lamplighters working up the street with the patience of a shift that did not know tonight was different.

"Third bell," she said. "North service door. Positions as given."

Jaina gathered her notes, folding ward diagrams into a leather case with the fast economy of someone used to carrying one emergency into the next.

"Third bell," Jaina said.

Corin was already on his feet, tucking chalk-marked access routes into his coat.

Havel stayed by the window. His map lay open on the table, still unfinished, one corner slipped over Jaina's discarded notes so that his lane covered part of the weapon signature he had failed to find.

Sylvanas did not look back at him. She stepped into the corridor and pulled the door shut behind her.
