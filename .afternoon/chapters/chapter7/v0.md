They stabled the horses at a smallholding two roads east of the gate, where the farmer's wife took the reins without asking questions and the animals went into a low stone barn that smelled of old hay and lamp oil. Lyriel handled the coin. Sylvanas watched the woman's hands—steady, indifferent, the grip of someone who had handled Thalassian stock before and knew not to fuss. Good enough.

The walk to the gate took ten minutes on a packed-earth track that joined the main road where wagon ruts had chewed the frost into brown slush. Cyndia moved out ahead by thirty paces, close enough to read but far enough to seem unattached. Lyriel hung back the same distance. Old habit, Farstrider spacing for urban approach, and neither of them needed telling.

Jaina walked beside her. Her shoulders had changed since the last mile of the ride—drawn tighter, jaw set harder—and Sylvanas could see the effort she was spending to hold whatever she held against the city's pressure. She said nothing about it. If the woman needed to stop, she would say so, and if she could not say so, Sylvanas would see it in her stride before it became a problem.

The gate opened onto a loading yard that stank of flour dust and animal sweat. Wagons backed up three deep on the road, their drivers standing beside them or sitting on the boards with the particular patience of men who had waited before and expected to wait again. Two guards flanked the arch, one leaning on a pike that had seen more ceremony than combat, the other marking something on a board. Neither looked dangerous. Both looked tired.

What mattered was the third post—a clerk's table set just inside the gate, with a stack of manifests weighted by a river stone and an ink bottle that had stained the wood around it in a permanent dark ring. The clerk was a thin man with bruised eyes and sleeves cuffed above the wrist to keep the ink from spreading further. He was checking every driver's seal against a list. New procedure, or Sylvanas had never seen a grain hub this size run its own gate desk.

She counted while they approached. Two guards visible; one more in the shadow of the arch, sitting on a stool with a crossbow across his knees. Choke point was the arch itself—ten feet wide, stone-walled, deep enough to trap anyone caught in it. The yard beyond opened wide, and the first cross street ran left toward what looked like warehouse roofs and right toward lower housing. Three exits from the yard that she could see. More once they were inside.

"Papers," the clerk said without looking up.

Jaina stepped forward and produced the documents she had drawn up the night before—Dalaran inspection authority, route authorization, grain-quality survey credentials stamped with a seal that looked genuine because it was. The clerk took them, turned the top page, and held it up to the light. His fingers left a faint ink mark on the corner.

"Dalaran," he said.

"Grain-quality survey," Jaina said. "We're expected at the inspection office."

The clerk looked at her, then past her at Sylvanas, then at the two documents again. His eyes moved like a man reading the same line twice because the first pass had not stuck. Overworked, not suspicious. Sylvanas kept her hands visible and her posture unremarkable—a hired escort, nothing worth a second thought.

"The inspection office moved last month," the clerk said. "East side of the granary square. Ask for Alderman Rennic." He stamped both documents, set them on the pile, and was already reaching for the next driver's manifest before Jaina had taken the papers back.

They walked through.

The arch smelled of damp stone and old grain—a sweetness that had gone stale and settled into the walls. Boot-packed mud gave way to cobblestones on the far side, and the city opened around them like a held breath releasing.

Andorhal was working.

That was the first thing, and it changed every expectation built on the road. The villages had been dying or dead or halfway between. The depots had been empty or stripped or manned by people too frightened to speak plainly. Here the granary towers stood full against the sky, their caps dark with weather, and the streets between them carried traffic—carts, porters, a woman with a flour sack balanced on one hip arguing with a man who held a tally board. Bakers' smoke rose from a dozen chimneys in the middle distance. A dog trotted across the intersection ahead of them with the unhurried purpose of an animal that knew where it was going.

Sylvanas took in the district the way she would take in new ground: exits first, then cover, then the flow of bodies. The main street ran straight from the gate toward a market square she could not yet see but could feel in the way traffic thickened ahead. Side lanes branched every forty paces, narrow enough that two carts could not pass abreast. The buildings were stone on the ground floor, timber-frame above, two stories mostly, three at the corners. Good sightlines from the upper windows. Bad sightlines from the street.

Grain dust lay in the cobble joints like pale sand. It was on the carts, on the porters' sleeves, on the hem of a clerk's robe as he hurried past with an armful of scrolls. The city breathed it. Every surface carried the residue of the thing that kept the place alive and was also, if everything they had learned on the road was true, the thing that was killing it.

She marked the guard pattern as they walked. Two at the gate. One foot patrol visible on the main street, moving south. Another pair standing at the mouth of a lane that led toward the granary towers—not guarding, exactly, but positioned where they could see who went in and out. The militia here was not large, but it was awake. Someone had told them to watch the grain traffic, and they were doing it the way men did who had been told what to watch but not what they were watching for.

Jaina walked a half step behind her now, head slightly turned, and Sylvanas could feel the effort radiating off her like heat from a banked fire. The woman's hands were loose at her sides, but her fingers moved—small flexions, the kind that came from holding a ward under strain and having nothing else to do with the tension.

"How bad?" Sylvanas asked, low enough that the street noise covered it.

"Worse than any of the villages. Not worse than the last mile of the road."

"Can you hold it?"

"I'm holding it." A pause. "The whole district is saturated. It's not a pocket—it's layered into the grain storage itself, floor to rafter. Whoever is doing this has been at it for months."

Sylvanas filed that beside what she already knew and kept walking.

The market square opened ahead of them like a clearing in a forest of rooflines—wide, cobbled, ringed by buildings that were taller and better-kept than the streets they had just come through. A fountain stood dry in the center, its basin stained with water marks from seasons past. Vendor stalls lined the east side, half of them shuttered. The inspection office sat where the clerk had said it would—a stone-fronted building on the east edge of the granary square, with a brass plate beside the door that someone had recently polished.

Cyndia was already there, standing near a water trough with the look of a woman resting her feet. She caught Sylvanas's eye and tipped her chin once toward the building's upper windows. No signal of trouble. Lyriel arrived from the south lane two minutes later and drifted past without stopping.

"Inside," Sylvanas said.

The inspection office was one long room with a plank floor, a desk at the far end, and shelves along both walls stacked with ledgers, sample bags, and the accumulated dust of paperwork that nobody had cleared in weeks. A window at the back let in grey afternoon light. The room smelled of ink and grain samples and the faint sourness of a lamp that had been burning cheap oil.

Two men waited for them.

The first stood by the window with his back to the light—a deliberate choice, Sylvanas noted, the kind a man made when he wanted to see faces before his own was read. He was stocky, broad through the shoulders, wearing militia leather that had street dust ground into its creases and a truncheon on his belt that showed the dark polish of daily use. His hands were scarred across the knuckles, and he kept one resting near the truncheon without touching it. Captain Havel. She had the name from the message that had reached them two days before Andorhal, passed through a chain she trusted only because each link in it had been chosen by people she had already tested.

The second sat at the desk with a stack of ledgers open in front of him and a quill behind his ear that he had apparently forgotten was there. Younger—mid-twenties, maybe—with ink-stained fingers and the bruised, sunken look of a man who had been sleeping badly for weeks and had stopped pretending otherwise. A Kirin Tor pin on his collar, half hidden by the turned-up edge of his coat. Corin. The mage Jaina's contacts had pointed them toward: a junior researcher assigned to Andorhal's grain records who had started finding things in the numbers that his superiors had no interest in hearing about.

Havel spoke first. "You're late."

"We're here," Sylvanas said.

His eyes moved across her the way a street officer's did—hands, belt, stance, and then the face. She let him look. He would see what she intended him to see: someone armed, someone competent, someone who was not going to explain herself twice.

"The inspection cover will hold for two days," Havel said. "Maybe three. After that, the alderman's office starts asking why Dalaran sent people nobody requested."

"Then we have two days." She pulled a chair from the wall and sat without being offered one. The wood creaked under her weight. "What do you have?"

Havel glanced at Corin. The mage straightened in his chair and pulled one of the ledgers toward him, turning it so the pages faced outward.

"Three months," Corin said. "That's when the pattern changed." His voice was quiet, precise, and carried the particular exhaustion of someone who had explained this before to people who had not listened. "Before that, the grain supply chain was normal—predictable origins, consistent volumes, the same carriers running the same routes they'd run for years. Then new suppliers appeared. Not many. Three, maybe four, feeding into the central granary. Their documentation was clean. Too clean."

"How so?" Jaina asked. She had come to stand beside the desk, and Sylvanas could see her reading the ledger upside down, eyes already tracking the columns.

"Perfect seals, perfect weights, perfect scheduling. No delays, no shortages, no complaints. Every other supplier in the district has variance—a late shipment, a disputed weight, a missing seal. These new ones never miss." He turned a page and pointed to a column of figures. "I started pulling their records against the contamination reports from the downstream villages. Every time I found tainted grain in a village that could trace its supply back to Andorhal, the lot numbers matched shipments from the new suppliers."

"You reported this," Sylvanas said. Not a question.

Corin's jaw tightened. "I sent three reports to Dalaran. Summary, evidence, lot-number cross-reference. The first one got a receipt notice and nothing else. The second one was acknowledged with a request for better samples—cleaner documentation, they said. More controlled methodology." Something bitter moved across his face and settled there. "The third I sent by courier two weeks ago. No answer."

Havel shifted his weight against the window frame. "While he was writing reports, I was watching the granary district."

"Tell me what you saw."

"Workers," Havel said. "New ones. Not many—a dozen, maybe fifteen across the district. They came in over the same three months. They work the loading docks, the packaging sheds, the overnight storage. On paper they're hires. The foremen signed them in. But they don't move like laborers."

Sylvanas's fingers found the bowstring at her hip without thinking—an old habit, the touch that meant she was weighing something. "How do they move?"

"Still," Havel said. "Quiet. They don't drink with the others after shift. They don't argue over wages. They work in pairs, always, and when one of them moves the other knows it before it happens. I've watched soldiers in garrison who don't coordinate that well." He folded his arms across his chest. "And they watch the approaches. Not the way a dock hand watches for the next cart. The way a sentry watches for someone he doesn't want coming through."

The room was quiet for a moment. Outside, a cart rolled past the window and someone shouted a price in the market square.

"His reports and my streets say the same thing," Havel said, nodding toward Corin. "The contamination is coming from inside the packaging chain. The new suppliers bring clean grain in. What leaves the granary is not clean. The work happens here, in Andorhal, between intake and dispatch."

Sylvanas looked at Jaina. The woman had gone still beside the desk—not the stillness of fatigue or the stillness of concentration but the sharp, locked quiet that meant a pattern had just clicked into place. Her hand rested flat on the open ledger, fingers spread across a column of lot numbers, and her eyes had the focused distance of someone seeing structure underneath surface.

"The grain is the carrier," Jaina said, "but it's being treated during packaging. Not before shipment, not at the farms, not on the road. Here." She traced a line down the page with one finger. "These lot numbers—the variance in contamination levels downstream makes sense if the treatment isn't uniform. Whoever is doing this is working by hand, not by formula. Some batches get more than others depending on who handles them and how long."

"That's what the samples show," Corin said, leaning forward. "I thought it was inconsistency in the source. It's inconsistency in the application."

"Which means the people doing it are in the packaging sheds," Sylvanas said.

"Some of them," Havel said. "The ones I can see. But they're not running themselves. Someone is directing traffic in that district, and I haven't found the room where the orders come from." He paused, and something shifted in his expression—the careful, controlled look of a man choosing his next words with more precision than the conversation seemed to warrant. "I've been trying to map the movement pattern for weeks. Every time I get close to the center of it, the schedule shifts. Whoever runs this knows how to read a patrol."

"You think they've made you," Sylvanas said.

"I think they know the militia watches the district and they've built their timing around it. I don't think they know me specifically. But I'm not going to pretend I can walk into the granary quarter and kick doors until something falls out." He straightened from the window frame and looked at her directly. "What I'm telling you is that this needs time. A clean approach. We map the full shift pattern, identify which workers are part of it, track the supply chain backward until we find the staging point, and then we move with enough force to take the whole operation at once."

Sylvanas heard it—the pull. Not toward caution, exactly. Toward control. Havel wanted this done on his ground, on his schedule, through his routes. Every recommendation he made circled back to things he could see and methods he already used. It was not stupidity. It was the reflex of a competent man who had been working alone for too long and could not imagine the problem being solved by anyone who did not work the way he did.

She did not trust it fully, and she did not dismiss it.

"How long?" she asked.

"A week. Maybe less if the schedule holds."

"We don't have a week."

His jaw set. "Then you risk tipping them off. They shift the timing once and everything I've built disappears. You'll be chasing ghosts in a grain district that goes quiet the minute it smells trouble."

"Or we take someone tonight," Sylvanas said, "and ask them where the room is."

Havel's hand moved to the truncheon—not a threat, just the reflex of a man whose body argued before his mouth caught up. "That is exactly the kind of move that—"

"That what?"

"That burns an operation that's been building for months."

"Your operation hasn't produced an arrest, a name, or a location in those months."

The words landed harder than she had aimed them, and she saw it in the way his shoulders squared and his chin came up. She had not meant to cut him. She had meant to move him, and the cut was a side effect of the speed.

"He's right that they'll shift if we're clumsy," Jaina said, quiet and precise, stepping into the silence the way she did—not to soften, but to redirect. "And you're right that we can't wait a week for a map that might never finish."

Corin looked between them. The quill behind his ear had migrated forward, and he pushed it back absently. "I know which workers are on the overnight shift tonight. Two of them always take the rear lane behind the third granary tower after the midnight bell. They go alone. No one else uses that lane at that hour."

Havel turned toward him. Something in his face—not anger, exactly. The expression of a man watching his careful work get picked apart by people who had arrived that afternoon.

"If we take one of them cleanly," Sylvanas said, "we don't need the full map. We need a name and a location. One person who knows where the orders come from."

"And if they don't talk?"

"Then you still have your map, and we've lost one night."

Havel looked at her for a long moment. His hand came off the truncheon. Outside, the market noise had dimmed as the afternoon wore on, and the grey light from the window was thinning toward dusk.

"Tonight," he said. It was not agreement. It was concession, and both of them knew it.

Sylvanas nodded once and turned to Jaina. "The packaging process. Can you confirm it?"

"Give me access to the granary and two hours with the intake records. If the treatment is being applied during packaging, there will be residue in the workspace—on the tools, in the grain dust, on the surfaces where they handle the sacks. I can find it."

"Corin goes with you."

Corin nodded, already pulling ledgers together into a stack with the automatic precision of a man who had organized these records enough times that his hands knew the order without instruction. The relief on his face was not hope—it was the raw, exhausted recognition of someone who had finally been heard after shouting into silence for weeks.

Havel watched the preparations with the expression of a man whose house had just been rearranged by guests. He said nothing. His hand stayed near the truncheon but did not touch it.

Sylvanas stood. The chair scraped against the plank floor. Through the window, Andorhal continued its ordinary work—carts in the lanes, smoke from the bakehouses, a clerk crossing the square with his arms full of scrolls, grain dust drifting in the late light like pollen from a flower that had already gone to seed.

"Two hours," she said. "Then we meet back here and plan the lane."

Jaina was already at the door. Corin gathered his ledgers and followed, and the eagerness in his step was the first thing Sylvanas had seen all day that looked like something other than endurance.

Havel stayed by the window.

"Captain," Sylvanas said.

He looked at her.

"Walk me through the district. Show me the lanes, the shift timing, and the workers you've marked. I want to see it with my own eyes before dark."

He straightened, and something in his posture unlocked—not warmth, but the plain relief of being given a task that used what he had instead of dismissing it. He picked up a battered leather cap from the windowsill, set it on his head, and moved toward the door.

"Stay close," he said. "The district knows my face. They don't know yours, and I'd rather keep it that way."

She followed him out into the fading light, and the city closed around them like a jaw.

---

The granary district was louder than the main road and denser in every way that mattered—bodies, carts, blind corners, the heavy air of grain storage venting through open loading doors. Havel walked half a pace ahead with his leather cap pulled low, and the labourers they passed gave him the nod that meant they knew the militia face and did not think twice about it. He moved through the district the way a man moved through his own house, turning corners before she could read the lane beyond, steering with his body instead of his words. That was either useful or the problem. She had not decided.

Somewhere east of them, through lanes she had not walked, Jaina was in the mage-side records with Corin's lot numbers spread in front of her and two hours on the clock. Sylvanas did not need to watch that half. She had sent the woman where the numbers led and taken the side that walked on feet—the side where the answers lived in how people moved, not what they wrote down. If it was the wrong call, she would know when the clock ran out.

Cyndia was already in the district. Sylvanas had caught the edge of her silhouette two rooftops back—a shadow that moved when the street below moved and went still when it went still. Lyriel had taken the south approaches. Neither needed instructions. The old Farstrider spacing held like a body's own pulse: scouts high and wide, commander on the ground, flanks covered by people who did not need to be told what covering meant.

Havel turned into a lane that ran between two warehouse walls, stone on the lower courses, timber-framed above, loading doors set every thirty paces at ground level. Most were padlocked—heavy iron, the hasps newer than the wood they bit into. Someone had been spending money on security in a district where the old locks had been rope latches and honest habit.

"Third tower." He pointed with two fingers held flat like a knife, the gesture of a man who directed patrols and never used one finger because one finger drew eyes. "Changed suppliers three months back. New intake documentation, clean weights, clean seals."

"Same window Corin found in the ledgers."

"Same window everyone found who bothered to look." The bitterness in his voice was compressed and old, the kind that came from months of being right in rooms where nobody wanted to listen. "Difference is, I watched what happened on the ground after the paperwork changed."

"Show me."

The lane opened into a loading yard where two carts stood empty, their beds stained dark with grain dust and something else—a faintly oily residue that caught the late afternoon light. Two workers sat on the tailgate of the nearer cart eating bread from a shared cloth. One of them laughed at something low and unhurried. Ordinary. Entirely ordinary.

Neither of them looked at Havel.

People always looked at the militia captain. It was reflex—the truncheon, the posture, the authority that carried in a working district the way a hawk carried in open sky. These two men sat with their backs half-turned and did not register his presence, and that meant either they saw him so often he had stopped mattering or they had been told not to notice him.

Sylvanas scanned the yard without turning her head. Loading bay to the left, empty. A narrow passage between two granary walls to the right, deep enough in shadow that the back end vanished. One man standing at the passage mouth with his arms folded and a flour sack at his feet that he was not loading. He was not watching the yard. He was watching the lane behind her—the approach, not the work.

A sentry watched approaches. A labourer watched his hands.

"How many?" she said, quiet.

"Eight I'm certain of. Probably more. They rotate, but the positions stay—same angles, every shift."

Eight trained watchers embedded in a grain operation. In a military camp, eight sentries covered a garrison of forty. In a covert cell, the ratio ran the other way—eight might protect ten or fifteen. She let the number settle against what she already knew and kept walking.

They moved through the yard. Havel gave the cart workers a nod they returned with the comfortable disinterest of men who saw him every day and had stopped caring about the routine of it. The man at the passage mouth did not nod. He shifted his weight—a small adjustment, nothing that would catch anyone who was not watching for it—and the shift opened his stance toward the lane and closed it toward the passage behind him. Covering his exit.

The next three blocks told the same story at different volumes.

Grain workers who went quiet when someone passed too close to the wrong door. Loading lanes where the sightlines had been broken by casually positioned carts that nobody was filling. A pair of women carrying sealed crates down a side lane at a pace that said urgency and a posture that said routine, and neither pace nor posture quite agreed with the other. Havel named what he could—shift times, routes, which granary each face was attached to—and the intelligence was good, detailed, honest as far as it went.

But every lane he walked her down circled back to ground he already knew. Every vantage he suggested used a rooftop or a window belonging to someone he had already spoken with. He was showing her the district through his map, and his map had edges she could feel in the way his routes always curved back toward the centre of what he controlled. Whether those edges marked the limits of his effort or the limits of what he wanted her to see—she did not know yet.

She filed that beside the rest and kept walking.

A grey cat sat on a windowsill above a loading bay, unhurried, washing its face with the single-minded devotion of an animal that had never once been disturbed in that spot. Behind it, the shutters were closed from the inside. A building that should have been open for ventilation during working hours—grain storage needed airflow the way a forge needed draft—but the shutters carried new hinges, bright against the weathered wood, and the glass behind them was dark.

"That one," she said.

"Storage annex. Supposed to be overflow." Havel spoke carefully now, choosing his words with the precision of a man watching someone pick up his tools. "I've never seen anyone go in or come out during a working shift."

She memorised the building's face—its door, a loading hatch on the upper level, the roof gap to the adjacent wall—and three fast routes from it to the nearest open street. Then she kept walking as though she had seen nothing worth stopping for.

The afternoon light was thickening toward amber, and grain dust drifted through it in slow turns that caught the low sun like pollen from a flower already gone to seed. She touched the bowstring without thought—the old habit, the pressure that said something was about to shift—and then she saw the handler.

He came out of a door at the end of the block. Plain wood in plain stone, no signage, no number—the kind of entrance that existed on every street in every city and vanished from memory the instant you passed it. Medium height, medium build, dressed in a labourer's wool coat with the sleeves rolled to the forearm. His hands were clean.

That was the first wrong thing. Every loading worker in the district carried grain dust ground into their knuckles, their nail beds, the dry creases between their fingers. This man's hands were clean the way you got clean when you washed with purpose, not habit.

The second was his walk. He moved at the pace of a man going somewhere, but he looked at nothing—no glancing, no reading of the street, no casual awareness of the traffic around him. He knew the route so well his body ran it without reference to anything outside his own feet. And he never turned his head toward the two people standing forty paces behind him on the same lane.

Her pulse settled. Not calm—readiness. The same evening-out that came when a target broke cover at range and the world narrowed to the distance between her hand and the string.

"Who—" Havel started.

"Stay here."

"That is not how—"

"Stay."

She crossed to the far side of the lane and matched the district's rhythm—purposeful, uninteresting, a woman with somewhere to be and no cause to be watched. The handler was pulling ahead. He turned right at the end of the block.

Sylvanas hit the nearest wall.

The stone was rough, old mortar between courses giving her finger-holds, and the timber beam above held her weight. She pulled herself onto the roof in three breaths and came up with grit and chimney soot ground into her palms. The roofline ran unbroken for thirty paces—flat clay tiles, a low parapet, washing lines strung between chimney stacks with shirts and bedding nobody had collected before dark. She moved low along the parapet, keeping the wool coat in sight as the handler turned onto a wider street below.

He did not look up. People in cities never looked up. It was the one gift that urban ground gave a ranger, and she had learned not to waste it.

Second street. He crossed at an angle, slipping between a stationary cart and a column of stacked barrels that gave him cover from the main approach without looking like he wanted cover. Discipline. Someone had trained this man to move through populated ground without drawing attention, and the training ran deep enough that his body did it without thought—the way hers walked a roofline without checking the tiles.

Third street. Narrower, darker, the buildings leaning close overhead. The gap between rooflines was tight enough that she had to jump it. She landed on tiles that shifted under her weight and held still until they settled. Below, the handler slowed. He checked the lane—left, right, the first time she had seen him look—and stepped toward the wall.

The dead drop was fast. He reached into his coat, came out with something small and folded—paper, a pouch, she could not tell from above—and pressed it into a crack in the mortar at waist height. One smooth motion. No pause, no backward glance. He kept walking as though his hand had never moved.

She marked it. Third building on the left side of the lane, twelve courses above street level, between a shuttered window and a drainpipe. Then she went still on the tiles and watched.

Sixty heartbeats. Ninety.

A woman came up the lane from the opposite direction—heavy-set, apron still on, the look of a kitchen worker between shifts. She stopped at the wall and pressed one hand flat against the stone as if catching her breath, and with the other she palmed the drop. From the street it was a tired woman leaning on a wall. From the roof Sylvanas saw the fingers close around the folded paper and tuck it into the apron pocket in one gesture.

The woman turned into a side lane and moved at a pace that said she knew exactly how much time this errand was supposed to cost her.

Sylvanas followed.

Two more rooftops. A gutter crossing that grabbed at her boot. A chimney stack she went around rather than over because the soot cap was crumbling and one tile breaking at that height would announce her to everyone below. The woman walked with the unhurried timing of someone whose route had been measured and practised, and she did not deviate.

She went straight to a building at the far edge of the granary district—low, one storey, a loading dock on one side and a single door on the other. From the street it should have had windows. It did not. They had been bricked over from the inside, and Sylvanas could see the colour difference in the mortar from the roofline—newer, paler, laid within the year. The loading dock was chained shut. The only working entrance was the door, and the woman knocked twice, paused, knocked once, and was let in.

Sylvanas lay flat on the roof of the building opposite and counted.

Three minutes. The door opened and a different figure stepped out—shorter, wearing a cloth mask pulled tight over nose and mouth and gloves that went past the elbows. He scanned the lane without the handler's discipline, the quick nervous look of someone who knew what was inside the building and did not want to be seen near it. He crossed to the loading dock, unchained it, hauled up a sack, and carried it back inside with both arms wrapped around it.

Through the gap where the loading dock doors did not quite meet, she caught it.

Dirty lamplight falling on a stone floor. Grain sacks in rows—some sealed, some open, the open ones gaping like wounds. Two masked figures moved between them with a rhythm that told its own story: pressing, kneading, working something thick into the loose grain with the repetitive patience of hands that had done this a hundred times before. They handled the sacks the way surgeons handled the sick—carefully, steadily, with gloves and cloth masks and no bare skin touching the thing they touched.

Her stomach turned—cold, slow, involuntary. Not nausea. Recognition. This was the weapon stripped of distance. Not plague rolling through a countryside, not a girl coughing blood in a roadside village, not a town that stopped answering its mail. Gloved hands in lamplight pressing poison into grain that would be loaded before dawn and carted to bakeries and market stalls and tables where people would eat it and not know what was in them until it was too late for knowing to matter.

She counted four figures inside. Two at the sacks. One at the door. The woman from the dead drop somewhere deeper, past the edge of the lamplight. The building was large enough for more, and she had no way to see the rest of the floor from this angle.

She stayed on the roof for ten more minutes. Fixed the exits: one door, one loading dock, no other ground-level opening. Roof access from the building to the north, where the walls nearly touched and a hard jump could bridge the gap. Three lane approaches and the traffic rhythm on each. The shift of light through the loading dock gap. The intervals at which the masked figures moved to the door.

Then she withdrew. Backward along the roofline, down a drainpipe that groaned under her but held, and onto a lane two blocks north of where she had started. Her hands were black with soot and grit, the residue of rooftops and chimney stacks pressed into the creases of her palms. She wiped them on her coat and walked back toward the market square at the unhurried pace of a woman with nowhere in particular to be.

Cyndia found her at the intersection—materialised from a doorway with the silent ease that meant she had been in position before Sylvanas arrived. She fell into step without a word.

"Secondary granary, far edge of the district," Sylvanas said, low enough that the street noise swallowed the words a pace behind them. "Bricked windows. Chained dock. At least four inside working the grain by hand. Dead-drop chain running instructions from the handler network to the site."

Cyndia's eyes narrowed—the only acknowledgement she gave—and she tipped her chin toward the south lane. Lyriel was there, standing near a water trough with the patient, unhurried look of a woman who had been watching approaches for the better part of an hour and had found nothing that required her to move.

The three of them walked back toward the inspection office as the last of the light left the granary towers and the first lamplighters appeared on the main street, their long poles working the dusk like fishing lines cast into a darkening river.

---

The inspection office was half-dark when they reached it—one lamp burning low on the table, the shutters drawn, and Jaina already standing over a spread of paper that covered the working surface from edge to edge. Corin sat at her right with a stack of intake records open to pages flagged with strips of torn linen, and his face carried the look of a man who had just been handed confirmation of something he had suspected for months and wished he'd been wrong about.

Sylvanas pulled the door shut behind her and felt the day settle into her shoulders—the rooftop work, the gutter crossings, the soot ground into the creases of her palms. Her knees held a faint ache from the drop off the drainpipe that she ignored the way she ignored all such complaints, by refusing to acknowledge them until the work was finished. The room smelled of tallow and old paper and the faint sourness of grain dust that clung to everything in this part of the city.

Havel appeared in the doorway a full minute later, moving with the unhurried ease of a man reestablishing himself in his own space, and took his position by the window without comment. His battered leather cap went back on the windowsill. His hand stayed near his belt.

Cyndia took the chair nearest the door. Lyriel stayed standing against the far wall, covering the angle Cyndia could not see from her seat, and neither of them discussed the arrangement because the arrangement did not require discussion.

Sylvanas sat. Wiped her palms on her coat once—grit and chimney soot, the residue of other people's rooftops—and met Jaina's eyes across the table.

"The granary district," she said. "Start."

Jaina did not look at her notes. She had already built the picture and was delivering it the way she delivered everything when the situation deserved clarity—fast, exact, the courtesy stripped down to its studs.

"The grain is being treated during packaging. Not in the field, not in transit—here. Inside the granary complex, by people with enough training to apply a necromantic carrier layer to loose grain without contaminating themselves."

She tapped one of the diagrams in the margin of her notes—a ward signature she had drawn by hand, its lines too angular for the Dalaran school, carrying an asymmetry that looked deliberate rather than damaged. Her fingers traced the shape the way another woman might trace a scar: knowing it, not admiring it.

"The carrier is layered. An outer shell that mimics natural grain oil—it survives milling, survives baking, survives anything a kitchen does to flour. Beneath that, a necromantic signature I have never seen outside of theoretical texts." She paused, and the pause was not hesitation. It was the brief, involuntary stillness that came when an answer was worse than the question that had produced it. "It is not crude. It is not hedge-work. Whoever designed this understood high-arcanum preservation bindings and adapted them to biological transit."

The room held. Even Havel, who had been shifting his weight by the window, went quiet.

"The carrier alone is not the whole weapon." Jaina's voice sharpened—the way it always sharpened when she was cornering a problem that kept getting worse the closer she came to it. "The necromantic signature activates after repeated ingestion. It needs the living body. The warmth, the blood, the metabolic cycle—the host finishes the work that the grain begins. One meal will not do it. A week of bread from a contaminated sack will."

Corin leaned forward. His knuckles had gone white against the table edge. "I found the lot numbers. Fourteen contaminated shipments in the last three months—all marked clean, all processed through the same intake window. The documentation was perfect. Matched seals, matched weights, every number where it should have been. If you only read the paper, you would never know."

Fourteen. Sylvanas let the number settle into the count she already carried—the dead villages, the girl coughing blood in the roadside hut, the emptied settlement at Millhaven where the wind had been the only thing still moving through the streets. Fourteen shipments through one intake window in one city, and the country beyond was already sick.

"I walked the district," she said. Her voice came out flat and steady, and she let it. "Dead-drop chain running from a handler network through at least two relay points to a treatment site at the far edge of the granary complex. Bricked windows, chained loading dock, one working door. Four handlers inside working the grain by hand—gloves, cloth masks, no bare skin touching the product. They are protecting themselves from the thing they touch." She let that sit. "Eight trained sentries embedded in labourer positions across the district, covering approaches with rotation discipline I have not seen outside a military operation."

She laid her count against Jaina's across the same surface. Jaina's neat hand beside the route marks Sylvanas had scratched on the back of a militia inventory sheet. Ward signatures beside sentry positions. The design beside the delivery—two halves of a mechanism that neither of them could have seen alone.

The quiet that followed was not silence. She could hear the street through the shutters—the last carts rattling toward their yards, a lamplighter calling to a colleague two lanes over, the ordinary sounds of a city grinding through the end of its working day. But nobody in the room moved, and the lamplight held steady on the table as though the air itself had forgotten how to shift.

"It is designed," Jaina said. Not a question. Not an opening for debate. The flat, finished certainty of someone who had hoped to be wrong and was not. "This is not contamination. It is not a leak, and it is not an accident. Someone with high-arcanum training built a weapon that uses the grain trade as its delivery system and the human body as its finishing furnace, and they built it well enough to hide it inside a working city's ordinary commerce."

Havel broke the quiet. "How many more cities?"

Nobody answered him. Not because the question was wrong—it was the only question that mattered now—but because nobody in the room had earned the right to guess.

Corin moved first. He pulled a folded sheet from inside his coat and spread it flat on the table beside Jaina's notes—access schedules, door codes, the internal layout of the secondary granary drawn from memory in a hand steady enough to trust. Chalk marks along the door routes where he had annotated the ventilation gaps and load-bearing walls, the kind of detail that came from walking a building's corridors often enough to know which boards gave underfoot.

"The treatment shift runs between the fourth and sixth bell," he said. "Two handlers inside, one at the door, at least one relay runner in the lanes outside. They feel safe—they have been doing this for months and nothing has touched them." His finger traced the north entry. "The service door has not been barred from inside since they bricked the windows. They need the ventilation channel, and the channel runs through the door frame."

"You," Havel said from the window. He spoke with the careful weight of a man choosing his objection like a mason choosing a keystone. "You are proposing to walk into a building where people handle a plague weapon."

"I am proposing to walk the people who can stop it through the door that opens."

"Through a door you found. On information you gathered. While posted to an office that has no authority over—"

"The authority question," Jaina said quietly, "stopped being relevant when fourteen shipments went through your inspection process unchecked."

Havel's jaw tightened. He did not yield the window. He pulled a creased district map from inside his coat—well-handled, the fold lines so deep they had started to tear—and spread it over Corin's notes with both hands flat. A knife and a ring of heavy keys weighted the curling edges, pinning the paper to the table like a body being held down.

"If you want to move on the site, you move through here." He drew his finger along a lane that ran from the militia post to the granary's southern approach—clean, open sightlines, well-lit by district standards. "Dawn patrol. My people. Inspection authority already in place. We go in with eight men, we control the exits, and we do it in daylight where my watch can see what they are walking into."

"Dawn gives them eight hours to finish the shift and scatter," Sylvanas said.

"Dawn gives us control."

She watched his hand on the map. The finger stayed on the same lane—his lane, his route, the approach that ran through his militia's ground and opened through doors his people held keys to. Every suggestion he had offered since the granary walk had curved back toward the same center: his routes, his timing, his men in position, his authority framing the shape of the operation. She had watched him do it three times in the district and she was watching him do it again now, and the repetition had a weight that pressed against the part of her mind that read intent instead of words.

It might be caution. A city man protecting his own from something bigger than his truncheon could handle. She had seen that instinct in every border garrison that refused Farstrider help until the next raid burned the refusal out of them, and she understood it the way she understood any fear that wore a uniform.

But caution looked inward. Control looked outward. And Havel's eyes, when she caught them across the lamplight, were watching who was about to move—not who was about to be hurt.

She filed it. Did not name it. A suspicion without proof was a rope without an anchor, and she had wasted enough of those in the field to know the difference between reading a man and convicting him.

Corin's notes still lay open on the table—door routes, shift patterns, the ventilation gap, the names of every inspector he had tried to warn before anyone with authority listened. Everything the man had, spread flat and given without reservation. The way a field officer surrendered intelligence when the cost of holding it outweighed the cost of being wrong.

"Corin." She kept her voice level. "When the north service door is unbarred—how many breaths from the opening to the first handler position?"

He did not hesitate. "Six, if they are working at the far table. Four, if they have moved the treatment station closer to the ventilation channel since last week."

He had counted the distance in breaths. Not paces, not seconds—breaths. The measure of a man who expected to be inside the building when things went wrong and had already decided the wrongness was worth the air it cost him.

"You are with us," she said.

Nobody argued. Not Jaina, who had trusted Corin with the records and the mage-side approach and been right to. Not Cyndia, whose silence from the door was assent. Not Lyriel, whose stillness against the wall carried the quality of a woman who had already chosen her ground and was waiting for the word that put her on it.

Havel drew breath. Whatever he meant to say, he kept it behind his teeth. His hand stayed on his map. His finger stayed on his lane.

"Tonight," Sylvanas said.

The word landed in the room the way a knife landed in a table—point first, no bounce, the sound of something that was not going to be picked up again.

Havel straightened. "Tonight is not—"

"The treatment shift runs between the fourth and sixth bell. They will be inside the building, handling the grain, masks on, hands occupied. That is the one window where they cannot run and cannot fight without abandoning the weapon they are building." She looked at Jaina. "Can you hold one?"

"If we take one alive, I can tell you what he knows before he decides whether to share it." No boast in the words—just the flat professional certainty of a woman who understood what her tools could do and had already committed to using them. Her hand moved to push the loose hair from her face, and the gesture was the only thing that gave away the fact that what she was agreeing to do frightened her at all.

"Corin. The north service door."

"I can have it open by the third bell. The lane between the second and third bell is clear—the relay runners change over and there is a gap in their coverage." He said it with the compressed readiness of someone who had spent weeks watching a clock he could not stop and had finally been given permission to move.

Sylvanas turned to her own. "Cyndia—south approach, rooftop position. You saw the loading dock. Nobody leaves through it."

Cyndia inclined her head. One gesture. All the confirmation a Farstrider needed or gave.

"Lyriel—north lane, ground level. Cover the service door after Corin opens it. If anyone comes out, they come into you."

Lyriel's hand moved to the short blade at her hip—a brief unconscious touch, the body's way of confirming a position the mind had already mapped.

"Jaina and I go through the door."

She said it the way she would have said any other operational assignment—plainly, as though the arrangement of two leads through the point of entry were as unremarkable as the arrangement of chairs around a table. The mage and the ranger, together, into the place where the poison was made. She did not dress it up. Jaina did not need her to.

Havel leaned forward. The candlelight caught the keys on the table, threw their shadows long and angular across Corin's chalk-marked routes. "I can have four men at the southern approach by the second bell. We contain the district, we close the—"

"No."

"This is my city. My jurisdiction. My—"

"Your routes." She looked at him—not hard, not cutting, the even level gaze she gave to officers whose arguments had run out of road. "Every lane you have drawn tonight runs through your ground. Every approach uses your people. Every timing suggestion gives the cell more hours to finish and disperse." She let the observation settle between them, plain and unadorned, occupying the same air as the lamplight and the grain dust and the smell of old paper. "Captain. I am not accusing you of anything. But I am not running this operation through your geometry."

The muscle in his jaw worked once. He held the map flat with both hands, and the creases in the paper looked like old wounds—lines that had been folded and refolded so many times the paper remembered the shape of his thinking better than the shape of the district it was supposed to describe.

"If it goes wrong in there—"

"Then it goes wrong with people who chose to be inside."

She stood. The chair scraped against the plank floor, and the sound carried the finality of a latch falling shut. Through the window behind Havel, Andorhal's evening moved on—wheels on cobblestone, a dog barking twice before going quiet, the faint amber glow of the lamplighters working their way up the main street with the patient rhythm of men who did the same route every night and would do it again tomorrow and the night after that, steady as a heartbeat in a body that did not yet know what was growing inside it.

"Third bell," she said. "North service door. Positions as given." Her pulse had settled into the low even cadence that came before action—not calm, never calm, but the particular stillness of a body that had stopped asking questions and started counting time.

Jaina gathered her notes and folded the ward diagrams into a leather case with the practised efficiency of a woman who had packed up from one emergency and moved to the next often enough that the packing had become part of the thinking. She did not look at Sylvanas for reassurance, and Sylvanas did not offer any, and the absence of both was its own kind of answer—the quiet recognition of two people who knew what the next few hours would cost and had already decided to pay it.

"Third bell," Jaina said.

Corin was already on his feet, gathering the chalk-marked access routes with hands that moved the way they had moved all evening—steady, precise, quick with the urgency of a man who had finally been told to run after months of standing still. He tucked the papers inside his coat and followed Jaina toward the corridor.

Havel stayed by the window. His map lay open on the table like an argument that had not been won, its edges curling inward where the weight of the keys and knife no longer held them flat. One corner had slipped over Jaina's discarded notes and covered a ward diagram—his lane, drawn in his hand, lying across the signature of the weapon it had failed to find.

Sylvanas did not look back at him. She stepped into the corridor and pulled the door shut, and the lamplight narrowed to a bright seam along the floor before it vanished, and somewhere below the office Andorhal's granary district went on with its evening the way a body went on breathing—steady, rhythmic, not yet aware of the thing that was already inside it.
