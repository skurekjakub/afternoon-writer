# Chapter 7: Andorhal

Andorhal looked worse from inside the gate than it had from the rise.

From a distance the city had been roofs, smoke, and pressure. Up close it was permits, seal wax, grain dust, and wagons inching through two inspection lanes under a low arch. Lanes for papers, not for sacks. Teamsters waited with reins looped loose in one hand and folded papers in the other. A clerk in brown wool pressed wax seals. Hoofbeats struck stone and came back flat from the walls. Behind it all ran the busy human noise of a place still certain it was feeding the kingdom.

That certainty was the danger.

Sylvanas let her horse edge half a step ahead of Jaina's as they joined the slow turn toward the table. Cyndia held the left rear. Lyriel kept the right, eyes moving over the queue, the slit windows in the gatehouse, the idle spears in the hands of men who did not yet know what they were guarding. None of the guards looked sick. None looked frightened. They looked bored, which was worse.

"Purpose?" the clerk asked.

Jaina answered before the pause had room to grow. "Quartermaster review and ward inspection. Dalaran correspondence pending local verification."

The man looked up then, took in the travel dirt, the horses, the elf at Jaina's shoulder, and grew more careful.

"Orders?"

Jaina handed over the copied authorization Corin had arranged for them. She had pared the lie down on the ride in until it sounded routine and therefore beneath curiosity. The clerk found the right seal and stopped caring about the rest.

The guard nearest the table glanced at Sylvanas's bow. "Elven quartermasters now?"

"Only on the days your roads get interesting," Sylvanas said.

He looked to Jaina for the safer argument.

"If your city tracks grain by district instead of by shipment," Jaina said, "I'd rather know before I waste the afternoon in the wrong office."

That did it. The clerk shoved the paper back and pointed with the butt of his seal press. "Inspection office. First building past the square. Mage quarter beyond. Don't stable in the main market if you're staying overnight. They'll rob you clean."

"What a relief," Sylvanas said. "I was worried Andorhal lacked civic character."

They moved on before he could decide whether she had insulted him.

Inside, the city crowded close. The street beyond the gate opened into a market broad enough for three wagon lines and still crowded at the edges with bread, onions, lamp oil, tack, cured meat, and the rest of the things a working city bought without thought. Storehouses began almost at once. Men rolled sacks off carts onto shoulder pads and carried them inward at a trot learned by repetition. A woman with flour on both forearms shouted figures through a counting window. Somewhere a baker cursed a late load. Somewhere else a child laughed.

Sylvanas ignored the life of it and read the shape. Where traffic thickened. Where it pinched. Which alleys would hide a body fastest. Which roofs looked onto the square. Which workers watched loads, and which watched the people watching loads. Andorhal had low walls because it had never expected war at the gate. Its real defenses were routine, speed, and the fact that half the kingdom needed what moved through these streets.

Jaina turned her head once and read the district with her own different attention. Grain dust had caught on her cuffs.

"Worse?" Sylvanas asked.

"Broader," Jaina said. "The whole district is carrying it."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the answer I have before I get into the right rooms."

The inspection office was a narrow stone building wedged between a cooper's yard and an exchange hall. Ledgers moved through it fast and in order. Three clerks looked up when the four of them entered. The one at the back looked as though he had forgotten to sleep for a month.

He was younger than Sylvanas expected. Twenty, perhaps. Ink stained two fingers on his left hand. Copied notices leaned in uneven towers around his desk, all of them marked with district names and dates. He looked like a man who had been trying to hold back a flood with forms.

Jaina gave her name.

The young mage made a sound that belonged to a man finding religion too late to profit from it. He stood so fast he struck his knee on the desk.

"Apprentice Corin, lady— Archmage— I mean, not yet Archmage, obviously, but—"

"Corin," Jaina said. "Breathe first. Collapse later."

His eyes flicked to Sylvanas, then to the two rangers at the door, and some of the panic in him sharpened into relief.

"Antonidas said if the pattern held you'd come," he said. "I thought he was being kind. I have everything. Or nearly everything. Not everything. Some of it went missing, but I copied most of what went missing before it missed itself."

Sylvanas liked him at once for that sentence alone.

"Show me the part that matters first," she said.

Corin cleared a place on the desk by moving two towers of notices onto the floor and opened a folder tied with blue cord.

"The routes first," he said, steadier now that he had paper under his hands. "Six weeks. Same marks east out of Andorhal. The shortages aren't real. Every time I ask for the first counts, somebody above me smooths the books flat. The granary wards flare on nights when no licensed spellwork should be happening. I sent three reports to Dalaran and one to the local magistracy. The magistracy asked if rats could explain it. Dalaran thanked me for my diligence."

He said the last line without self-pity. That improved him.

Jaina bent over the copied route marks. Corin moved with her and laid the sheets out in the order his mind preferred. Sylvanas stayed standing where she could watch the door, the window, and the other clerks who pretended not to listen.

"You have names?" she asked.

"No reliable ones. Rotations, yes. Faces, some. A few handlers who sign for loads they never touch. Not enough to arrest anybody with."

"Have you seen the mechanism?"

"No." The answer cost him. "Residue. Disturbed wards. Missing stock that reappears in the books with the same total and a different route history. Enough to know the books are lying. Not enough to make anyone useful believe it."

Jaina looked up. "These ward flares. Show me the pattern."

He already had those pages separated. Of course he did.

"They don't match guild work," he said. "Too shallow for formal repair, too regular for accident. At first I thought somebody was moving unauthorized enchantment through the district. Then the sickness reports started piling up under the bad counts and I stopped believing in separate problems."

"Good," Sylvanas said.

He blinked at her.

"You've wasted your time on the right enemy."

That landed harder than praise.

Corin got them through the next doors quickly. He knew which porter to bribe with rank language, which senior functionary would rather not meet Jaina's eyes, which shelf of copied ledgers had been "reorganized" and therefore needed to be reached through the room behind it. On the ground, the boy was excellent.

By the time they left the exchange office the sun had moved across the square. Corin had given them route books, flare records, two names worth watching, and one better thing than names: a room where Jaina could work without clerks breathing through the door.

"There's a storeroom above the old assay chamber," he said as he led them back into the street. "Nobody uses it because the roof leaks, and because the leak only happens when it rains from the east, which everyone takes as a personal insult from the weather instead of a maintenance problem. The residue samples are there. I moved them after the second set went missing from the archive."

"Sensibly criminal of you," Sylvanas said.

They found Havel in the granary district itself, because that was where a man with his face belonged. Captain Aldric Havel wore militia leather powdered white with the same grain dust coating every beam and lintel in the district. Broken nose. Tired eyes. He carried the patience of a man too used to being asked why he had not solved a problem outside his reach.

When Corin brought them in through the side yard of a counting house, Havel looked Sylvanas over, looked at Jaina's cuffs, looked at Cyndia and Lyriel taking the corners without orders, and chose usefulness over ceremony.

"You the ones asking why my workers keep dying in clusters no physician can agree on?" he asked.

"Among other things," Sylvanas said.

"Good. I was afraid you were tax people."

He took them to a narrow office overlooking three loading yards and the alley between them. From the window the district showed its bones: grain moving in and out, hoists, tables, sweepers, cartwrights, porters. Enough honest labor to hide a war inside.

Havel knew the place by habit. He named streets without checking, then the men on them. The crews that drank too much. The ones that stole. The ones that fought. The ones who did none of it and worried him more. He had no court-ready case. He had pattern. A tall laborer with a limp who never touched stock but always knew when inspectors changed route. Two brothers on the west yard who worked sober, clean, and never spent wages in the taverns with the rest. Night crews that had grown too regular after a new supplier line opened three months ago. Sickness clustering around men who spent less time near public wagons and more near the repack tables under awnings.

"You've been watching them as suspects," Sylvanas said.

"I have suspects," Havel said. "What I don't have is proof that survives daylight."

"Three months?" Jaina asked.

"Near enough. New pressure before that, but three months since I started seeing men in ordinary aprons stand like drilled soldiers whenever anybody shouted wrong in the yard."

That matched the road.

Havel gave them shift changes, yard assignments, and the storehouses whose quiet hours made no commercial sense. Sylvanas listened with one eye on the yard below. Most men watched the load. Some watched each other. Eight, she thought after ten minutes, then revised it to at least eight. Too many laborers already expecting knives.

Cyndia appeared in the doorway without sound. "Tall one with the limp just checked the alley mouth instead of the incoming team. Third time in ten minutes."

Havel's jaw set. "That's Teren. Hired on as dock transfer six months ago. Never drank, never courted, never once took a day to complain."

"How inconsiderate of him," Sylvanas said.

Lyriel came in behind Cyndia. "North yard pair trading marks with the counting window. Left hand to sleeve, right hand low. Not local street talk."

Havel turned to the window too fast to hide how badly he needed to see it himself. Whatever last piece of doubt he had been carrying left his face when he did.

Corin had the mage-side rooms. Havel had the yards. Jaina could read the contamination where it lived in residue, ward flare, sample cloth, and treated grain. Sylvanas could read the men who guarded those things and the routes that joined them. Keeping both halves together any longer would not be caution. It would be drag.

They stepped into the alley behind Havel's office. Yard noise rolled over the wall in bursts: shouted counts, rope creak, a wagon bed slamming once against its brace. Corin held two brass keys and three copied flare records. Havel already had shift blocks sketched on the back of a spoiled manifest. Cyndia and Lyriel waited with the horses in a strip of shade, each turned a different way.

Jaina looked at the keys in Corin's hand, then at the district beyond Sylvanas's shoulder. She didn't ask permission.

"The residue room first," she said. "Then the repack floor if he can get me through without announcing it to the entire quarter."

Corin nodded too quickly. "I can."

Sylvanas took the manifest from Havel, scanned it once, and handed it back. "You take the north and west lanes with me. We watch the reliefs, the hands, who leaves sober, who leaves in pairs, and who checks exits before they check loads."

Jaina's gaze touched hers briefly.

"If you find the floor treatment before I come back," Sylvanas said, "I want the useful part first."

"You usually do."

"And if your useful part turns out to be 'they've poisoned the whole building'?"

"Then I'll do better than that."

That was enough.

They split.

The granary district gave up its people more readily than its rooms.

For the next hours Sylvanas moved through its seams with Havel at one shoulder and the rangers stretched where sightlines demanded. Cyndia drifted the outer lane, left hand near her bow grip, attention cutting across rooflines and alley mouths. Lyriel took the tighter right, quicker, more visible when visibility was the point. Havel played local authority well. He knew when to nod at a foreman, when to ask about missing tally chalk, when to complain loudly enough that workers lowered their eyes and returned to their motions.

From inside that ordinary patrol the wrong men began to stand out.

One never wiped sweat from his face because he never glistened, not even in the trapped heat between storehouses. Another kept his gloves on while handling sackcloth that made every other porter strip down to skin. Two more left the break alley separately and reached the far corner together with the timing of men who had practiced not meeting in public. A clerk in a plain vest signed three loads without once glancing at the wagons attached to them. Teren checked exits at each shift turn. Another man near the west wall counted heads each time the yard bell struck, though headcount belonged to no part of his work.

Sylvanas marked them by behavior instead of face. Faces shifted too easily in a city. Habits took longer. Hidden route marks showed where honest books went crooked: chalk scuffs on post bases, a nick on a loading hook, twine tied twice around a latch instead of once. Havel led her to an outbuilding for damaged sacks. Behind a false stack of spoiled barley she found oilcloth bundles holding ledger scraps, preserved powders, and two small vials wrapped in felt.

"They've got caches under civilian cover," Havel said quietly.

"No," Sylvanas said. "They are civilian cover."

By dusk she had the cell's bones: at least eight of them, men on the floor and men above them, supplies moving off-ledger through spoilage rooms and repack tables, quiet discipline in the men who skipped the taverns because drunk laborers talked and these men could not afford to.

Enough to carry back to Jaina.

The room Corin had found for Jaina lay above an assay chamber that smelled of old stone, damp plaster, and disturbed reagents. Rain had not come from the east that day, so the leak had spared the floor. Corin had laid out the residue samples on a trestle table under three shuttered lamps. Jaina stood at the center of them with her sleeves rolled and grain dust up both arms. Blue light moved and died between her fingers in brief passes. She looked colder than she had at noon, which usually meant she was close to an answer.

Sylvanas stopped in the doorway and took in the table before she spoke. Corin's copied notes sat open under stone weights. A square of treated cloth lay pinned by knives at the corners. One tray held sifted grain from three yards. Another held scrapings from repack boards and the lip of a mixing basin that should have carried nothing but flour. Jaina had reduced the city to objects. Good work.

Corin straightened first from where he had been holding one lamp high to catch sheen on a board sample.

"Don't say anything for one breath," Jaina said.

Sylvanas counted one in silence.

Then Jaina stepped back from the table.

"They're treating the grain here," she said. "After milling. During repack. The farms grow it. The roads carry it."

"How?"

"A preserving shell worked into the outer dust. Fine enough to survive handling that should ruin a simpler weave. Moisture drags it deeper. Milling spreads it. Baking won't kill it. It only has to last long enough to get into the body. After that the body finishes the work."

Corin looked like a man standing too near thunder and trying not to grin.

Jaina lifted the pinned cloth. Grain dust shimmered in the lamp light.

"This came off the repack floor beams. The strongest trace is in the dust nobody bothers to sweep from the high boards. They treat the loads after weighing. Light enough to vanish into ordinary loss, regular enough to put every sack through the same chain."

"And the dead?" Sylvanas asked.

"Still repeated exposure. Still the host finishing what the grain begins. That part hasn't changed." Jaina's mouth tightened as she thought. "But the work is cleaner than I thought. This isn't zealots smearing rot into grain and praying. Someone built it to survive the process."

"Useful."

Her eyes flicked up, tired and bright. "You wanted the useful part first."

"This qualifies."

Corin made a small sound of agreement and looked embarrassed by it.

"What about the body trigger?" Sylvanas asked.

"Not here." Jaina shook her head. "This is only the part that gets it into people. Clean enough that I'd trust the read in the field. Whatever wakes it after that is somewhere else. But Andorhal gives us the packaging method plain. Whoever runs this place isn't poisoning grain by guesswork. They're dosing a kingdom."

"At least eight inside the district," Sylvanas said. "Clerks and labor on the floor. Handlers above them. Off-ledger caches in spoilage rooms. They check exits before stock. They don't drink after work. They count each other."

Jaina set the cloth down slowly. "Same hands."

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

They laid it together on the trestle table after that: yard map over residue sheets, flare records crossing shift blocks, route marks pinned under knives at the corners. The yard map and the residue board stopped belonging to different rooms.

Havel arrived midway through it and went still at Jaina's residue board aligned with his yard sketch.

"Those rooms on the west repack line," he said and tapped one square. "If that's where the treatment happens, the night crew changes there at second bell. Short overlap. Enough confusion to take someone."

"Take?" Corin echoed.

"A live one," Sylvanas said. "If we keep chasing residue and route marks, we learn what this layer already knows. If we pull a live one out tonight, we may reach the hands above him before the district knows we've cut into it."

Jaina's finger rested on the flare sheet. "I still want the alchemical trail preserved. If they scrub the boards before dawn, we lose some of the proof."

"And if we wait for perfect proof, the men who can erase themselves walk home and sleep."

"I didn't say wait until dawn."

"No. You said preserve the trail first."

The room tightened. Not because either of them was wrong. Because each had reached the same answer from a different door and hated leaving any piece of it untouched.

Havel looked between them, jaw tight. He needed someone to choose before the district chose for them.

Corin, to his credit, kept breathing.

Jaina planted both hands on the table and went still for one hard beat.

"Fine," she said. "We do both. Capture first, but I mark the room beforehand. If they torch or scour anything after, I'll know what changed. And if the prisoner talks nonsense, the residue work lets me test which part was built to mislead."

Better.

"Cyndia and Lyriel hold the outer line," Sylvanas said at once. "Havel gives us yard timing and local exits. Corin keeps the city-side access quiet and closes the mage doors behind us. Jaina reads whatever the prisoner gives us against what she found upstairs. I take the body."

"You make everything sound charming," Jaina said.

"It's a gift."

They built the capture plan over candlelight and chalk: fallback routes through the spoilage yard, a cooper's lane, a drainage wall if the district alarmed; which door Havel could open without a key, which clerk would notice a missing handler first, where Corin's authority stopped being useful and started being suspicious. Cyndia and Lyriel came in wet-eyed from the grain dust and took their instructions without comment. Lyriel asked only where the second body should fall if the first body did not cooperate.

By the time the plan held, the city outside had shifted into its night shape: wagon noise gone, tavern noise up, a different economy moving through the streets. Through the wall came the scratch of Corin recopying names in a steadier hand. Havel had gone to place the right men where their absence would not show. Cyndia and Lyriel were walking the perimeter turns outside. Jaina had taken the next room to turn her sample notes into something she could use fast if the night went wrong.

Sylvanas stayed at the trestle table with the chalk map and the cooling candle stubs.

She disliked naming what had changed. The names came out soft, and the thing itself was not.

The city would not have opened for her alone. She could have read the yards, the exits, the discipline, the men who held themselves wrong for labor. She could not have found the shell hidden in dust on beams and in the lip of a basin no clerk had thought worth cleaning. Jaina had done that out of sight and brought back an answer fit to move on.

From the next room came the dry rasp of a page turned, then the brief pause that meant Jaina was weighing one set of notes against another. Work.

Good.

Sylvanas looked down at the route one last time. North yard, west repack, spill room, alley, wall. Simple enough to fail honestly if it failed at all.

Take Jaina out of this city tonight and half the answer went with her. Leave her where she was and the city stayed open.

That was fact enough.

No softness in it. Just the next move, and the fact that it now depended on work Sylvanas could not watch while it happened.

Outside, boots turned on the stones below the window. Cyndia on one pass, Lyriel on the next, the perimeter keeping its own time. In the room beyond, Jaina's quill scratched, stopped, scratched again.

Sleep could wait. The capture could not.

Sylvanas took the map and went to wake the night.
