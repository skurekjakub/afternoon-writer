# Tomas

## Chapter 4 state

Tomas matters less as a person than as a revelation about the enemy, but the prose still gives him a human shape. He is not introduced like a polished cult lieutenant. He is a young man in work clothes with the hunted look of someone who has been holding one dangerous corner of a job and hoping no one important ever came to inspect it. That makes him more disturbing, not less. The network is not built only from famous villains. It is built from boys stationed in granaries with lamps, jugs, and orders.

The key thing to remember is that Tomas is frightened before he is fanatical. He reaches for destruction immediately because that is the task he has been trained for. Under questioning he does not transform into a sneering ideologue. He collapses toward panic, denial, and smallness. Jaina reads him correctly: a cog, not a mastermind.

His death is the real point. The poison capsule proves discipline above the village line. Tomas exists to show that the route is watched, that evidence will be burned if possible, and that even a low-level operative has been prepared to die before giving names. Future chapters should remember him not as a major character but as the first clean proof that the plague network has trained silence into its smallest working parts.
