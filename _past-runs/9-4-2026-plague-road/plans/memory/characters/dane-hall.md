# Dane Hall

## Chapter 4 state

Dane Hall is the kind of village captain who would be easy to flatten into a generic obstruction if the writer is careless. Chapter 4 gives him more dignity than that. He does resist the riders at first, but not because he is foolish. He resists because he is still trying to keep his village inside the frame of ordinary authority. If this is only sickness, then armed elves and a foreign mage are an intrusion. The chapter lets that instinct read as responsibility before it reads as denial.

What breaks him is not rhetoric alone. It is Millhaven, then proof. Once Jaina earns the diagnosis and the granary confirms deliberate contamination, Dane does not become eloquent or heroic. He becomes useful. He listens. He asks the right practical questions. He takes the weight of ugly orders that will make his own people hate him by nightfall if he enforces them properly.

That is his defining note going forward. Dane Hall is not a glamorous authority figure. He is a local one. Thick-built, tired, stubborn, and very human. When the truth lands, he stays behind to do the least romantic work in the book: deny bread, lock grain, count the still-living, and try to keep a village from slipping over the same edge as Millhaven.
