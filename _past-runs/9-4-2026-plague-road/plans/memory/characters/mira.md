# Mira

## Chapter 3 state

Mira is the chapter's surviving human cost. Everything about her is scaled small: thin face, dirt on one cheek, breath caught in a body that has spent too long hiding in a cellar with the dead above her. But the prose does not make her decorative. She is the chapter's witness. Through her, the village stops being an arrangement of clues and becomes a place where a child watched her father die, rise later, and leave with the rest.

Her testimony comes in broken pieces rather than clean order. That is important to preserve. Mira remembers images: sweetness on breath, teeth making noise, being shoved under the table, the lane full at night, everyone moving north like they were listening. She cannot tell the story neatly, but she can tell it truthfully. That brokenness is part of why the scene works.

She attaches to Neral almost immediately because Neral gives her a shape for survival. By the end of the chapter Mira has narrowed her whole world down to one blanket, one pommel, one rider she trusts enough to ride north with. She should be remembered not as a generic survivor child but as Millhaven's only living witness and the point where the chapter's horror becomes personal.
