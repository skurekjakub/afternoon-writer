# Arthas Menethil

## Overview
Also known as Prince Arthas, Prince of Lordaeron, paladin of the Silver Hand. First appears in chapter6 and is current through chapter22.

## Presence on the page
Chapter 20 keeps him physically absent but renders him through aftermath: enlarged docks, stripped harbor function, and a lead measured in weeks rather than hours, turning his body into missing distance and infrastructure damage. Chapter 21 puts him back on the page transformed by Frostmourne: thinner, colder, rime spreading from the blade over gauntlet and wrist, face still recognizably his but emptied of hesitation. Chapter 22 renders Arthas through public aftermath rather than direct body: painted cloth over city gates, smiling stick-figure celebrations, and a palace already reorganizing itself around the prince who came home with Frostmourne and made the kingdom mistake corruption for triumph.

## Voice and behavior
Chapter 20 keeps him voiceless. His 'voice' is logistical residue now — the scale of harbor expansion and the hard fact that he pushed inland without leaving any easy point of interception behind. Chapter 21 removes what warmth remained. His speech is flat, certain, and stripped of persuasion: a man announcing completion, not debating consequence. No live voice on page in chapter22. His presence now speaks through civic celebration, resumed audiences, and a palace confidence so complete it no longer needs him physically present in the scene to dominate it. Looks straight up at the wall after the purge, holding the witness line long enough to make the pause feel chosen. Chapter 21 makes stillness itself wrong around him: pulling an arrow free without hurry, stopping ten paces away to watch the formation fail, and declining pursuit because the outcome is already certain to him. Chapter 22 gives no direct body, only the bodily imprint of his occupation: relaxed guards, palace routine, and celebratory iconography stretched over the city like a second skin.

## What chapter 22 changes
Claims Frostmourne before Sylvanas’s formation arrives, abandons Light’s Vengeance outside the cave, and answers Muradin, Jaina, and Sylvanas as if the choice is already complete. Uses Frostmourne’s field and the cave itself as weapons, then allows the retreat instead of needing to pursue it. Has already returned to Capital City, accepted public triumph, resumed audiences, and installed Northrend men on interior rotation by the time the pursuit reaches Lordaeron again. By chapter20 the pursuit knows he landed at Daggercap Bay weeks earlier, expanded the docks, and moved inland fast enough that catching him at the shoreline is no longer possible. By chapter21 the pursuit knows he has already claimed Frostmourne, discarded Light’s Vengeance, and become something no longer legible as a prince who can be reached by ordinary means. By chapter22 the protagonists know Arthas has been in Capital City for four days, with extra loyal guards inside the palace and no public interruption strong enough to touch him.

## Capabilities
Survives ordinary lethal shots and direct spell pressure as if his body and the blade now answer to different rules than a living prince’s. Combines Frostmourne’s field with overwhelming speed and environmental violence, turning the cave itself into part of his attack pattern. Exerts strategic and political gravity in absence, reorganizing a kingdom around his return so thoroughly that routine itself becomes cover.

## Current end-of-chapter state
At the end of chapter22, Hold the palace and continue presenting himself to Lordaeron as its returned victor Under that sits Finish sealing Frostmourne’s corruption into state ceremony and interior control before any meaningful witness can reach the room Emotionally, the chapter leaves Arthas Menethil Off-page and unreadable except as confidence translated into routine, celebration, and the total absence of visible fear inside his own capital

## Arc position
The working lie remains: A threat that cannot yet be struck cleanly can wait behind the one already burning in daylight The deeper truth pressing against it is: The invisible war in supply, bodies, and timing cannot be beaten by speed alone, no matter how righteous the speed feels Right now the character stands at: Chapter 22 carries Arthas one step deeper into institutional corruption. He is no longer only the blade’s wielder returning home; he is the prince already installed inside ceremony, audience, and palace routine

## Chapter 23 update
Chapter 23 is where Arthas finally stops being an advancing pressure on the map and becomes a closed event in the throne room itself. He enters the chapter already crowned by implication, murders Terenas before rescue can cross the chamber, and fights like someone whose body no longer obeys ordinary consequences. Throat wounds close. Chest wounds fail. The real target is not the man but the bond between the man and the sword.

That distinction matters for continuity. What finally drops Arthas is not simple martial superiority or a last appeal to the person he used to be. Jaina forces open Frostmourne’s rhythm. Sylvanas uses the opening. Only then does his body stop acting like a protected extension of the blade. The chapter should therefore be remembered as the point where Arthas ceases to function as the story’s primary moving threat and Frostmourne remains behind as the still-active remainder.

His final visible state is also important. Once the bond tears, pain and old human shape briefly show through the damage. Then he dies on the steps beneath his father’s throne, not redeemed, not reached, but finally made mortal enough for consequence to catch him.

## Chapter 24 update
Chapter 24 keeps Arthas present only as aftermath. He is dead, but the chapter makes clear that his death is not closure. The body becomes evidence in a courtroom narrative, and the blade he trusted keeps reaching past him.

For continuity purposes, remember him here not as an active agent but as proof that killing the wielder did not solve the deeper problem. His political story is over. His damage is not.
