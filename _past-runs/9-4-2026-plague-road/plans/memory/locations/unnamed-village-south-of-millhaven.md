# Unnamed village south of Millhaven

## Chapter 4 state

This village matters because it is not dead yet. Where Millhaven was aftermath, this place is the hour before understanding. The road into it still looks normal enough to lie: smoke where smoke ought to be, children moving, carts creaking, bread coming off boards, the square still trying to behave like a square. That ordinary surface is the location's defining trait. The horror here is embedded, not announced.

Inside the village, the plague has already entered routine. Dane Hall becomes a makeshift sickroom. The worst cases lie on borrowed cots and pallets. Bread baskets and crumbs are still part of the furniture of care, which makes the revelation crueler when Jaina proves the bread itself is helping kill them. Future scenes should remember the layering: bakehouse smoke and wet earth over sweet wrong breath, practical village noise over fear, grain dust over ledgers and sabotage.

The granary is the location's second face. Once the answer locks, the village stops being merely a medical site and becomes a logistics site. The barred doors, stored sacks, chalk marks, desk ledgers, treated barrels, and shattered clay jug reveal that this place is one spoke in a larger supply web. That is the village's long-term continuity value: it is the first living settlement where the plague is read simultaneously as illness, routine, evidence, and distribution system.
