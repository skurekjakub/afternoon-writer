# Sylvanas Windrunner / Uther the Lightbringer

## Current state
Professional respect deepens into parallel moral clarity. Uther makes the public refusal; Sylvanas makes the witness line. Chapter 17 leaves them choosing different lanes for the same judgment.

## Power dynamic
Uther still names the moral line in public. Sylvanas still carries the harder tactical witness of what happens when Arthas ignores it.

## Chapter 17 evidence
- Sylvanas accepts Uther’s backing of Jaina’s lane without argument, treating his judgment as one more hard piece of usable evidence. (chapter15)
- Uther’s public refusal at the gate confirms the warning he gave Sylvanas in earlier chapters. (chapter17)
- Sylvanas does not follow Uther out, but her wall witness becomes another form of agreement with his refusal: she will not let Arthas’s method pass for anything cleaner than atrocity. (chapter17)

## Physical dynamic
- They never need much direct interaction in chapter 17 because the chapter makes them legible through mirrored stillness on opposite sides of Arthas’s choice. (chapter17)
- Uther breaks left with the men he can save from the order while Sylvanas breaks into the city’s side lanes with the witness she can save from isolation. (chapter17)

## Trajectory
Shared alarm about Arthas -> active cooperation -> chapter 17’s parallel moral alignment under rupture.

## Chapter 23 update
Chapter 23 finally puts Sylvanas and Uther in the same room after all the earlier warning, witness, and parallel judgment. The meeting is not warm. It is disciplined. Uther arrives carrying too much grief to waste time, and Sylvanas is too badly hurt and too clear-eyed about the sword to stage a pointless last stand.

What matters is the mutual read. Uther does not hand her over to fools. Sylvanas recognizes that he is the only authority present who understands the room well enough to be obeyed for now. Their relationship therefore advances into a sharper form of respect: not agreement without tension, but cooperation because the alternatives are worse.

Future chapters should remember that custody here is not equivalent to discredit. Uther’s decision to take Sylvanas himself is part suspicion, part protection, and wholly significant.
