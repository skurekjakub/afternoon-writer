# Chapter 8: The Hunters Hunted

The west-line street worked at night.

Wagons rolled through the dark with hooded lamps under their axles. Men cursed at balking mules. Flour dust flashed pale whenever a door opened. Jaina waited in the seam between two storehouses and let that ordinary noise bear the weight of their trap.

Across the lane, Lyriel stood beside a stack of empty grain frames with her hood back and a porter's hook over one shoulder, slight enough in the dark to look harmless. Cyndia was gone. Havel leaned in the shadow of a counting-house arch with the posture of a bored local officer. Corin kept his head down beside him with a ledger board under one arm, one more clerk settling a discrepancy too late at night.

Sylvanas stood nearest Jaina, bow unstrung in her hand, pale hair hidden under a dark scarf. The stillness Jaina had first mistaken for reserve now read as readiness.

"Second cart after the mill team," Havel murmured without moving his mouth. "Limp on the right. Checks exits before he checks men."

Jaina touched the ward-thread she had already laid over the lane. Slight work: a muffling spread thin through nailheads, hinges, lamp brackets, anything with enough metal or built intention to hold a whisper of spellcraft. Not enough to hide a battle. Enough to put a hand over the bell if someone tried to ring one.

The cart came.

The first man beside it wore labor well enough. Loose shoulders. Slow feet. A man tired enough to forget his supper. The second glanced once at the alley mouth before he looked back to the rut.

There.

Sylvanas moved on Jaina's breath. One moment she stood at Jaina's shoulder; the next she stepped from shadow behind the limping man with one hand over his mouth and the other locking his knife arm at the elbow. Lyriel caught the cart horse by the bit before it could shy. Jaina smothered the cheap warding on the prisoner's collar under her palm.

Like a hand over a bell.

The magic kicked once, startled and hard, then flattened. Cyndia rose behind the first escort and folded him into the dark without spectacle. Havel blocked the lane's view from the arch. Corin kept walking, ledger under his arm, as if men vanished in Andorhal every night.

The whole snatch took less time than one wagon wheel turning.

Jaina reached the prisoner's face as Sylvanas dragged him into the seam between buildings. He bit down hard enough to bloody his own lip. Jaina struck the hinge of his jaw with two fingers, and whatever he meant to break in his mouth stayed whole.

"Try again," Sylvanas said quietly. "I prefer not to pull teeth in the street."

His eyes cut to Jaina's hand and widened.

Good, she thought. Fear was faster than persuasion.

They hustled him through a side door Havel had already opened into a cooper's shed that smelled of damp wood, vinegar, and grain mold. Broken hoops leaned against the wall. A shutter hung crooked over the single back window. The room was narrow and forgettable.

Cyndia bound the unconscious escort in three efficient turns and left him under spoiled sacking. Lyriel took the back corner near the window slit. Havel barred the door. Corin set his ledger on a barrel and wiped his palm against his coat.

The operative sat because Sylvanas pushed him into the chair. His gaze flicked over window, door, rafters, Lyriel, Cyndia's sword, Jaina's hands, then back to Sylvanas.

Not panic. Inventory.

His limp had been partly true. One boot was built up half an inch at the heel. His hands were a laborer's hands if one ignored the scar-web under the calluses and the pale crescents where old ink had once sat. Not a dockside brute. A trained man wearing roughness well.

"Name," Havel said.

The man spat blood at the floor.

"Local militia has already tried shouting at your people," Sylvanas said. "We can spare ourselves the repetition."

She crouched in front of him. No raised voice. No theatrics. She pressed where the break would tell her the most.

"Who handles your reliefs on west line?"

Nothing.

"Who signs spoilage after second bell?"

His shoulders tightened at reliefs.

"You built the repack schedule to hide treated loads among damaged stock," Jaina said. "The preserving shell is elegant. Wasteful in one or two places, but elegant."

His gaze snapped to her.

There it was.

"You know enough to be useful," she said. "Not enough to have designed it."

"Careful," Sylvanas said without taking her eyes off him. "He'll start enjoying being underestimated."

"He isn't enjoying anything."

The prisoner's mouth twitched. "You think this city belongs to clerks and wagoners because that's what it smells like."

"No," Sylvanas said. "I think you were taught to mistake concealment for ownership."

Hatred flashed. The balance in the room shifted. She had found the pride under the fear.

"Better," Sylvanas said. "Who sits above the yard cells?"

He breathed through his nose too fast.

Jaina let a thread of magic slide over him, not into mind or blood, only near enough to read the charge moving through him. Sweat shone on his upper lip. His pulse struck high in his throat. Fear, yes--but not of pain or prison. It ran upward. Toward an absent rank.

"You don't answer to a crowd," she said.

He kept his mouth shut.

"Nor to some cellar lord with hired fools. This was designed. Grain treatment. The host trigger kept away from the carrier work. The routes stay clean. Men wait when anything goes wrong. One mind laid that down."

"Mind," he said, laughing once through his teeth. "You say it like that's the part worth admiring."

Corin made a small noise behind her, half disgust and half vindication.

"No," Jaina said. "I say it because it means you are not important enough to die for."

The words hit. His contempt cracked. Not glory. Inclusion, perhaps. Proximity. The privilege of serving brilliance.

Men had built worse gods from less.

Sylvanas rose. He flinched though she had not touched him again.

"Field necromancers," she said. "How many in the city?"

He stared at her.

"Don't waste my time pretending you don't know what I mean. Not planners. The hands that keep your infected stock obedient."

True surprise crossed his face.

"Three?" Sylvanas guessed. "No. More than that, or you would have hidden them better. Five? Rotating by district?"

He swallowed before he could stop himself.

"They don't know the whole of it," he said suddenly. "None of us below the line do."

"Below whose line?" Jaina asked.

His eyes flicked to her, then shut tight.

"Good," Sylvanas said. "Stay with that mistake."

He opened them again. Wet at the edges now. The fear in him had sharpened until Jaina could almost feel it scraping the air.

"There's one hand above the city cells," he said. "One design. We build the routes. We carry what we're given. We hold mouths shut when the time comes. The acolytes keep the stock obedient. The true work sits higher."

"North?" Havel asked.

That drew another involuntary look.

"How far north?" Jaina said.

"Far enough that Andorhal is only the throat, not the--"

He stopped. His chest jerked once.

Then the air changed.

Cleaner than the ambient taint she had spent the day tracing through dust and sackcloth. Narrower too. A line driven through distance and stone with such control that its precision was worse than force. A needle through distance. A chosen point.

"Down!" she snapped.

Too late.

The strike punched through him like a pin through wet parchment.

He made one soft sound, and every muscle in his body seized. Black raced through the veins at his throat. Blood spilled from his nose in two thin threads. His eyes locked on nothing. The word he had almost spoken died in his mouth.

Sylvanas was already moving, knife out, searching for a source no body in the room had made. Lyriel turned for the shutter. Cyndia hit floor, wall, back corner, checking angles while Havel slammed the bar harder across the door.

Jaina caught the prisoner's jaw and reached for life out of reflex. Cold punched into her palm. He was dying before she found the seam of the spell. Whoever had cast it had chosen the one measure between certainty and economy and driven it straight through his heart.

"No," Corin whispered.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

"There are searchers already on the street," Jaina said.

She reached for the district again and found motion waiting. Living necromantic signatures moved in disciplined lines through the neighborhood, each one leashed tight enough to stand apart from the city's wider sickness. Three to the east. Two cutting south. Another spreading across the roofs.

The room was no longer an interrogation room. It was a marked point on someone else's map.

"Back window," Havel said.

"Not yet," Jaina said. "Roofline north. Street team on the alley mouth in seconds."

Sylvanas nodded once. "Rear wall, then cut west before the cooper yard opens. Cyndia, take the body if you can do it cheap. If not, leave him."

"Cheap," Cyndia said, hoisting the dead man's chair back with one hand so she could cut the wrist ties.

They went through the inner door into a storage run barely wide enough for two men. Jaina did not waste power on concealment. That had failed. She spread her attention instead, feeling pressure slide through streets beyond plaster and timber. A week ago she would have called it inelegant. Tonight it was survival.

"Right at the lane end," she said.

Sylvanas altered course at once, caught Lyriel by the sleeve, and sent them left through a hatch into a yeast room rank enough to sting Jaina's nose. Havel shoved aside a grain cart, found the service door behind it, and got them into a yard where sacks rose in pale towers under canvas.

Someone shouted two streets over.

Not alarm. Position.

Ordinary noise thinned instead of broke. Teamsters stopped swearing. Hoists slowed. Men moved because movement had been ordered.

Standing orders, Jaina thought. Breach. Seal. Search. Remove.

"Roof pair crossing. Three houses north."

Sylvanas cut them through an alley before Jaina finished the sentence. Lyriel flowed ahead to the mouth, glanced once, then vanished around it. Cyndia kept the rear with the dead operative across her shoulders like spoiled grain. Corin stumbled on a rut, and Havel caught him hard by the arm without turning.

"Breathe later," Havel muttered.

They reached a broader street full of night carts and turned with it, not against it. Jaina kept her head down and listened with the skin behind her eyes. Two signatures split behind them. One rose. One cut low. Another waited ahead where the street narrowed between granary walls.

"Don't take the arch," she said.

"I wasn't going to," Sylvanas said.

That steadied her more than praise would have.

They wheeled instead into a side street so abruptly a baker's apprentice swore and dropped a basket. Lyriel caught the boy by the shoulder, set him upright, and kept moving. Cyndia turned once at the far end of the lane and drew. Her shot went back into the dark with clean economy. Jaina heard a body strike timber and slide.

No slowing. Not for that.

The pressure ahead thickened.

"Two on the roofline above the dye house. One street level behind the rain barrel. Mage left hand, not right."

"How sure?"

"Enough."

Sylvanas planted a foot on the dye house wall, vaulted the rain barrel, and put an arrow into the place Jaina had felt before the hidden man fully rose. Cyndia and Lyriel split without needing the order, one taking the street line, one the stair to the roof. Havel shoved Corin through a door hanging half open on its latch. Jaina followed into a tailor's workroom with shirts strung overhead and a woman frozen behind her own hand.

"Stay there," Jaina said.

The woman obeyed at once.

They crossed that room, a back court, a midden passage, then another street. The escape became nothing but decisions. Left because Jaina felt a cold line gather at the right-hand corner. Down because Sylvanas saw fresh boot marks on the stair. Halt because Lyriel heard men on the other side of the wall. Run because Cyndia's line finally had to collapse inward.

Once, hostile magic skimmed so near Jaina's shoulder she tasted iron.

Sylvanas's hand caught the back of her coat and yanked her through a doorway a breath before the bolt struck the brick where her throat had been.

The jolt spun her against Sylvanas's chest. For one instant there was only the narrow dark between them, Jaina's breath knocked thin, Sylvanas already looking past her for the next danger.

"Stair," Sylvanas said.

Jaina took the order and climbed.

They came out over a brew house, crossed three joined roofs under a moon dulled by dust, slid down into an alley lined with broken casks, and found the shelter because Havel had prepared for losing the first one.

A washerwoman's abandoned store cellar under a chapel annex too poor for stained glass. The entrance hid under stacked coal bins in a yard with no line of sight from the street. Inside, the room was low, cold, and dry. Shelves held water jars, old blankets, lamp oil, two sealed crates of biscuits, and a packet of copied keys in waxed cloth.

Corin bent over with his hands on his knees as soon as the door shut. His whole body shook once.

"That was not a search," he said hoarsely. "That was a net."

"Yes," Havel said.

Cyndia lowered the dead operative to the floor. Lyriel stripped the collar from the corpse and handed it to Jaina. The cheap ward on it had burned out under her first suppression. The man himself had gone slack into the ugly peace of a dead end.

Jaina knelt and touched the bruise-dark veins at his throat again, though she knew what her fingers would find.

Nothing useful. No lingering hook to follow back. The strike had come from someone who knew how to leave nothing of himself on the work.

"This was ready," she said.

"Ready?" Corin asked.

"He was lost the instant he missed his report window. They already had their people in place to close the district around the failure."

Havel braced both hands on the shelf. "I thought I was looking at smugglers with a few grave-minded fanatics behind them."

"You were," Sylvanas said. "And men with standing orders."

Corin went paler still. "If they planned for capture, then they planned for investigators. For us."

"Eventually," Jaina said. "Not for us by name. For the fact that someone competent would follow the books far enough to put a hand on one of theirs."

"Comforting."

"No."

She cleared space on the central crate. Corin, grateful for work that was not terror, brought his copied route sheets at once. Havel added the spoiled manifest with its shift blocks and district notes. Jaina spread them flat, weighted the corners with lamp bases, and set the burned collar beside the map.

The cellar lamp smoked. Grain dust clung at her sleeves. When she laid two fingers on the route marks and called the faintest thread of power into them, the lines she had traced all day began to answer each other.

Out of Andorhal.

Not around it. Not within it. Out.

Road west to village ovens that could never have named the source of what arrived in their flour. North to smaller storehouses, each thinking itself only a stop in trade. East to market towns, garrison bread, kitchens where women would slap dough onto boards before dawn and never know what had been worked into the dust.

She went still.

"What changed?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina looked at the map, at the room beneath it, at the whole city under her hands.

Plain enough to hurt.

"I kept treating Andorhal as the place where it became true," she said.

Even Corin kept still.

"It isn't. This is only where they make it ordinary."

Corin shut his eyes. Havel swore under his breath.

Jaina traced one route line, then another. The preserving shell, the repack work, the false spoilage, the shift discipline, the hidden caches: none of it had been designed to poison Andorhal. It had been designed to pass through Andorhal wearing the face of food.

"How far?" Sylvanas asked.

"North Lordaeron at least. Bakeries, settlements, probably way stations and garrison contracts. Not every route. Enough."

"Enough to start a war with bread," Havel said.

"The war started before we got here," Sylvanas said. "This is only the proof."

Jaina pressed her mouth shut. The proof felt like a ledger arriving after the grain wagons left the yard.

Knowledge, again. Clean, hard, late.

Corin stared at the northbound lines. "There were stories. From carters. About a scholar north of the city buying old houses through proxies and never showing his face. I thought it was tavern rot."

Havel looked over. "And supply runs that vanished into farm roads they had no business using. Men got frightened when his people came up in drink. Wouldn't say much. Only that the name started with Kel."

Sylvanas's attention sharpened. "Kel what?"

Corin's head came up fast. "Kel'Thuzad."

Jaina did not move.

For one heartbeat she could not.

The name had not been active in her mind for years. A dismissed conversation. A cautionary tale told in halls that liked to believe expulsion solved corruption. Brilliant. Difficult. Increasingly grotesque in his appetites for forbidden study. Antonidas colder each time his former colleague's petitions crossed the table. Then the final break.

Kel'Thuzad.

The dead man in the cooper's shed had almost had its shape in his mouth.

"I only know what apprentices repeat when they want to sound frightened on purpose," Corin said. "He was Kirin Tor once. That's all. Or close enough."

"More than enough," Jaina said.

Havel frowned. "You know him."

"I know what he was." Her eyes stayed on the map. "Archmage. Council of Six. Antonidas cast him out when necromancy stopped being argument and became work."

The route lines kept their severe order beneath her fingers. The plague's carrier side had always shown a mind trained to love durable solutions. Milling. Storage. Transport. Loss rates. Timing. She had spent days recognizing the intelligence in the work without wanting to see whose it was.

Now it had a face.

A man trained under the same ceilings that had trained her. Taught to think in systems, in elegant sufficiencies, in the pleasure of solving what others called impossible. Antonidas's world had rejected him. It had made him first.

She became aware of the others by edges: Cyndia at the rear shelf, wiping her blade; Lyriel watching Jaina instead of the door; Havel grim and practical; Corin looking as though he had said something irreversible; Sylvanas nearest the map, nearest Jaina, saying nothing.

Nothing was the right choice. Jaina did not need comfort. She needed the next action before stillness hardened into delay.

She reached into her coat for the sending stone. It had gone warm from her body.

"Now?" Corin asked.

"Now," Jaina said.

"If the line isn't secure--"

"It never is."

She fed power into the stone. Light woke under her thumb, muted blue in the cellar dark. One breath. Two. Then Antonidas answered with no formal preamble, only her name and the roughness of interrupted sleep already stripped away by alarm.

"Jaina."

"We have the name."

Silence.

Very faintly, cloth moved against stone on his end.

"Say it," he said.

"Kel'Thuzad."

The silence that followed was worse than any curse. It lasted long enough for Jaina to understand that the name needed no explanation. When Antonidas spoke again, every last piece of academic padding was gone. No lecture. No careful preface. Only age, recognition, and the speed too slow for the field but as fast as the institution knew how to move.

"Are you certain?"

"As certain as I can be from fragments. Corin knew the old stories. Havel had the northbound rumors. The dead man almost said it, and the structure of the work fits him too cleanly. It's him."

Another brief silence. Not disbelief this time. Calculation, perhaps, and memory cutting across it.

"Then leave Andorhal alive," Antonidas said. "Write everything. Carry nothing in one head that can be lost with the body. I will start what I can from here."

What I can.

Enough truth sat in that phrase to make argument pointless.

"He's north of the city," Jaina said. "We turn that way next."

"I know."

Two words. Worn flat.

She had the strange urge to ask whether he remembered Kel'Thuzad's first lecture, the patience in that voice, the hands that never shook, the way brilliance made a room forgive its own disgust for longer than it should. She asked none of it. There was no room for grief yet.

"Master?" Corin whispered from somewhere behind her, forgetting himself.

Antonidas heard the title and ignored it.

"Report again once you're clear of the city," he said. "And Jaina--"

She waited.

Whatever had meant to follow the name failed him. Or he killed it before it left his mouth.

"Go," he said instead.

The stone dimmed.

Jaina closed her fingers around the cooling edges until they bit her palm. Then she set it beside the map, straightened the route sheets once because her hands needed work, and looked at the others.

"Corin, you and Havel keep the records split. Nothing in one cache. Nothing in one hand. If they lose one site, they lose a fraction."

Havel nodded before Corin had found his breath. "We'll salt false trails too. Make them spend men on ghosts."

"Good."

Sylvanas was still watching her, not prying, only measuring whether she had finished standing still.

Jaina met it. "Eastweald next."

"I assumed so."

"You usually do."

The corner of Sylvanas's mouth shifted, not enough to be called a smile. "Get some water first, or your splendid revelations will fall over on the road."

Dry enough, ordinary enough, that Jaina nearly laughed. The sound did not come, but the knot under her ribs eased.

Outside the shelter, the city would still be loading bread onto wagons, still moving under routines that knew how to hide murder inside commerce. They had the name now. They had the mind on the work. They also had the bitterer thing: proof that being early by every ordinary measure could still mean arriving after the grain had already gone out through the gates.

She had wanted clarity to be leverage.

Tonight had cured her of that.

Jaina bent over the map once more and priced roads, time, and loss with the right hardness at last.
