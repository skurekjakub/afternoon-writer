# Chapter 19: The Decision

Stratholme still stained the northern sky.

From a mile south, with one ridge and a stand of stripped birch between the camp and the city, the smoke should have looked smaller than it did. Distance ought to have done something useful. It did not. The separate plumes had flattened into one long bruise, brown at the bottom, black above, and every time the wind turned the camp got a little of it anyway: char, wet ash, grain gone bitter in the ovens, the city still teaching after the walls were out of sight.

Sylvanas stood at the edge of the picket line and let her eyes move over what remained.

Not much. Enough.

Three Farstrider field horses stood nose to tail under the low trees, light tack dark with yesterday's sweat, lower legs dusted white where the road had dried and the mud cracked away. Cyndia had brushed them down as far as time allowed, checked every strap, reset the bedroll ties, and hung feed where the animals could reach it without tangling themselves. The gray mare Vereesa would later ride in on had not yet come into camp; at present the camp belonged to the four of them and the little pocket of order Cyndia had dragged out of a ruin of a day. A cookfire no bigger than a helmet. Waterskins in the shade. Map case on supply crates. Spare arrows laid by type instead of in the loose miserable heap travel usually reduced such things to.

Jaina had come out of the tent with papers in both hands before she touched the food Cyndia had set by the fire.

That mattered more than appetite would have.

The mage looked used-up in ways a road could not fix in one night. Smoke still clung to the blue wool of her sleeves. The braid she had redone this morning had already started to loosen at the nape from handling notes and pushing hair back while she thought. Yet the stunned vacancy from the wall was gone. She held the papers flat with one ink-stained hand and weighed them with the heel of the other, trying to keep the answer still long enough to name it.

Sylvanas stepped to the crate table. The canvas map cover had been stretched tight over the top and pinned with a knife through one corner. A second knife held down the east edge where the breeze worried it. Jaina had marked trader roads in iron gall, relay points in blue, contamination sites in a thin red hand that crossed and recrossed the same lanes until half Lordaeron looked wounded.

Camp behind them. Smoke ahead. The map between.

"Show me," Sylvanas said.

Jaina did not waste words asking where to begin. Good.

"Here," she said, setting one page over the southern routes. "If we keep riding village to village, we'll refine numbers. Exposure rates. Which bakeries got the worst flour first. Which roads carried it fastest."

Her finger moved north before the sentence finished.

"But not the part that matters."

Sylvanas followed the line. Andorhal. Hearthglen. Stratholme. Then farther, where the neat confidence of the kingdom's roads thinned toward rougher country and port names written small along the coast.

"The grain delivered it," Jaina said. She was using the full register now, building the thought instead of cutting it down for duller company. "It did not complete it. Stratholme proved that. The strongest contamination in the stock was already burning out by the time we left, and the deeper pattern was still there. Stronger than before, if anything. Grain carries. Bodies keep. Something else wakes them."

No lecture in it. No careful simplification. Just the shape as she had it.

Sylvanas laid two fingers on the map south of the city.

"And down here?"

"Dead work." Jaina looked at the red marks she had made through the southern roads and did not flinch from them. "Useful if our only goal is proving what we already know to people who have decided proof is a condition they can always postpone. Not useful if the goal is finding who built this to survive its own carriers."

That, too, gave Antonidas and Lor'themar more in common than either city deserved.

Sylvanas looked outward, over the camp, to where the horses shifted at the smell of the fire.

The old border lie still had hands. It kept offering the same smaller job: secure the lane out, count the dead, send a cleaner report home, pretend the thing stayed bounded because the map wished it would. But south had been nothing but an exit road since the wall. Bodies had taken them away from Stratholme. The answer had not gone with them.

"Arthas," she said.

Jaina's mouth tightened once. "North."

"Your model."

"North."

"The last useful relays?"

"North," Jaina said again, then corrected herself with a little motion of the page against the canvas. "As far as the coast, yes. After that the signal line ends and we'd be working from whatever the harbors still have to lend."

Sylvanas bent over the map. A patrol rider's view of the country came first: ridge lines, open stretches, the places a tired horse might be ruined if pushed at the wrong hour. Then the rest of it layered over. Port towns. Supply roads. The places a prince with too much certainty and not enough patience would strip for ships.

"How much do we gain by hunting one more granary south?" she asked.

Jaina was silent long enough that the fire cracked twice and one of the horses snorted behind them.

"Paper," she said at last. "Neater paper. Better arguments for men who will still arrive after the dead."

"And what do we lose?"

Jaina's eyes flicked north over the map. "Sea lead. If he has already sailed, every day we spend proving the same theorem gives him another day on the water and another inland after that."

There it was.

Sylvanas straightened. Cyndia crossed the far edge of the camp with a feed scoop in one hand and did not look over, which was its own courtesy. The scout knew from the silence what kind of conversation this was and left it alone.

The wind shifted again. Smoke rolled low across the trees, and under it, fainter, there was something else now. Not sea yet. Only the hint of it. A colder trace under the burned city smell, far enough off to be promise rather than fact.

"Pack the map," Sylvanas said.

Jaina blinked once. Not surprise. More a held-breath stillness, the kind before a piece clicked into place.

"You're certain."

"No." Sylvanas folded the canvas flap over the marked roads herself. "I'm done pretending certainty is required."

Jaina's mouth almost moved. Not a smile. Nothing so easy. But some tight thing around it eased.

"That," she said, gathering her papers at last, "is a better standard than most councils manage."

Sylvanas took the dead center of the map case in one hand and turned toward the tent.

"Eat while you can, Jaina."

"You say that," Jaina said behind her, dry enough to survive the smoke, "as if the coast road is famous for pauses."

"It isn't."

Behind her, pages rustled into a square stack. Then the faint scrape of Jaina finally reaching for the bowl Cyndia had left. Work first. Food second. Better than the wall.

The bird-call came out of the trees before Sylvanas reached the tent.

Three short notes. Friendly.

Cyndia's head turned at once. Her left hand was already up, fingers spread for stillness, before the answering call came back from farther off along the road. The horses took the shift in the camp faster than the fire did. One of them lifted its head hard enough to pull the lead taut, then settled when Cyndia made a sound low in her throat that carried more habit than comfort.

Friendly, then.

Urgent anyway.

Sylvanas turned toward the south approach. The riders broke from the birches at a run.

Not enemy formation. Too clean. No spread for arrow fire, no attempt to own the ridgeline, no one hanging back to read the camp before committing. Home trouble, then, exactly as Cyndia had judged. The lead horse was lathered through the chest, gray coat dark under the foam, and the rider sat the saddle with the compact stiffness of someone who had been in it for too many hours and refused to show the road any of the cost.

Cyndia moved first, crossing the yard with no wasted motion and lifting two fingers for the camp edge post. The escort behind the lead rider reined down at the signal, good discipline even half-dead with haste.

The lead rider came on another six lengths before Sylvanas had the face.

Vereesa.

Not a clerk. Not a magistrix. Not some chamber pet with clean gloves and a safe horse.

Vereesa Windrunner, dust to the knees, one glove gone, braid coming apart at the shoulder, riding like the Convocation had reached down into the family and chosen the hand most likely to hurt.

Of course they had.

She swung down before the mare had fully stopped. The landing nearly cost her once; the right knee took the jolt and locked, then the correction vanished under the next step. Ink marked two fingers of her left hand. Good vellum rode in the dispatch tube at her hip. Family first in the face. Institution first in the kit.

"Ranger-General," Vereesa said.

The formal register landed before the sister did. Another choice made somewhere up the road.

Sylvanas handed the map case to Jaina without looking at her. "Water for the horse."

Cyndia was already on it. She had the mare's rein before any of the escort had properly dismounted, one glance enough to take stock of blown flanks, half-spent tack, and riders who needed to sit down before they remembered to ask.

Vereesa's eyes flicked once past Sylvanas to the camp beyond: the notes, the mage, the picketed horses, the working shape of a mission the Convocation had hoped to interrupt before it hardened into one.

Then she took the vellum from the tube.

Red wax. Council seal. Better paper than any road deserved.

She held it out. Sylvanas did not take it.

The beat that followed told its own story in front of everyone listening.

Vereesa's jaw set. Her arm did not lower.

"By order of the Convocation of Silvermoon," she said, and now the words were not hers at all, only the chamber speaking through a Windrunner mouth, "the Ranger-General is recalled from all operations in foreign territory. Your authority to deploy Farstrider assets beyond the borders of Quel'Thalas is suspended effective immediately. Failure to comply will constitute desertion under the Articles of Military Service."

The camp had gone very quiet.

Not empty quiet. Working quiet. Cyndia easing the gray mare's bit free one buckle at a time. Leather creaking at the picket line. One of the escort swallowing too hard to hide it. Jaina still somewhere at Sylvanas's right shoulder with the map case in hand and enough sense not to come nearer.

Sylvanas looked at the paper. At the seal. At Vereesa's fingers holding it steady when the road still lived in her joints.

"That's the chamber voice," she said. "Do you have your own?"

Vereesa breathed out through her nose.

Some of the formal chill left her face. Not much. Enough.

"Noncompliance means exile by military law," she said, lower now, and the crack in the formality showed where the sister had been held behind it. "Not censure. Not review. Desertion. They want it clean."

"They always do."

Vereesa let the document hang between them a moment longer, then lowered it without folding it. That hurt her more than the words had. Sylvanas knew the movement. The instant when duty failed to force obedience out of the person facing it.

"Lor'themar fought them," Vereesa said.

There. The real report.

Sylvanas said nothing. Silence was easier on some wounds than the first question.

"He spoke for an hour." Vereesa's voice had gone fast in the way it did when feeling threatened to outrun control. "I've never heard him do that. No wit left in it, nothing to make them laugh and call themselves reasonable afterward. He read figures. Contamination rates, relay counts, Eastweald dead, projected march speeds if the whole thing kept moving north. Just numbers. Names. Dates. Grain totals. He sounded—"

She stopped and looked away once, toward the trees where Cyndia was rubbing the gray mare down with a scrap of cloth.

"He sounded finished," Vereesa said.

That landed cleaner.

"What did he buy?" Sylvanas asked.

"A forward mobilization." The contempt in Vereesa's mouth was not for Sylvanas. Not even for the chamber. For arithmetic itself. "Small. Relay support to the northern harbors if anyone still obeys the orders by the time we reach them. A little horse. A little feed. Enough that they can claim they acted."

"At cost."

"At all of it." Now she did look directly at Sylvanas, and there was the younger sister under the captain at last — wide-eyed, furious, tired enough to stop hiding it. "He had nothing left by the end except records. He read those too. Vote counts. Favors owed. Who abstained because they were afraid to be seen choosing. He isn't winning anymore, Sylvanas. He's only spending."

Cyndia had gone still at the horse line. Not intruding. Listening anyway. Good. Let the camp hear what the chamber cost.

Sylvanas took one step closer. Not enough to turn it private. Enough that Vereesa had to tilt her head back a little to hold the eye line.

"Then he should stop."

Vereesa laughed once, sharp and unbelieving.

"You know he won't."

No. He would not.

The thought of Lor'themar stripped of his dry little cuts, reading contamination lists until his own voice wore down to bone, put an old tired anger under Sylvanas's ribs. Not at him. Never at him. At the machine that could hear a city count its dead and call the answer procedure.

Something in Sylvanas's face slowed Vereesa's voice. When she spoke next, she was choosing every word.

"Come home," she said.

Not the document. Not the chamber.

The camp narrowed around the sentence.

"Vereesa—"

"No." She stepped in before Sylvanas could turn it back into rank. "No, don't do that. Don't stand there and answer me as if I'm one of your captains. Alleria is gone. Lirath is dead. If they brand you deserter and seal the order behind you, you do not come back from that. Not to the city. Not to us."

The last word nearly shook. Nearly. She held it.

"The border will still be there when you return," Sylvanas said.

"That isn't the point."

"It's exactly the point."

Vereesa made an angry sound that would have been a laugh in a kinder hour.

"Lady Sun." The old Thalassian name slipped out before she could catch it. Childhood, not command. "The family is not a relay post you can abandon because the road beyond it looks interesting."

The name hit harder than the document had.

Sylvanas kept her face still. That cost less than reacting.

"Interesting," she said. "Is that what you rode two days to call it?"

"Don't be cruel."

"Don't ask for dishonesty."

Vereesa stared at her. The vellum crackled once in her grip.

"You think this is only politics."

"No." Sylvanas let the smoke, the horses, the map case under Jaina's arm, all of it stand behind the words before she gave them. "I think this is family speaking with the chamber's hand on its throat."

That one landed. Vereesa flinched in the eyes only.

"Then hear the family part," she said. "Come home now. Before they make it impossible. Before the name stops protecting anything. Before Lor'themar spends the last of himself for a road you refuse to leave."

Sylvanas looked past her, north, to the bruise in the sky where Stratholme still burned.

The wall came back. The districts cut and finished one by one. Arthas walking out beneath all that smoke with washed hands. The day Sylvanas had aimed him at one crisis because it was useful and watched him become this on the road after. Jaina's model on the crate table. Relay reports. Ships on the northern coast. The old lie had nowhere left to stand except in speeches like this one, where home pretended geography could save it.

When she spoke, she did not raise her voice.

"The threat to Quel'Thalas is wherever Arthas goes next."

The sentence crossed the camp clean as an arrow.

No one pretended not to hear it.

Vereesa went very still. The document remained unfolded in her hand, untouched by Sylvanas's.

"So that's it," she said.

"That's the truth."

"The truth," Vereesa repeated, and the word carried all the things the chamber had called recklessness for months. "And if the truth leaves home behind?"

"Then home had better learn to ride farther."

For a breath Sylvanas thought Vereesa might slap her.

Not because she was childish. Because she was a Windrunner, because the anger had nowhere else to go, because the sister in her had finally outrun the captain and found a hand instead of a sentence.

She did not.

She folded the recall with slow, exact care and held it at her side like something that had failed both of them.

"I hate you when you do this," Vereesa said softly.

Sylvanas almost answered with a joke. Something dry enough to keep the blood out of it. The habit came up and died before it reached her mouth.

"I know."

Vereesa looked down once, hard, then back up. Whatever she had hoped to hear from that answer, it had not been there.

Good. Better a clean wound.

At the edge of the camp, amber light touched Jaina's hands.

Sylvanas turned before she meant to. The mage had moved away during the worst of it, not far, only to the line where the firelight fell thin and the first trees started. The sending-stone sat in her palm with a low, warm pulse, gold at the core and honey-pale around it. Not blue as Antonidas's line had been on the road south from Stratholme. Different angle. Different use. Jaina had called this time.

Good.

Better that way.

She stood with her spine straight enough to look nailed there. One hand held the stone. The other had gone still at her side, fingers bent around nothing. From here Sylvanas could not hear a word of the exchange. Wind, horses, the rustle of leather, Cyndia moving the escort toward the fire — all of that reached her first. The stone did not.

It had to stay that way.

No interior borrowed. No stolen line crossing the camp because she wanted it.

So she watched what the body gave her.

Jaina did not pace. Did not hunch around the light to shelter it. She stood in the open and spoke into it with the rigid economy of someone delivering news that had already gone past discussion. Once the fingers of her empty hand flexed against her skirt. Once her chin lifted by a degree, not defiance exactly, more the motion of a woman choosing the shape of a sentence before it left her mouth. The stone's glow flared brighter, tightened to a harder amber. Jaina's shoulders set against that. Then the light thinned. Held. Thinned again.

Antonidas pressing. Jaina refusing.

That was all Sylvanas could know. It was enough.

Vereesa followed her gaze and read the scene for what it was in one glance. Some of the fury left her face. Weariness took its place.

"She isn't coming back either," Vereesa said.

"No."

The word sat between them without comfort in it.

Vereesa looked toward the camp edge for another moment, toward the amber pulse in Jaina's hand and the rigid line of her shoulders. Then she gave one short nod. Verdict accepted, whether she wanted it or not.

"You've found each other in the wrong place," she said.

Sylvanas let that pass. Agreement would have done too much. Denial would have been a lie.

The stone darkened.

Not gradually. It went from warm amber to dead crystal in a breath. Jaina stood looking at it a moment longer, measuring the absence. Then she slid it into her coat pocket instead of the easy reach of her belt. Tool stored. Line closed. No expectation of using it again soon.

When she turned back toward the camp, there was nothing on her face Sylvanas could name cleanly from this distance. Not relief. Not grief. Only the afterlook of something cut through rather than soothed.

That tightened Vereesa's mouth.

"I should go before the light fails entirely," she said.

There it was. No repair. No miraculous family understanding. Only the work of leaving.

Cyndia brought the gray mare up already watered, coat darkened where the cloth had gone over the lather. Efficient as ever. One of Vereesa's escort had found his strength enough to retie the bedroll. Another held the bridle and looked carefully at nothing.

Vereesa slid the folded recall into her saddlebag. Not tucked away in a pouch. Not hidden. Visible when the flap shifted, red wax catching what remained of the day. She set a hand on the saddle, then stopped.

"If they ask whether you took it," she said without looking at Sylvanas, "what answer do you want me to carry?"

"The true one."

Now she did look.

"Which is?"

Sylvanas gave her the only mercy left in the scene.

"That you delivered it."

Vereesa's throat moved once. The mercy hurt more than the refusal had.

"And that you refused."

"Yes."

She mounted cleanly this time. Pain masked under practice, but there if you knew the signs. The escort riders swung up after her. Cyndia stepped back with two fingers still on the gray mare's rein until the horse settled under the new weight, then let go.

For a moment the younger sister sat above them with the camp behind and the northern road at her back, all the things she had ridden here to save already lost.

"I will tell him," Vereesa said.

Lor'themar, not the chamber. Good.

"Tell him to stop spending himself on men who count walls and not what climbs them."

The old dry answer would have been easier. Vereesa had no patience for easier anymore.

"I will tell him you said that," she said. "He'll ignore the order and hear the rest."

That, at least, was Lor'themar enough.

Then Vereesa looked once past Sylvanas to Jaina, once to Cyndia, once to the map case, the horses, the little camp that had already begun to reshape itself around a road north none of them had formally named.

When she spoke again the formality was back, but only on the surface.

"Safe road, Ranger-General."

Not the old farewell. Not the stranger's cursed one from the Eastweald roads Cyndia knew too well. A real Farstrider leave-taking, narrowed by anger and blood.

"Ride hard, Captain."

Vereesa bared her teeth at that. It might once have become a smile. Not now.

She turned the gray mare toward the east-cut relay road, the one that would carry her back toward Silvermoon.

The thing Sylvanas had expected, or feared, or some colder word between the two, did not happen. Vereesa did not look back. She took the horse into a steady canter at once, speed the only dignity left her, and the escort followed in a spray of dry dust and cooling light. For a few breaths the sound of hooves stayed loud. Then it went thin. Then it was only dust hanging over the road where the family had been.

The camp shrank at once.

Jaina came back from the trees while the dust was still in the air. She had the map case again under one arm; at some point Cyndia must have passed it back to her. She did not ask what Vereesa had said after the refusal. She did not offer what Antonidas had said into the stone. Good.

Parallel cuts. No need to compare whose bled worse.

Only one question mattered now.

Sylvanas held out a hand for the map case.

Jaina gave it over. "The relay line dies at the harbors," she said. Her voice was rougher than it had been an hour ago and stripped of every useless edge. "After that we borrow passage or we don't go."

"We go."

Jaina's gaze met hers for one brief, level beat.

"Yes," she said.

Nothing softened in the word. Nothing needed to.

Sylvanas turned toward the camp.

"Strike it."

Cyndia moved before the second syllable died. Fire kicked apart and dirted over. Cook gear packed wet. Bedrolls cinched. The escort horses that were staying with them shifted from feed to tack with the quiet, drilled obedience of Silvermoon-bred road stock. Leather snugged. Buckles lay flat. No jangling iron anywhere. Farstrider work, even here, even after all of this.

Jaina bent to gather the loose pages she had not yet stowed. One tried to escape under a crate in the wind and she caught it against the toe of her boot before it went. Smoke made another pass through camp, thinner now, and beneath it the colder trace from the north touched clearer than before.

Salt.

Still far. Real now.

Sylvanas checked her own horse's girth once, found the animal bunch under her hand, and swung into the saddle. The mare came up beneath her with the easy lift of a horse bred for long patrol miles instead of theater. Fine-boned. High-headed. Steady when the world made no sense. Cyndia mounted a breath later on the left. Jaina on the near rear after that, a little slower through the saddle soreness but with no wasted motion, already falling into the space the formation had made for her long ago and only now chosen aloud.

The last of Vereesa's dust had not yet settled.

"North," Sylvanas said.

One word.

That was enough.

The horses turned onto the north road to the coast without hesitation. Cyndia kicked clear of the fire-scar first to take the left lane. Jaina's mare came into Sylvanas's left-rear quarter without needing direction, exactly where the line ran strongest if trouble broke from the roadside. The escort slid behind them in a narrow file. Camp became absence in less than a minute: stamped earth, a little crushed grass, one cold patch of fire and then nothing at all.

They rode.

At first the road gave them only what it had been giving for days. Packed earth, wheel-ruts hardened and broken again, low hedges clawing at boot leather where the path narrowed. The body accepted the old punishments at once. Saddle rub waking in the inner thigh. Knees remembering yesterday before today's miles had fully begun. Smoke in cloth gone cool now that it was no longer backed by a burning city's heat.

Then the country started to change.

The farms thinned. Stone showed through the soil in wider shelves. The road's comfortable breadth narrowed by degrees into the kind of track merchants still used because trade made them, not because any lord cared enough to keep them easy. Wind found them more often. When it came off the north-east it carried salt before it carried sea, a raw mineral edge that dried the tongue and put a colder line under the evening air. The horses took that shift too. Ears working. Breath lifting in pale threads when the sun lowered.

Cyndia rode half a length out, reading the roadside and the tree line with the old relentless sweep. Right. Right again. Forward-left. The pattern barely showed unless one lived by such things. Sylvanas read it anyway and said nothing.

Jaina rode quieter than usual. Not vacant. Working. Every few minutes her face altered by some tiny amount, the way it did when she had a line no one else could hear. Once her hand went to the front of her coat, checking the sending-stone's place without drawing it. Once she closed her eyes for three breaths and opened them with the hard look she got when a theorem chose her least convenient moment to sharpen.

"Stronger?" Sylvanas asked, not turning.

"Yes."

No need for more. Jaina gave it anyway after a short silence.

"Not cleaner. Just louder. Like getting closer to a horn under water."

Sylvanas filed the image for what it told her operationally and left the rest alone.

"Can you follow it on the coast?"

"If the weather doesn't turn ugly and if the water doesn't distort it beyond use." Jaina shifted in the saddle, wincing once when the mare took a rut badly. "I'm less worried about the signal than the ship."

"Good."

Jaina glanced at her from the rear quarter. "Good?"

"Worrying about the ship means you trust the road."

That earned the smallest sound from her. Not laughter. Close enough that the difference did not matter.

The first relay post they passed after dusk was little more than a stone rise above the road and a bronze dish dark against the paling sky. One ranger on duty. Two spare horses. Feed under canvas. The northern edge of usable infrastructure looked exactly like every system did when it started to fail: not dramatic, only thinner than it ought to have been.

Sylvanas drew rein long enough to take the rider's report.

Two harbor stations still answering. One had sent south for additional oats. No word yet of available ships larger than fishing craft. The coast road passable though soft in places near the marsh. No sign of Alliance troops this far north; Arthas had stripped the larger routes bare on his way to the sea.

Useful. Not comforting.

She handed the rider on with a spare dispatch for the next post and moved them out again before the horses cooled too much.

Night came on in layers. First the red left the clouds. Then the road lost its color and became touch more than sight, the horses reading with their feet where the riders could not. Last of all the north-east horizon changed from dark land to something flatter and colder than land had any right to be.

Open water ahead.

Not visible yet. Certain anyway.

The cold worked deeper as they rode into it. It reached the backs of Sylvanas's hands first, where the reins crossed the gloves. Then the jaw hinge, where clenching against the wind turned into ache. The horses started blowing harder through the nose, not from panic, only from the labor of moving warm breath into rougher air. Sea country. Harder country. The kind that stripped everything down to what function it still had left.

Behind them Stratholme kept its place in the sky as long as the dark allowed. Not the city now. Only the stain where it had been.

Ahead, nothing waited except coast, failing relays, borrowed passage, and the prince they had not stopped in time.

Good.

At least it was the right nothing.
