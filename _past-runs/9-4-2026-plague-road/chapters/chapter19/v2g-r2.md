# Chapter 19: The Decision

Stratholme still stained the northern sky.

From a mile south, with a ridge and stripped birch between camp and city, the smoke should have looked smaller. It had flattened into one long bruise, brown at the base, black above, and every turn of the wind still brought char and wet ash over the trees. The heat was gone from it now. Only the residue remained, cold in the lungs and stubborn in cloth.

Sylvanas stood at the picket line and took stock.

Three Farstrider mares waited under the low trees, fine-boned and long-backed, breastcollars dark with old sweat, tack muted for road work so nothing rang when a hand did not want it to. Yesterday's mud had dried pale on their pasterns and cracked away where Cyndia had brushed them down in haste. She had done what time allowed: checked throatlatches, reset bedroll ties, hung feed clear of tangles, wrapped the spare bit rings in leather, laid arrows out by head instead of letting travel turn them into a heap. A cookfire no bigger than a helmet. Waterskins in shade. Map case on supply crates. Spare reins coiled where a ranger could grab them in the dark and not curse for it.

Jaina had come out with papers in both hands before she touched the food beside the fire.

Smoke clung to her sleeves. The braid she'd redone that morning was already loosening at the nape from handling notes and pushing hair back while she thought. The vacancy from the wall was gone. She held the pages flat with one stained hand and tried to keep the answer still long enough to name it.

Sylvanas stepped to the crate table. Jaina had annotated trader roads in iron gall, relay points in blue, contamination sites in a thin red hand until half Lordaeron looked cut open with a knife. One corner still wore a chalk smear where Jaina had worried the broadcaster model over the page the night before.

Camp behind them. Smoke ahead. The map between.

"Show me," Sylvanas said.

"Here." Jaina set one page over the southern routes and pinned it with the heel of her hand against a gust trying to lift it. "If we keep riding village to village, we'll sharpen the count. Which bakeries got the worst flour first. Which roads the worst flour reached fastest. Which places sickened fastest."

Her finger moved north.

"The part that matters lies north."

Sylvanas followed the line. Andorhal. Hearthglen. Stratholme. Then farther, where the kingdom's roads thinned toward rough coast and the smaller port marks that mattered more now than any city seal.

"The grain delivered it," Jaina said. She gave it to Sylvanas plain now, no softening, no needless translation. "It didn't complete it. Stratholme proved that. The strongest contamination in the stock was already burning out when we left, and the deeper pattern was still there. Stronger, if anything. Grain carries. Bodies keep. Something else wakes them."

"And down here?"

"Dead work." Jaina looked at the red marks through the southern roads. "Useful if our only goal is proving what we already know to people determined not to move. Not useful if the goal is finding who built this to survive its own carriers."

Jaina's thumb dragged a fresh red crescent across the southern roads. Antonidas and Lor'themar would both want one more proof to carry back into rooms already closing.

Sylvanas looked past the camp to the horses under the trees. Her thumb found the map case strap again. Road out. Dead counted. One cleaner dispatch home. The old border job still offered itself in pieces she could carry. North still sat on the page.

"Arthas," she said.

Jaina's mouth tightened. "North."

"Your model."

"North."

"The last useful relays?"

"As far as the coast, yes." Jaina shifted the page against the crate lip and flattened the map case strap where it tried to curl over a harbor mark. "After that the signal line ends and we're working from whatever the harbors still have."

Sylvanas bent over the map. Ridge lines first. Open stretches. Wagon-grade switchbacks that would slow them if rain got there before they did. The ground that would ruin a tired horse if pushed at the wrong hour. Then the rest: port towns, supply roads, the places a prince with too much certainty and not enough patience would strip for ships.

"How much do we gain by hunting one more granary south?"

Jaina let the fire crack twice before she answered. "Paper. Better arguments for men who'll still arrive after the dead."

"And what do we lose?"

"Sea lead. If he's already sailed, every day we spend proving the same thing gives him another day on the water and another inland after that."

Cyndia crossed the far side of camp with a feed scoop and kept her eyes off them. Courtesy. Also discipline. A ranger who knew when the answer on the crate table mattered more than whatever sat in the bowl by the fire.

The wind shifted again. Smoke rolled low through the birches, and under it came a thinner edge, colder, mineral, not yet sea and no longer only ash.

"Pack the map," Sylvanas said.

Jaina went still. "You're certain."

"No." Sylvanas folded the canvas flap over the annotated roads herself and cinched the strap. "I'm done pretending certainty is required."

Something eased around Jaina's mouth.

"That," she said, gathering her pages at last, "is a better standard than most councils manage."

Sylvanas took the map case and turned toward the tent.

"Eat while you can, Jaina."

"You say that," Jaina said behind her, dry enough to survive the smoke, "as though the coast road is known for pauses."

"It isn't."

Behind her came the rustle of papers squared into order, then the faint scrape of Jaina finally reaching for the bowl Cyndia had left. Work first. Food second. Better than the wall.

The bird-call came out of the trees before Sylvanas reached the tent.

Three short notes. Friendly.

Cyndia's hand went up for stillness before the answer came back from farther down the road. One of the mares lifted its head hard enough to tug the line, then settled when Cyndia made a low sound in her throat and laid two fingers against the cheekpiece.

Friendly, then.

Urgent anyway.

Sylvanas turned toward the south approach. The riders broke from the birches at a run.

Home trouble. No spread for arrow fire, no one hanging back to read the camp, no uncertainty about whether the edge post would admit them. The lead horse was lathered through the chest, gray coat dark under foam, and the rider sat the saddle with the compact stiffness of someone who had paid for the speed and meant to hide it. Two shafts were missing from the quiver. Not combat. Road spent.

Cyndia crossed the yard and signaled the camp edge post. The escort behind the lead rider reined down at once.

The lead horse came on another six lengths before Sylvanas had the face.

Vereesa Windrunner, dust to the knees, one glove gone, braid coming apart, the dispatch tube knocking her hip and her mouth already braced around the title she had come to use.

Of course they had.

She swung down before the mare had fully stopped. The landing nearly cost her; the right knee locked, then she walked it out with one hard breath through the nose. Ink marked two fingers of her left hand. Good vellum rode in the dispatch tube at her hip.

"Ranger-General," Vereesa said.

The formal title arrived before the sister did.

Sylvanas handed the map case to Jaina without looking at her. "Water for the horse."

Cyndia was already moving. One glance took in blown flanks, half-spent tack, riders who needed to sit down before they remembered to ask. She loosened the gray mare's throatlatch on the way in, all business, no theater.

Vereesa's eyes flicked over the camp: notes, mage, horses, the working shape of a mission the councillors had sent her south to interrupt before it hardened.

Then she pulled the vellum from the tube.

Red wax. Council seal. Better paper than a road dispatch usually got. The thing even smelled wrong for a field camp: resin, wax, the dry expensive finish of a chamber desk.

She held it out. Sylvanas did not take it.

Vereesa's jaw set. Her arm stayed level.

"By order of the Convocation of Silvermoon," she said, and the words were chamber words now, not hers, "the Ranger-General is recalled from all operations in foreign territory. Your authority to deploy Farstrider assets beyond the borders of Quel'Thalas is suspended effective immediately. Failure to comply will constitute desertion under the Articles of Military Service."

The camp went quiet.

Leather at the picket line. Cyndia easing the gray mare's bit free one buckle at a time. One of the escort swallowing too hard to hide it. Jaina at Sylvanas's right shoulder with enough sense not to come nearer, the map case braced upright against her leg, its leather edge tapping her boot whenever the wind caught it.

Sylvanas looked at the seal, at Vereesa's fingers holding steady while yesterday's ride still ached in her joints.

"That's the chamber voice," she said. "Do you have your own?"

Vereesa's shoulders dropped a fraction.

Some of the formality left her face.

"Noncompliance means exile by military law," she said, lower now. "Desertion. They want it clean."

"They always do."

Vereesa let the document hang a moment longer, then lowered it without folding it. The wax seal turned red as fresh blood in the firelight.

"Lor'themar fought them," she said.

Sylvanas said nothing.

"He spoke for an hour." Vereesa's voice had gone fast, feeling pushing through control. "No wit left in it. He read figures. Contamination rates, relay counts, Eastweald dead, how fast it would move north if it kept going. Just numbers. Names. Dates. Grain totals."

She looked away toward Cyndia rubbing the mare down with a scrap of cloth.

"He sounded finished."

"What did he buy?" Sylvanas asked.

"A token mobilization." Contempt sharpened the words. "Small. Relay support to the northern harbors if anyone still obeys by the time we reach them. A little horse. A little feed. Enough that they can claim they acted."

"At cost."

"At all of it." Now she faced Sylvanas directly, furious and too tired to hide it. "He had nothing left by the end except records. Vote counts. Favors owed. Who abstained because they were afraid to be seen choosing. He's only spending."

Cyndia had gone still at the horse line. Listening anyway. Good.

Sylvanas stepped closer. Enough that Vereesa had to tilt her head back.

"Then he should stop."

Vereesa laughed once, sharp and unbelieving. "You know he won't."

No. He would not.

Vereesa's vellum clicked against Sylvanas's vambrace. Sylvanas's hand tightened on the bracer seam until leather bit her palm. Casualty lists, vote counts, exile articles—same cowardice in cleaner clothes.

"Come home," Vereesa said.

Everyone in camp went still around the sentence.

"Vereesa—"

"No." She stepped in before Sylvanas could turn it back into rank. The vellum knocked once against Sylvanas's vambrace and hung there between them. "Don't answer me as if I'm one of your captains. Alleria is gone. Lirath is dead. If they brand you deserter and seal the order behind you, you do not come back from that. Not to the city. Not to us."

She held the last word steady.

"The border will still be there when I return," Sylvanas said.

"That misses it."

"It's exactly the point."

Vereesa made an angry sound. "Lady Sun. The family is not a relay post you leave manned while you ride after the next fire."

The old Thalassian name hit harder than the document had.

"Interesting," Sylvanas said. "Is that what you rode two days to call it?"

"Don't be cruel."

"Don't ask for dishonesty."

Vereesa stared at her. The vellum crackled once in her grip.

"You think this is only politics."

"No." Sylvanas let the smoke, the horses, the map case under Jaina's arm stand behind the words. "I think they put the seal in your hand because they knew whose voice would cut deepest."

Vereesa flinched, just once.

"Then hear the family part," she said. "Come home now. Before they make it impossible. Before the name stops protecting anything. Before Lor'themar spends the last of himself for a road you refuse to leave."

Sylvanas looked north, to the bruise in the sky where Stratholme still burned.

The wall came back. Districts cut and finished one by one. Arthas walking out beneath that smoke with washed hands. Jaina's red road marks on the crate. Harbor signs on the northern coast. Vereesa's recall still warm from her hand. Men at home still counting wards and shorelines against a prince who had already burned one kingdom behind him.

When she spoke, she did not raise her voice.

"The threat to Quel'Thalas is wherever Arthas goes next."

Her words cut across the camp clean as an arrow.

Vereesa went still. The document remained unfolded in her hand.

"So that's it," she said.

"That's the truth."

"And if following the truth leaves home behind?"

"Then home lies farther north."

Vereesa might slap her. Windrunner anger, outrunning sentence and finding a hand.

She did not.

She folded the recall with slow care and held it at her side like something that had failed both of them.

"I hate you when you do this," Vereesa said softly.

Sylvanas almost answered with something dry enough to keep the blood out of it. She stopped the dry answer before she spoke it.

"I know."

Vereesa dipped her chin once, hard, then raised it again. Whatever she'd wanted from that answer, it was not there.

Amber light touched Jaina's hands at the edge of camp.

Sylvanas turned before she meant to. Jaina had moved only as far as the first birches beyond the fire line, where the camp thinned into tree shadow and the road out could still be watched without hearing the call. The sending-stone sat in her palm with a low warm pulse, gold at the core and honey-pale around it. A different line from Antonidas's colder blue on the road south of Stratholme. Jaina had opened this one.

Good.

Better that way.

She stood so still the stone-light only moved where her pulse touched her wrist. One hand held the stone. The other hung still at her side, fingers curled around nothing. From here Sylvanas heard only wind, horses, leather, Cyndia moving the escort toward the fire. She learned nothing from it.

It had to stay that way.

So she watched Jaina's posture for clues.

Jaina stood in the open and spoke into the light with the rigid economy of someone delivering news already past discussion. Once the fingers of her free hand flexed against her skirt. Once her chin lifted before she spoke. The stone flared harder amber and lit the seam of her cuff, the edge of her jaw, the pale knuckles around the crystal. Jaina's shoulders set against it. Then the light thinned. Held. Thinned again.

Antonidas pressing. Jaina refusing.

That was all Sylvanas could know.

Vereesa followed her gaze and read the scene in a glance. Some of the fury left her face. Weariness took its place.

"She isn't coming back either," Vereesa said.

"No."

Vereesa watched Jaina another moment, then gave one short nod.

"You've found each other in the wrong place," she said.

Sylvanas let that pass.

The stone darkened from warm amber to dead crystal in a breath. Jaina looked at it a moment longer, then slid it deep into her coat pocket, closer to her ribs than to her hand.

When she turned back, there was nothing on her face Sylvanas could name from this distance. Only a mouth gone thin and a jaw set too hard.

That tightened Vereesa's mouth.

"I should go before the light fails entirely," she said.

Cyndia brought the gray mare up already watered, coat dark where the cloth had gone over the lather. One escort rider had found strength enough to retie the bedroll. Another held the bridle and fixed on nothing.

Vereesa slid the folded recall into her saddlebag, wax still visible when the flap shifted. She set a hand on the saddle, then stopped.

"If they ask whether you took it," she said without looking at Sylvanas, "what answer do you want me to carry?"

"The true one."

Now she did look. "Which is?"

Sylvanas gave her the only mercy left in the scene.

"That you delivered it."

Vereesa's throat moved once.

"And that you refused."

"Yes."

She mounted cleanly this time. Cyndia kept two fingers on the rein until the mare settled under the new weight, then let go.

The younger sister sat above them with the camp behind and the relay road east at her back.

"I will tell him," Vereesa said.

Lor'themar, not the chamber. Good.

"Tell him to stop spending himself on men who count walls and not what climbs them."

"I will tell him you said that. He'll ignore the order and hear the rest."

That, at least, was Lor'themar enough.

Then Vereesa looked once to Jaina, once to Cyndia, once to the map case, the horses, the little camp already reshaping itself around a road north none of them had formally named.

When she spoke again the formality was back, but only on the surface.

"Safe road, Ranger-General."

"Ride hard, Captain."

Vereesa bared her teeth at that. It might once have become a smile.

She turned the gray mare toward the relay road east.

The thing Sylvanas had expected did not happen. Vereesa did not look back. She took the horse into a steady canter at once, speed the only dignity left her, and the escort followed in dry dust and cooling light. For a few breaths the hooves stayed loud over the packed road. Then they thinned. Then it was only dust hanging above the ditch grass where the family had been.

Jaina came back from the trees while the dust still hung in the air. She had the map case under one arm. She did not ask what Vereesa had said after the refusal. She did not offer what Antonidas had said into the stone.

Sylvanas held out a hand for the case.

Jaina gave it over. "The relay line ends at the harbors," she said. Her voice was rougher than an hour ago and stripped of every useless edge. "After that we borrow passage or we don't go."

"We go."

Jaina held still for one brief level beat. "Yes."

Nothing softened in the word.

Sylvanas turned toward the camp.

"Strike it."

Cyndia moved before the second syllable died. Fire kicked apart and dirted over. Cook gear packed wet. Bedrolls cinched. Feed sacks redistributed so the staying horses carried weight balanced for distance, not parade. The mares that were staying with them shifted from nosebags to tack with the quiet drilled obedience of Silvermoon road stock. No jangling iron anywhere.

Jaina bent to gather the loose pages she had not yet stowed. One slid under a crate and she caught it against her boot. Smoke passed through camp again, thinner now, and beneath it the colder trace from the north sharpened.

Salt.

Still far. Real now.

Sylvanas checked her horse's girth and swung into the saddle. The mare came up beneath her with the easy lift of long-patrol breeding. Cyndia mounted a breath later on the left. Jaina after that, slower through saddle soreness but with no wasted motion, heel set and reins gathered as her mare folded straight into Sylvanas's left rear quarter before the word had fully cleared the camp.

The last of Vereesa's dust had not yet settled.

"North," Sylvanas said.

The horses turned onto the coast road without hesitation. Cyndia took the left lane. Jaina's mare came into Sylvanas's left rear quarter without needing direction, exactly where the line ran strongest if trouble broke from the roadside. The escort slid behind them in narrow file. Camp became absence in less than a minute: stamped earth, crushed grass, one cold patch of fire, then nothing.

They rode.

At first they rode through the same packed earth, broken ruts, and low hedges clawing at boot leather where the path narrowed. Saddle rub waking in the inner thigh. Yesterday still ached in her knees before today's miles had fully begun. Smoke in cloth gone cool now that it no longer had the city's heat behind it.

The farms thinned. Stone showed through the soil in wider shelves. The road narrowed into the kind of track merchants still used because merchants had worn it in, not because any lord cared to keep it easy. Wind found them more often. When it came off the north-east it carried salt before it carried sea, a raw mineral edge that dried the tongue and put cold under the evening air. The horses took the shift too. Ears working. Breath lifting pale when the sun lowered.

Cyndia rode half a length out, reading roadside and tree line with the old relentless sweep. Right. Right again. Forward-left. The pattern barely showed unless one lived by such things. Sylvanas read it anyway and said nothing.

Jaina rode quieter than usual. Still working. Every few minutes her face changed by some small amount, the way it did when she had a line no one else could hear. Once her hand went to the front of her coat, checking the sending-stone without drawing it. Once she closed her eyes for three breaths and opened them with the hard look she wore when she reached an answer at the worst possible time to do it.

"Stronger?" Sylvanas asked, not turning.

"Yes."

Jaina shifted in the saddle when the mare found a rut and let the breath out through her teeth before adding, "Just louder. The back of my teeth won't stop humming, and it gets worse every mile north."

Sylvanas kept the image for what it told her operationally and left the rest alone.

"Can you follow it on the coast?"

"If the weather holds and the water doesn't twist it beyond use." Jaina settled the mare with one hand on the rein and one brief touch at the neck. "I'm less worried about the signal than the ship."

"Good."

Jaina glanced at her from the rear quarter. "Good?"

"Worrying about the ship means you trust the road."

That earned the smallest sound from her, close enough to laughter.

The first relay post they passed after dusk was little more than a stone rise above the road and a bronze dish dark against the paling sky, its face tilted north to catch what remained of the line. One ranger on duty. Two spare horses under canvas. Oats stacked in half the quantity the station should have held this late in the year. The northern edge of the relay network was thinner than it ought to have been.

Sylvanas drew rein long enough to take the report.

Two harbor stations still answering. One had sent south for more oats. No word of ships larger than fishing craft. The coast road passable though soft in places near the marsh. No sign of Alliance troops this far north; Arthas had stripped the larger routes bare on his way to the sea.

Useful. Not comforting.

She left a spare dispatch with the station ranger, took fresh distance estimates, and moved them out again before the horses cooled too much.

Night came on in layers. First the red left the clouds. Then the road lost its color and became touch more than sight, the horses reading with their feet where the riders could not. Last of all the north-east horizon changed from dark land to something flatter and colder than land had any right to be.

Open water ahead.

Certain anyway.

The cold worked deeper as they rode into it. It reached the backs of Sylvanas's hands first where the reins crossed the gloves, then the jaw hinge where clenching against the wind became ache. The horses blew harder through the nose, not from panic, only from the labor of moving warm breath into rougher air. Sea country. Harder country.

Behind them Stratholme's stain stayed in the sky as long as the dark allowed. Only the stain where it had been.

Ahead waited coast, failing relays, borrowed passage, and the prince they had not stopped in time.

Good.
