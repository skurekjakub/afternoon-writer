# Chapter 19: The Decision

Stratholme still stained the northern sky.

From a mile south, with a ridge and stripped birch between camp and city, the smoke should have been smaller. It had flattened into one long bruise, brown at the base, black above, and each turn of the wind brought char and wet ash over the trees. The heat had gone. The residue had not.

Sylvanas took stock at the picket line.

Three Farstrider mares waited under the low trees, tack wrapped for road silence, yesterday's mud dried pale on their pasterns. Cyndia had done what haste allowed: checked throatlatches, reset bedroll ties, wrapped the spare bit rings in leather, sorted arrows by head instead of leaving them to rattle together. Small fire. Waterskins in shade. Map case on supply crates. Spare reins where a ranger could find them in the dark.

Jaina stood at the crate table with papers in both hands. Smoke clung to her sleeves. The braid she'd redone that morning was already loosening at the nape. The vacancy from the wall was gone. She held the pages flat and tried to pin the answer long enough to name it.

"Show me," Sylvanas said.

Jaina laid one page over the southern routes and trapped it under her palm. "Village to village sharpens the count. Which bakeries got the worst flour first. Which roads sickened fastest." Her finger moved north. "The part that matters lies here."

Sylvanas followed the line past Andorhal and Hearthglen to Stratholme, then farther, where the roads thinned toward the smaller northern harbors.

"The grain delivered it," Jaina said. "It didn't finish it. Stratholme proved that. The stock there was already burning out when we left, and the deeper pattern held. Stronger, if anything. Grain carries. Bodies keep. Another hand wakes them."

"And the south?"

"Dead work. Useful if all we want is better proof for men determined not to move. Not useful if we mean to find who built this to survive its own carriers."

Her thumb dragged a red crescent across the southern roads. Antonidas and Lor'themar would both want one more proof to carry back into rooms already closing.

Sylvanas touched the map case strap again. Road out. Dead counted. Cleaner dispatch home. The old border job still offered itself in pieces she could carry. North sat on the page.

"Arthas."

"North."

"The last useful relays?"

"As far as the coast." Jaina flattened the curling edge of the page. "After that we work from whatever the harbors still have."

Sylvanas bent over the map. Ridge lines. Open ground. Wagon tracks that would bog if rain reached them first. Harbors. Supply roads. The places a prince with too much certainty and not enough patience would strip for ships.

"How much do we gain by one more granary south?"

Jaina let the fire crack twice before answering. "Paper. Better arguments for men who'll arrive after the dead."

"And what do we lose?"

"Sea lead. If he's already sailed, every day we spend proving the same thing gives him another day on the water and another inland after that."

Wind moved through the birches. Under the ash lay a thinner edge now, colder, mineral, not yet sea and no longer only smoke.

"Pack the map," Sylvanas said.

Jaina stopped. "You're certain."

"No." Sylvanas folded the canvas flap over the marked roads and cinched the strap. "I'm done pretending certainty is required."

The line of Jaina's mouth eased.

"That," she said, gathering her pages at last, "is a better standard than most councils manage."

Sylvanas turned toward the tent. "Eat while you can, Jaina."

"As though the coast road is known for pauses."

"It isn't."

Behind her came the rustle of papers squared into order, then the scrape of a bowl finally lifted from the ground.

The bird-call came out of the trees before Sylvanas reached the tent.

Three short notes. Friendly.

Cyndia's hand went up for stillness before the answer came back from farther down the road. One of the mares jerked at the line, then settled under two quiet fingers at the cheekpiece.

Friendly, then.

Urgent anyway.

Sylvanas turned toward the south approach as the riders broke from the birches at a run.

Home trouble. No spread for arrow fire, no one hanging back to read the camp. The lead horse was lathered through the chest, gray coat dark under foam, and the rider sat the saddle with the compact stiffness of someone who had paid for the speed and meant to hide it.

The face resolved six lengths out.

Vereesa Windrunner, dust to the knees, one glove gone, braid coming apart, dispatch tube knocking at her hip and her mouth already braced around the title she had come to use.

Of course they had.

She swung down before the mare had fully stopped. The landing nearly cost her; the right knee locked, then she walked it out with one hard breath through the nose. Ink stained two fingers of her left hand. Good vellum rode in the tube at her hip.

"Ranger-General," Vereesa said.

The title came out first, hard and formal, before the rest of her face caught up.

Sylvanas handed the map case to Jaina without looking at her. "Water for the horse."

Cyndia was already moving. One sweep took in blown flanks, half-spent tack, riders who needed to sit before they remembered to ask. She loosened the gray mare's throatlatch on the way in, all business.

Vereesa took in the camp: notes, mage, horses, the shape of a mission the councillors had sent her south to interrupt before it hardened.

Then she drew the vellum from the tube.

Red wax. Council seal. Better paper than a road dispatch deserved. It even smelled wrong for a field camp: resin, wax, the dry expensive finish of a chamber desk.

She held it out. Sylvanas did not take it.

Vereesa's jaw set. Her arm stayed level.

"By order of the Convocation of Silvermoon," she said, and now the chamber voice had her completely, "the Ranger-General is recalled from all operations in foreign territory. Your authority to deploy Farstrider assets beyond the borders of Quel'Thalas is suspended effective immediately. Failure to comply will constitute desertion under the Articles of Military Service."

The camp went quiet.

Leather at the picket line. Cyndia easing the gray mare's bit free one buckle at a time. Jaina at Sylvanas's right shoulder with enough sense not to step closer.

Sylvanas studied the seal, then Vereesa's fingers holding steady while yesterday's ride still lived in her joints.

"That's the chamber voice," she said. "Do you have your own?"

Vereesa's shoulders dropped a fraction.

"Noncompliance means exile by military law," she said, lower now. "Desertion. They want it clean."

"They always do."

Vereesa lowered the document without folding it. The wax turned red as fresh blood in the firelight.

"Lor'themar fought them," she said.

Sylvanas said nothing.

"He spoke for an hour. No wit left in it. Just numbers. Names. Dates. Grain totals." Her mouth tightened. "He sounded finished."

"What did he buy?"

"A token mobilization. Relay support to the northern harbors if anyone still obeys by the time we reach them. A little horse. A little feed. Enough that they can claim they acted."

"At cost."

"At all of it." Now she faced Sylvanas directly, furious and too tired to hide it. "He had nothing left by the end except records. Vote counts. Favors owed. Who abstained because they were afraid to be seen choosing. He's only spending."

Cyndia had gone still at the horse line. Listening anyway. Good.

Sylvanas stepped closer. Enough that Vereesa had to tip her head back.

"Then he should stop."

Vereesa laughed once, sharp and unbelieving. "You know he won't."

No. He would not.

"Come home," Vereesa said.

Everyone in camp went still around the words.

"Vereesa—"

"No." She stepped in before Sylvanas could turn it back into rank. The vellum knocked once against Sylvanas's vambrace and hung there between them. "Don't answer me like I'm one of your captains. Alleria is gone. Lirath is dead. If they brand you deserter and seal the order behind you, you do not come back from that. Not to the city. Not to us."

She held the last word steady.

"The border will still be there when I return," Sylvanas said.

"That misses it."

"It's the point."

Vereesa made an angry sound. "Lady Sun. The family is not a relay post you leave manned while you ride after the next fire."

The old Thalassian name hit harder than the recall had.

"Interesting," Sylvanas said. "Is that what you rode two days to call it?"

"Don't be cruel."

"Don't ask for dishonesty."

Vereesa stared at her. The vellum crackled once.

"You think this is only politics."

"No." Sylvanas let the smoke, the horses, the map case under Jaina's arm stand behind the words. "I think they put the seal in your hand because they knew whose voice would cut deepest."

Vereesa flinched, just once.

"Then hear the family part," she said. "Come home now. Before they make it impossible. Before the name stops protecting anything. Before Lor'themar spends the last of himself for a road you refuse to leave."

Sylvanas turned north, to the bruise in the sky where Stratholme still burned.

The wall came back. Districts cut and finished one by one. Arthas walking out beneath that smoke with washed hands. Jaina's red road marks on the crate. Harbor signs on the northern coast. Men at home still counting wards and shorelines against a prince who had already burned one kingdom behind him.

When she spoke, she did not raise her voice.

"The threat to Quel'Thalas is wherever Arthas goes next."

Her words crossed the camp clean as an arrow.

Vereesa stopped moving. The document remained open in her hand.

"So that's it," she said.

"That's the truth."

"And if following the truth leaves home behind?"

"Then home lies farther north."

She might have slapped her. Windrunner anger had done worse for less.

She did not.

She folded the recall with slow care and lowered it to her thigh, thumb over the softening wax.

"I hate you when you do this," Vereesa said softly.

Sylvanas let the old answer die before it reached her mouth.

"I know."

Amber light touched Jaina's hands at the edge of camp.

Sylvanas turned before she meant to. Jaina had gone only as far as the first birches beyond the fire line, where the road out could still be watched. The sending-stone sat in her palm with a low warm pulse, gold at the core and honey-pale around it. Not the colder blue Antonidas had left on the road south of Stratholme.

At this distance the call was only light, wind, and posture. That was enough.

Jaina stood in the open and spoke into the amber with the rigid economy of someone delivering news already past argument. Once the fingers of her free hand flexed against her skirt. Once her chin lifted before she answered. The light thinned. Held. Thinned again.

Antonidas pressing. Jaina refusing.

That was all Sylvanas could know.

Vereesa followed her attention and read the scene in one pass. Her mouth loosened. Some of the road stiffness left her shoulders.

"She isn't coming back either," Vereesa said.

"No."

Vereesa watched Jaina another moment, then gave one short nod.

"You've found each other in the wrong place."

Sylvanas let that pass.

The stone darkened from amber to dead crystal in a breath. Jaina held it a moment longer, then slid it deep into her coat pocket, closer to her ribs than to her hand.

When she turned back, her mouth had gone thin and her jaw was set too hard.

"I should go before the light fails entirely," Vereesa said.

Cyndia brought the gray mare up already watered, coat dark where the cloth had passed over the lather. One escort rider had found strength enough to retie the bedroll. Another held the bridle and fixed on nothing.

Vereesa slid the folded recall into her saddlebag, wax still visible when the flap shifted. She set a hand on the saddle, then stopped.

"If they ask whether you took it," she said without facing Sylvanas, "what answer do you want me to carry?"

"The true one."

Now she did turn. "Which is?"

Sylvanas gave her the only mercy left in the scene.

"That you delivered it."

Vereesa's throat moved once.

"And that you refused."

"Yes."

She mounted cleanly this time. Cyndia kept two fingers on the rein until the mare settled, then let go.

"I will tell him," Vereesa said.

Lor'themar, not the chamber. Good.

"Tell him to stop spending himself on men who count walls and not what climbs them."

"I will tell him you said that. He'll ignore the order and hear the rest."

That, at least, was Lor'themar enough.

Then Vereesa took in Jaina, Cyndia, the map case, the horses, the little camp already bending toward the road north.

"Safe road, Ranger-General."

"Ride hard, Captain."

Vereesa bared her teeth at that. It might once have become a smile.

She turned the gray mare toward the relay road east.

Vereesa did not look back. She took the horse into a steady canter at once, speed the only dignity left her, and the escort followed in dry dust and cooling light. The hooves stayed loud over the packed road for a few breaths. Then they thinned. Then only dust hung above the ditch grass where the family had been.

Jaina came back from the trees while the dust was still in the air. She had the map case under one arm. She did not ask what Vereesa had said after the refusal. She did not offer what Antonidas had said into the stone.

Sylvanas held out a hand for the case.

Jaina gave it over. "The relay line ends at the harbors. After that we borrow passage or we don't go."

"We go."

Jaina held for one brief level beat. "Yes."

Her empty hand closed once before she turned for her mare.

Sylvanas faced the camp.

"Strike it."

Cyndia moved before the second syllable died. Fire kicked apart and dirted over. Cook gear packed wet. Bedrolls cinched. Feed sacks shifted so the mares carried for distance, not parade.

Jaina bent to gather the loose pages she had not yet stowed. One skated under a crate and she trapped it against her boot. Smoke crossed camp again, thinner now, and beneath it the colder trace from the north sharpened.

Salt.

Still far. Real now.

Sylvanas checked her mare's girth and swung into the saddle. Cyndia mounted a breath later on the left. Jaina after that, slower through soreness but with no wasted motion, reins gathered as her mare folded into Sylvanas's left rear quarter before the word had fully cleared camp.

The last of Vereesa's dust had not yet settled.

"North," Sylvanas said.

The horses turned onto the coast road without hesitation. Cyndia took the left lane. Jaina's mare settled into the rear quarter where the line held strongest if trouble came off the roadside. In less than a minute there was only stamped earth behind them, crushed grass, and one cold patch of fire where the camp had been.

They rode.

At first through packed earth, broken ruts, and low hedges clawing at boot leather where the track narrowed. Yesterday still ached in her knees before today's miles had fully begun. Smoke in cloth had gone cold now that the city's heat no longer rode behind it.

The farms thinned. Stone showed through the soil in wider shelves. The road became the kind of track merchants kept alive by use alone. Wind found them more often. When it came off the north-east it carried salt before it carried sea, a raw mineral edge that dried the tongue and put cold under the evening air.

The horses took the shift too. Ears working. Breath lifting pale when the sun lowered.

Cyndia rode half a length out, reading roadside and tree line with the old relentless sweep. Right. Right again. Forward-left. The pattern barely showed unless one lived by such things. Sylvanas read it anyway and said nothing.

Jaina rode quieter than usual. Once her hand went to the front of her coat, checking the sending-stone without drawing it. Once she closed her eyes for three breaths and opened them with the hard look she wore when an answer arrived at the worst possible time.

"Stronger?" Sylvanas asked.

"Yes."

Jaina shifted when the mare found a rut. "Just louder. I can feel it in the back of my teeth now, and it gets worse every mile north."

Sylvanas kept the image for distance and timing and left the rest alone.

"Can you follow it on the coast?"

"If the weather holds and the water doesn't twist it beyond use. I'm less worried about the signal than the ship."

"Good."

Jaina's head turned. "Good?"

"Worrying about the ship means you trust the road."

That earned the smallest sound from her, close enough to laughter.

The first relay post after dusk was little more than a stone rise above the road and a bronze dish dark against the paling sky. One ranger on duty. Two spare horses under canvas. Oats stacked in half the quantity the station should have held this late in the year.

Sylvanas drew rein long enough to take the report.

Two harbor stations still answering. One had sent south for more oats. No word of ships larger than fishing craft. The coast road passable though soft near the marsh. No sign of Alliance troops this far north; Arthas had stripped the larger routes bare on his way to the sea.

Useful. Not comforting.

She left a dispatch with the station ranger, took fresh distance estimates, and moved them out again before the horses cooled too much.

Night came on in layers. First the red left the clouds. Then the road lost its color and became more touch than sight, the horses reading with their feet where the riders could not. Last of all the north-east horizon changed from dark land to a line flatter and colder than land had any right to be.

Open water ahead. The wind off it laid salt on her lips and pulled colder through the gloves. Sea country. Harder country.

Behind them Stratholme's stain stayed in the sky as long as the dark allowed.

Ahead waited coast, failing relays, borrowed passage, and the prince they had not stopped in time.

Sylvanas bent lower into the wind and kept the mare moving.
