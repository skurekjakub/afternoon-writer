The cold came up through the floor.

Sylvanas learned the cell in eight paces because the alternative was lying still and letting the room name her first. Three long steps from the barred door to the back wall. Two across. Cot bolted to stone. Bucket in the right corner. A slit of grate high on the corridor side where torchlight fell through in narrow bands and never reached the flagstones whole. Iron at her wrists, lighter than field shackles and more insulting for it: palace custody dressed up as restraint.

The room should have smelled of damp mortar, rust, stale straw, and old piss.

Instead it smelled of frost and old mortar gone sharp in the seams.

The kind that stung the nose before she touched the floor. Even the wool blanket on the cot had taken it. When she put her palm flat to the flagstones, the chill bit upward through skin and bone.

Above them, in the evidence vault a box had not solved, cold kept leaking down the stairwell and into the mortar joints.

She could not see the vault. She could read what it had already done to the palace around it. Corridor torches along this wing burned low and thin, each flame narrowed to a blue pin at the base. Guards posted outside the dungeon mouth crossed themselves before taking the stairs down. One man farther off had laughed too loudly at something, the sound ringing bright in the stone corridor, and cut himself off so fast the silence after it landed like a blow.

Through the wall on her left came the only useful thing left in the world.

Jaina's breathing.

Too faint for human ears through that much masonry. Plain enough for hers. Uneven. Longer when the pain loosened its grip, shorter when it caught again. Sometimes another trace came with it through the mortar: the stale bakery smell that always clung to Jaina after hard spellwork, now fouled by ash and old wool and the copper edge of blood swallowed too often. Bread. Ash. Proof.

Sylvanas sat on the cot and listened.

In. Hold. Out.

The rhythm faltered. Settled. A chain touched stone once.

Alive.

Bootsteps stopped outside her door. Not the lazy drag of dungeon rotation. Measured. Official. A key turned. Two guards entered with a clerk between them, the man already uncapping his ink bottle with his thumb. Palace officer. Clean cuffs despite the stairs. Writing board under one arm. He looked at the cell, at the prisoner, and arranged his face into the expression officials wore when they meant to pass off fear as routine.

"Ranger-General Windrunner," he said.

"Former, if the corridor is to be believed."

One guard's mouth twitched before he killed it. The clerk ignored both of them. "Your statement will be taken for the tribunal."

"Will it be heard there?"

"That depends on its contents."

No. It depended on whether anyone in the chamber above wanted truth before they heard what shape it took.

The guards set down the stool they had brought. The clerk sat, squared the board on his knees, and began before the first question had properly landed. Name. Rank. Nation. Purpose in the capital. Why she and Jaina Proudmoore had entered the royal chamber armed. Why the prince and king lay dead when the doors opened to witnesses.

Written before he came in.

The order gave it away. Not a man seeking sequence. A man sent to fit sequence to a frame already chosen.

So she told him all of it.

She counted it to him on her shackled hands while he wrote. Millhaven. Andorhal. The grain road. Scholomance. Stratholme. Northrend. Muradin. Frostmourne. Arthas returning with death nailed into him and a sword that closed wounds as fast as arrows made them. Terenas already dead when they reached the chamber. The crack in the blade's field. Frost first, then arcane, then fire. The final shot through the eye.

The pen moved hard through the first part. Slowed at Stratholme. Slowed again at Northrend. By the time she reached the sword singing after Arthas hit the throne steps, the clerk had the flat mouth of a man enduring madness.

He kept writing, sand already ready beside his knee, as if the page mattered more than whether the page was true.

One guard shifted from attention to endurance. The other watched Sylvanas's face the way men watched gamblers, certain the lie would twitch somewhere around the mouth if they stared long enough.

When she finished, the clerk shook sand over the page and blew the excess off.

"You say," he said carefully, eyes still on the lines rather than on her, "that Crown Prince Arthas Menethil was corrupted by an enchanted blade recovered in Northrend."

"I watched it happen."

"And Archmage Proudmoore will corroborate this account?"

Through the wall, Jaina's chain touched stone once.

"Ask her."

The clerk glanced toward the masonry between the cells as if he might somehow see through it by looking hard enough. "In time."

"You have the wrong order."

That pulled his eyes up. "Explain."

Sylvanas leaned forward just enough for his hand to tighten once around the pen. "If the sword is still in this palace, whatever you think of me matters less."

The nib hovered over the page, ink gathering black at the tip. One guard let out a short swallowed laugh.

"The weapon is secured," the clerk said.

"So was Arthas."

That landed. She saw it land. The tiny recoil in the shoulders before office locked him back into shape.

He sanded the page again though it did not need it, capped the ink, and stood. "Your statement has been entered into the record."

The key turned again after they left. Iron settled into place. Their steps receded up the corridor.

Then Jaina's voice came through the wall, low enough that a human ear at the door would have caught only a change in the stones.

"Sylvanas."

She was already off the cot. She knelt beside the left wall and put her ear near the mortar. "Here."

Only breath at first. Then: "It's looking."

Two words. Dragged raw enough to thin the breath behind them.

Sylvanas set her palm flat to the stone. The cold there had thickened since the clerk came in. "At whom?"

"Don't know yet." A dry swallow rasped through the wall. "Not random."

The cold under the flagstones shifted with the blade's pressure moving through the foundations.

"Can you hear it clearly?"

"Clear enough."

"How long?"

Jaina took too long to answer. In the silence chain rasped once, then breath came again from higher in the cell, careful and upright against the wall.

"Shorter than it was," Jaina said at last.

"How much shorter?"

"I need time."

There it was.

Not enough of it to spend.

Sylvanas leaned her forehead briefly against the wall. The mortar gave her nothing but deeper cold. "Then count."

"I am."

After that the cell held only breath, torch hiss, and stone.

Then, from somewhere above and farther in, the flagstone gave one dry tick under her palm and the cold hitched with it. Not loud. Not dramatic. Something inside the oak-and-lead chest had turned once against its wards and found a seam it preferred.

Real.

Sylvanas opened her eyes.

The cell mattered less than the vault above it. The law was only one prison here. Frostmourne was the other, and the hungrier of the two.

The sounds changed above her before the guards came: benches, voices, more feet on stone than any morning shift should carry. Then the daylight chains.

The guards took her up narrow service stairs that smelled of lamp oil, wet wool, and the sour leather of men who had been standing too long in armor. Two paladins watched a crossing passage with the fixed stillness of men obeying an order they did not trust. Beyond them the cold deepened under her bare soles through boot-thin leather.

The vault was there.

Even through stone, oak, lead, and wards, the blade drew cloud from every mouth that passed its door.

Jaina climbed ahead of her between two guards. Her hair had been braided back by rougher hands than her own. One side of her face still carried the bruise from the sword's guard. She moved too straight, every step placed as if disorder itself might spill her open. She did not look back.

She did not need to.

The tribunal hall opened beyond the stair. Afternoon light striped the flagstones, and a guard shoved her between the benches before she had room to turn her wrists. High windows. Pale shafts of light turning dust above the benches to visible columns. Tapestries hanging motionless on the walls. A raised bench loomed over the flagstones with three dark chairs, stacked papers, and enough polished wood to make every word sound official. Guards stood at the side aisles, and the center table waited under watch between the accused and the bench.

At the far end, three dark seats waited. Only the center was occupied.

Thornton sat there with papers squared before him and a pen aligned precisely to the desk's edge. Silver hair. Spare face. The look of a man who had not slept enough and had still taken the time to tie every knot on his robes correctly because disorder anywhere else in the room was going to have to work around him.

Ashcombe stood below him in black trimmed with palace blue, elegant as a duelist who had chosen law because it cut more cleanly. She turned one glove finger by finger while the guards settled the accused into their places.

The chains were lighter here than dungeon irons.

That was part of the room too.

Not dungeon iron but courtroom iron: light enough to walk in, loose enough for every bench in the hall to read prisoner before person.

Jaina was seated on Sylvanas's right with one guard behind her chair and one at the aisle. Across the center of the chamber stood a heavy oak table banded in iron. On it sat the chest.

Lead-lined, by the look of it and the smell in the room. Silver ward paste ringed the lid seam, already blackening at one rear corner. Even shut, it pulled warmth out of the hall.

Ashcombe began without raising her voice.

Ashcombe paced one slow line beside the center table, glove leather whispering as she turned each page. Months of investigation. Warnings issued beyond proper authority. Unauthorized travel. Contacts with foreign magi. Hidden correspondence. Armed intrusion into the palace interior. She did not invent. She turned. Every true fact, half a degree.

Scholomance became prior knowledge. Northrend became pursuit of a weapon rather than a prince. Relay reports were reread as preparation. The speed with which Sylvanas and Jaina had reached correct conclusions along the grain road became proof they had known the shape of events in advance. Even Stratholme served her. When Ashcombe suggested that only women already bent toward regicide kept turning up armed over royal bodies, someone on the front bench pulled breath through his teeth.

Every true fact Ashcombe laid down left another marked page on Thornton's desk and another head in the gallery turning her way before the next lie bent it.

Sylvanas stopped watching the prosecutor and watched Thornton instead. Ashcombe kept the room moving, but Thornton was the one checking each cited page with a finger and leaving it on the record. He never once looked hurried. He wanted order, and Ashcombe was feeding it to him one neat line at a time.

At the word Northrend, Jaina's breath caught audibly two chairs away. She kept her face forward. Good.

Ashcombe ended where the room had always intended to end. Armed entry. Dead king. Dead prince. An enchanted blade known in suspicious detail before any royal scholar had been allowed to examine it. Two foreign women returned from the north with every apparent reason to silence a prince before he could speak first.

The gallery murmured. Nobles, officers, palace clerks, a few priests in pale robes, all packed close enough on the benches for wool and perfume and cold sweat to mix in the air.

Thornton lifted one hand.

Bench wood creaked once, then even the gallery cloth went quiet.

 "Call the exhibit," he said.

Four palace mages brought the chest to the center table as if they were carrying a bier.

They were sweating though the chamber sat cold. One had linen wrapped around his right hand where fresh blisters showed through. Another kept swallowing before every breath, as if the air had turned rough inside his throat. The silver paste around the lid hissed when the clasps were turned back. When the top opened a finger's breadth, half the room inhaled together.

Cold rolled out first.

It skimmed the flagstones and climbed the chair legs. Breath fogged from the front bench to the back gallery in a single ripple. Ink filmed at the lip of the nearest clerk's pot. The torch sconces along the wall guttered without any draft the hall should have had.

Then the lid came up farther. The front bench recoiled first, one priest caught the rail, and breath broke across the room in one ragged pull.

Frostmourne lay on black velvet inside the lead box, frost already whitening the nap where the metal rested.

The crack near the seam showed plainly now. Thin. Bright. Ugly. Light pulsed through it too weakly to illuminate the blade and strongly enough to keep dragging the eye back. Not dead. Damaged.

Somebody in the gallery whispered a prayer to the Light.

Sylvanas looked at Jaina despite herself.

Jaina's hands had gone white on the chair arms. Worse than white. Locked. The tremor that had lived in her fingers below was gone. Her whole arm had tightened against it. Sweat stood dark at her hairline though the air bit with winter cold.

Ashcombe saw it and smiled with one corner of her mouth.

"Tell the tribunal," she said to the first palace mage, "what the accused claimed about this object."

The mage dragged his tongue over dry lips. "That it gets into people's heads, my lady. That it answers back. That the damage it took in the throne room weakened the enchantment but did not end it."

"And what have you observed yourself?"

"Rooms go cold when it remains in them. Wards begin to rot around the seams. Ordinary instruments misbehave. Men on watch stop giving straight answers and rub their chests as if the cold got inside them."

Ashcombe let the phrase sit. "Inside them."

"Yes, my lady."

"Would those already familiar with the blade's reputation show that effect more strongly?"

He hesitated, free hand finding the linen on his burns. "They could."

Ashcombe turned slightly, just enough to bring Jaina and the blade into the same line of sight for the bench. "So the person who knows the weapon best may appear the most affected by it."

Jaina finally looked at her. Pain had flattened everything in her face except the mouth. No pleading in it. Only the effort of staying vertical while the weapon two strides away kept worrying at whatever torn place it had left in her.

Sylvanas wanted a bow in her hand badly enough to taste the string.

They kept the chest open too long. Whether Ashcombe meant to or merely knew the fear was useful did not matter. One clerk rubbed his breastbone as if trying to settle a sudden chill inside it. The silver paste blackened further around the lid corners. A guard at the gallery door shifted his weight away from the center table and then froze when he realized others had seen him do it.

Thornton watched all of it. He let the mage step down only when the reaction in the room had steeped long enough to become evidence in its own right.

Uther came to the witness chair in a plain surcoat instead of armor, white and gold dulled by travel and old grief. Bareheaded. Unarmed. He was still too broad for the furniture and too grave for the room's pretense of balance. When he swore to speak, the silence changed.

He gave them Hearthglen first, then Stratholme, each place set down in the same grave voice while his hands stayed braced on the rail. Arthas's deepening refusal of counsel. Northrend. The sword. The return. He spoke as paladin and veteran both: plain, grave, and with no ornament the dead had not earned.

Then the chamber itself. Too late. Terenas already gone. Arthas with Frostmourne in hand. Jaina and Sylvanas between the prince and whatever else he intended to kill. The damaged blade. The final arrow. Custody taken after.

He held the room.

Even Thornton looked up from the papers more than once while Uther spoke. For a few breaths the hall remembered what witness was meant to do.

Ashcombe rose for cross-examination and did not attempt to break him.

She did not need to.

"You opposed the prince at Stratholme," she said.

"I did."

"You were removed from command after that disagreement."

"Yes."

"You loved the late king."

Uther's jaw tightened once. "Yes."

"You instructed the late crown prince in arms and conduct."

"I did."

"You did not witness His Majesty's death."

"No."

"You did not see who struck first in the chamber."

"No."

"You did not enter the room until after the killing."

"After the king's death, yes."

Each true answer gave Thornton one cleaner line on the page and left Uther less room to fight from the chair.

When Ashcombe asked whether grief and long conflict with Arthas might have colored his reading of the scene, Uther's hands closed once on the witness rail hard enough to make the wood creak.

"I know what I saw," he said.

"After the act," Ashcombe replied.

No one on the benches moved. Even the clerk's pen paused before it scratched on again.

Uther did not either. "After the king's death, yes."

"With two armed foreigners in the chamber."

"With two women standing between the kingdom and a damned weapon."

That struck. People all through the gallery went still. For one heartbeat Sylvanas thought the blow had landed cleanly enough to matter.

Ashcombe bowed her head with almost devotional politeness. "A weapon you did not see taking His Majesty's life."

There it was.

The missing minutes.

Nothing in Northrend had cost them more.

Uther left the chair with the same rigid control he had brought to it, and if Thornton's mouth had tightened, it had not tightened enough.

Sylvanas caught the blue of Dalaran robes in the gallery before the clerk called the next submission.

Antonidas stood three rows up with two Kirin Tor magi at his shoulders. His robes still hung clean, but the skin under his eyes had gone grey and one cuff sat twisted where somebody had dressed him in a hurry. He looked worn to the bone. He had come in person. That made the empty witness chair worse, not better.

When he rose to object, every head in the hall turned toward him.

"You have a blade in this chamber that no palace school can judge safely," he said. The voice came from training alone. The rest of him looked tired enough to break if the sentence ran longer than it needed to. "Delay these proceedings. Let Dalaran examine it before you build a verdict around a weapon none of you understand."

Thornton did not look impressed. He looked tired in a different, more dangerous way. "By whose authority, Archmage?"

That stopped Antonidas for half a breath.

Because no one behind him stepped forward.

"By mine," he said at last.

Ashcombe moved before Thornton had to. "Contact between a foreign order and the object at the center of this case would constitute tampering, not rescue."

One of the magi behind Antonidas had gone pale the moment the chest opened. He stared at Frostmourne now with the fixed attention of a man trying not to hear a voice only he suspected might answer if he did. The other held her jaw so tight it had gone rigid under the skin. Neither stepped into the argument.

Thornton denied the continuance.

Antonidas remained standing one breath too long, then sat because he had found the end of whatever leash Dalaran had placed around his throat and discovered it was iron.

Jaina never turned toward him.

Vereesa arrived with a sealed document.

No herald. Only the clerk naming Ranger Captain Vereesa Windrunner and a hush moving through the room before she reached the witness chair. She wore field leathers beneath a travel-stained cloak pinned with the Windrunner crest. Gold wax sealed the folded vellum in her hand.

Family should never have been brought here.

The Convocation had made certain it was.

Vereesa broke the seal with steady fingers. Only those. Her jaw was too tight. She kept her face away from Sylvanas until the last possible moment, and even then turned it only once.

She read the disavowal in the chamber voice until one word scraped raw and she had to press the vellum flatter to keep her hands steady.

Unauthorized action beyond sovereign borders. Expired military authority. No recognized alliance, obligation, or debt arising from the acts of the former Ranger-General and those under her command. Quel'Thalas would not shelter, answer for, or defend.

Every word chosen by people who had not ridden the road and would sleep more easily for that.

Sylvanas knew Vereesa's breathing. Knew the tiny hitch before one sentence. Knew the cost of keeping the paper from shaking.

When Vereesa finished, she folded the vellum once and creased it hard enough that the fold stayed white.

Ashcombe thanked her.

Vereesa stepped back.

She did not leave the hall.

That mattered too.

Thornton wrote for some time after the last statement. Below him, the clerks kept their pens moving in smaller answer-script across narrow ledgers. Nobody in the gallery spoke above a whisper. Frostmourne sat in its chest and turned every breath in the room visible, cattle-steam in slaughter weather. Jaina had begun counting against her thumb with one chained hand, tiny pressures along the iron, too small for anyone who had not shared walls and battlefields with her to notice.

Intervals.

When Thornton finally raised his head, every face in the room had turned toward the bench.

"On the charges of conspiracy against the Crown, unlawful foreign armed action within the royal seat, and the murders of His Majesty King Terenas Menethil and Crown Prince Arthas Menethil—"

Jaina's counting stopped.

"—this tribunal finds the accused guilty."

The word crossed the chamber and every bench answered it at once—people leaning, chains tightening, one sleeve muffling a single sob. Thornton kept reading in the same level voice while the room absorbed the first blow.

Death would make martyrs. The city had no use for martyrs. So the sentence was commuted. Indefinite imprisonment under royal and ecclesiastical authority pending further determination and stabilization of the enchanted weapon.

The sentence sounded administrative because that was what they wanted: living prisoners, not dead martyrs.

The chain bit Sylvanas's wrists before any softer part of the ruling could have pretended to matter. Guards hauled them to their feet. Jaina's face had gone blank with a body's refusal to spend itself on the smaller catastrophe while the larger one was still upstairs in a box.

They were taken below before the chamber had fully emptied.

Past the vault corridor.

Past the door that made the torches burn thin.

Past the same paladins, now colder and more tired than before.

This time no clerk came after the lock closed. No questions. No paper. Only the shove, the key, and the return of stone.

For a while the dungeon held only receding steps and the rough noises two people made on opposite sides of a wall.

Then—

tap.

Sylvanas crossed the cell before the second sound came.

Tap. Tap. Pause.

Another pattern. Quicker this time. Not random.

She put two fingers to the mortar and listened through the stone and the rhythm under it.

Jaina tapped again, then spoke, voice worn to a rasp. "Days."

"Certain?"

"Enough."

Days.

Whatever span men with seals imagined while they passed orders up staircases and argued jurisdiction over the dead had already burned away in the vault above them.

Days.

The air had grown colder since the verdict instead of warmer. Frost rode the edge of the blanket now. Beyond the corridor grate, the torch burned blue at its base.

"Count it again," Sylvanas said.

"I am."

A pause. Longer than the last. Then, quieter: "Elven mages might feel it."

Sylvanas opened her eyes in the dark. "From the vault?"

"From the foundations, maybe. Better than humans."

She held one fact in place: elven mages above the vault had felt the same wrongness through the paving.

"Good," she said.

Jaina gave one humorless breath that roughened into pain before it could become a laugh. "That wasn't optimism."

"I know."

The wall stayed cold beneath her hand. She kept it there anyway.

When the kitchen smells from above had gone stale and the corridor had settled to low guard-talk and boot creak, new steps entered the wing.

Not human.

Elven.

She knew the cadence before the guard at the corner barked his warning. Lighter on the heel. Cleaner through the ball of the foot. More than one set at first, then fewer as rank was allowed farther in than the rest.

Sylvanas was already at the bars when Lor'themar stepped into the torchlight.

Road dust lay gray on his cloak where court polish should have been. His hair had half-fallen from its military tail. No wit lived anywhere on his face. Two Farstriders waited back in the dark behind him, and farther off the human guards had yielded him only so much corridor. He stopped one pace from the grate.

Neither of them spoke at once.

He took in the cell, the chains, and her face, then stopped on the bruise at her temple and stayed there long enough to count it.

"You look terrible," he said.

The old answer almost came by reflex. "Court thought otherwise."
It died there when his face did not change.

He was too far past humor for either of them to spend it cheaply.

"Vereesa told me enough to ride before the vote finished," he said. "Not enough to get here before it did."

There it was.

Too late, plain and unsheltered.

"How many?" Sylvanas asked.

"Three hundred Farstriders. Two dozen mages." He tipped his chin toward the ceiling. "The mages felt it in the paving outside the lower court before they saw the vault guard. They thought the city's wards were failing."

"They were wrong."

"Yes."

If the mages had felt it in the paving, the wrongness had already spread farther than it should have been able to reach.

"Can they testify?"

"Tomorrow, perhaps. If there is anyone left in this palace willing to hear what doesn't flatter the ruling." A dry scrape crossed his voice with no warmth in it. "Humans improve once condemned women become someone else's problem."

That, at least, was still him. Scraped down to iron.

From the cell beside hers came the drag of chain over stone and Jaina's voice through the wall, thin with distance and pain. "Lor'themar?"

He turned toward the masonry at once, not toward the bars. "Lady Proudmoore."

"Days," she said.

His head shifted by a fraction. He took the number and left the fear under it untouched. "Understood."

"Your mages feel it?"

"Enough to swear the wrongness is real. Not enough to name it. Yet."

"Faster," Jaina said. The word nearly frayed apart on the last breath. "It's getting faster."

Lor'themar put one hand through the bars.

There was not much the chains allowed. Sylvanas gave him her wrist because the iron left little else to offer. His grip closed around the bone above the shackle: callused, cold, familiar as old field gear.

"I should have been here sooner," he said, low now, for her alone.

"You are here."

"An inspiring standard."

"Use what you brought."

His thumb pressed once to the inside of her wrist where the pulse ran. Field shorthand. Still alive. Still capable of orders.

"Vereesa stayed," he said.

Sylvanas's attention sharpened.

"She delivered the Convocation's line, then informed the courier assigned to escort her back north to go to hell in language delicate enough to survive transcription. She's upstairs with the mages."

Sylvanas's hand closed once against her own palm.

"The verdict stands," Lor'themar said. "The doors do not open tonight. But there are witnesses now."

Yes.

Elven boots and road dust stood outside the bars at last, and even the human guards farther down the wing had started watching the corridor differently.

From somewhere above came the same wrong hitch in the cold, small and sharp as a knife tip finding a seam.

Lor'themar missed it. She knew the instant he missed it.

But he saw her react.

Years together had taught him that much.

"Days?" he asked.

"Less if the wards fail early."

He accepted it without flinch. "Then I won't spend the night arguing the verdict."

"No."

"I'll spend it on the blade."

Better.

Bootsteps sounded at the corner again. Human this time. Impatient. Somebody coming to end privileges before they could harden into precedent.

Lor'themar heard them and did not release her wrist until the last possible moment.

"Hold," he said.

She almost told him not to insult her.

There were only so many words worth spending in a night this bad.

"Go," she said instead.

His hand left hers. Cold took the skin at once.

He stepped back as the guard rounded the corner with a lantern raised high and annoyance already arranged on his face. Farther down the wing, elven shadows waited where the humans had permitted waiting and no farther.

Lor'themar gave the guard a look that promised future trouble in formal clothing. Then he turned back to Sylvanas once.

The guard opened his mouth. Lor'themar turned away before the first word could get cleanly out.

Road dust still marked his boots as he headed up the corridor toward the colder dark above.

Sylvanas stayed at the bars until the torchlight swallowed him.

Then she crossed back to the wall and set her hand against the stone between her and Jaina.

On the other side came three light taps.
