The cold came up through the floor.

Sylvanas learned the cell in eight paces because the alternative was seeming to accept waiting. Three long steps from door to back wall. Two across. Cot bolted to stone. Bucket in the corner. A slit of grate high on the corridor side where torchlight striped the opposite wall and never touched the floor whole. Iron at her wrists, lighter than field shackles and more insulting for it.

The room smelled of frost instead of damp, rust, and old piss.
Sharper than winter air. Meaner than river ice. It came through the seams between the stones until even the blanket carried it. When she set her palm to the floor, the chill bit upward.

Above them, the blade sat in a box, and its cold had already worked through the palace.

She could not see the vault. She could read the building around it. Corridor torches burned low and thin for no draft she could hear. Guards crossed themselves at this wing. One man laughed too loudly farther down the hall and stopped so fast the silence after him sounded struck.

Through the wall on her left came the only useful thing left in the world: Jaina's breathing.

Too faint for human ears through that much masonry. Easy enough for hers. Uneven. Deeper when the pain backed off, shorter when it caught again. Sometimes the stale bakery smell that clung to Jaina after hard spellwork reached through the mortar as well. Bread. Ash. Old wool. Proof.

Sylvanas sat on the cot and listened.

In. Hold. Out.

The rhythm faltered. Settled. A chain clicked once.

Alive.

Bootsteps stopped outside her door. A key turned. Two guards entered with a clerk between them, the man already uncapping his ink. Palace officer. Clean cuffs. Writing board under one arm. He looked at the cell, at Sylvanas, and put on the face officials used for necessary boredom.

"Ranger-General Windrunner," he said.

"Former, according to the corridor."

One guard's mouth twitched. The clerk ignored it. "Your statement will be taken for the tribunal."

"Will it be heard there?"

"That depends on its contents."

No. It depended on whether anyone in the room wanted truth before it arrived.

He sat on the stool the guards had brought and started before the first question had fully landed. Name. Rank. Nation. Purpose in the capital. Why she and Jaina Proudmoore had entered the royal chamber armed. Why the prince and king lay dead when the doors opened.

Written before he came in. The order gave it away.

So she told him all of it.

Millhaven. Andorhal. Scholomance. Stratholme. Northrend. Muradin. Frostmourne. Arthas returning with death nailed into him and a sword that closed wounds as fast as arrows made them. Terenas already dead when they reached the chamber. The crack in the blade's field. Frost first, then arcane, then fire. The final shot through the eye.

The pen moved hard through the first part. Slowed at Stratholme. Slowed again at Northrend. By the time she reached the sword singing after Arthas hit the steps, the clerk had the flat mouth of a man enduring madness out of duty.

He kept writing because he still had to go through the motions of fairness after belief was gone. One guard shifted from attention to boredom. The other watched Sylvanas's mouth for the lie he expected to see.

When she finished, the clerk blew on the page.

"You say," he said carefully, "that Crown Prince Arthas Menethil was corrupted by an enchanted blade from Northrend."

"I watched it."

"And Archmage Proudmoore will corroborate this account?"

Through the wall, Jaina's chain touched stone once.

"Ask her."

The clerk turned toward the masonry. "In time."

"You have the wrong order."

That pulled his attention up. "Explain."

"If the sword is still in the palace, whatever you think of me matters less."

His pen hesitated. One guard made a swallowed laugh.

"The weapon is secured," the clerk said.

"So was Arthas."

He felt it and locked himself back down.

He sanded the page, shook it dry, and stood. "Your statement has been entered."

The key turned again after they left. Iron settled. Their footsteps faded. Then Jaina's voice came through the wall, low enough that a human at the door would have caught only a change in the stones.

"Sylvanas."

She knelt by the left wall, ear near the mortar. "Here."

Only breath. Then: "It's looking."

Two words. Nothing in them except the way Jaina spent them.

Sylvanas put her palm to the stone. The cold there had thickened. "At whom?"

"Don't know yet." A swallow. "Not random."

The floor shifted under her for half a heartbeat.

"Can you hear it clearly?"

"Clear enough."

"How long?"

Jaina took too long to answer. Sylvanas pictured her anyway: back to the wall, wrists chained, face white with the effort of sitting upright near that thing.

"Shorter than it was," Jaina said at last.

"How much shorter?"

"I need time."

There it was. Not enough of it to spend.

Sylvanas leaned her forehead briefly to the wall. It gave her only more cold. "Then count."

"I am."

After that the cell held only breath and stone. Then, from somewhere above, a wrong little pulse moved through the foundations. A hitch in the cold. Real now. Something in the vault was testing the wards.

Sylvanas opened her eyes.

The cell mattered less than the vault above. Above them, the blade kept testing the wards.

Hours later men came with daylight chains.

They took her up narrow service stairs that smelled of lamp oil and damp wool, past two doors watched by paladins, past a corridor where the cold deepened under her soles. The vault was there. Even through stone, oak, lead, and wards, Frostmourne drew cloud from every mouth.

Jaina climbed ahead of her between two guards. Her hair had been braided back again by rougher hands than her own. One side of her face still carried the bruise from the sword's guard. She moved with the careful straightness of somebody ordering pain to stay where it belonged. She did not look back. She did not need to.

Pale light fell from the high windows through dust onto the raised bench. Banners hung without moving. At the far end, three dark seats waited, though only the center one was filled. Thornton sat there with papers squared before him and a pen aligned to the desk's edge. Silver hair. Spare face. A man who had not slept enough and had still tied every knot on his robes correctly.

Ashcombe stood below him in black trimmed with palace blue, elegant as a duelist. She turned one glove finger by finger while the guards settled the accused into their chairs.

The chains made less noise in chairs. The iron still bit when they sat.

Jaina was placed at Sylvanas's right with one guard behind her and one at the aisle. At the chamber's center stood a heavy oak table banded in iron. On it sat the chest.

Lead-lined, by the smell and the weight of the room. Silver ward paste ringed the rim, already black in one corner. Even shut, it pulled warmth out of the air.

Ashcombe began without raising her voice.

Months of investigation, warnings, travel beyond authority, covert entry, hidden letters, contact with foreign mages. She turned every true thing half a degree.

Scholomance became prior knowledge. Northrend became pursuit of a weapon rather than a prince. Relay reports became preparation. The speed with which Sylvanas and Jaina had reached the right conclusions became proof they had known events in advance. Ashcombe turned even Stratholme to her use. Only women already bent toward regicide, Ashcombe suggested, would stand so near repeated royal disasters and still be found armed over the bodies at the end.

She had built it out of true things. A trap built by somebody who respected the spring.

Sylvanas watched Thornton while Ashcombe spoke. Thornton wanted order. He tracked the papers with one finger and checked each link as it came. Ashcombe gave him one link after another.

Once, at the word Northrend, Jaina's breath caught audibly from two chairs away. She kept her face forward. Good.

Ashcombe ended at the throne room. Armed entry. Dead king. Dead prince. An enchanted sword known in suspicious detail before any royal scholar had examined it. Two women returned from the north with every reason to silence a prince before he spoke first.

The gallery murmured.

Thornton lifted one hand. Everyone in the room went still.

"Call the exhibit."

Four palace mages brought the chest to the center table as if carrying a bier. They were sweating though the room sat cold. One had wrapped linen around his hand where blisters showed. The silver on the lid hissed when the clasps were turned. When the top opened a finger's breadth, half the chamber inhaled together.

Cold rolled out first.

It skimmed the floor and climbed the chair legs. Torchlight thinned. Breath fogged from benches to magistrate's seat in a single breath. Ink filmed at the lip of the nearest clerk's pot.

Then the lid came up and everyone in the room felt the edge of its wrongness.

Frostmourne lay in lead and black velvet, a relic laid out for prayer.

The crack near the seam showed plainly now. Thin. Bright. Ugly. Light pulsed through it, too weak to light the blade and strong enough to keep dragging the eye back.

Somebody in the gallery whispered a prayer.

Sylvanas turned to Jaina despite herself.

Jaina's hands had gone white on the arms of her chair. Worse now. The tremor had hardened into stillness. Sweat showed at her hairline though the air bit with winter cold.

Ashcombe caught it and smiled with one corner of her mouth.

"Tell the tribunal," she said to the first palace mage, "what the accused claimed about this object."

He dragged his tongue across dry lips. "That it gets into people's heads, my lady. That it answers back. That the crack it took in the throne room hurt the enchantment but did not kill it."

"And what have you seen yourself?"

"Rooms go cold when it stays in them. Wards start to rot. Ordinary instruments misbehave. Unwarded staff become unsettled."

"Unsettled."

"Yes, my lady."

"Would those already familiar with the blade's reputation show it more strongly?"

He hesitated from knowing exactly which answer they wanted and disliking how near it stood to the truth. "They could."

Ashcombe turned. "So the person who knows the weapon best may appear the most affected by it."

Jaina finally turned to Ashcombe. Her face had gone too still for pleading. The look was stripped down to calculation and pain.

Sylvanas wanted a bow in her hand badly enough to taste it.

They kept the chest open too long. Whether Ashcombe meant to or merely understood that fear was useful, everyone in the room felt it. The silver paste blackened further at the lid corners. One clerk rubbed his breastbone.

He came to the witness chair in a plain surcoat instead of armor, white and gold dulled by travel and old grief. Bareheaded and unarmed, he was just a man too broad for the furniture and too tired for pretense. When he swore to speak, the silence changed.

He gave them Hearthglen. Stratholme. Arthas's growing refusal of counsel. Northrend. Obsession. The return. He spoke as both paladin and soldier: plain, grave, with no ornament the dead had not earned.

Then the chamber. Too late. Terenas already gone. Arthas with Frostmourne in hand. Jaina and Sylvanas between the prince and whatever else he meant to kill. The damaged blade. The final arrow. Custody after.

Uther held the room. Thornton checked the room over the tops of his papers more than once. Uther was clear.

Ashcombe rose for cross-examination and ruined nothing.

She made him the man who came after the killing.

Yes, Lord Uther had opposed the prince at Stratholme. Yes, he had been removed from command after that dispute. Yes, he loved the dead king and had tutored the dead prince. Yes, he had not seen Terenas die. No, he had not seen Arthas strike the first blow. No, he had not seen who first entered the chamber, or under what pretext, because he arrived after the killing.

Each true answer tightened the case instead.

When Ashcombe asked whether grief and old conflict might have colored his reading of the scene, Uther's hands closed once on the rail so hard the wood creaked.

"I know what I saw," he said.

"After the act."

"After the king's death, yes."

"With two armed foreigners in the chamber."

"With two women standing between the kingdom and a damned weapon."

People all through the room went still. Good. For one heartbeat Sylvanas thought the blow had landed cleanly.

Ashcombe bowed her head. "A weapon you did not see taking His Majesty's life."

There it was. The missing twenty minutes. Nothing in Northrend had done half so much damage.

Uther did not bend. He left the chair with the same rigid control he had brought to it, and if Thornton's mouth had tightened, it had not tightened enough.

Sylvanas caught a flash of blue in the gallery.

Antonidas stood three rows up with two Kirin Tor magi at his shoulders. His robes still hung clean, but the body inside them looked worn to the bone. He had come in person.

When he rose to object, every head in the chamber turned toward him.

"You have a blade in this chamber that no palace school can judge safely," he said. His voice came from training alone. "Delay these proceedings. Let Dalaran examine it before you build a verdict around a weapon none of you understand."

Thornton stayed bent over the bench. "By whose authority, Archmage?"

That stopped Antonidas for half a breath.

Because the authority he needed had not been given.

"By mine," he said at last.

Ashcombe moved before Thornton could answer. "Contact between a foreign order and the sword at the center of this case would be tampering."

One of the magi behind Antonidas had gone pale the moment the chest opened. He stared at Frostmourne now with the fixed attention of a man trying not to listen to it. The other held her jaw tight enough to ache. Neither stepped forward.

Thornton denied the delay.

Antonidas stayed standing one breath too long, then sat because he had found the end of whatever leash Dalaran had put around his throat and discovered it was iron.

Jaina never turned toward him.

Vereesa arrived with a sealed document.

No herald. Just the clerk naming Ranger Captain Vereesa Windrunner and a hush moving through the benches before she reached the witness chair. She wore field leathers under a travel-stained cloak pinned with the Windrunner crest.

Family should not have been in this. The Convocation had made certain it was.

Vereesa broke the seal with steady hands. Only those. Her jaw was too tight. She kept her face from Sylvanas until the last possible instant, and even then turned it only once.

She read the disavowal in a voice so formal it bled.

Unauthorized action beyond sovereign borders. Expired military authority. No recognized alliance, obligation, or debt arising from the acts of the former Ranger-General and those under her command. Quel'Thalas would not shelter, answer for, or defend.

Every word chosen by people who had not ridden the road and would sleep easily for that.

Sylvanas knew Vereesa's breathing. Knew the hitch before one sentence. Knew what it cost to keep the paper from shaking.

When Vereesa finished, she folded the document once with such exact care that the motion itself became violence.

Ashcombe thanked her.

Vereesa stepped back and did not leave the hall.

Thornton wrote for some time after the last submission. No one spoke above a whisper. Frostmourne sat in its chest and made the whole chamber steam at the mouth, cattle-breath in slaughter weather. Jaina had begun counting against her thumb with one chained hand, tiny pressures Sylvanas would have missed once and never again.

Intervals.

When Thornton finally raised his head, every face in the room had turned toward the bench.

"On the charges of conspiracy against the Crown, unlawful foreign armed action within the royal seat, and the murders of His Majesty King Terenas Menethil and Crown Prince Arthas Menethil—"

Jaina's counting stopped.

"—this tribunal finds the accused guilty."

The word crossed the chamber and everyone in the room leaned toward it. Guards tightened chains. Gallery breath let go. Someone sobbed once into a sleeve. Thornton kept reading in the same level voice while the chains tightened.

Death would make martyrs. The city's rulers found martyrs expensive. So the sentence was commuted. Indefinite imprisonment under royal and ecclesiastical authority pending further determination and stabilization of the enchanted weapon.

The chain bit her wrists before any softer part of the verdict could have landed. Guards hauled them up. Jaina's face had gone blank with a body's refusal to spend itself on the smaller disaster when the larger one was already at the door.

They were taken below again before the hall had fully emptied.

Past the vault corridor. Past the cold.

This time no clerk came. No questions. Only the key, the shove, the lock.

For a while the dungeon held only bootsteps fading and the rough noise of two people getting themselves back under command on opposite sides of a wall.

Then—tap.

Sylvanas crossed to the stone at once.

Tap. Tap. Pause.

Another pattern. Faster. Not random. She put two fingers to the mortar and listened through them.

Jaina tapped again, then spoke, voice worn raw. "Days."

"Certain?"

"Enough."

Days.

Whatever time men with seals might imagine while they wrote letters and filed orders had already burned away.

Days.

The air had grown colder since the verdict. Frost rode the blanket now. The torch beyond the grate burned blue at its base.

"Count it again," Sylvanas said.

"I am."

There was a long pause. Then, quieter: "Elven mages might feel it."

Sylvanas opened her eyes in the dark. "From the vault?"

"From the foundations, maybe. Better than humans."

She began piecing together a plan under her ribs. Small. Bad. Alive.

"Good," she said.

Jaina gave one humorless laugh and stopped because it hurt. "That wasn't optimism."

"I know."

The wall stayed cold between them. Sylvanas kept her hand on it anyway.

Much later, when the corridor had fallen to low voices and the kitchen smells from above were long dead, new footsteps came down the wing.

Elven.

She knew the cadence before the guard at the corner barked a warning. Lighter on the heel. Cleaner through the ball of the foot. More than one set, then fewer as rank was allowed farther in than the rest.

Sylvanas was already at the bars when Lor'themar stepped into the torchlight.

Road dust on his cloak. Hair half-fallen from its military tail. No humor anywhere on his face. He stopped one pace from the grate.

For a moment neither spoke.

He looked thinner than when she had last seen him. Worn down to the edges. He took in the cell, the chains, and her face, then stopped on the bruise at her temple.

"You look terrible," he said.

She nearly gave the old answer by reflex. "Court thought otherwise."

Nothing. His face stayed blank.

"Vereesa told me enough to ride before the vote finished," he said. "Not enough to get here before it did."

There it was. Too late, plain.

"How many?"

"Three hundred Farstriders. Two dozen mages." He tipped his chin toward the ceiling. "The mages picked it up in the paving outside the lower court. They thought the city's wards were failing. They were wrong."

Good.

Horrible. Good.

"Can they testify?"

"Tomorrow, perhaps. If there is anyone left willing to hear." A dry spark crossed his voice with no warmth in it. "Humans improve once condemned women become other people's problem."

That at least was him, scraped bloody.

From the cell beside hers came the drag of chain and Jaina's voice, thin through stone. "Lor'themar?"

He turned toward the wall at once, not the bars. "Lady Proudmoore."

"Days," she said.

Lor'themar's head shifted a fraction. The field officer in him took the number and left the fear alone. "Understood."

"Your mages feel it?"

"Enough to swear the wrongness is real. Not enough to name it. Yet."

"Faster," Jaina said. The word nearly frayed apart on the last breath. "It's getting faster."

Lor'themar put one hand through the bars. Sylvanas gave him her wrist because the chains allowed little else. His grip closed around the bone above the iron—callused, cold, familiar as a bowstring scar.

"I should have been here sooner," he said, low now, for her alone.

"You are here."

"An inspiring standard."

"Use what you brought."

His thumb pressed once to the inside of her wrist where the pulse ran. Field shorthand. Still alive. Still capable of orders. Good.

"Vereesa stayed," he said.

Sylvanas faced him.

"She delivered the order, then told the Convocation's courier to go to hell in words delicate enough to survive transcription. She's upstairs with the mages."

Sylvanas's hand closed once against her own palm.

"The verdict stands," Lor'themar said. "The doors do not open tonight. But there are witnesses now."

Yes. Outside pressure. Witnesses. More than nothing.

From somewhere above came the same wrong hitch in the cold.

Lor'themar missed it; she caught that at once. But he caught her reaction. He had learned that much over years together.

"Days?" he asked.

"Less if the wards fail early."

He accepted it without flinch. "Then I won't spend the night arguing the verdict."

"No."

"I'll spend it on the blade."

Better.

Bootsteps sounded at the corner again. Human this time. Impatient. Someone coming to end privileges before they turned into precedent.

Lor'themar caught them and did not release her wrist until the last possible moment.

"Hold," he said.

She almost told him not to insult her. There were only so many words worth spending in a night this bad.

"Go," she said instead.

His hand left hers. Cold took the skin at once.

He stepped back as the guard rounded the corner with a lantern lifted high and annoyance already arranged on his face. Farther down the wing, elven shadows waited where the humans had permitted waiting and no farther.

Lor'themar gave the guard a look that promised future trouble in formal clothing. Then he turned back to Sylvanas one last time.

Rescue would come later, if it came at all. Tonight was for witnesses in the hall and mages near the vault.

The guard started to speak. Lor'themar turned away before the first word left him. Road dust still clung to his boots as he headed up the corridor toward the cold above.

Sylvanas stayed at the bars until the torchlight swallowed him.

Then she crossed to the wall and set her hand against the stone between her and Jaina.

On the other side came three light taps.
