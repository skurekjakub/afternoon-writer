The cold came up through the floor.

Sylvanas learned the cell in eight paces because there was nothing else to do with her body that did not look like waiting. Three long steps from door to back wall. Two across. Cot bolted to stone. Bucket in the corner. A slit of a grate high in the corridor side where torchlight striped the opposite wall and never reached the floor whole. Iron at her wrists, lighter than field shackles and more insulting for it.

The place should have smelled of old damp, rust, piss soaked into mortar over twenty years of palace shame.

It smelled like frost instead—sharper than winter air, meaner than river ice, thin as metal on the tongue. It threaded up through the seams between the stones and lay over the dungeon stink until even the blanket on the cot carried it. When she put her hand flat to the floor the chill bit her palm from below, as if the season had turned under the foundations and was climbing.

Above them.

She could not see the vault, only read the building around it. Torches in the corridor burned with mean little flames that leaned and guttered for no draft she could hear. Guards crossed themselves under their breath when they passed this wing, or quickened their step without admitting to it. Once, farther down, a man laughed too loudly at nothing and the sound cut off as if somebody had put a hand over his mouth.

The blade had gone into a box and was still teaching the palace its name.

Through the wall on her left came the only useful thing left in the world: Jaina's breathing.

Too faint for human ears through that much masonry. Easy enough for hers. Uneven. Deeper when the pain backed off for a handful of breaths, shorter when it caught her again. Beneath it, when the torch in the corridor smoked just right, the stale bakery smell that clung to Jaina after hard spellwork still made it through the mortar. Bread, ash, old wool. Proof.

Sylvanas sat on the cot at last because standing changed nothing and closed her eyes to listen better.

In. Hold. Out.

The rhythm faltered. Resettled. A chain moved once with a small iron click.

Alive.

Bootsteps stopped outside her door. A key turned. Two guards came in with a clerk between them, the man already uncapping his ink with chilled fingers. Palace officer, not soldier. Clean cuffs. A narrow board tucked under one arm. He looked at the cell once, looked at Sylvanas, and rearranged his face into the sort of patience people wear when duty has given them something tedious.

"Ranger-General Windrunner," he said.

"Former, according to the corridor."

One guard's mouth twitched. The clerk did not smile. "Your statement will be taken for the tribunal."

"Will it be heard there?"

"That depends on its contents."

No. It depended on whether the room wanted the truth before the truth arrived.

He sat on the stool the guards had brought and balanced the board on his knees. The scrape of his pen started before his first question finished. Name. Rank. Nation. Purpose in the capital. Why she and Jaina Proudmoore had entered the royal chamber armed. Why the prince and king lay dead when the doors opened.

Written before he came in. She could hear it in the order, in the way each question already leaned toward its answer.

So she told him the whole thing.

Millhaven. Andorhal. Scholomance. Stratholme's smoke. Northrend. Muradin. The sword. Arthas home with death nailed into him and a blade that closed wounds as fast as arrows made them. The king already dead when they entered. The seam in Frostmourne's field. Frost first, then arcane, then fire. The final shot through the eye.

The pen scratched hard for the first part. Slowed at Stratholme. Slowed again at Northrend. By the time she reached the sword singing after Arthas hit the steps, the clerk's mouth had gone flat with the patience people wear when they believe madness is choosing them for an audience.

This is what a liar chooses when she needs the lie too large to answer.

He kept writing because procedure required the motions of fairness even after belief had died. One guard shifted his weight from attention to boredom. The other watched Sylvanas's mouth as if waiting for the point where the madness would show on it.

When she finished, the silence in the cell held for three breaths.

The clerk blew on the page. "You claim," he said carefully, "that Crown Prince Arthas Menethil had been corrupted by an enchanted blade from Northrend."

"I do not claim it. I watched it."

"And Archmage Proudmoore will corroborate this account?"

Through the wall, Jaina's chain touched stone. Once.

"Ask her."

The clerk's gaze slid left, toward the masonry between them, then back. "In time."

"You have the wrong order."

That got his eyes up. Good. "Explain."

"If the sword is still in the palace, whatever you think of me is smaller than that."

His pen hesitated. One of the guards made a sound like a swallowed laugh.

"The weapon is secured," the clerk said.

"So was Arthas."

That landed. Not enough. But it landed. She saw him feel the sentence and refuse it. The good officers were always harder to move. They mistook the steadiness of their own hands for steadiness in the world.

He sanded the wet lines, shook the page dry, and stood. "Your statement has been entered."

Entered the wrong mouth, she thought.

The key turned again after they left. Iron settled. The corridor swallowed their footsteps. Through the wall came another faint chain-sound, then Jaina's voice low enough that a human guard at the door would hear only a change in the stones.

"Sylvanas."

She got off the cot and knelt by the left wall, ear almost to the mortar. "Here."

For a moment there was only breath, rough and measured. Then: "It's looking."

Two words. Nothing in them except the way Jaina spent them.

Sylvanas put her palm to the stone. Cold had thickened there. "At whom?"

"Don't know yet." A swallow. "Not random."

The floor seemed to dip under her for half a heartbeat, though she was kneeling still. "Can you hear that clearly?"

"Clear enough."

"How long?"

Jaina did not answer at once. Sylvanas pictured her without wanting to: back to the wall, wrists chained, face gone white with the effort of sitting upright near that thing while the palace called her accomplice. When the voice came again it was flatter.

"Shorter than it was."

"How much shorter?"

"I need time."

There it was. Not enough of it to spend.

Sylvanas leaned her forehead briefly to the stone. Stupid gesture. It gave her nothing except the colder bite of the wall. "Then count."

"I am."

The silence after that held only breath and stone. From above, so faint at first she might have taken it for imagination if Jaina had not spoken, a wrong little pulse moved through the foundations. A hitch in the cold. A tiny tug under the bones of the building, as if something overhead were testing doors with patient hands.

Sylvanas opened her eyes.

The law was one prison. The blade above them was another, and hungrier.

Hours later men came with chains meant for daylight.

They took her up narrow service stairs that smelled of lamp oil and damp wool, past two doors where armed paladins stood without speaking, past one corridor she knew by the sudden drop in warmth beneath her soles. The vault was there. Even through stone and oak and lead and wards, Frostmourne breathed on the passage hard enough to raise cloud from every mouth.

Jaina climbed the stairs ahead of her between two guards. Her hair had been braided back again by rougher hands than her own. One side of her face still showed the fading bruise where the sword's guard had struck in the chamber, and she moved with the careful straightness of somebody holding pain in by command. She did not look back. She did not need to. The small set of her shoulders told Sylvanas everything worth having.

The tribunal hall had once been built to make kings seem inevitable.

Long windows stood high enough that the light came down in pale shafts, leaving the walls in a soft dusk where banners hung without stirring. The stone underfoot had been polished by a century of ceremony. At the far end a raised bench of dark wood carried three seats, though only the center one was occupied. Thornton sat there with papers laid square before him and a pen aligned to the edge of the desk. Silver hair. Spare face. A man who had not slept enough and had still tied every knot on his robes correctly. Too careful to be dismissed as a butcher. Worse for them than that.

Ashcombe stood below him to the right in black trimmed with the blue of palace law, elegant as a duelist. No armor. No wasted cloth either. She held one glove in her off hand and turned it finger by finger while the guards settled the accused into their chairs.

Chairs instead of a dock or bare floor.

The chains made less noise for that kindness, and still said the same thing.

Jaina was placed to Sylvanas's right with one guard behind her and one at the aisle. Across the center of the chamber stood a heavy oak table banded in iron. On it sat the chest.

Lead-lined, if the smell and weight of the room were anything to judge by. Wards painted along the rim in silver paste already gone black in one corner. Even shut, it drew warmth out of the air. The clerk at Thornton's elbow kept flexing his fingers over his notes to keep them loose.

Ashcombe began without raising her voice.

That was the first clean cut.

Months of investigation. Months of warnings. Months of travel beyond proper authority, of covert entry, of hidden correspondence, of contact with foreign magical orders, of unsanctioned movement between plague sites and military theaters. She took every true thing the road had contained and turned it half a degree. Enough.

Scholomance became prior knowledge. Northrend became pursuit of a weapon, not a prince. Relay reports became preparation. The speed with which Sylvanas and Jaina had reached correct conclusions became proof they had known the course of events in advance. Even Stratholme served her. Only people already bent toward regicide, Ashcombe suggested, would stand so near repeated royal disasters and still be found armed over the bodies at the end.

Clean. Beautiful, even, in the way a trap sometimes was when the maker respected the spring.

Sylvanas watched Thornton while Ashcombe spoke. The prosecutor's hands were not the room. Thornton listened with his head tilted slightly down, one finger resting beside a line in the papers as if checking each step of the chain against the record. Competent. Calm. He did not want nonsense. He wanted sequence. Ashcombe fed him one.

Once, when she said Northrend, Jaina's breath caught audibly enough for Sylvanas to hear it from two chairs away. She kept her face forward. Good.

Ashcombe finished with the throne room itself. Armed entry. A dead king. A dead prince. An enchanted sword known of in suspicious detail by the accused before any royal scholar had examined it. Two women who had every reason to silence a prince returned from a ruinous northern expedition and prevent him speaking first.

The gallery murmured then. Not loud. Just enough cloth and throat and bench-creak to say the story had found somewhere to land.

Thornton lifted one hand. The room went still at once.

"Call the exhibit."

Four palace mages brought the chest to the center table as if carrying a bier. They were sweating already though the room sat cold. One had tied a strip of linen around his dominant hand where blisters showed at the thumb web. The silver along the lid hissed when the clasps were turned. When the top opened a finger's breadth, half the chamber inhaled together.

Cold rolled out first.

It passed low over the floor and climbed the chair legs. Torchlight thinned. Breath fogged all at once from the benches to the bench above. The nearest clerk's ink filmed over at the lip.

Then the lid was raised and the wrongness in the room found its edge.

Frostmourne lay in lead and black velvet like a saint's bone set out for prayer.

The crack near the seam showed plainly now. Thin, bright, ugly. Light breathed through it in little starving pulses, too weak to illuminate the blade and strong enough to keep dragging the eye back.

Somebody in the gallery whispered a prayer.

Sylvanas looked to Jaina despite herself.

The mage's hands had closed white on the arms of the chair. No tremor in them now. Worse. The tremor had gone into stillness too hard held. Her mouth had flattened. There was a sheen of sweat at her hairline though the air bit like late winter. She was listening somewhere farther in than the room.

Ashcombe saw it and smiled with one corner of her mouth.

"Describe for the tribunal," she said to the first palace mage, "what the accused claimed regarding this object."

The man wet his lips. "That it exerts corruptive influence, my lady. That it appears responsive. That the crack sustained in the throne room damaged but did not extinguish its enchantment."

"And what have you yourself observed?"

"Temperature collapse in any chamber where the object remains for long. Ward degradation. Disturbance to ordinary magical instruments. Emotional agitation among unwarded staff."

"Emotional agitation."

"Yes, my lady."

"In those already familiar with the weapon's alleged properties, would such agitation be more pronounced?"

He hesitated. Not because he did not know. Because he knew perfectly well what answer was wanted and disliked how near it stood to the truth. "It could be."

Ashcombe turned, graceful as ever. "So the one who knows the blade best will appear the most affected by it."

Jaina finally looked at her then. Her face had gone too still for pleading or outrage. The look was all stripped calculation and pain.

Sylvanas wanted a bow in her hand badly enough to taste it.

They kept the chest open too long. Perhaps Ashcombe knew what she was doing. Perhaps she only knew fear was useful and wanted the room steeped in it. Either way the silver paste at the lid corners darkened further while the mages stood by it with their shoulders creeping higher and higher. One of Thornton's clerks began rubbing the heel of his hand against his breastbone as if cold had lodged there.

By the time Uther was called, the room was ready to believe anything that put blame outside itself.

He came to the witness chair in a plain surcoat instead of armor, white and gold dulled by travel and old grief. No helm. No hammer. Just a man too broad for the furniture and too tired for pretense. Yet when he swore to speak, the words landed differently from everything before. Even the gallery felt it. Sylvanas heard the change in the silence.

He gave them Hearthglen. Stratholme. Arthas's growing refusal of counsel. Northrend. Obsession. The return. He spoke like a paladin and a soldier both—plain, grave, no ornament that was not required by the size of the dead.

Then the chamber. Too late. Terenas already gone. Arthas standing with Frostmourne in hand. Jaina and Sylvanas between the prince and whatever else he meant to kill. The damaged blade. The final arrow. Custody after.

For a few precious minutes the case Ashcombe had built looked like what it was: craft. Sylvanas watched Thornton's eyes lift from the papers more than once. Watched one gallery lord lean forward with his hands clasped so tight the knuckles showed bone through skin. Uther was not spectacular. He was worse for the prosecution than that. He was clear.

Ashcombe rose for cross-examination and ruined nothing.

She only changed the light.

Yes, Lord Uther had opposed the prince at Stratholme. Yes, Lord Uther had been removed from command after that dispute. Yes, Lord Uther loved the dead king and had once tutored the dead prince. Yes, Lord Uther had not seen Terenas die. No, Lord Uther had not seen Arthas strike the first blow in the throne room. No, Lord Uther had not seen who first entered that chamber or under what pretext, because he arrived after the killing.

Each answer true. Each answer laid in place like another stone on a grave.

When Ashcombe asked whether grief and prior conflict might have colored his interpretation of the scene he found, Uther's hands closed once on the witness rail so hard the wood gave a small complaining sound.

"I know what I saw," he said.

"After the act."

"After the king's death, yes."

"With two armed operatives of foreign powers in the chamber."

"With two women standing between the kingdom and a damned weapon."

The room pulled tight. Good. For one heartbeat Sylvanas thought the blow had landed cleanly.

Ashcombe bowed her head as if in respect. "A weapon you did not witness taking His Majesty's life."

There it was. The missing twenty minutes. Nothing in all Northrend had done half so much damage.

Uther did not bend. He left the chair with the same rigid control he had brought to it, and if Thornton's mouth had tightened, it did not tighten enough.

From the gallery, blue caught Sylvanas's eye.

Antonidas stood three rows up with two Kirin Tor magi at his shoulders. The old man's robes hung clean, but the body inside them looked used to the bone, exhaustion nailed through with purpose. He had come in person. Worse luck for everyone. Presence drew hope the way blood drew flies.

When he rose to object, the whole chamber noticed.

"This tribunal has before it an artifact no palace school is equipped to evaluate," he said. His voice carried on training, not strength. "Grant continuance. Permit controlled examination by Dalaran before you fix a narrative around a weapon none of you understand."

Thornton's gaze did not leave the bench. "By whose authority, Archmage?"

That stopped him for half a breath.

Not because he lacked one. Because the one he needed had not been given.

"By mine," Antonidas said at last.

Ashcombe moved before Thornton answered. "Unsanctioned contact by a foreign order with the central exhibit in a regicide hearing would be tampering, not aid."

One of the magi behind Antonidas had gone pale the moment the chest opened. He looked at the sword now as if it were whispering behind his teeth. The other kept her jaw set hard enough to ache from here. Neither stepped forward.

Thornton denied the continuance.

Antonidas remained standing one breath longer than etiquette allowed. Then he sat because he had reached the edge of whatever leash Dalaran had put around his throat and found it iron.

Jaina never turned toward him.

That hurt more to watch than if she had.

Vereesa arrived with a sealed document.

No herald's flourish. Just the clerk naming Ranger Captain Vereesa Windrunner and a hush running the benches before she even reached the witness place. She wore field leathers under a travel-stained cloak pinned with the Windrunner crest. Road dust along the hem. Hair tied back simple and hard. The palace had not had time to polish her into ceremony, and the lack of it made every face in the room sharpen.

Family should not have been in this.

The Convocation had made certain it was.

Vereesa broke the seal with steady hands. Only those. Her jaw was too tight. Her eyes did not go to Sylvanas until the last possible instant and even then only once, quick as a knife glint.

She read the disavowal in a voice so formal it bled.

Unauthorized action beyond sovereign borders. Expired military authority. No recognized alliance, obligation, or debt arising from the acts of the former Ranger-General and those operating under her command. Quel'Thalas would not shelter, answer for, or defend.

Every word chosen by people who had not ridden the road and would sleep easily for that.

The room listened to the state, not the sister.

But Sylvanas knew Vereesa's breathing. Knew the minute hitch before one sentence. Knew what it cost her to keep the paper from shaking. The Convocation had sent blood because blood bruised deeper. Efficient. Contemptible. Very nearly admirable for that, if admiration were not wasted on snakes.

When Vereesa finished, she folded the document once with such exact care that the motion itself became violence.

Ashcombe thanked her.

Vereesa stepped back from the chair and did not leave the hall.

That was the first crack that mattered.

Thornton wrote for some time after the last submission. No one spoke above a whisper. Frostmourne sat in its chest and made the whole chamber steam at the mouth like cattle in slaughter weather. Jaina had begun to count against her thumb with one chained hand, tiny pressures Sylvanas might not have seen if she had not been looking there already.

Intervals.

When the magistrate finally raised his head, the room had gone beyond silence into appetite.

"On the charges of conspiracy against the Crown, unlawful foreign armed action within the royal seat, and the murders of His Majesty King Terenas Menethil and Crown Prince Arthas Menethil—"

Jaina's counting stopped.

"—this tribunal finds the accused guilty."

The word crossed the room and everything in it moved a fraction toward it. Guards tightened chains. Gallery breath released. Somebody sobbed once and hid it in a sleeve. Thornton kept reading, voice level, carrying each count, each finding, each careful justification built from true premises turned rotten by sequence.

Death would make martyrs. The political situation made martyrs expensive. Therefore sentence was commuted. Indefinite imprisonment under royal and ecclesiastical authority pending further determination and the stabilization of all circumstances surrounding the enchanted weapon.

Storage, dressed in the robes of mercy.

The chain bit her wrists before the verdict reached any softer place. Metal tugged as the guards prepared to move. Across from her, Jaina's face had gone blank with the sort of shock that came not from surprise but from a body's sudden refusal to spend more reaction on one disaster when a larger one was already at the door.

They were hauled down again before the hall had fully emptied.

Past the vault corridor. Past the cold.

Back into stone boxes under a kingdom that had just named its own saviors regicides.

This time no clerk came. No questions. Only the key, the shove, the lock.

For a while the dungeon held nothing except bootsteps receding and the ragged noise of two people getting their bodies back under them on opposite sides of a wall.

Then—tap.

Sylvanas got off the cot at once and went to the stone.

Tap. Tap. Pause.

Another pattern. Faster. Not random. She put two fingers against the mortar and listened through them.

Jaina tapped again, then spoke, voice worn raw. "Days."

"Certain?"

"Enough."

Days.

The long ugly stretch a court order might imagine while men wrote letters and filed seals and prayed over lead had already burned away.

Days.

The air had grown colder since the verdict, not warmer. As if the blade above had heard the law conclude and taken it for permission. Sylvanas could smell frost in the blanket now even from where she stood. The torch beyond the grate burned low and blue at its base.

"Count it again," she said.

"I am."

There was a long pause. Then, quieter: "Elven mages might feel it."

Sylvanas opened her eyes in the dark though she had not remembered closing them. "From the vault?"

"From the foundations, maybe. Better than humans."

Something like a plan moved under her ribs and would not fit there properly. Small, useless, still alive. "Good."

Jaina laughed once without humor and stopped because it hurt. "That wasn't optimism."

"I know."

The wall stayed cold between them. She kept her hand on it anyway.

Much later—late enough that the corridor had gone to low voices and the kitchen smells of the upper palace were long dead—new footsteps came down the wing.

Elven, not palace.

She knew the cadence before the guard at the corner barked a warning. Lighter on the heel. Cleaner through the ball of the foot. Weight carried by people used to ground that answered back. Elven boots after road miles. More than one set, then fewer as somebody with rank was allowed farther in than the rest.

Sylvanas was already at the bars when Lor'themar came into the torchlight.

Road dust on his cloak. Hair in its military tail, half-fallen from hard riding. No humor anywhere on his face. That hurt more than the dust. He stopped one pace from the grate as if the iron itself had struck him.

For a moment neither of them spoke.

He looked thinner than when she had last seen him. Worn down to the edges. The nose-bridge rub she had noticed months ago had worn a faint rawness at the skin. His eyes moved once over the cell, the chains, her face, and sharpened on the bruise along her temple from Arthas's blow.

"You look terrible," he said.

Her mouth nearly went the old way by reflex. "Court thought otherwise."

Nothing. Not even the shadow of the right-corner smile.

"Vereesa told me enough to ride before the vote finished," he said. "Not enough to get here before it did."

There it was. Too late carried plain.

"How many?"

"Three hundred Farstriders. Two dozen mages." He glanced once up, not toward the vault exactly but toward the weight of the palace above them. "The mages felt it in the paving outside the lower court. They thought at first the city's wards were failing. They were wrong."

Good.

Horrible. Good.

"Can they testify?"

"Tomorrow, perhaps. If there is a room left willing to hear." Dryness flickered then, a spark with no warmth in it. "Humans improve markedly once condemned women become other people's problem."

That at least sounded like him, scraped bloody.

From the cell beside hers came the drag of chain and then Jaina's voice, thin through stone. "Lor'themar?"

He turned at once toward the wall, not the bars, as if he could see through it by force. "Lady Proudmoore."

"Days," she said. Just that.

Lor'themar's head shifted a fraction. The field officer in him taking the number, not the fear around it. "Understood."

"Your mages feel it?"

"Enough to swear to wrongness. Not enough to name it. Yet."

"Faster," Jaina said. The word nearly frayed apart on the last breath. "It's getting faster."

Lor'themar put one hand through the bars. Sylvanas gave him her wrist because the chains did not allow much else. His grip closed around the bone above the iron—callused, cold from the corridor, familiar as a bowstring scar. The only contact the chapter of the day had left her.

"I should have been here sooner," he said, low now, for her alone.

"You are here."

"An inspiring standard."

"Use what you brought."

His thumb pressed once against the inside of her wrist, where the pulse ran. Field shorthand. Still alive. Still in command enough to be unbearable. Good.

"Vereesa stayed," he said.

Sylvanas looked at him.

"She delivered the order," Lor'themar went on. "Then she told the Convocation's courier to go to hell in terms delicate enough to survive transcription. She's abovestairs with the mages."

Something hard and old in Sylvanas shifted a finger's width.

"The verdict stands," Lor'themar said. "The doors do not open tonight. But the room has changed."

Yes. Outside reinforcement without release. Not enough. More than nothing.

From somewhere above them, very faint through a palace full of stone, came the wrong little hitch in the cold.

Lor'themar gave no sign of hearing that part himself; she saw it at once. But he saw her hear it. Saw the way her head angled, the way the muscles at the back of her jaw tightened. Years together had taught him which silence meant arrow-flight, which meant bad ground, which meant an enemy neither of them had expected.

"Days?" he asked.

"Less than that if the wards fail early."

He accepted it without flinch or argument. Another field habit. "Then I won't spend the night arguing the verdict."

"No."

"I'll spend it on the blade."

Better.

Bootsteps sounded at the corner again. Human this time. Impatient. Somebody coming to end privileges before they looked like precedent.

Lor'themar heard them and did not release her wrist until the last possible breath.

"Hold," he said.

She almost told him not to insult her. Did not. There were only so many words worth saying in a night like this.

"Go," she said instead.

His hand left hers. The cold took the skin immediately where it had been.

He stepped back from the grate as the guard rounded the corner with a lantern raised high and annoyance already arranged on his face. Farther down the wing elven shadows moved under torchlight—three of them, maybe four, waiting where the humans had permitted waiting and no farther.

Lor'themar gave the guard a look that promised future trouble in formal clothing. Then he looked at Sylvanas one last time.

Rescue would come later, if it came at all.

Corroboration. Witness. Pressure from outside the wall.

Enough to make the next hours matter.

The guard started to speak. Lor'themar turned away before the man could spend the first word. His boots carried the road with them as he went, the Farstrider cadence fading up the corridor toward the cold above.

Sylvanas stayed at the bars until the torchlight swallowed him.

Then she crossed to the wall and put her hand against the stone between her and Jaina.

On the other side, after a moment, came three light taps.

Counting still.
