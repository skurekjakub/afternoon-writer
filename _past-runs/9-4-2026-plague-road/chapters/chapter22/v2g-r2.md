# Chapter 22: The Return

Baelgun's camp sat in a snowfold below Muradin's wall, lower and meaner than the fighting line itself. No carved gate. No challenge trench. Just a yard stamped hard by dwarf boots and stretcher drags, two hide roofs lashed low against the Dragonblight wind, a forge trench breathing sparks into daylight, and dwarves already coming at the Farstriders as soon as they cleared the last drift.

The final slope reached Jaina in broken pieces. Muradin's weight had pulled Sylvanas and the two rangers one fraction short of their usual stride. Cyndia kept ranging ahead and back through the dragon-bone country, making a road by force of habit where Dragonblight offered none. Jaina had come beside them with both hands bare in the killing cold, tracing the same crooked hitch through Frostmourne's field until her fingers shook too badly to hold the shape. Now the yard opened under them and dwarf camp labor came fast over trampled snow and frozen sled grooves.

"Here," Baelgun said.

He was broader than Muradin and older about the eyes, beard split by iron clasps gone white with rime. He took one look at the blood stiffening Muradin's leathers, one at the drag in his breath, and stepped in before anyone finished speaking.

"Get him under. Now. You two, shoulders. Mind the left side. If ye jar that again I'll use your ribs for tent pegs."

The dwarves answered the way field dwarves answered everything worth doing: with hands already on it. Muradin went out of Sylvanas's grip, out of the rangers' hold, onto a hide stretcher dragged over from the yard wall. His head rolled once toward Jaina. Blood had thawed in his beard braids and frozen again there, dark under the frost.

Speak dwarf.

She had. Too late, but she had.

Baelgun caught her looking and read enough off her face. "Alive," he said. "That's the word ye get. More than that belongs to the healers and whatever gods still answer this place."

Jaina tried to answer and found her throat gone dry from cold and breath and everything the cave had left in it. Sylvanas did it for her.

"Can you keep him?"

"Aye." Baelgun took them in once: elf commander, half-spent mage, Cyndia with her bow still in hand, three rangers crusted in cave frost and old blood. "Can ye stay?"

No one said yes. The forge snapped behind him. Wind skimmed the timber lip of the yard and brought the glacier's iron taste down with it.

Baelgun snorted. "Thought not."

Muradin vanished through the nearest hide flap. The leather dropped. A bucket rang once inside, then healer voices took him.

Warmth reached Jaina a heartbeat later, leaking from the forge trench and the open treatment shelter beside it. Real heat. The first true heat since the galley stove on the *Wolfheart* and bodies packed close against the crossing north. Her hands hurt at once. Her knees nearly folded with it.

"Inside," one of the dwarves said, already hauling a second flap wide. "Hot drink. Sit before ye fall."

Jaina's fingers had gone back to the air before she knew they had moved. Three short marks. Hook. Pause. Again.

Baelgun followed the gesture. "That from the cave?"

"Yes."

"Useful?"

"Maybe."

"Then take it south." He jerked his chin toward the white waste beyond the yard wall. "Whatever put my commander on a stretcher has the prince now. If ye're the only fools left breathing who understand any of it, my camp won't help by warming ye."

Sylvanas stood with one hand still flexing after Muradin's weight. Frost had dried white over the leather of her bracer and gone chalk-pale in the seams. "How long before the weather closes the trail back to Daggercap Bay?"

"Long enough if ye go now. Not long enough if ye wait for comfort." His stare cut to Jaina. "Can she walk?"

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

Baelgun kept looking at Jaina. "Can she keep walking?"

Jaina made herself lift her head. "Yes."

"Then walk. We'll keep Muradin if keeping's possible. You carry the rest."

They left the camp behind.

Cyndia was already turning, signaling the rangers back into file. A waterskin thumped into Jaina's palm. Another dwarf shoved wrapped bread at Sylvanas and got it taken without ceremony. Behind the hide flaps, Muradin's world became awl, needle, hot water, and dwarven hands. The flap stirred once under hurried movement, then settled.

Jaina looked once at the treatment shelter. The hide flap lifted under hurried hands, then dropped flat and left only bucket noise and healer voices behind it.

Then the Dragonblight wind got under her hood, and she saw the switchback above Daggercap Bay as cleanly as if she were already on it: black water below, the *Wolfheart* at the planks, tar biting through the cold.

They left without taking the offered bench. Six strides later the camp heat was gone.

Muradin vanished behind hide and timber. The cave behind that. The hitch in Frostmourne's field kept pace with her walk, turning over between one bootfall and the next.

On the way in there had been a prince to catch and a cave to reach. On the way back there were only dragon ribs humped under snow, shattered vertebrae white as ruined pillars, powder hissing into their eyes, and the long trudge through open bone-country toward the bay.

Sylvanas ranged half a pace ahead, reading footing and drift line because someone had to. Cyndia moved on the left. The rangers traded rear and right flank without being told. Jaina stayed where they had left room for her and kept listening.

The turn again.

Away from the blade it was thinner and easier to hear. In the cave it had flattened every other thought. Out here, with nothing but cold, distance, and the drag of the march, she could hold one part of it still long enough to hear what came before and after.

Take.

Throw.

A hitch she could count through clenched teeth.

She wrote it in the air. Three short marks for the inward pull. The hook for the turn. The pause where the rhythm changed hands.

Bread rose warm in the back of her throat. Yeast. Salt. A loaf split near a hearth.

It had been a flare on the northbound crossing once, a warning that came when she pushed too hard and faded when she stopped. She could no longer pretend it belonged only to the work. Now the smell sat on her skin even in Dragonblight's killing cold, thin but constant, as wrong there as lamplight in a crypt. Her hands shook whether she cast or not.

Kel'Thuzad would have loved this.

She stumbled.

He was monstrous; that part was easy. Powder snow hissed across her face and blood salted her tongue while she pictured him bent over the hitch in the field, forgetting his own pulse for the neatness of it. He would have gone straight for the turn, for the point where horror became elegant enough to study.

So had she.

She kept the pattern in her head anyway.

Jaina licked blood from her split lip and kept walking.

"You're favoring the left," Sylvanas said without looking back.

"I know."

"That was not a request for confirmation."

Jaina spent a beat catching up to the sentence through cold and rhythm. "My right hand is worse."

"Both are worse."

Her split lip lifted at one corner. "Thank you."

Ten steps of snow-crust and dragon-bone shadow passed before Sylvanas answered, drier still. "You are welcome."

By midday the cold had worked through every seam in her clothes. Snow glare turned the whole waste viciously bright. The bread-smell thickened until even Cyndia's head tipped a fraction from the left flank. One ranger drifted nearer. Another closed the gap behind Jaina. No one named it.

When the pull in Frostmourne's field lined up cleanly with the rhythm of her steps, Jaina understood one shade more.

She wanted a flaw that split and held still long enough to hit.

If the blade had a weakness plain enough for brute force, Muradin would have found it months ago or Arthas would have died on the first swing. The weakness lived in the turn itself, the instant when the weapon had to be both states at once.

The taking had to open into throwing for one instant, and that exposed seam was the only place she could catch.

Something built on frost would catch the turn. Arcane could go through while it was open. And fire—

Fire could drive through the opened seam and turn the binding against itself.

She stopped dead in the file.

Snow hissed under the others' boots before they halted.

Sylvanas turned. "What?"

Jaina had one hand locked around the other wrist to quiet the tremor. "Three schools."

The words came faster because if she slowed down she might lose the sequence back into the wind. "The turn. Frost catches it. Arcane goes through while it's open. Then fire before it closes again. To change the binding from the inside."

No one answered at first. Cyndia's mare edged half a stride closer. Her bow hand stayed loose, and she kept Jaina in the same watch lane she used for bad treeline.

Sylvanas said, "Can you do it?"

"I can build it," Jaina said. "Doing it at sword range without dying is a different question."

"That is later."

Yes. Later. If later still existed by the time they reached Daggercap Bay, the *Wolfheart*, the southbound water, Lordaeron again.

They moved.

The last stretch back to the harbor came a step at a time: shin-deep crust giving underfoot, frozen sled grooves catching boots, lungs burning raw in the cold. By the time the cliffs above Daggercap Bay came into view, Jaina's knees almost folded on the downslope.

Daggercap looked worse in retreat.

Daylight laid the harbor bare again: black wreckage frozen into the edge, hacked docks, ropes gone stiff as iron, old burn scars in the pilings where Arthas had spent ships like kindling. Below, the *Wolfheart* was already being turned with handspikes, curses, and shouted seamanship. Crew swarmed the lines. Ice knocked loose from the pilings in cracked plates. Dagny Ironhull stood in the middle of it all with both gloves tucked through her belt, her voice carrying flat and hard over the bay.

She looked up once as they came down the switchback.

"No blankets," she barked before anyone reached the planks. "No hammocks. No one lies down unless they're dead enough to stop arguing. Harl, loose those lines. Bren, now. If the mage falls over, feed her first and ask questions after."

Jaina hit the dock and got Daggercap all at once: tar, kelp, torn pitch, old char, and under it the sweeter wrongness of her own skin after too many days of strain. Dagny came close, read her once in captain's measure, and her mouth flattened.

"You look bad," the captain said.

"Thank you."

"Ship measure." Dagny turned to Sylvanas. "Can she still push?"

"Enough," Sylvanas said.

"We'll see."

Before the schooner cleared the outer ice, they spent every warning they had.

Sylvanas wrote the Farstrider version first because hers was the faster hand for urgency stripped of all argument: prince returned, blade corrupted, palace danger, do not trust the celebration. The rangers tied brass cylinders with numbed fingers while carrier birds fought the wicker dark in their cages. One by one the pigeons went up over the frozen water, pale bodies flinging themselves south across a reach too wide to care.

Jaina took the stone.

Amber lit her hand, weak and familiar and terrible for that. Antonidas answered on the third pulse, too fast for a man caught unready.

"Jaina."

Only her name.

"We've been to the source," she said. "Or part of it. Arthas has Frostmourne. The blade is built on the same plague magic."

"You are too late to warn the city," Antonidas said. The voice that came through the stone had travel in it now, not tower air: frayed, thinner, rubbed down by argument and bad roads. "He entered Capital City four days ago. The court thinks he ended the threat in Northrend. There were bells. Processions."

Behind her, one of Sylvanas's birds clawed free of a ranger's hands and beat south over the black water.

Jaina gripped the stone harder. "And you?"

"In the city. With two magi. Nothing official enough to matter. Uther is here as well and no one who should listen is listening." A breath. Then, plainer: "Tell me you have more than warning."

She looked out over Daggercap's ruined mouth shrinking behind them, then toward Sylvanas three paces away already reading sail angle as if the sea were another contested field.

"Maybe," she said.

"Jaina."

"I have a way at it." The words steadied as she gave them shape. "Three parts. Frost, arcane, fire. The blade turns from taking to throwing. It leaves one exposed instant between the two."

"And the cost?"

Too much, she thought. Out loud: "I don't know yet."

Open water took the amber light quickly.

"Come fast," Antonidas said. "That is all they'll still permit."

The stone died in her hand before she could answer.

They crossed anyway.

Nothing supernatural tried to shoulder the schooner broadside on the way south. No impossible headwind. No source-spite reaching over the sea to slow them. Which meant every extra knot came out of Jaina instead.

Dagny worked the *Wolfheart* the way a hard captain worked any strained rig: sail angle, hull line, timing, line discipline. Jaina stood at the stern when ordered, one hand braced against the rail slick with spray and old salt, feeding just enough frost and force into the wind to keep canvas full without tearing the rigging apart. Then food shoved into her hands because the next push would be worthless if she shook herself empty before dusk. Then down again when blood started dripping over the deck planks.

"Count to twenty," Sylvanas said the first time.

Jaina, kneeling by the stern ladder with blood on the back of her hand and salt in her mouth, said, "That won't fix it."

"No. It will stop you lying."

Twenty was a long way when the deck would not hold still. She made it to sixteen before Dagny's mate pushed dried fish at her face and told her to chew or be thrown overboard as deadweight. She chewed.

By the second night Jaina could still feel the day's work humming through the timbers after her hands left the rail.

"Again," Dagny said at sunset, watching the bellies of the sails and the black water ahead. "Short this time. I need another mile before dark and not a corpse on my deck."

Short this time. As though the bakery smell rising off Jaina's skin were the real trouble.

Jaina laughed once at that, soft and humorless, and put her hand back on the rail.

Nosebleed by dusk. Again by moonrise. Again the next morning. The blood ran bright, then went black where salt dried it into the grain of the boards. Dagny ignored it until the fourth time.

"Someone keep wiping that up," she snapped at nobody in particular. "My crew do not need to start thinking the ship wants sacrifices."

Cyndia did it herself, crouching with a rag and a face gone blank. One ranger brought water. Another stayed close enough that if Jaina pitched sideways under the next push, she would not crack her skull on the capstan base.

At night the coil of rope under Jaina shifted with every roll. The mast knocked once above her, the hull slapped black water below, and she had to brace her shoulder to the timber before she could force the answer into plainer words.

The blade turned from taking to throwing.

Freeze the turn with frost.

Arcane through the gap.

Then fire before it closed.

Sylvanas sat beside her through most of the second night, close enough for Jaina to speak the sequence aloud and hear where it broke on actual use.

"Three to five seconds," Jaina said into the dark. "Less and the arcane won't make it through. More and the whole turn settles and throws me back out."

"At sword range."

"Yes."

"While you hold still."

"Yes."

Timbers creaked under them. Far forward, a block knocked once against the mast and went quiet.

"Then the question isn't the spell," Sylvanas said.

"Who buys the seconds?"

Sylvanas's hand had found the tar-stiff rope beside them without seeming to mean to. Her fingers tightened once, then eased.

Below them black water slapped the hull, and the line bit across Sylvanas's palm. Neither woman said anything.

Southshore rose out of morning mist in ordinary pieces: piers, gulls, slant-roofed cottages, nets drying on frames, a road running inland through fields still innocent of what was already living in the palace at Capital City.

Jaina stepped off the gangplank and her knees gave.

The moment she stopped feeding the sails, the spent strength hit all at once.

The dock rushed toward her face.

Sylvanas's hand came up without quite taking her weight. Close enough to catch if the fall finished, leaving Jaina the last inch of pride.

"Stand," Sylvanas said quietly.

Jaina did.

They bought horses because there was no time to reclaim the ones left farther north, swallowed the stablemaster's questions, and took the King's Road at a pace just shy of reckless.

* * *

Warm damp sat wrong on Sylvanas.

It slicked the reins, plastered cloth to skin, and sharpened her temper with every mile.

Dragonblight had been honest. Ice, bones, wind, death-country. Lordaeron in autumn was all softness and wheat stubble and the damp dark smell of turned earth under hedges. Orchards hung heavy with late fruit. Smoke rose straight from farm chimneys. A cart piled with pumpkins gave them half the road while the driver shouted after them, "Capital's still mad with celebration. You'll never get a room."

Celebration.

Jaina rode two lengths ahead of the rear guard and one behind Sylvanas because nobody had discussed it and the formation had settled that way anyway. Bread-warm smell followed her even at speed now, wrong as fresh loaves in an apothecary. The ranger on Jaina's right drifted closer when the road narrowed between hedges. Cyndia took the left without looking to see whether Jaina noticed. She did. Her head turned once, then forward again. No protest.

By midday they reached the first village.

Goldenrod garlands looped from fence post to fence post. Someone had tied blue ribbons around the square pump. A painted board outside the tavern showed a blond prince in impossible armor with one hand raised over a crowd of smiling stick figures. Children had added flowers around the boots in newer paint.

Jaina saw the painted prince. Her head turned away too fast.

That made Sylvanas angrier. They were cheering the prince who had "saved" them, ribbons snapping blue over the square pump while a woman carrying wash to the line smiled at the riders, taking them for soldiers late to a feast.

"Long road from the coast?" she called.

"Yes," Sylvanas said, and did not slow.

No warning here. These people deserved it, and speaking now would still cost cover, time, and whatever thin chance remained at the palace. Silence left a bitter metal taste in her mouth.

They were nearly clear of the village when a relay runner came pounding north with a brass cylinder strapped against his chest. Horse blown. Face running with sweat under road dust. He hauled up hard enough to spray pebbles across the verge at the sight of Farstrider colors.

"Farstriders?" he said.

"Speak."

He thrust the cylinder toward her. The brass was hot from his body. "From the capital relay. Late, they said. Important enough to ride it through."

Sylvanas broke the seal one-handed while her horse danced under her.

Installed in the palace four days.  
Public audiences resumed.  
Additional personal guard from Northrend on interior rotation.  
Routine gate posture unchanged.

Four days.

Long enough to settle guard shifts and teach the palace to think that was normal.

Her body went still in the exact way it did when another day vanished off a campaign map.

Jaina had already turned her mare around. "How bad?"

"Worse by a day than the stone said."

The runner read enough from their faces to stop asking for the rest.

"Take a message back," Sylvanas said. "No names. Something inside the palace has turned. Keep the road to it open if you can. Quietly."

"Yes, commander."

He was gone in a scatter of dirt before the last word settled.

The village kept living. Bells started somewhere deeper in the square, cheerful and witless.

His face was on painted boards now. Blue ribbon crossed the pump behind it, and the baker came out under his triumph colors still shaking flour from her apron.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

They moved.

The next call to Antonidas came on the road because there was no room left for ceremony. Jaina nearly dropped the stone when the blue light answered. Sylvanas saw the tremor in her hand and the way she locked her wrist to hide it on the second pulse.

Antonidas's face held this time, thinned by the spell and by travel both. No tower light around him. Only service-passage torchlight behind him, hooded and poor against stone that should have been warmer than that.

"Report," Sylvanas said before courtesy could waste a breath.

The old man did not bristle.

"No clean road remains," he said. "Only confirming reports reach the courtiers still calling it triumph. Uther has demanded audience three times. Denied once, delayed twice, and now buried alive under manners." His mouth tightened. "He is in the palace, but not in the room that matters."

"And you?"

"Closer than I was yesterday, farther than I need to be. Two of my magi can feel the blade through the palace stone at the inner court. That is all the kingdom will permit them to say aloud."

Jaina cut in, thin from fatigue and sharper for it. "Can you get us inside without a parade?"

"Yes."

"How far?"

"East side of the palace. Service arch. Dawn if your horses hold. I can keep it quiet for a very short time."

He promised no safety. Good.

"Uther?" Jaina asked.

Antonidas turned a fraction at her voice. "He believes you. It no longer matters whether he believes you."

Jaina's jaw set once and only once.

Sylvanas said, "Stay at the arch. If it closes before we arrive, leave a mark to the next opening and go. You are more useful alive than dead in a side yard."

Antonidas gave her a look that might have become irritation in a less exhausted man. "Lady Windrunner, I do know what failure looks like."

"Good," she said. "We are bringing you more of it."

The stone went dark.

The road south widened, then narrowed again as traffic thickened toward Capital City. Flower wreaths over door lintels. Painted prince's crests on ale casks. Boys with sticks acting out some invented heroics of Northrend in the dust while their mothers laughed and told them not to break their teeth before supper.

Sylvanas stopped seeing individual faces after the third village. Better that way. What mattered was the palace.

And the woman riding three paces back on her right because the road had narrowed under willow shade and the riders had tucked themselves tighter around the center.

Jaina governed every measure now. How hard they could run the horses. How long to the city. Whether there would be enough mage left, once the doors were in front of them, to spend three schools without her heart simply refusing.

Capital City rose out of late haze exactly as Antonidas had said it would: pale walls, high towers, the palace dome taking the last of the sun. The outer roads were still dirty with parade aftermath. Flower paste ground into the cobbles by four days of wheels. Shreds of blue ribbon in the gutter water. Over the northern gate someone had hung a painted cloth so large the words were readable from the final bend in the avenue.

WELCOME HOME, PRINCE ARTHAS

The guards beneath it stood easy on their halberds, checking wagons with the bored courtesy of men expecting contraband and drunkenness. Routine trouble. Nothing more.

Antonidas waited where he said he would, under a narrow service arch off the eastern court where kitchen carts must have passed all day without anyone ever looking up. Gone were the tower robes. He wore a travel cloak with the hood down. Two Kirin Tor magi stood behind him in plain dark blue, hands hidden under their sleeves against a cold that had no business living in palace stone.

"Quickly," he said. "The outer wards are still the old peacetime lattice. My two can soften one service lock and turn eyes from the arch for a moment. No more."

One of the magi, a thin woman with a scar across her mouth, said, "The blade is wrong all the way into the subfloor. We can feel it from here."

"Yes," Jaina said. Her voice had gone nearly flat with effort. "I know."

Antonidas turned to her then, and fear showed on his face before he buried it back under work.

"Tell me the plan in words a judge could follow."

Sylvanas almost respected him for that.

Jaina braced one hand against the arch wall. White stone. Fine mortar. Cold where it should not have been cold. She spoke without looking up.

"The blade turns from taking to throwing. Frost catches the turn. Arcane goes through before it closes. Fire changes the binding while it's bare." Her hand pressed harder into the mortar. "I need three to five seconds within reach of it. Close enough to touch."

The scar-mouthed magus shut her eyes briefly. The other made the mistake of glancing at Sylvanas when he understood what the words meant.

Antonidas did not.

"Who gives you the seconds?" he asked, though he already knew.

Silence held in the service arch.

Then Sylvanas said, "I do."

No one there said anything.

Antonidas's face changed under the answer. His mouth tightened. Fear showed cleanly now.
The scar-mouthed magus looked down at the lock plate as though the metal might offer a kinder number.

"At sword range," he said quietly.

"Yes."

"Against a bearer who has already ignored an arrow through the throat."

"Yes."

Jaina pushed off the wall. "If you want me to soften it, there isn't time."

"I do not," Antonidas said.

Good. Columns could break a line for half a stride. The dais would trap her instead of cover her. From throne to doors the marble lay open and white, with nowhere in it worth trusting. The truth remained what it had been on the *Wolfheart*: Sylvanas had to stand in Frostmourne's reach and keep standing long enough for Jaina to finish the spell.

The broader magus moved to the service door and laid both hands against the lock plate. Blue-white Kirin Tor light sank into old metal. Somewhere inside the palace a servant laughed at a joke far enough away to belong to another world.

Cyndia and the three rangers took position without waiting to be told. Two back toward the court turn, one to the arch mouth, Cyndia nearest Jaina and a half-step wider by reflex. Her head turned through the same protective sweep as ever—door, corner, roofline, Jaina, back again.

"Once you are through," Antonidas said, "my help ends."

Sylvanas looked at the crack of dark opening between the door and its frame. "It already ended."

The door widened enough for single-file entry. Lye smell from the kitchens. Grease worn into stone by a hundred unnoticed carts. Beyond the court, kitchen work still went on.

Sylvanas went first. Of course.

Jaina followed.

They vanished into the service passage. Narrow stone. Dim torchlight.

Behind them the door closed, cutting off the blue-white Kirin Tor glow, the kitchen clatter, and the last witness who might still have spoken for them outside the room.
