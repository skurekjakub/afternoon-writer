# Chapter 22: The Return

Baelgun's camp sat in the snowfold below Muradin's wall, lower and meaner than the fighting camp itself. No carved gate, no challenge line, no room for pride. Just a packed yard, two hide roofs dragged low against the wind, smoke from a forge trench, and dwarves already moving at the Farstriders' approach.

The last stretch came to her in pieces. Muradin's weight had left Sylvanas and the two rangers one step shorter than their usual stride. Cyndia kept ranging ahead and back, making the route exist. Jaina had walked in the lane beside them with both hands bare in the cold, tracing the same hitch through Frostmourne's field until her fingers shook too badly to hold the shape cleanly. Now the yard opened in front of her and dwarven hands were coming fast over the trampled snow, and Muradin was about to become other people's work.

"Here," Baelgun said.

He was broader than Muradin and older about the eyes, beard split by iron clasps gone white with frost. Anger lived on him more cleanly than fear. He took one look at the blood on Muradin's leathers, one at the drag of his breathing, and stepped in before anyone finished speaking.

"Get him under. No, now. You two, on the shoulders. Mind the left side. If ye jar that again I'll use your ribs for tent pegs."

The dwarves answered with movement, not talk. Muradin went out of Sylvanas's grip, out of the rangers' hands, onto a hide stretcher hauled in from the yard. For one second his head rolled toward Jaina. Frost had gone grey in his beard where blood had thawed and frozen again.

Speak dwarf.

She had. Too late, but she had.

Baelgun caught her looking and read enough. "Alive," he said. "That's the word ye get. More than that belongs to the healers and whatever gods still answer this place."

Jaina tried to answer and found her throat gone dry again. Sylvanas did it for her.

"Can you keep him?"

"Aye." Baelgun's gaze flicked over the formation in one hard sweep: elf commander, half-spent mage, Cyndia with her bow still in hand, the three rangers crusted in cave frost and old blood. "Can ye stay?"

No one said yes. The forge cracked behind him. Wind skated over the yard wall and brought the glacier's thin iron taste with it.

Baelgun snorted once. "Thought not."

Muradin disappeared into the nearest shelter. The flap dropped. Work swallowed him whole.

That should have felt like relief. Instead it made the loss smaller and worse. Cave. Prince. Blade. Seam. Retreat. A string of facts with no place to stop and turn human.

Warmth reached her a moment later, leaking from the forge trench and the open shelter door beside it. Real heat. The first she'd felt that wasn't stolen from a ship's galley or a body standing too close. Her hands hurt at once. Her knees nearly folded.

"Inside," one of the dwarves said, already dragging a second hide flap aside. "Hot drink. Sit down before ye fall down."

Warmth here was delay dressed kindly.

Jaina's fingers had gone back to the air before she knew it. Three short marks. Hook. Pause. Again.

Baelgun followed the movement. His mouth flattened. "That from the cave?"

"Yes," she said.

"Useful?"

The right answer would have been no. Too early, too thin, and still turning under her skull like a splinter under skin.

"Maybe."

"Then take it south." He jerked his chin toward the open white beyond the wall. "Whatever took my commander down in there, it has the prince now. If ye're the only fools left breathing who understand any part of it, sitting in my camp won't improve the matter."

Angrier than shocked. The plan had warned her about that too. Anger was the only useful shape left.

Sylvanas stood with one hand still flexing after Muradin's weight. Frost had dried white over the leather of her bracer. "How long before the weather closes the trail back to the bay?"

"Long enough if ye go now. Not long enough if ye wait for comfort." Baelgun's stare cut to Jaina. "Can she walk?"

Jaina wanted, absurdly, to give him a cutting answer. One about having crossed an ocean to be asked whether she could cross a yard. What came out instead was plain.

"Yes."

Baelgun accepted that without softness. "Then walk. We'll keep Muradin if keeping's possible. You carry the rest."

There it was. The burden narrowed cleanly, just as the plan had promised it would. The expedition fell away. The dwarves fell away. Even the kingdom, for one ugly moment, fell away. What remained was the pair who had stood close enough to Frostmourne to carry one wrong thing out of the room alive.

Cyndia was already turning, signaling the rangers back into file. A waterskin landed against Jaina's palm. Another dwarf had shoved wrapped bread at Sylvanas and gotten the package taken without ceremony. Somewhere behind the hide flaps, Muradin's camp was becoming hands, cloth, needle, heat. Whatever happened there next would happen without them.

Jaina looked once at the shelter that had swallowed him.

Then the wind got in under her hood and reminded her what waited farther south.

They left without taking the offered bench. After six strides the camp warmth fell away so completely it felt imagined.

Muradin gone behind hide and timber. The cave behind that. Frostmourne still in front of her somehow, because the field had not stayed in the cavern where it belonged. Bootfalls made a count of it. Snow gave her a measure. The crooked turn came and went, came and went, a catch between one state and the next. Between taking and throwing. The hinge.

She kept time to it until the march itself became the counting.

Dragonblight did the rest. The same vast dead country as yesterday, but uglier now that they were leaving it empty-handed. The wind had changed. On the way north it had felt like opposition. On the way back it was only a pressure that never relented, the cold grinding at lashes, the old dragon bones whitening the horizon until sky and earth looked filed out of the same material.

Sylvanas ranged half a pace ahead, reading the ground because someone had to. Cyndia moved on the left. The rangers traded the rear and right flank without needing to be told. Jaina stayed where the file had made room for her and did the one thing her body was begging her not to do.

The seam again.

It was narrower away from the blade and somehow easier to hear for that. In the cave the force of it had crushed every thought flat. Out here, with nothing but cold and distance and the drag of the march, she could hold one phase in place long enough to notice what came before it and after.

Take. Throw.

Consume. Radiate.

One cycle, not one state.

Her fingers wrote it in the air because there was no paper quick enough and because paper would have made it too official too soon. Three short marks for the inward pull. The hook for the turn. The pause where the whole structure changed its mind.

Bread rose warm in the back of her throat.

Yeast. Salt. The smell of a loaf split fresh near a hearth.

It had been a flare on the ship. A warning that came when she pushed too hard and went when she stopped. That lie had not survived Northrend. Now the smell sat on her skin even in the killing cold, thin but constant. Her hands shook whether she cast or not. When she swallowed, she tasted a bakehouse where there should have been ice.

Kel'Thuzad would have loved this.

The thought arrived complete enough to make her stumble.

He was monstrous; that part was easy. Worse was how quickly he would have loved the elegance first. The turning mechanism. The exact place where a thing built to do one impossible task had been forced to reveal the second impossible task hidden inside it. He would have bent over this problem with that terrible scholarly stillness and forgotten his own pulse for an hour.

So had she.

The difference, if there was one, had better be more than disgust.

Wind drove powder snow against the side of her face. Jaina licked blood from the inside of her lip where the cold had split it and kept walking.

"You're favoring the left," Sylvanas said without looking back.

"I know."

"That was not a request for confirmation."

Jaina hated that she had to spend a beat understanding the sentence. "My right hand is worse."

"Both are worse."

That almost pulled a laugh out of her. Almost. "Thank you."

Silence for ten steps. Then, drier: "You are welcome."

The line held that and nothing more.

By midday the cold had worked its way through every seam in her clothes. Snow glare turned the world viciously bright. The bread-smell thickened once, twice, until even Cyndia's head tipped a fraction on the left flank, reading the change without a name for it and filing it under danger all the same. One of the rangers drifted nearer without being told. The protective lane that had once belonged to Jaina's work and not to Jaina herself was tightening around both.

She should have objected. She did not have the spare breath.

When the pull in Frostmourne's field lined up cleanly with the rhythm of her steps, the answer opened one shade farther.

A shatter point would have been kinder.

Of course not. If the blade had a crack plain enough for brute force, Muradin would have found it months ago or Arthas would have died under his first own-handed blow. No, the weakness lived in transition. The instant where one mode became the next and the binding had to admit both at once.

Phase change, she thought, and then flinched from her own vocabulary. Too dead. Too academic. Too much lecture hall, not enough cave.

The blade had to breathe to change what it was doing.

There.

That belonged to the body.

Something built on frost would match the turn. Something arcane could go through the opened hinge because arcane force was structure before it was damage. And fire—

Fire would not kill it. Fire changed things from the inside. Made a binding answer to a different law than the one it preferred.

The thought came hard enough to leave her standing still in the middle of the file.

Snow hissed under the others' boots before they halted.

Sylvanas turned. "What?"

Jaina had one hand locked around the other wrist to quiet the tremor. "Three schools."

The words came faster because if she slowed down she might lose them. "It isn't a break point. It's the turn. Frost catches the rhythm where it changes. Arcane goes through the opening. Then fire before it settles. It won't burn the blade apart. It changes what the blade is binding while the seam is open."

No one answered at first. Cyndia held on her the way she held on treeline that had moved once when it shouldn't have.

Sylvanas said, "Can you do it?"

The honest answer lived several miles behind honesty.

"I can build it," Jaina said. "Doing it in a room with the blade in reach is a different question."

"That is later."

Yes. Later. If later still existed by the time they reached the bay, the ship, the southbound sea.

They moved again.

The rest of the march to Daggercap blurred into repetition: white ground, dark bones, the file holding together on discipline alone. By the time the cliffs above the harbor came into view, Jaina's legs felt detached from decision. She kept going anyway. Distance had become personal. A thing that had insulted her once on the northbound crossing and was about to insult her again in reverse.

Daggercap Bay looked worse in retreat.

The harbor was the same black wreckage, the same hacked docks and frozen ropes, but now it wore knowledge. They knew what had left from here. They knew what it had taken inland. No revelation left in it. No point standing on the cliff to rediscover burned hulls as if grief improved on a second viewing.

Below, the *Wolfheart* was already being turned by hand and curse and profanity. Crew swarmed the lines. Ice broke loose from the pilings in cracked plates. Dagny Ironhull stood in the middle of it all with both gloves tucked through her belt and her voice carrying over the harbor like thrown iron.

She looked up once as the formation came down the switchback.

That was all it took.

"No blankets," she barked before anyone reached the dock. "No hammocks. No one lies down unless they're dead enough to stop arguing. Harl, get those lines loose. Bren, I said now. If the mage falls over, feed her first and ask questions after."

Jaina hit the last plank of the dock and smelled tar, kelp, old char, and the worse sweetness of her own skin under too many days of strain. Dagny came close enough to read her once, expression flattening not with pity but with a professional recalculation.

"You look bad," the captain said.

"Thank you."

"That wasn't an insult. It's ship math." Dagny's gaze slid to Sylvanas. "Can she still push?"

"Enough," Sylvanas said.

"We'll find out."

No hope beyond leave now. The bay had nothing else to offer them.

Before the schooner cleared the outer ice, they spent every warning lane left in the world.

Sylvanas wrote the Farstrider version first because hers was the faster hand for urgency that had no room for argument: prince returned, blade corrupted, palace danger, do not trust the celebration. The rangers tied message cylinders with numbed fingers while carrier birds fought the wind in their wicker dark. One by one the pigeons went up over the frozen water, small pale bodies flinging themselves south into distance too large for mercy.

Jaina took the stone.

Amber lit her hand, weak and familiar and terrible for that. Antonidas answered on the third pulse with a silence so intent she knew he had been waiting on the chance of this call for days.

"Jaina."

No title. No endearment. Her name only.

"We've been to the source," she said. "Or part of it. Arthas has Frostmourne. The blade is tied to the same work as the plague."

The pause that followed was worse than disbelief. Calculation already kneeling in the rubble of four lost days.

"You are too late to warn the city," Antonidas said. His voice came through thin, travel-frayed, stripped of the tower's old composure. "He entered Capital City four days ago. The court thinks he ended the threat in Northrend. There were bells. Processions."

Behind her, one of Sylvanas's birds clawed free of a ranger's hands and beat south.

Jaina gripped the stone harder. "And you?"

"In the city. With two magi. Nothing official enough to matter. Uther is here as well and no one who should listen is listening." A breath. Paper sounds near the stone, voices beyond it, the movement of men forced into corridors instead of chambers. "Tell me you have more than warning."

She looked out over the dark water, at the ruined harbor shrinking behind them, at Sylvanas three paces away already reading sail angle as if the sea were another battlefield.

"Maybe," she said.

"Jaina."

"I have a framework." The words steadied as she gave them air. "Three parts. Frost, arcane, fire. The blade's field cycles. There is a turn in it."

He understood enough not to waste her time on awe.

"And the cost?"

There. Mentor to apprentice in one question. What does the answer demand of the body doing it.

Too much, she thought. Out loud: "I don't know yet."

The amber light thinned. The sea had a way of eating magic even when it was not trying.

"Come fast," Antonidas said. "That is all the city will still allow you."

The stone died in her hand before she could answer.

They crossed anyway.

The southbound run had none of the northbound malice. No impossible headwind shouldering the schooner broadside. No deep pressure in the weather trying to force them back toward land. Which meant speed could be bought cleanly, and cleanliness made the price uglier.

Dagny worked the *Wolfheart* like a woman tuning a dangerous engine she had not built. Sail angle. Hull line. Jaina at the stern on command, one hand braced against the rail, pouring just enough frost and force into the wind to keep it hard on the canvas without tearing the rigging apart. Then food shoved into her hands because the next push would be useless if she shook herself empty before dusk. Then down again when blood started dripping over the planks.

"Count to twenty," Sylvanas said the first time.

Jaina, kneeling by the stern ladder with blood on the back of her hand and salt in her mouth, said, "That won't fix it."

"No. It will stop you lying."

Twenty was a long way when the deck would not hold still. She made it to sixteen before Dagny's mate pushed dried fish at her face and told her to chew or be thrown overboard as deadweight. She chewed.

The ship carried everything through its timbers. Every hard turn. Every new push. By the second night Jaina could feel the day's work still humming under the planks after her hands left the rail. The *Wolfheart* had learned her and resented the lesson.

So had Dagny.

"Again," the captain said at sunset, not looking at Jaina at all, only at the sail bellies and the black water ahead. "Short this time. I need another mile before dark and not a corpse on my deck."

Short this time. As if the problem were discipline and not the fact that her body had begun to smell like a bakery in midsummer.

Jaina laughed once at that, soft and humorless, and put her hand back on the rail.

Nosebleed by dusk. Again by moonrise. Again the next morning. The blood ran bright for a moment, then went black where the salt dried it into the grooves of the wood. Dagny pretended not to notice until the fourth time.

"Someone keep wiping that up," she snapped at nobody in particular. "My crew don't need to start thinking the ship wants sacrifices."

Cyndia did it herself, crouching with a rag and a face gone deliberately blank. One of the rangers brought water. Another stood close enough that if Jaina pitched sideways under the next push, she would not hit the deck hard.

Protected core. Visible now even if nobody named the shift.

At night the sea stopped being a road and became black movement under blacker sky. Jaina sat on a coil of rope with her back to the mast and made the framework say itself in plainer words until it could survive contact with another mind.

No resonance differential. No phased oscillation. None of the elegant dead terms Kel'Thuzad would have used because elegance had been one of the ways he lied.

The blade changed how it breathed.

Catch the breath with frost.

Drive through the opened breath with arcane force.

Then fire to change what it had open in its hands before it closed again.

Sylvanas sat beside her through most of the second night, close enough for the framework to survive being spoken aloud and tested.

"Three to five seconds," Jaina said into the dark. "Less and the arcane won't have time to get through the turn. More and the whole cycle settles and throws me back out."

"At sword range."

"Yes."

"While you hold still."

"Yes."

Timbers creaked under them. Far forward, a block knocked once against the mast and went quiet.

"Then the question isn't the spell," Sylvanas said.

Jaina knew what the next question was and still let Sylvanas ask it.

"Who buys the seconds?"

The sea offered no answer. Neither did she.

By landfall the framework existed. That was the only kindness the crossing gave her.

Southshore came up out of morning mist in ordinary shapes: piers, gulls, slant-roofed cottages, nets drying on frames, a road running inland through fields that had not the slightest idea they were living inside a lie. New place, new softness under the light, new air in her lungs after Northrend's iron cold. The town smelled of fish guts, wet rope, fresh bread from somewhere up the rise.

Jaina stepped off the gangplank and her knees gave.

No dramatic collapse. Nothing worth anyone naming later. Just the simple fact that the minute she stopped feeding the sails, the body keeping count underneath everything else presented its bill.

The dock rose hard toward her face.

Sylvanas's hand came up without quite taking her weight. Close enough to catch if the fall finished, leaving Jaina the one inch of pride that mattered.

"Stand," Sylvanas said quietly.

Jaina did. That was almost worse.

Lordaeron earth felt soft after Northrend ice. Warm too, by comparison, though the morning air still bit with autumn. Townsfolk moved past them with baskets, poles, barrels. Nobody stared longer than strangers on a dock deserved. No one ran for a magistrate. No one pointed south and said the prince had come home wrong.

Ordinary, calm, fatally unwarned.

They bought horses because there was no time to recover the ones left farther north, swallowed the questions the stablemaster tried to make into conversation, and took the King's Road at a pace just below reckless because anything slower would have felt like consent.

* * *

Warm country was harder on Sylvanas than the cold had been.

The body handled it. The temper did not.

Dragonblight had at least possessed the honesty to announce itself as murder-country. Lordaeron in autumn was all softness and wheat stubble and the damp dark smell of turned earth under hedges. Orchards stood heavy with late fruit. Smoke rose straight from farm chimneys. A cart piled with pumpkins gave the road to their formation while the driver touched two fingers to his brow and shouted after them, "Capital's mad with celebration still. You'll never get a room."

Celebration.

Jaina rode two lengths ahead of the rear guard and one length behind Sylvanas because nobody had discussed the spacing and the spacing had arrived anyway. Bread-warm smell followed her even at speed now. Intermittent, but often enough. A wrong domestic thing hanging around a woman whose skin should have held only horse sweat, salt, and old magic.

The ranger on Jaina's right drifted closer when the road narrowed through hedges. Cyndia took the left lane without looking to see whether Jaina caught the adjustment. She did. Her head turned once, then forward again. No objection.

That, more than the rearrangement itself, settled it for Sylvanas. The mage had stopped treating care as interruption.

One local win, the plan had warned. Needed or the whole chapter would grind its teeth.

There it was. Small as a change in formation and more useful than optimism.

By midday they reached the first village.

Goldenrod garlands looped between fence posts. Someone had tied blue ribbons around the pump handle in the square. A painted board outside the tavern showed a blond prince in impossible armor, one hand raised to a crowd of smiling stick figures. Children had added flowers around the boots.

The painted prince caught Jaina's eye. Her head turned away too fast.

The villagers were not fools. That was the part that made Sylvanas angrier. They were living with the information available to them, which was a worse kind of innocence than stupidity. A woman carrying wash to the line smiled at the riders as if they were only more soldiers late to a victory feast.

"Long road from the coast?" she called.

"Yes," Sylvanas said, and did not slow.

No warning here. These people deserved it, and truth would still cost cover, time, and whatever thin chance remained at the palace. Silence sat badly in the mouth. She carried it anyway.

They were almost through when a relay runner came pounding north with a brass cylinder strapped against his chest. Horse blown. Face running with sweat under road dust. He hauled up hard enough to spray pebbles across the verge at the sight of their colors.

"Farstriders?" he said.

"Speak."

He thrust the cylinder toward her. The metal was hot from his body. "From the capital relay. Late, they said. Important enough to ride it through."

Sylvanas broke the seal one-handed while the horse danced under her.

Installed in the palace four days.
Public audiences resumed.
Additional personal guard from Northrend on interior rotation.
Routine gate posture unchanged.

Four days.

He was no longer arriving. He was settled.

Her body went still in the exact way it did when the mathematics worsened.

Jaina had pulled her mare around before Sylvanas looked up. "How bad?"

"Worse by a day than the stone said."

The runner read enough from their faces to stop asking questions. Good training. Or simple fear.

"Take a message back," Sylvanas said. "No names. Capital interior compromised. Keep lanes open to the palace if they can be kept. Quietly."

"Yes, commander."

He was gone in a spray of dirt before the last word settled.

The village went on with its day. Bells started somewhere deeper in the square, cheerful and stupid. A baker came out her door shaking flour from her apron.

Arthas had been home long enough to become decorations.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

They moved.

The next call to Antonidas came on the road because there was no room left for ceremony. Jaina nearly dropped the stone once when the blue light answered; Sylvanas caught the tremor in her hand and the way she locked the wrist before it could show a second time.

Antonidas's face held this time, thinned by the spell and by travel both. No tower light around him. Only service-passage torchlight, hooded and poor. He looked like a scholar who had been made to sleep in his clothes and had not done much sleeping anyway.

"Report," Sylvanas said before courtesy could get in the way.

The old man did not bristle. Useful in him.

"No formal lane remains," he said. "The court hears only what confirms the prince's return as triumph. Uther has demanded audience three times. Denied once, delayed twice, and now contained by manners." His mouth tightened. "He is in the palace, but not in the room that matters."

"And you?"

"Closer than I was yesterday, farther than I need to be. Two of my magi can feel the blade through the stone at the inner court. That is all the kingdom will permit them to say aloud."

Jaina's voice cut in, thin from fatigue and sharper for it. "Can you get us inside without a parade?"

"Yes."

One word. Best thing he'd given them in weeks.

"How far?"

"Service arch on the east side of the palace. Dawn if your horses hold. A steward's lane, not a guard's lane. I can keep it unremarked for a very short time."

He promised no safety. Only a lane that might go unremarked. Good. He was speaking in road-useful truth now.

"Uther?" Jaina asked.

Antonidas's eyes shifted a fraction, hearing the older wound behind the new question. "He believes you. It no longer matters whether he believes you."

That landed. Jaina's jaw set once and only once.

Sylvanas said, "Stay at the arch. If the lane closes before we arrive, leave a mark that points to the next opening and go. You are more useful living than witnessless in a side yard."

Antonidas gave her a look that might have become irritation in a less exhausted man. "Lady Windrunner, I do know what failure looks like."

"Good," she said. "We are bringing you more of it."

The stone went dark.

The road south widened, then narrowed again as the capital approaches pulled traffic inward. Every mile showed the same offense in a different dress. Flower wreaths nailed over door lintels. Painted prince's crests on ale casks outside inns. Boys with sticks re-enacting some invented heroics of Northrend in the dust while their mothers laughed and told them not to break their teeth before supper.

Sylvanas stopped seeing individual faces after the third such village. Better that way. The kingdom's lie had installed itself thoroughly enough without her inventorying each believer.

What mattered was the palace.

And the woman riding three paces back on her right now because the road had narrowed under willow shade and the formation had tucked itself smaller around the center.

Jaina's condition governed every calculation. How long to the city. How fast they could take the last rise. Whether there would be enough mage left, once the doors were finally in front of them, to spend three schools in sequence without her heart simply declining the instruction.

Sylvanas had spent most of her life measuring terrain, range, enemy disposition, weather, morale. Now she measured a kingdom in what it would cost Jaina to cross it.

Capital City lifted out of late-afternoon haze exactly as Antonidas's briefing had promised: pale walls, high towers, the dome of the palace catching the failing sun. The road into the outer districts was still dirty with parade aftermath. Flower paste ground into cobbles by four days of wheels. Shreds of blue ribbon in the gutter water. Over the northern gate someone had hung a painted cloth so large the words could be read from the last bend in the avenue.

WELCOME HOME, PRINCE ARTHAS

The guards beneath it stood easy on their halberds, checking wagons with the bored courtesy of men who expected theft, contraband, drunkenness. Royal treason in the flesh never entered their calculations.

Antonidas waited where he said he would, under a narrow service arch off the eastern court where kitchen carts must have passed all day without anyone ever looking up at the stone over their own heads. Gone were the tower robes. He wore a travel cloak with the hood down. Two Kirin Tor magi stood behind him in plain dark blue with their hands already set under their sleeves against the cold that should not have existed in the palace stones.

Road-tired instead of tower-composed. The plan had gotten that right too.

"Quickly," he said. "The outer wards are still the old peacetime lattice. My two can soften one service lock and hold attention off the arch for a moment. No more than that."

One of the magi, a thin woman with a scar across her mouth, said, "The blade is wrong all the way into the subfloor. We can feel it from here."

"Yes," Jaina said. Her voice had gone nearly flat with effort. "I know."

Antonidas looked at her then, and the human thing in his face slipped past the archmage's training before he could stop it. Fear first. Then anger at the fear's uselessness. Then work.

"Tell me the plan as if I have to explain it to a judge and not a mage."

Sylvanas almost respected him for that.

Jaina braced one hand against the arch wall. White stone. Fine mortar. Cold where it should not have been cold. She spoke without looking up.

"The blade changes state in a repeating cycle. Frost catches the turn. Arcane goes through the turn before it closes. Fire changes the binding while it's exposed." She swallowed. "I need three to five seconds within reach of it. Close enough to touch."

The scar-mouthed magus shut her eyes briefly. The other, broader and older, made the mistake of glancing at Sylvanas when he understood what the statement actually meant.

Antonidas did not.

"Who gives you the seconds?" he asked, though he already knew.

Silence answered him for one beat.

Then Sylvanas said, "I do."

No evasions left in it. No formation talk. No promise that the room would somehow solve itself.

I.

The word settled across the little service court harder than any oath.

Antonidas's face changed under it. He was too intelligent for protest. What showed instead was the visible recalculation of a man hearing the price become attached to one particular body.

"At sword range," he said quietly.

"Yes."

"Against a bearer who has already ignored an arrow through the throat."

"Yes."

Jaina pushed off the wall. "If you want me to soften it, there isn't time."

"I do not," Antonidas said.

Good. Because there was nothing to soften. Columns, perhaps. Angles. The terrain a throne room might offer to a ranger who preferred forests and had been given polished marble instead. But no cleaner version than the core truth: Sylvanas had to stand in Frostmourne's reach and keep standing long enough for Jaina to finish the spell.

The broader magus moved to the service door and laid both hands against the lock plate. Blue-white light sank into old metal. Somewhere inside the palace, a servant laughed at a joke far away enough to belong to another world.

Cyndia and the three rangers took their lanes without waiting to be assigned. Two back toward the court turn, one to the arch mouth, Cyndia nearest Jaina and then a half-step wider by reflex. Her head turned through the same old protective sweep — door, corner, roofline, Jaina, back again. Observable pattern. Unnamed shift.

Antonidas's gaze flicked to it and away. He said nothing. Good again.

"Once you are through," he said, "my help ends."

Sylvanas looked at the crack of dark opening between the door and its frame. "It already ended."

No cruelty in it. Mere accounting.

The door widened enough for single-file entry. Lye smell from the kitchens. Grease in the stone. The ordinary invisible machinery of a palace breathing just beyond a murder.

Jaina went in second. Sylvanas first. Of course.

The service passage swallowed them whole. Narrow stone. Dim torchlight. The sort of corridor that had never been built for princes and would outlive them all the same.

Behind them the door closed.

It closed with the final sound of the last public-facing help in the kingdom being cut off by one thickness of wood and one iron latch.
