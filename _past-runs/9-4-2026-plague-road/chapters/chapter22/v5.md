# Chapter 22: The Return

Baelgun's camp sat in a snowfold below Muradin's wall, meaner than the fighting line itself. No gate. No trench. Just hard-packed yard, two hide roofs lashed low against the wind, a forge trench breathing sparks, and dwarves already moving as the Farstriders came in off the drift.

The climb down had broken Jaina into pieces. Muradin's weight had dragged Sylvanas and the rangers out of stride. Cyndia kept ranging ahead and back through the dragon bones, finding a road by habit where Dragonblight offered none. Jaina had come beside them with both hands bare in the killing cold, tracing the hitch in Frostmourne's field until her fingers shook too badly to hold it. Now the yard opened and camp hands came fast over the snow.

"Here," Baelgun said.

Broader than Muradin, older about the eyes, beard clasped in iron gone white with rime. He took one look at the blood in Muradin's leathers and stepped in. "Get him under. Now. You two, shoulders. Mind the left side. If ye jar that again I'll use your ribs for tent pegs."

Field dwarves answered with hands already on the work. Muradin went out of Sylvanas's grip and onto a hide stretcher. His head rolled once toward Jaina. Blood had thawed in his beard and frozen again there, dark under the frost.

Speak dwarf.

She had. Too late, but she had.

Baelgun read enough from her face. "Alive," he said. "That's the word ye get. More than that belongs to healers and whatever gods still answer this place."

Jaina's throat had gone dry. Sylvanas asked for her. "Can you keep him?"

"Aye." Baelgun took in elf commander, half-spent mage, Cyndia with bow still in hand, three rangers crusted in cave frost and old blood. "Can ye stay?"

No one said yes. The forge snapped behind him. Wind skimmed the yard and carried glacier iron with it.

"Thought not."

Muradin vanished through a hide flap. Warmth leaked from the forge trench a heartbeat later, real enough to hurt. The first true heat since the *Wolfheart*. Her knees nearly folded with it.

"Inside," a dwarf said, hauling another flap wide. "Hot drink. Sit before ye fall."

Jaina's fingers had already gone back to the air. Three short marks. Hook. Pause. Again.

Baelgun followed the motion. "That from the cave?"

"Yes."

"Useful?"

"Maybe."

"Then take it south." He jerked his chin toward the white waste beyond the yard. "Whatever put my commander on a stretcher has the prince now. If ye're the only fools left breathing who understand any of it, my camp won't help by warming ye."

Sylvanas flexed one hand after Muradin's weight. "How long before the weather closes the trail back to Daggercap Bay?"

"Long enough if ye go now. Not long enough if ye wait for comfort." His stare cut to Jaina. "Can she walk?"

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

Baelgun kept looking at Jaina. "Can she keep walking?"

Jaina lifted her head. "Yes."

"Then walk. We'll keep Muradin if keeping's possible. You carry the rest."

They left without taking the bench. A waterskin thumped into Jaina's palm. Wrapped bread went to Sylvanas. Behind the flaps, awls clicked, water steamed, and dwarven hands went to work.

Then the camp fell away and the Dragonblight took them back.

On the way in there had been a prince to catch and a cave to reach. On the way out there were only dragon ribs humped under snow, powder hissing into their eyes, and the long trudge toward the bay. Sylvanas ranged half a pace ahead reading footing. Cyndia held the left. The rangers shifted rear and right flank without being told. Jaina stayed where they had left room for her and listened.

The turn again.

Away from the blade it was thinner. In the cave it had flattened every other thought; out here she could hold one part still long enough to hear what came before and after.

Take.

Throw.

A hitch she could count through clenched teeth.

She wrote it in the air: three short marks for the inward pull, the hook for the turn, the pause where the rhythm changed hands.

Bread rose warm in the back of her throat. Yeast. Salt. Hearth-split loaf.

It had started on the northbound crossing as a flare when she pushed too hard. Now it sat on her skin even in the killing cold, thin but constant, as wrong there as lamplight in a crypt. Her hands shook whether she cast or not.

Kel'Thuzad would have loved this.

She stumbled.

That part was easy. Powder snow hissed across her face while she pictured him bent over the hitch in the field, forgetting his own pulse for the neatness of it.

So had she.

She kept the pattern in her head anyway.

"You're favoring the left," Sylvanas said without looking back.

"I know."

"That was not a request for confirmation."

Jaina caught up to the sentence through cold and rhythm. "My right hand is worse."

"Both are worse."

Her split lip lifted. "Thank you."

Ten steps later Sylvanas answered, drier still. "You are welcome."

By midday the cold had worked through every seam. Snow glare turned the waste viciously bright. The bread-smell thickened until even Cyndia's head tipped a fraction from the flank. One ranger drifted nearer. Another closed the gap behind her. No one named it.

Then the pull in Frostmourne's field lined up cleanly with her steps, and one more piece dropped into place.

The weakness lived in the turn itself. Not a flaw that sat still and waited to be hit. Muradin would have found that months ago. The taking had to open into throwing for one instant, and that bare seam was the only place she could catch.

Frost would catch the turn. Arcane could go through while it was open. Fire could drive into the opened seam and turn the binding against itself.

She stopped dead.

Snow hissed under the others' boots before they halted.

"What?" Sylvanas said.

Jaina locked one hand around the other wrist to quiet the tremor. "Three schools."

The words came faster because if she slowed down she might lose it again. "The turn. Frost catches it. Arcane goes through while it's open. Then fire before it closes. Burn the join while it's bare."

Cyndia's mare edged half a stride closer. Her bow hand stayed loose, and she kept Jaina where she would keep a bad treeline: in the corner of her eye and one turn from the bow.

"Can you do it?" Sylvanas asked.

"I can build it. Doing it at sword range without dying is a different question."

"That is later."

If later still existed by the time they reached Daggercap Bay and the *Wolfheart*.

They moved.

The harbor came back under a hard white light: black wreckage frozen at the edge, hacked docks, ropes stiff as iron, old burn scars in the pilings. Below, the *Wolfheart* was already being turned with handspikes, curses, and shouted seamanship. Dagny Ironhull stood in the middle of it with both gloves tucked through her belt.

"No blankets," she barked before anyone reached the planks. "No hammocks. No one lies down unless they're dead enough to stop arguing. Harl, loose those lines. Bren, now. If the mage falls over, feed her first and ask questions after."

Jaina hit the dock and got Daggercap all at once: tar, kelp, torn pitch, old char, and under it the sweeter wrongness of her own skin after too many days of strain. Dagny came close, read her once in captain's measure, and flattened her mouth.

"You look bad."

"Thank you."

"Ship measure." Dagny turned to Sylvanas. "Can she still push?"

"Enough."

"We'll see."

Before the schooner cleared the outer ice, they spent every warning they had. Sylvanas wrote the Farstrider version first because hers was the faster hand for urgency stripped of argument: prince returned, blade corrupted, palace danger, do not trust the celebration. The rangers tied brass cylinders with numbed fingers while pigeons fought the wicker dark. One by one the birds went south over the frozen water.

Jaina took the stone.

Amber lit her hand, weak and familiar and terrible for that. Antonidas answered on the third pulse.

"Jaina."

Only her name.

"We've been to the source," she said. "Or part of it. Arthas has Frostmourne. The blade is built on the same plague magic."

"You are too late to warn the city," Antonidas said. His voice had travel in it now, not tower air. "He entered Capital City three days ago. The court thinks he ended the threat in Northrend. There were bells. Processions."

Behind her, one of Sylvanas's birds clawed free of a ranger's hands and beat south over the black water.

"And you?"

"In the city. With two magi. Nothing official enough to matter. Uther is here as well and no one who should listen is listening." Then, plainer: "Tell me you have more than warning."

Beyond Daggercap's ruined mouth, Sylvanas stood three paces away reading sail angle as though the sea were another contested field.

"Maybe."

"Jaina."

"I have a way at it. Three parts. Frost, arcane, fire. The blade turns from taking to throwing. It leaves one exposed instant between the two."

"And the cost?"

Too much. Out loud: "I don't know yet."

"Come fast," Antonidas said. "That is all they'll still permit."

The stone died before she could answer.

They crossed anyway.

No supernatural headwind came for the schooner on the way south. That only meant every extra knot came out of Jaina instead.

Dagny worked the *Wolfheart* by the inch, snapping for line, angle, and weight while the schooner shuddered under every burst Jaina bought. Stern rail when ordered, one hand braced against spray-slick wood, feeding just enough frost and force into the wind to keep canvas full without tearing the rigging apart. Then food shoved into her hands because the next push would be worthless if she shook herself empty before dusk. Then down again when blood started dripping over the deck planks.

"Count to twenty," Sylvanas said the first time.

Jaina, kneeling by the stern ladder with blood on the back of her hand, said, "That won't fix it."

"No. It will stop you lying."

Twenty was a long way when the deck would not hold still. She made it to sixteen before Dagny's mate pushed dried fish at her face and told her to chew or be thrown overboard as deadweight. She chewed.

By the second night she could still feel the day's work humming through the timbers after her hands left the rail.

"Again," Dagny said at sunset. "Short this time. I need another mile before dark and not a corpse on my deck."

Short this time. As though the bakery smell rising off Jaina's skin were the real trouble.

Jaina laughed once at that and put her hand back on the rail.

Nosebleed by dusk. Again by moonrise. Again the next morning.

"Someone keep wiping that up," Dagny snapped on the fourth time. "My crew do not need to start thinking the ship wants sacrifices."

Cyndia did it herself, crouching with a rag and a face gone blank. One ranger brought water. Another stayed close enough that if Jaina pitched sideways under the next push she would not crack her skull on the capstan base.

At night the answer had to be forced into plain words again and again.

The blade turned from taking to throwing.

Freeze the turn with frost.

Arcane through the gap.

Then fire before it closed.

Sylvanas sat beside her through most of the second night, close enough for Jaina to say the steps aloud and hear where they broke on actual use.

"Three to five seconds," Jaina said into the dark. "Less and the arcane won't make it through. More and the whole turn settles and throws me back out."

"At sword range."

"Yes."

"While you hold still."

"Yes."

Timbers creaked under them. Far forward, a block knocked once against the mast and went quiet.

"Then the question isn't the spell," Sylvanas said.

Who buys the seconds.

Her hand had found the tar-stiff rope beside them without seeming to mean to. The line bit across her palm. Neither woman said anything.

Southshore came out of the mist as piers, gulls, slant-roofed cottages, and a road inland through fields no one had bothered to fortify. Jaina stepped off the gangplank and her knees gave. The dock rushed toward her face.

Sylvanas's hand came up without quite taking her weight. Close enough to catch if the fall finished, leaving Jaina the last inch of pride.

"Stand," Sylvanas said quietly.

Jaina did.

They bought horses, swallowed the stablemaster's questions, and took the King's Road at a pace just shy of reckless.

* * *

Warm damp sat wrong on Sylvanas.

It slicked the reins, plastered cloth to skin, and sharpened her temper with every mile. Dragonblight had been honest. Ice, bones, wind, death-country. Lordaeron in autumn was all softness and wheat stubble and the damp dark smell of turned earth under hedges. Smoke rose straight from farm chimneys. A cart piled with pumpkins gave them half the road while the driver shouted, "Capital's still mad with celebration. You'll never get a room."

Celebration.

Jaina rode between Sylvanas and the rear guard because the riders had settled that way without discussion. Bread-warm smell followed her even at speed now, wrong as fresh loaves in an apothecary. The ranger on her right drifted closer when the road narrowed. Cyndia took the left without checking. Jaina's head turned once. No protest.

By midday they reached the first village. Goldenrod garlands looped from fence post to fence post. Blue ribbons circled the square pump. Outside the tavern a painted board showed a blond prince in impossible armor with one hand raised over a crowd of smiling stick figures. Children had added flowers around the boots.

The painted prince caught Jaina side-on. Her head turned away too fast.

That made Sylvanas angrier than the painting itself. These people were cheering the prince who had "saved" them while a woman carrying wash to the line smiled at the riders, taking them for soldiers late to a feast.

"Long road from the coast?"

"Yes," Sylvanas said, and did not slow.

No warning here. These people deserved it, and speaking now would still cost time, cover, and whatever thin chance remained at the palace.

They were nearly clear of the village when a relay runner came pounding north with a brass cylinder strapped to his chest. Horse blown. Face running with sweat under road dust. He hauled up hard at the sight of Farstrider colors.

"Farstriders?"

"Speak."

He thrust the cylinder toward her. "From the capital relay. Late, they said. Important enough to ride it through."

Sylvanas broke the seal one-handed while her horse danced under her.

Installed in the palace four days.  
Public audiences resumed.  
Additional personal guard from Northrend on interior rotation.  
Routine gate posture unchanged.

Four days.

Long enough for guard shifts to settle. Long enough for Northrend men to start moving those halls as if they belonged there.

"How bad?" Jaina asked.

"Worse by a day than the stone said."

The runner read enough from their faces and stopped asking.

"Take a message back," Sylvanas said. "No names. The palace is wrong inside. Keep the road to it open if you can. Quietly."

"Yes, commander."

He was gone in a scatter of dirt before the last word settled.

The village kept living behind them. Bells started somewhere deeper in the square, cheerful and witless.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

They moved.

The next call to Antonidas came on the road because there was no room left for ceremony. Jaina nearly dropped the stone when the blue light answered.

"Report," Sylvanas said before courtesy could waste a breath.

Antonidas's face held this time, thinned by the spell and by travel both. No tower light around him. Only service-passage torchlight behind him.

"No clean road remains," he said. "Only confirming reports reach the courtiers still calling it triumph. Uther has demanded audience three times. Denied once, delayed twice, and now buried alive under manners."

"And you?"

"Closer than I was yesterday, farther than I need to be. Two of my magi can feel the blade through the palace stone at the inner court. That is all the kingdom will permit them to say aloud."

Jaina cut in, thin from fatigue. "Can you get us inside without a parade?"

"Yes."

"How far?"

"East side of the palace. Service arch. Dawn if your horses hold. I can keep it quiet for a very short time."

He promised no safety. Good.

"Uther?" Jaina asked.

"He believes you," Antonidas said. "It no longer matters whether he believes you."

Jaina's jaw set once.

"Stay at the arch," Sylvanas said. "If it closes before we arrive, leave a mark to the next opening and go. You are more useful alive than dead in a side yard."

"Lady Windrunner, I do know what failure looks like."

"Good," she said. "We are bringing you more of it."

The stone went dark.

The road thickened toward Capital City: flower wreaths over door lintels, painted prince's crests on ale casks, boys with sticks acting out Northrend heroics in the dust while their mothers laughed and told them not to break their teeth before supper.

Sylvanas stopped seeing individual faces after the third village. Better that way. What mattered was the palace, and the woman riding three paces back on her right because every measure now belonged to Jaina: how hard they could run the horses, how long to the city, whether enough mage would remain to spend frost, arcane, and fire without her heart simply refusing.

Capital City rose out of late haze exactly as Antonidas had said: pale walls, high towers, the palace dome taking the last of the sun. The outer roads were still dirty with parade aftermath, flower paste ground into the cobbles, shreds of blue ribbon in the gutter water. Over the northern gate someone had hung a painted cloth large enough to read from the final bend:

WELCOME HOME, PRINCE ARTHAS

The guards beneath it stood easy on their halberds, checking wagons with the bored courtesy of men expecting contraband and drunkenness.

Antonidas waited where he had promised, under a narrow service arch off the eastern court. Gone were the tower robes. He wore a travel cloak with the hood down. Two Kirin Tor magi stood behind him in plain dark blue, hands hidden in their sleeves against a cold that had no business living in palace stone.

"Quickly," he said. "The outer wards are still the old peacetime lattice. My two can soften one service lock and turn eyes from the arch for a moment. No more."

One of the magi, a thin woman with a scar across her mouth, said, "The blade is wrong all the way into the subfloor. We can feel it from here."

"Yes," Jaina said, nearly flat with effort. "I know."

Antonidas turned to her once, and fear showed cleanly before he put it back under work.

"Tell me the plan in words a judge could follow."

Sylvanas almost respected him for that.

Jaina braced one hand against the arch wall. White stone. Fine mortar. Cold where it had no right to be cold.

"The blade turns from taking to throwing. Frost catches the turn. Arcane goes through before it closes. Fire turns the binding against itself while it's bare. I need three to five seconds within reach of it. Close enough to touch."

The scar-mouthed magus shut her eyes briefly. The other made the mistake of glancing at Sylvanas.

"Who gives you the seconds?" Antonidas asked, though he already knew.

Silence held in the service arch.

"I do," Sylvanas said.

No one there said anything.

"At sword range," Antonidas said quietly.

"Yes."

"Against a bearer who has already ignored an arrow through the throat."

"Yes."

Jaina pushed off the wall. "If you want me to soften it, there isn't time."

"I do not," Antonidas said.

Good. Nothing beyond that door changed the job. Open marble. Sword range. No cover worth trusting. Sylvanas had to stand in Frostmourne's reach and keep standing long enough for Jaina to finish the spell.

The broader magus moved to the service door and laid both hands against the lock plate. Blue-white Kirin Tor light sank into old metal. Somewhere inside the palace a servant laughed at a joke far enough away to belong to another world.

Cyndia and the three rangers took position without waiting to be told. Two toward the court turn, one to the arch mouth, Cyndia nearest Jaina and half a step wider by reflex. Her sweep stayed the same as ever: door, corner, roofline, Jaina, back again.

"Once you are through," Antonidas said, "my help ends."

Sylvanas watched the crack of dark opening between the door and its frame. "It already ended."

The door widened enough for single-file entry. Lye smell from the kitchens. Grease worn into stone by a hundred unnoticed carts.

Sylvanas went first. Of course.

Jaina followed.

Behind them the door closed, cutting off the blue-white Kirin Tor glow and the last witness who might still have spoken for them outside the room.
