# Chapter 22: The Return

Baelgun's camp sat in the snowfold below Muradin's wall, lower and meaner than the fighting camp itself. No carved gate. No challenge line. Just a packed yard, two hide roofs dragged low against the wind, smoke from a forge trench, and dwarves already moving at the Farstriders' approach.

The last stretch came to Jaina in pieces. Muradin's weight had left Sylvanas and the two rangers one step short of their usual stride. Cyndia kept ranging ahead and back, making the road by force of habit. Jaina had walked beside them with both hands bare in the cold, tracing the same hitch through Frostmourne's field until her fingers shook too badly to hold it steady. Now the yard opened in front of her and dwarven hands were coming fast over the trampled snow.

"Here," Baelgun said.

He was broader than Muradin and older about the eyes, beard split by iron clasps gone white with frost. He took one look at the blood on Muradin's leathers, one at the drag of his breathing, and stepped in before anyone finished speaking.

"Get him under. Now. You two, shoulders. Mind the left side. If ye jar that again I'll use your ribs for tent pegs."

The dwarves answered with movement, not talk. Muradin went out of Sylvanas's grip, out of the rangers' hands, onto a hide stretcher dragged in from the yard. His head rolled once toward Jaina. Frost had gone grey in his beard where blood had thawed and frozen again.

Speak dwarf.

She had. Too late, but she had.

Baelgun caught her looking and read enough. "Alive," he said. "That's the word ye get. More than that belongs to the healers and whatever gods still answer this place."

Jaina tried to answer and found her throat gone dry again. Sylvanas did it for her.

"Can you keep him?"

"Aye." Baelgun took them in once: elf commander, half-spent mage, Cyndia with her bow still in hand, three rangers crusted in cave frost and old blood. "Can ye stay?"

No one said yes. The forge cracked behind him. Wind skimmed over the yard wall and brought the glacier's thin iron taste with it.

Baelgun snorted. "Thought not."

Muradin disappeared into the nearest shelter. The flap dropped. Work swallowed him.

Warmth reached her a moment later, leaking from the forge trench and the open shelter door beside it. Real heat. The first true heat since the galley stove and bodies pressed close. Her hands hurt at once. Her knees nearly folded.

"Inside," one of the dwarves said, already dragging a second flap aside. "Hot drink. Sit before ye fall."

Jaina's fingers had gone back to the air before she knew it. Three short marks. Hook. Pause. Again.

Baelgun followed the movement. "That from the cave?"

"Yes."

"Useful?"

"Maybe."

"Then take it south." He jerked his chin toward the white beyond the wall. "Whatever put my commander on a stretcher has the prince now. If ye're the only fools left breathing who understand any of it, my camp won't help by warming ye."

Sylvanas stood with one hand still flexing after Muradin's weight. Frost had dried white over the leather of her bracer. "How long before the weather closes the trail back to the bay?"

"Long enough if ye go now. Not long enough if ye wait for comfort." His stare cut to Jaina. "Can she walk?"

"Yes."

"Then walk. We'll keep Muradin if keeping's possible. You carry the rest."

There it was. The camp fell away. What remained was the pair who had stood close enough to Frostmourne to carry one wrong thing out of that room alive.

Cyndia was already turning, signaling the rangers back into file. A waterskin thumped into Jaina's palm. Another dwarf shoved wrapped bread at Sylvanas and got it taken without ceremony. Behind the hide flaps Muradin's camp became cloth, hands, needle, heat. Whatever happened there next would happen without them.

Jaina looked once at the shelter that had swallowed him.

Then the wind got under her hood, and she remembered what waited farther south.

They left without taking the offered bench. Six strides later the camp warmth fell away so completely it might have been imagined.

Muradin gone behind hide and timber. The cave behind that. Frostmourne still in front of her somehow. Bootfalls counted it out. The crooked turn came and went, the catch between taking and throwing.

Dragonblight did the rest. The same vast dead country as yesterday, only uglier now that they were leaving it empty-handed. On the way back the wind only ground at lashes and whitened the dragon bones until sky and earth seemed cut from the same pale ruin.

Sylvanas ranged half a pace ahead, reading the ground because someone had to. Cyndia moved on the left. The rangers traded rear and right flank without being told. Jaina stayed where they had left room for her and kept listening.

The turn again.

Away from the blade it was thinner and easier to hear. In the cave it had flattened every thought. Out here, with nothing but cold and distance and the drag of the march, she could hold one part of it still long enough to hear what came before and after.

Take.

Throw.

A repeating breath.

Her fingers wrote it in the air. Three short marks for the inward pull. The hook for the turn. The pause where the rhythm turned.

Bread rose warm in the back of her throat. Yeast. Salt. A loaf split near a hearth.

It had been a flare on the ship, once. A warning that came when she pushed too hard and went when she stopped. By Northrend, the lie was dead. Now the smell sat on her skin even in the killing cold, thin but constant. Her hands shook whether she cast or not.

Kel'Thuzad would have loved this.

The thought hit hard enough to make her stumble.

He was monstrous; that part was easy. Worse was how quickly he would have bent over this and forgotten his own pulse. The beauty of the trap first. The neat turn inside it.

So had she.

The difference, if there was one, had better be more than disgust.

Wind drove powder snow against the side of her face. Jaina licked blood from her split lip and kept walking.

"You're favoring the left," Sylvanas said without looking back.

"I know."

"That was not a request for confirmation."

Jaina spent a beat catching up to the sentence. "My right hand is worse."

"Both are worse."

That almost pulled a laugh out of her. "Thank you."

Silence for ten steps. Then, drier: "You are welcome."

By midday the cold had worked through every seam in her clothes. Snow glare turned the world viciously bright. The bread-smell thickened until even Cyndia's head tipped a fraction on the flank. One ranger drifted nearer. Another closed the gap behind Jaina. No one named it.

When the pull in Frostmourne's field lined up cleanly with the rhythm of her steps, Jaina understood one shade more.

A crack would have been kinder.

If the blade had a weakness plain enough for brute force, Muradin would have found it months ago or Arthas would have died on the first swing. No. The weakness lived in the turn itself, the instant when the blade had to be both things at once.

The blade had to breathe to change what it was doing.

Something built on frost would catch the turn. Arcane could go through while it was open. And fire—

Fire changed things from the inside.

The thought came hard enough to stop her in the middle of the file.

Snow hissed under the others' boots before they halted.

Sylvanas turned. "What?"

Jaina had one hand locked around the other wrist to quiet the tremor. "Three schools."

The words came faster because if she slowed down she might lose them. "The turn. Frost catches it. Arcane goes through while it's open. Then fire before it closes again. To change the binding from the inside."

No one answered at first. Cyndia held on her the way she held on treeline that had moved once when it should not have.

Sylvanas said, "Can you do it?"

"I can build it," Jaina said. "Doing it in a room with the blade in reach is a different question."

"That is later."

Yes. Later. If later still existed by the time they reached the bay, the ship, the southbound sea.

They moved again.

The rest of the march to Daggercap blurred: white ground, dark bones, the line holding on stubbornness alone. By the time the cliffs above the harbor came into view, Jaina's legs felt detached from choice.

Daggercap Bay looked worse in retreat.

The harbor was the same black wreckage, the same hacked docks and frozen ropes. Below, the *Wolfheart* was already being turned by hand and curse and shouted command. Crew swarmed the lines. Ice broke from the pilings in cracked plates. Dagny Ironhull stood in the middle of it all with both gloves tucked through her belt, her voice carrying flat and hard over the harbor.

She looked up once as they came down the switchback.

That was enough.

"No blankets," she barked before anyone reached the dock. "No hammocks. No one lies down unless they're dead enough to stop arguing. Harl, loose those lines. Bren, now. If the mage falls over, feed her first and ask questions after."

Jaina hit the last plank of the dock and smelled tar, kelp, old char, and the worse sweetness of her own skin under too many days of strain. Dagny came close, read her once, and her mouth flattened.

"You look bad," the captain said.

"Thank you."

"Ship measure." Dagny turned to Sylvanas. "Can she still push?"

"Enough," Sylvanas said.

"We'll see."

Before the schooner cleared the outer ice, they spent every warning they had.

Sylvanas wrote the Farstrider version first because hers was the faster hand for urgency with no room left for argument: prince returned, blade corrupted, palace danger, do not trust the celebration. The rangers tied message cylinders with numbed fingers while carrier birds fought the wind in their wicker dark. One by one the pigeons went up over the frozen water, small pale bodies flinging themselves south into distance too large for mercy.

Jaina took the stone.

Amber lit her hand, weak and familiar and terrible for that. Antonidas answered on the third pulse with a silence so intent she knew he had been waiting on the chance of this call for days.

"Jaina."

No title. No endearment. Only her name.

"We've been to the source," she said. "Or part of it. Arthas has Frostmourne. The blade is tied to the same work as the plague."

"You are too late to warn the city," Antonidas said. His voice came through thin, travel-frayed, stripped of the tower's old composure. "He entered Capital City four days ago. The court thinks he ended the threat in Northrend. There were bells. Processions."

Behind her, one of Sylvanas's birds clawed free of a ranger's hands and beat south.

Jaina gripped the stone harder. "And you?"

"In the city. With two magi. Nothing official enough to matter. Uther is here as well and no one who should listen is listening." "Tell me you have more than warning."

She looked out over the dark water, at the ruined harbor shrinking behind them, at Sylvanas three paces away already reading sail angle as if the sea were another field.

"Maybe," she said.

"Jaina."

"I have a way at it." The words steadied as she gave them air. "Three parts. Frost, arcane, fire. The blade keeps changing how it breathes. There is a turn in it."

He understood enough not to waste her time on wonder.

"And the cost?"

Too much, she thought. Out loud: "I don't know yet."

The amber light thinned. The sea had a way of eating magic even when it was not trying.

"Come fast," Antonidas said. "That is all they'll still permit."

The stone died in her hand before she could answer.

They crossed anyway.

The southbound run had none of the northbound malice. No impossible headwind trying to shoulder the schooner broadside. Speed could be bought cleanly, and that made the price uglier.

Dagny worked the *Wolfheart* the way a hard captain works a strained rig: sail angle, hull line, timing. Jaina stood at the stern on command, one hand braced against the rail, feeding just enough frost and force into the wind to keep it hard on the canvas without tearing the rigging apart. Then food shoved into her hands because the next push would be worthless if she shook herself empty before dusk. Then down again when blood started dripping over the planks.

"Count to twenty," Sylvanas said the first time.

Jaina, kneeling by the stern ladder with blood on the back of her hand and salt in her mouth, said, "That won't fix it."

"No. It will stop you lying."

Twenty was a long way when the deck would not hold still. She made it to sixteen before Dagny's mate pushed dried fish at her face and told her to chew or be thrown overboard as deadweight. She chewed.

By the second night Jaina could still feel the day's work humming under the planks after her hands left the rail.

"Again," Dagny said at sunset, watching the sail bellies and the black water ahead. "Short this time. I need another mile before dark and not a corpse on my deck."

Short this time. As though the bakery smell rising off her skin were the trouble.

Jaina laughed once at that, soft and humorless, and put her hand back on the rail.

Nosebleed by dusk. Again by moonrise. Again the next morning. The blood ran bright, then went black where the salt dried it into the grain of the wood. Dagny ignored it until the fourth time.

"Someone keep wiping that up," she snapped at nobody in particular. "My crew don't need to start thinking the ship wants sacrifices."

Cyndia did it herself, crouching with a rag and a face gone blank. One ranger brought water. Another stayed close enough that if Jaina pitched sideways under the next push, she would not crack her skull on the deck.

At night the sea stopped being a road and became black motion under blacker sky. Jaina sat on a coil of rope with her back to the mast and forced the answer into plainer words until it could live outside her own head.

No neat corpse-word for it. None of Kel'Thuzad's pretty dead language.

The blade changed how it breathed.

Catch the breath with frost.

Drive through it with arcane force.

Then fire, before it closed its hand again.

Sylvanas sat beside her through most of the second night, close enough for the thing to be said aloud and tested.

"Three to five seconds," Jaina said into the dark. "Less and the arcane won't make it through. More and the whole turn settles and throws me back out."

"At sword range."

"Yes."

"While you hold still."

"Yes."

Timbers creaked under them. Far forward, a block knocked once against the mast and went quiet.

"Then the question isn't the spell," Sylvanas said.

Jaina knew what came next and still let Sylvanas ask it.

"Who buys the seconds?"

She found no answer in the sea. She gave none either.

By landfall the answer existed. That was the only good thing she got from the crossing.

Southshore came up out of morning mist in ordinary shapes: piers, gulls, slant-roofed cottages, nets drying on frames, a road running inland through fields still innocent of what was riding toward them.

Jaina stepped off the gangplank and her knees gave.

No dramatic collapse. The moment she stopped feeding the sails, the spent strength hit all at once.

The dock rushed toward her face.

Sylvanas's hand came up without quite taking her weight. Close enough to catch if the fall finished, leaving Jaina the last inch of pride.

"Stand," Sylvanas said quietly.

Jaina did. That was almost worse.

They bought horses because there was no time to recover the ones left farther north, swallowed the stablemaster's questions, and took the King's Road at a pace just short of reckless.

* * *

Warm damp sat wrong on Sylvanas.

It slicked the reins, plastered cloth to skin, and sharpened her temper with every mile.

From the first mile, Dragonblight had been murder-country. Lordaeron in autumn was all softness and wheat stubble and the damp dark smell of turned earth under hedges. Orchards stood heavy with late fruit. Smoke rose straight from farm chimneys. A cart piled with pumpkins gave the road to their riders while the driver shouted after them, "Capital's mad with celebration still. You'll never get a room."

Celebration.

Jaina rode two lengths ahead of the rear guard and one behind Sylvanas because nobody had discussed the spacing and the spacing had arrived anyway. Bread-warm smell followed her even at speed now. A wrong domestic thing hanging around a woman who should have smelled of horse sweat, salt, and old magic.

The ranger on Jaina's right drifted closer when the road narrowed through hedges. Cyndia took the left without looking to see whether Jaina noticed. She did. Her head turned once, then forward again. No protest.

That settled it for Sylvanas.

By midday they reached the first village.

Goldenrod garlands looped between fence posts. Someone had tied blue ribbons around the pump handle in the square. A painted board outside the tavern showed a blond prince in impossible armor, one hand lifted to a crowd of smiling stick figures. Children had added flowers around the boots.

The painted prince caught Jaina's eye. Her head turned away too fast.

The villagers were not fools. That was the part that made Sylvanas angrier. They were cheering the story they'd been given. A woman carrying wash to the line smiled at the riders, taking them for soldiers late to a feast.

"Long road from the coast?" she called.

"Yes," Sylvanas said, and did not slow.

No warning here. These people deserved it, and speaking now would still cost cover, time, and whatever thin chance remained at the palace. Keeping silent sat badly in her mouth. She carried it anyway.

They were almost through when a relay runner came pounding north with a brass cylinder strapped against his chest. Horse blown. Face running with sweat under road dust. He hauled up hard enough to spray pebbles across the verge at the sight of their colors.

"Farstriders?" he said.

"Speak."

He thrust the cylinder toward her. The metal was hot from his body. "From the capital relay. Late, they said. Important enough to ride it through."

Sylvanas broke the seal one-handed while the horse danced under her.

Installed in the palace four days.  
Public audiences resumed.  
Additional personal guard from Northrend on interior rotation.  
Routine gate posture unchanged.

Four days.

Long enough to settle his guard and routines.

Her body went still in the way it did when the numbers worsened.

Jaina had pulled her mare around before Sylvanas looked up. "How bad?"

"Worse by a day than the stone said."

The runner read enough from their faces to stop asking questions.

"Take a message back," Sylvanas said. "No names. Something inside the palace has turned. Keep the road to it open if you can. Quietly."

"Yes, commander."

He was gone in a spray of dirt before the last word settled.

The village went on with its day. Bells started somewhere deeper in the square, cheerful and witless. A baker came out her door shaking flour from her apron.

Arthas had been home long enough to become decorations.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

They moved.

The next call to Antonidas came on the road because there was no room left for ceremony. Jaina nearly dropped the stone when the blue light answered; Sylvanas saw the tremor in her hand and the way she locked her wrist to hide it the second time.

Antonidas's face held this time, thinned by the spell and by travel both. No tower light around him. Only service-passage torchlight, hooded and poor.

"Report," Sylvanas said before courtesy could get in the way.

The old man did not bristle.

"No clean road remains," he said. "Only confirming reports reach the courtiers still calling it triumph. Uther has demanded audience three times. Denied once, delayed twice, and now buried alive under manners." His mouth tightened. "He is in the palace, but not in the room that matters."

"And you?"

"Closer than I was yesterday, farther than I need to be. Two of my magi can feel the blade through the stone at the inner court. That is all the kingdom will permit them to say aloud."

Jaina cut in, thin from fatigue and sharper for it. "Can you get us inside without a parade?"

"Yes."

"How far?"

"East side of the palace. Service arch. Dawn if your horses hold. I can keep it quiet for a very short time."

He promised no safety. Good.

"Uther?" Jaina asked.

Antonidas turned a fraction, hearing the older wound under the new question. "He believes you. It no longer matters whether he believes you."

That landed. Jaina's jaw set once and only once.

Sylvanas said, "Stay at the arch. If it closes before we arrive, leave a mark to the next opening and go. You are more useful alive than dead in a side yard."

Antonidas gave her a look that might have become irritation in a less exhausted man. "Lady Windrunner, I do know what failure looks like."

"Good," she said. "We are bringing you more of it."

The stone went dark.

The road south widened, then narrowed again as the capital pulled traffic inward. Every mile showed the same offense in a different dress. Flower wreaths over door lintels. Painted prince's crests on ale casks. Boys with sticks acting out some invented heroics of Northrend in the dust while their mothers laughed and told them not to break their teeth before supper.

Sylvanas stopped seeing individual faces after the third such village. Better that way. What mattered was the palace.

And the woman riding three paces back on her right because the road had narrowed under willow shade and the riders had tucked themselves tighter around the center.

Jaina governed every measure now. How hard they could run the horses. How long to the city. Whether there would be enough mage left, once the doors were in front of them, to spend three schools without her heart simply refusing.

Capital City lifted out of late-afternoon haze exactly as Antonidas had said it would: pale walls, high towers, the dome of the palace catching the failing sun. The road into the outer districts was still dirty with parade aftermath. Flower paste ground into cobbles by four days of wheels. Shreds of blue ribbon in the gutter water. Over the northern gate someone had hung a painted cloth so large the words could be read from the last bend in the avenue.

WELCOME HOME, PRINCE ARTHAS

The guards beneath it stood easy on their halberds, checking wagons with the bored courtesy of men who expected contraband and drunkenness. Routine trouble.

Antonidas waited where he said he would, under a narrow service arch off the eastern court where kitchen carts must have passed all day without anyone ever looking up. Gone were the tower robes. He wore a travel cloak with the hood down. Two Kirin Tor magi stood behind him in plain dark blue with their hands under their sleeves against the cold that should not have been in the palace stones.

"Quickly," he said. "The outer wards are still the old peacetime lattice. My two can soften one service lock and turn eyes from the arch for a moment. No more."

One of the magi, a thin woman with a scar across her mouth, said, "The blade is wrong all the way into the subfloor. We can feel it from here."

"Yes," Jaina said. Her voice had gone nearly flat with effort. "I know."

Antonidas turned to her then, and fear showed plain on his face before he buried it in work again.

"Tell me the plan in words a judge could follow."

Sylvanas almost respected him for that.

Jaina braced one hand against the arch wall. White stone. Fine mortar. Cold where it should not have been cold. She spoke without looking up.

"The blade keeps changing how it breathes. Frost catches the turn. Arcane goes through before it closes. Fire changes the binding while it's bare." She swallowed. "I need three to five seconds within reach of it. Close enough to touch."

The scar-mouthed magus shut her eyes briefly. The other made the mistake of glancing at Sylvanas when he understood what the words meant.

Antonidas did not.

"Who gives you the seconds?" he asked, though he already knew.

Silence held for one beat.

Then Sylvanas said, "I do."

I.

The word settled across the little court harder than any oath.

Antonidas's face changed under it. Too intelligent for protest. What showed instead was fear at the thought of Sylvanas in Frostmourne's reach.

"At sword range," he said quietly.

"Yes."

"Against a bearer who has already ignored an arrow through the throat."

"Yes."

Jaina pushed off the wall. "If you want me to soften it, there isn't time."

"I do not," Antonidas said.

Good. Because there was nothing to soften. Columns, perhaps. Angles. Whatever cover a throne room might offer to a ranger who preferred forests and had been given polished marble instead. But no cleaner version than the truth: Sylvanas had to stand in Frostmourne's reach and keep standing long enough for Jaina to finish the spell.

The broader magus moved to the service door and laid both hands against the lock plate. Blue-white light sank into the old metal. Somewhere inside the palace, a servant laughed at a joke far away enough to belong to another world.

Cyndia and the three rangers took their places without waiting to be told. Two back toward the court turn, one to the arch mouth, Cyndia nearest Jaina and a half-step wider by reflex. Her head turned through the same old sweep—door, corner, roofline, Jaina, back again.

"Once you are through," he said, "my help ends."

Sylvanas looked at the crack of dark opening between the door and its frame. "It already ended."

The door widened enough for single-file entry. Lye smell from the kitchens. Grease in the stone. Beyond the court, kitchen work went on.

Jaina went in second. Sylvanas first. Of course.

They vanished into the service passage. Narrow stone. Dim torchlight. The sort of corridor never built for princes.

Behind them the door closed.
