# Chapter 16: Before the Gates

North of Hearthglen, the north road into Stratholme had broken down into ruts and shoulder.

Deep wagon ruts shoved the horses onto the firmer verge where ditch grass still held. Yesterday's mud had dried in hard ridges and cracked under the hooves. Jaina rode low over the gelding's neck, one hand light on the reins, the other braced on the pommel when the ground slammed up through the saddle. The horse's mouth had gone white. Cyndia's mare carried the same crust around the bit. On Jaina's right, Sylvanas's mount took the bad footing cleanly and never wasted a step.

Behind them, Arthas's column was only a dust smear over the Eastweald.

Jaina checked it once, then forced herself forward again. The lead was measured in ovens now. Shutters opening onto breakfast tables. Bakery boys carrying trays out back before the prince's outriders even reached the walls.

"Market Row first if the formal gate chokes," she called. "If we can take the northern service road into the warehouse district, we start there. Biggest stockpiles first. Anything still in sacks. Anything still stacked."

"And if the grain's already split?" Cyndia asked.

"Then we go where the bread leaves heat."

Sylvanas rode close enough to hear without making Jaina shout. "Say it in streets."

Jaina swallowed and did. "Main stores by the service gate. Bakehouses on Market Row. Any yard with district carts still loading. If I can spoil a full stock before it moves, that quarter loses the next wave. If it's already in loaves, we're late there."

"How late?"

Another jolt drove up through the saddle. "I don't know yet."

"Smaller words."

She had a sharp answer ready and bit it back before it left her mouth. Sylvanas was shaving the thought down to what could be used.

"If children are already eating in the doorways," Jaina said, "that block is gone."

Sylvanas nodded once and turned enough to mark the fork ahead. Cyndia caught it too, lifted two fingers, and angled left before anyone had to tell her.

As they closed on the city, traffic thickened on the road. Farm wagons. Flour bins knocking in empty carts. Men in aprons riding the rear boards to save time. A priest's mule stumbling under brass lamps wrapped in sacking. Bells thinned through the damp air, the sound of an ordinary morning that had not yet learned what it was carrying.

Long barns, mill sheds, kitchen plots ragged from harvest traffic, and smoke from ovens too small to be anything but household work made the city's appetite plain. Beyond them the wall flashed between bare trees, grey stone and tower caps over the last rise. Jaina had run the map a hundred times in her head. The shift in the wind mattered more than the map in her head.

Warm yeast.
Woodsmoke.
Sweetness underneath.

"There." She pointed. "That smoke line. Multiple ovens. Market side."

Sylvanas tracked it, then climbed the wall with a quick scan: tower, bunched traffic, two city guards trying to sort shouting into order, carts already half unloaded beyond the plainer northern gate where the warehouse yards began.

"Service gate's open," Cyndia said.

"Because nobody expects to defend a city from its own bread," Jaina said.

Sylvanas said, "Good. Saves time."

They took the last rise at a gallop, slid down through churned mud, and forced a lane through the knot outside the gate. Flour to the elbows. A split wheel. Loaves carried with the care used for infants.

A guard threw up a hand too late. "You can't just—"

"Move," Sylvanas said, already past him.

He did.

Cyndia peeled off, took in the horse lines and the cart mouths in one sweep, then pointed across the yard with her knife. Long timbered building. Steep slate roof. High vents. Loading doors.

The first store.

Jaina swung down before the gelding had settled. Her knees buckled. She caught herself on the horse's shoulder, shoved upright, and went for the doors with the staff already in hand. Flour dust lay thick in the threshold cracks. Cart boards had chewed the sill smooth. Two teamsters dragged a dolly out and argued over district marks painted on the sacks.

"Stop moving anything," Jaina said.

One weighed her robes, then the staff. "Who in the Light are you?"

"Trying to keep you alive. Put it down."

He turned for a guard. Sylvanas filled the doorway instead, bow in hand, while Cyndia took the yard angle where anyone might get foolish.

"Down," Sylvanas said.

They set the dolly down.

Inside, the store rose into beams and shadow. Sacks stood in ranks under the loft. Dust hung in the light from the high vents. Men had been working here since dawn; the room still held their track marks, spilled grain, sweat. Under all of it sat the necromantic drag.

The pull thickened in the center rows.

Jaina crossed to it fast, boots sinking in the spill, and laid her free hand against the nearest stack. Cold climbed into her palm at once, wet and patient and greedy, sunk through with the same buried drag she had felt in Hearthglen's stores before the dead rose inside the walls.

Here it still held together.
Here it could still be broken.

"This one matters," she said. "Shut the doors. Nobody in, nobody out. If there are ledgers, bring them. If carts already left, I need the routes."

"How long?" Sylvanas asked.

"Minutes."

"You have two."

Jaina dropped into the spilled grain and drove the ferrule of her staff into the boards.

Only sacks, dust, rough planks, and the buried thread Kel'Thuzad had spent so long teaching dead flesh to answer. She reached for it the way a hand reaches into foul water for the bottom. Cold ran up her arm. The grain answered sluggishly, then all at once, a murmur through the whole store turning toward her.

Spoil, she thought, and rammed frost through the pattern where it wanted continuity.

The sacks under her hand changed first. Dryness clotted and went soft. The smell turned in a rush. Sweet grain dropped past food into cellar rot. Moisture bled through burlap. Top sacks sagged against one another.

Someone shouted outside.

Jaina held the pressure. Beauty had nothing to do with it. She found the preserving shell, the part built to survive road, handling, oven heat, and split it where the weave held tightest. Once that went, the rest followed badly. Flour thickened to paste inside the cloth. Grain below it darkened, clumped, and died.

Her teeth hurt from clenching. The pull in the grain dragged once more against the staff, then broke.

Sound rushed back in. Teamsters shouting. Hooves on stone. Wood dropped in the next lane. But the inward pull that had sat at the store's center was gone. The place was only a building again.

Jaina pulled her hand free and nearly went over where she knelt.

Sylvanas caught her elbow. "Done?"

Jaina swallowed hard against the taste climbing her throat. "This stock's dead."

A guard had edged in just far enough to see the nearest split bag. Wet grey clumps bulged through the seam and dropped to the boards with the soft sound of ruined meal. "By the Light."

"Save it," Sylvanas said. "Where did the loaded carts go?"

That moved him. He jerked toward the office nook where a clerk had been trying to vanish behind a ledger board.

"District rounds," the clerk said. "Market Row, east quarter, two church kitchens, and private contracts along Festival Lane. Morning loads already left. Two more before dawn."

Jaina shut them for one beat.

Too many.

"Maps." She held out her hand. "Street packet, if you have one."

He fumbled under the board and passed her a folded city sheet smudged with thumb grease.

"How many other big stores?" she asked.

"Two on this side of the city. More by the southern market."

"Which one still has stock?"

"North Lane warehouse, if the guild didn't clear it at first bell."

Sylvanas let go of her arm only when she was sure Jaina's knees would hold. "Move."

They were out the door before the guard found another prayer.

Outside, bread was already in children's hands.

A girl no older than ten carried two small loaves wrapped in cloth. A boy behind her tore off a heel and stuffed it into his mouth while his mother argued over coppers with a cart man whose apron still held flour handprints. Bells kept time over the roofs. Deeper toward Market Row, oven doors thudded and reopened.

Jaina stopped dead enough that Cyndia had to slip around her.

Bread steam drifted through the lane.

"Jaina."

Sylvanas did not touch her.
She did not need to.

Jaina opened the map with shaking hands. "North Lane warehouse. Half a district east. If it still has enough stock, we hit it. After that we count. No more guessing off the map."

"Good."

Cyndia had already gone ahead to clear the first turn. She came back at a run and pointed with her knife. "Service lanes. Market's jammed with handcarts."

They cut east.

People still moved through the service lanes on morning errands. Service alleys behind market houses. Slop buckets waiting for collection. Stable boys hauling feed. A line of novices in chapel grey carrying baskets under cloth covers. Through one kitchen door, a woman floured a board while her younger son stole scraps from the dough and laughed when she slapped his wrist. That landed harder than the dead store had. Grain in sacks was arithmetic.

They broke into North Lane with the second warehouse ahead and trouble already spilling from its doors.

Half the stock was gone. Jaina read that before she hit the threshold. Empty pallet rows on the left. Remaining sacks scattered into quick loads for carts waiting three deep outside. Worse, the men inside moved with the careful brittleness of people holding routine together by force. One sweated through his collar despite the chill. Another had flour on his arms and the pinched grey mouth of sickness starting under the skin.

A whistle cut through the lane.

Three short notes.
One long.
Direction.

Jaina turned. He stood by the rear loading ramp in a plain wool coat and warehouse cap, another warehouse man at a glance. The whistle gave him away. So did the way the nearest people flinched half a beat after he used it, and the corpse in the side alley hauling itself upright before the family edging away understood what they were seeing.

"There," she said.

Sylvanas was already drawing.

The arrow took the handler through the throat as he turned to shout again. He hit the ramp rail, clawed at the shaft, and toppled backward between two carts.

Workers shouted, carts jolted, and people broke in every direction.

The workers nearest the doors froze. One sick man jerked around at the fall, went stiff from heel to crown, and stayed too straight while his jaw worked open. Farther down the alley another body answered the whistle late, rose on a broken knee, and lunged for the nearest living thing with blind hunger sharpened by the command it had just heard.

"Cyndia, alley!" Sylvanas snapped.

Cyndia took it at once, bow up, arrow in the first corpse's eye before the second had finished climbing the wall.

Jaina drove through the warehouse door and slammed her senses into the remaining stock hard enough to make her vision swim.

Bad. Worse than the first. The weave sat in broken pockets now, some stripped thin where the morning loads had gone, some still thick in the center stacks. Enough to spoil. Not enough to matter if she wasted time doing it neatly.

Behind her, a cart horse screamed.

"Quickly," Sylvanas said.

A raw sound caught in Jaina's throat and never became a laugh. There was no other way left.

She planted the staff in spilled meal and ripped the weave open.

This store was filthier to break. The contamination had already been broken into smaller bodies, smaller movements, smaller destinations. No single heavy pull in the room now. A nest of them, each still tied to stock already on wheels and in kitchens and on hands. She found the deepest pockets and drove cold straight through them until the burlap darkened and the grain inside collapsed into foul wet weight. One stack went over with a slap and burst against the boards. Grey paste ran under the loading platform.

The shouting outside changed note.

Pain now.

Jaina broke the last pocket and yanked the staff free. The surviving stock slumped out of use in front of her.

She staggered back into the lane.

Cyndia stood at the alley mouth with one boot on a corpse's shoulder, wrenching her arrow free. Two more bodies lay in the gutter beyond, both dressed like laborers, one still wearing a delivery strap across the chest. Sylvanas stood by the carts, bow half raised, watching the corners. No second signal came. The first one to try was choking on her arrow.

All around them, warm rolls sat under cloth, half a loaf rested on an old man's knee, and bread cooled on a board behind an open kitchen door.

A baker's apprentice stood in the nearest cart with a tray of little morning rolls under cloth. He still had not dropped them, even after the horse screamed. Across the lane an old man sat on his doorstep with half a loaf on his knee, fixed on the ruined grain spilling from the warehouse threshold. The kitchen door behind him stood open. More bread cooled on a board.

Jaina smelled char, sour magic, horse foam, bread.

She reached the cart and flipped back the cloth.

Rolls. Still warm. The top one gave under her fingers and sprang back. The apprentice jerked away, baffled more than afraid. Behind him another boy crossed the intersection with baskets hooked over each arm. A woman came out of a side kitchen with loaves bundled in a towel against her chest. More loaves, baskets, and hands crossed in front of her faster than she could hold them in her head.

How many mouths already outran them?

Bread was already in hands, houses, and bodies. She could strike a third site and save one street. At most two. Then Arthas would reach the gate and ask if the city could still be saved, and she would answer too late.

Sylvanas stepped closer and read her once. "Worth another?"

Jaina turned past her toward the houses, shutters open over breakfasts already eaten. Kitchen smoke rode low with the taint now, one sour thread through the ordinary life of the street. Above them the bells of Alonsus Chapel marked the hour.

"No," she said.

Cyndia came out of the alley, wiped the arrowhead on a dead man's coat, and measured them once. She did not ask.

"We go back," Jaina said. "Now."

No one argued.

They ran for the horses.

They drove back through the service roads hard. Open shutters. Plates already scraped. Crusts in a midden bucket. A priest carrying bread under linen toward a hospice. Two sisters on a stoop sharing a heel while their mother shook a rug over the rail.

At the yard their mounts came up wild-eyed but ready. Cyndia was in the saddle first. Sylvanas passed Jaina the clerk's map without being asked, then mounted in one hard motion that made the exhausted horse find another stride.

"How many stores?" Sylvanas asked as they drove for the gate.

"Enough that one more won't save it."

"And?"

Jaina kept her attention on the lane ahead because the words were easier thrown at stone than at Sylvanas's face. "Too many have already eaten."

Sylvanas said nothing after that. She only rode.

At the southern approach, Arthas's column hit first as sound, then sight. Iron. Tack. Shouted files. The deep beat of many hooves brought up fast and reined in hard at the wall. Gate guards flattened themselves against the stone to clear rank. Falric was already under the arch, mud to his knees, voice gone hoarse from turning officers and carts all morning.

"Where is he?" Jaina asked.

Falric read the answer in her before he fully placed her face. His jaw tightened once. "Main gate. Just came in with Lord Uther."

They left the horses in a snarl of reins and sweating flanks and shoved through the last line of guards on foot.

The southern gate was the formal one: high arch, worked iron, the Stratholme crest picked out above it for anyone entering under the city's authority. Arthas stood there with Light's Vengeance still in hand, lather drying on his horse and road dust filmed over older damage on his plate. Uther beside him stood broader than the gatework, one scarred hand on the older hammer at his side. Alonsus Chapel's bells still carried over the roofs. Beside that gate, he looked less like another officer than the city's oldest oath dragged into armor. Soldiers were still filing in behind them. Nobody here had yet entered the city in force.

Arthas found Jaina at once and stepped down before a groom reached him. He came at her through the churned mud with the speed of a man who had carried one question in his teeth for half the road.

"Well?" he said.

Jaina still had flour on one sleeve and ash in her mouth from the second store. At her back, carts rattled deeper in the streets while smoke kept rising over the roofs.

"One granary held enough stock for me to spoil it before the carts moved," she said. "A second warehouse still had enough left to kill. The method works on a single store."

Arthas sharpened at once. His gauntlet closed harder on Light's Vengeance. "Then we still have a chance."

"Listen to the rest."

He did, though the muscle in his jaw jumped.

"Two stores won't turn the city back," Jaina said. "Morning loads were out before we reached the gate. Bread is already in houses, church kitchens, street carts, and private contracts. It's crossed from stock into people. I can ruin what still sits in sacks. I cannot drag it back out of ovens, stomachs, or blood."

The officers around them fell silent.

Uther did not move. His face stayed on hers, grave and steady. He waited.

Arthas came one step closer, mud sucking at his sabatons. "How many districts?"

Jaina shook her head. "Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It's the only honest one I have in the time you left me."

His nostrils flared. "Can the city still be saved?"

Flour and ash had dried on her tongue. She tasted both when she opened her mouth.

"No," Jaina said. "Not cleanly. Not now."

Arthas did not blink. His mouth flattened. His eyes went blank.

Uther shut his.

Jaina stood with Stratholme at her back and spoiled grain drying under her nails.
