# Chapter 16: Before the Gates

The road north of Hearthglen had already lost the patience to be a road.

Wagon ruts bit so deep the horses had to take the shoulder where the ditch grass still held. Mud dried in ridges from yesterday's traffic and broke under the hooves in hard flakes. Jaina rode low over the gelding's neck and kept one hand loose on the reins, the other around the saddle pommel when the ground jarred too hard. The horse's mouth had gone white. Cyndia's mare on the left carried the same crust around the bit. On Jaina's right, Sylvanas's mount took the bad footing cleanly and never wasted a step.

Behind them, far enough to be only a stain in the southern haze, Arthas's main column kept throwing dust into the air.

Jaina looked back once and stopped herself from looking again. The distance did not matter in miles now. It mattered in ovens. In shutters opening onto breakfast tables. In bakery boys carrying trays out the back doors before the prince's first outriders even saw the walls.

"Market Row first if the formal gate's choking," she said. The words came out rough from the pace and the cold air cutting at her throat. "If we can reach the north stores through the service road, we start there. Large concentrations first. Anything still in sacks. Anything still stacked."

"And if the grain is already split?" Cyndia called.

"Then we go where the bread leaves heat."

Sylvanas rode close enough to catch that without making Jaina raise her voice. "Say it in streets."

Jaina swallowed and did. "Main stores by the service gate. Bakehouses along Market Row. Any yard with district carts still loading. If I can spoil a full stock before it moves, that quarter loses the next wave. If it is already in loaves, we are late there."

"How late?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina tightened her hand on the pommel through another hard jolt. "I don't know yet."

"Use smaller words."

She almost snapped back. The answer died before it reached her mouth. Sylvanas was not cutting at her. She was cutting the thought down to bone.

"If children are already eating in the doorways," Jaina said, "that district is gone past anything I can fix with sacks and fire."

Sylvanas nodded once. That was all. She turned in the saddle far enough to mark the fork ahead where the service road split from the broader southern run. Cyndia saw it at the same moment, lifted two fingers, and angled left before anybody had to tell her.

The road changed as they closed on the city. Farm wagons thickened first, then carts heading the other direction with empty flour bins knocking against one another in the back. Men in aprons rode standing on the rear boards to save time. A priest's mule went by at a stumble, brass lamps thudding from the packs. Bells carried thin and bright through the damp air, the ordinary civic kind meant to order a morning, not warn a wall.

Stratholme's outer farms wore the city's appetite openly. Long barns, mill sheds, fenced kitchen plots gone ragged from hard harvest traffic, smoke rising from ovens too small to be anything but private work for households or inns. The walls came in and out between stands of leafless trees, grey stone already familiar from maps and relay strips and all the talk they had spent on it. Jaina had been inside the city a hundred times in her head. Service gate. Market Row. Bakehouse lanes. North stores. District cuts. None of that gave her the measure of the place the way the bread smell did when the wind shifted.

Warm yeast. Wood smoke. A sweetness under it that turned her stomach.

"There." She pointed with two fingers. "That smoke line. Multiple ovens. Market side."

Sylvanas's gaze went where she pointed, then climbed to the wall, the tower, the line of traffic bunched outside the less-ornate northern gate. Teamsters, servants, two guards in city colors trying to sort shouting into order, and a pair of carts already half unloaded in the yard beyond.

"Service gate's open," Cyndia said.

"Because nobody expects a city at breakfast to need defending from its own bread," Jaina muttered.

At that, the corner of Sylvanas's mouth moved once, not a smile, only the old dry edge coming through under speed. "Good. That saves time."

They hit the last rise at a gallop, slid down through churned mud, and forced a lane through the knot outside the gate. Jaina caught faces in passing: a baker red to the elbows with flour, a drover swearing over a split wheel, a woman holding two round loaves against her hip with the possessive care used for infants. Nobody had the look of a city under siege. Nobody had been told the day was over. Habit still ran the place, and habit had always been the plague's ally.

The nearest guard put up a hand too late. "You can't just—"

"Move," Sylvanas said, already past him.

The man did, because Sylvanas's voice left no room for argument. Cyndia peeled off to the side, took the horse lines and the cart mouths in one sweep, then caught Jaina's gaze and pointed across the yard to a long timbered building under a steep slate roof. High vents. Loading doors. Sack traffic.

The first store.

Jaina swung down before the gelding had settled, her knees nearly folding from the ride. She used the horse's shoulder for one hard beat, shoved herself upright, and went for the doors with the staff already in her hand. Flour dust lay thick in the cracks of the threshold. Cart boards had chewed the sill smooth. A pair of teamsters were hauling a dolly out and arguing over district marks painted on the sacks.

"Stop moving anything," Jaina said.

One of them stared at her blue robes, then at the staff. "Who in the Light are you supposed to be?"

"Somebody trying to keep you alive. Put it down."

He looked past her for permission from the city guard straggling in behind them. What he got instead was Sylvanas stepping into the doorway with her bow in hand and Cyndia taking the yard angle where anybody foolish might decide to run.

"Down," Sylvanas said.

They set the dolly down.

Inside, the store opened upward in shadow and beams. Sacks rose in ranks almost to the loft. Grain dust hung where the light came through the high vents in pale slants. Men had been working here all morning. That much stayed in the air and on the floorboards and in the fresh track lines around the loading platforms. Beneath it sat the other thing.

Wrongness gathered thickest where the grain waited in the center rows. Jaina crossed to it fast, boots sinking in the spill underfoot, and put her free hand against the nearest stack. Cold climbed into her palm at once. Not the clean cold of her own school. This one was wet, patient, and greedy, sunk through with the same buried pull Hearthglen's stores had carried before the dead rose inside the walls.

Here, though, it still had shape. Here it still answered to concentration.

"This one matters," she said. "Seal the doors. Nobody in, nobody out. If there are ledgers, I want them. If there are carts gone already, I need the routes."

"How long?" Sylvanas asked.

"Minutes."

"You have two."

Jaina knelt in spilled grain and drove the ferrule of the staff into the boards.

She had no circle room, no prepared floor, no quiet. Only sacks, dust, rough planks, and the deep buried thread Kel'Thuzad had spent so many hours teaching dead flesh to answer. She reached for that thread the way a hand reaches into filthy water to find the bottom. Cold went up her arm. The grain answered sluggishly at first, then all at once, a storehouse-deep murmur turning its face toward her.

Spoil, she thought, and gave it frost where the pattern wanted continuity.

The first change ran through the sacks under her hand. Dryness thickened, clotted, then went slack. The smell turned. Sweet grain soured in a single breath and kept going, falling past food into cellar rot. Moisture sweated through burlap. The top sacks slumped against one another.

Outside, somebody shouted.

Jaina kept the pressure on.

The point was not beauty. The point was rupture. She found the preserving shell, the part built to ride road and handling and oven heat, and shoved cold into the places where the weave held tightest until it lost the clean relation between one sack and the next. Once that broke, the rest followed badly. Flour thickened to paste inside the cloth. Grain below it darkened, clumped, and died.

Her teeth set hard enough to hurt. The room gave one last necromantic drag against the staff, then let go.

Silence rushed in behind it.

Not real silence. Teamsters still swore in the yard. Hooves struck stone outside the gate. Somebody dropped wood in the next lane. But the low inward pull that had sat in the middle of the store since she entered was gone. The place had become only a building again.

Jaina pulled her hand off the sacks and almost sat down where she was.

Sylvanas caught her elbow before the floor reached her. "Done?"

Jaina swallowed against the taste rising in the back of her throat. "This stock is dead."

One of the guards had edged in far enough to stare at the nearest split bag. Wet grey clumps bulged through the seam and dropped to the boards with the soft sound of ruined meal. "By the Light."

"By the Light nothing," Sylvanas said. "Where did the loaded carts go?"

That got the man moving. He jerked toward the office nook on the left where a clerk had been trying to make himself smaller behind a ledger board.

"District rounds," the clerk said. "Market Row, east quarter, two church kitchens, and private contracts along Festival Lane. Morning loads already left. Two more before dawn."

Jaina shut her eyes for one beat.

Too many.

"Maps." She held out her hand. "Street packet, if you have one."

The clerk looked from her to the store to the sacks failing on the floor and finally understood that denial would not put the grain back together. He fumbled through the shelf beneath the board and handed her a folded ward of city parchment smudged with thumb grease.

"How many other large stores?" she asked.

"Two on this side of the city. More by the southern market."

"Which one still has stock?"

"North lane warehouse," he said. "If the guild didn't clear it at first bell."

Sylvanas let go of Jaina's arm only when she was sure Jaina's knees had decided to hold. "Move."

They were out the door before the guard found another prayer.

The yard outside killed the triumph before it had time to become anything dangerous.

A girl no older than ten was carrying two small loaves wrapped in a cloth. A boy behind her tore a heel off one and shoved it into his mouth while his mother argued over coppers with a cart man whose apron still wore flour handprints. The city moved around the spoiled store without changing pace. Bells kept time over the roofs. Somewhere deeper in Market Row, oven doors thudded and reopened.

Jaina stopped short enough that Cyndia had to veer around her.

Bread steam drifted through the lane in little warm breaths.

The first store mattered. The first store was real. The first store would save whoever had not yet eaten from that stock. It would not pull a single mouthful back out of the bodies already carrying the city toward its own death.

"Jaina." Sylvanas did not touch her this time. She did not need to. The word landed hard enough.

Jaina opened the map with hands that had started to shake. "North lane warehouse," she said. "Half a district east. If it still has enough volume, we hit it. After that we count. No more guessing from the map."

"Good."

Cyndia had already gone ahead to clear the first turn. She came back at a run, braid hitting between her shoulders, and pointed with the short knife she kept for signaling when her bow would only frighten crowds. "Faster through the service lanes. Market's blocked with handcarts."

They cut east.

Stratholme held together under their boots by nothing but habit and traffic law. Service alleys behind the market houses. Slop buckets waiting for collection. Stable boys hauling feed. A line of novices in chapel grey carrying baskets under cloth covers. Jaina caught a glimpse through one open kitchen door of a woman flouring a board while her younger son stole scraps from the edge of the dough and laughed when she slapped his wrist. The sight hit harder than the dead store had. Grain in sacks still belonged to arithmetic. Dough on a mother's board belonged to people who had not done anything but get up hungry.

They burst into North Lane with the second warehouse in sight and chaos already spilling from its doors.

Half the stock was gone. Jaina could read that before she reached the threshold. Empty pallet rows yawned on the left. The remaining sacks were scattered, not ranked, broken apart into fast pickups for carts waiting three deep outside. Worse: men inside the warehouse moved with the careful, brittle obedience of people trying very hard to keep a routine from snapping. One was sweating through his collar despite the chill. Another had flour on his arms and the grey pinched mouth of sickness beginning under the skin.

Then the whistle cut through the lane.

Three short notes. One long.

Not alarm. Direction.

Jaina turned toward the sound. He stood near the rear loading ramp in a plain wool coat and a warehouse cap, the sort of face a lane forgot as soon as it passed. The whistle made him real. So did the way everybody close by flinched half a heartbeat after he used it and the nearest corpse in the side alley dragged itself upright before the family trying to back away could understand what the movement meant.

"There," she said, and pointed.

Sylvanas was already drawing.

The arrow took the handler through the throat at the exact moment he turned to shout again. He struck the ramp rail, fingers clawing at the shaft, and toppled backward into the gap between two carts.

The lane erupted.

The workers nearest the loading doors did the worst possible thing: they froze. One of the sick men jerked around at the sound of the fall, went stiff from heel to crown, and stayed standing too straight while his jaw worked open. Another body farther down the alley answered the whistle late, rose on a broken knee, and lunged toward the closest living thing with blind hunger sharpened into line by the command it had just received.

"Cyndia, alley!" Sylvanas snapped.

Cyndia took it at once, bow up, arrow in the first corpse's eye before the second had finished climbing the wall.

Jaina drove through the warehouse door and hit the remaining stock with her senses hard enough to make her vision swim.

Bad. Worse than the first. The preserving shell sat across the store in broken pockets, some already stripped thin where morning loads had gone, some still thick in the center stacks. Enough to spoil. Not enough to matter if she spent too long making it clean.

Behind her, a cart horse screamed.

"Quickly," Sylvanas said.

Jaina almost laughed at the word. There was no other way left to do this anyway.

She planted the staff in a patch of spilled meal and tore the weave open by force.

This time the store fought dirtier. Contamination had already been broken into smaller bodies, smaller movements, smaller destinations. Instead of one heavy pull in the room, she had a nest of them, all trying to keep relation to stock already on wheels and in kitchens and on hands. She found the deepest remaining pockets and drove cold straight through them until the burlap darkened and the grain inside collapsed into foul wet weight. One stack went over with a slap and burst against the floorboards. Grey paste ran under the nearest loading platform.

Shouting outside changed note.

Not panic now. Pain.

Jaina broke the last pocket and wrenched the staff free. The surviving stock slumped out of usefulness in front of her eyes.

She staggered back through the door and into a lane with too many answers in it.

Cyndia was at the alley mouth with one boot on a corpse's shoulder, hauling her arrow free. Two more bodies lay down in the gutter beyond, both dressed like laborers, one still wearing a delivery strap across the chest. Sylvanas stood by the carts, bow half raised, watching the corners for the next command source that never came because the first one had died with her arrow in his throat.

And all around them, the city kept confessing.

A baker's apprentice stood in the back of the nearest cart with a tray of little morning rolls under cloth. He had not dropped them even when the horse screamed. Across the lane, an old man sat on a doorstep with half a loaf on his knee and stared at the warehouse floor where ruined grain was spreading from the threshold like slop. The kitchen door beyond him stood open. Inside, more bread cooled on a board.

Jaina smelled char, sour magic, horse foam, bread.

She reached the nearest cart and flipped the cloth back.

Rolls. Still warm. The top one gave under her fingers and sprang back. The apprentice holding the tray jerked away from her hand, baffled more than frightened. Behind him another boy crossed the intersection with a basket hooked over each arm. A woman came out of a side kitchen with loaves wrapped in a towel against her chest. A novice in chapel grey hurried past with bread stacked to his chin. The lane kept multiplying the count faster than her mind could hold it.

How many mouths already outran them? Ten thousand? Less? Enough.

The count slammed into place without numbers.

Not one more store. Not one more lane. The line had already crossed from stock to bodies, from bodies to households, from households to a city that still thought it had time to complain over prices and get children fed before noon. She could strike a third site and save one street, perhaps two. Then Arthas would reach the gate and ask the question that mattered, and she would answer it late because she had been spending the truth one district at a time to avoid speaking it whole.

Sylvanas stepped closer and searched her once. "Worth another?"

Jaina looked past her toward the houses, the shutters open to breakfasts already eaten. Kitchen smoke rode low with the necromantic taint now, one sour current threaded through the ordinary life of the street. Somewhere above them the cathedral bells marked the hour.

"No," she said.

The word came clean. It hurt less than she had expected and more.

Cyndia came out of the alley, wiped the arrowhead on a dead man's coat, and looked from one of them to the other. She did not ask.

"We go back," Jaina said. "Now."

No one argued.

They ran for the horses.

The return through the service roads went faster because Jaina had stopped looking for one more place to try. Every turn now measured only time to the gate. Different things rose now that the search had gone out of her. Open shutters with plates already scraped. Crusts in a midden bucket. A priest carrying bread under a linen cloth toward a hospice. Two sisters on a stoop, sharing a heel between them while their mother shook a rug over the rail. The city did not know it had already answered her.

At the service yard they found their mounts wild-eyed but ready. Cyndia was in the saddle first. Sylvanas handed Jaina the clerk's map without being asked and then mounted in one fluid motion that made the exhausted horse beneath her look young again out of sheer refusal to fail.

"How many stores?" Sylvanas asked as they broke for the gate.

"Enough to prove the plan," Jaina said.

"And?"

Jaina kept her eyes on the lane ahead because the words were easier aimed at stone than at Sylvanas's face. "Too many mouths."

Sylvanas said nothing after that. She only rode.

By the time they reached the southern approach, Arthas's column had become sound before sight. Iron, tack, shouted files, the deep rolling beat of many hooves brought up fast and reined in hard at the wall. Gate guards had flattened themselves against the stone to clear the way for rank. Falric was already there under the arch, mud to his knees and his face cut down to pure function, turning officers and carts with a voice gone hoarse from hours of it.

"Where is he?" Jaina asked.

Falric recognized the answer in her before he recognized her face. His jaw tightened once. "Main gate. Just came up with Lord Uther."

They left the horses in a confusion of reins and sweating flanks and pushed through the last line of guards on foot.

The southern gate of Stratholme was the formal face of the city: high arch, worked iron, stone broad enough to make a prince look right riding through it. Arthas stood under that arch with Light's Vengeance still in hand, lather drying on his horse and his own plate filmed with road dust over older damage. Uther beside him looked broader than the gatework, one scarred hand on the older hammer at his side. Soldiers were still filing in behind them. Nobody here had yet entered the city in force. The wall and the prince and the city held themselves on the lip of decision.

Arthas's gaze found Jaina first.

He stepped down from the saddle before a groom reached him and came toward her through the churned mud with the speed of a man who had been holding one question in his teeth for half the road.

"Well?" he said.

No title. No courtesy. Just that.

Jaina still had flour on one sleeve and ash in her mouth from the second store. The city sat at her back like a hand she could not pry off.

"One granary held enough concentration for me to spoil it before the stock moved," she said. "A second warehouse still had volume. I broke that too. The method works at the node level."

Arthas's eyes sharpened. "Then we still have a chance."

"Listen to the rest."

He did, though the muscle in his jaw jumped once.

"The city is already past the point where two stores matter," Jaina said. "Morning loads were out before we reached the gate. Bread is already in houses, church kitchens, street carts, private contracts. The contamination has crossed from stock into people. I can still ruin what sits in sacks. I cannot take it back out of stomachs, ovens, or blood."

Silence hit the officers around them in a visible ring.

Uther did not move. His eyes stayed on Jaina's face, grave and steady, as if he had known the shape of the answer before she gave it and had still needed to hear it spoken plain.

Arthas came one step closer. "How many districts?"

Jaina shook her head. "Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It's the only honest one I have in the time you left me."

His nostrils flared. "Can the city still be saved?"

Underneath it sat the real question. Cleanly. Without doing what he already knew he would do. Without making him choose between his people and the horror moving under their skin. Without proving Uther right about him. Without making Sylvanas stand witness to another prince reaching for steel because nothing else was left.

There was no answer there for her to give.

Flour and ash had dried on her tongue. She tasted both when she opened her mouth.

"No," Jaina said. "Not cleanly. Not now."

The words hung in the gate between them.

Arthas did not blink. Whatever he had hoped to hear died so fast in his face that it almost looked like relief.

Uther shut his eyes once.

Jaina stood with the city at her back and the ruined grain still on her hands and knew, with a clarity uglier than any theorem Kel'Thuzad had ever laid before her, that being right had brought them exactly this far and no farther.
