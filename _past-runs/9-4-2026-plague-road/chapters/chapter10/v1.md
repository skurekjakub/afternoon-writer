# Chapter 10: The Estate

Eastweald had the insolence to look healthy.

Jaina rode through wagon ruts pressed deep into an old road, her Thalassian mare sweating under the cold while orchard walls and trimmed ditches kept proving that somebody here still paid men to keep the land neat. Apple branches lifted black and bare against the morning. Frost silvered the low grass. Cart tracks crossed and recrossed the road with the heavy, patient logic of harvest work done at scale. House Barov fed half a province and meant to go on feeding it.

The wrongness lived under that polish.

It tugged at her hands through the reins first, a thin live tingle under the skin, then thickened as the road bent south-east. The surface country said grain, rent rolls, stone walls, labor. The pull beneath it said wards laid in layers, old work built over newer work, and a denser knot of necromantic pressure beyond the house itself. Past the orchards. Past the estate road. Out toward the lake.

She would have heard Sylvanas ask how long this took.

The thought arrived cleanly, without ache. How long to the gate. How long to a room with too many exits. How long back out if the house smiled and closed behind her. Sylvanas would have priced all of it before the estate came into view. Cyndia would have checked the treeline and the service road together. Lyriel would have been on the right flank if the world had not chosen otherwise.

Jaina had a horse, a packet of copied route scraps, Cyndia's approach notes folded inside her sleeve, and enough Kirin Tor authority to make a noble house hesitate before it lied outright. No escort. No bow lane. No patience for pretending she could ghost up a wall like a ranger.

So she rode straight at the front gate.

The Barov crest hung worked in iron above it, dark against pale stone. Two men in estate colors had been speaking with a wagon driver just inside the arch. They stopped when they saw her. One of them glanced at her staff, then at the horse, then at the quality of the robes, and the calculation tightened his mouth before he could smooth it away.

Good. Let the house react first.

The nearer man stepped out into the road and bowed just enough to be respectful without pretending humility.

"My lady?"

"Jaina Proudmoore," she said. "Protégée of Archmage Antonidas. I require an audience with Lord Barov."

He blinked once at the name, once at the tone. A better guard might have hidden the second. "Is Lord Barov expecting—"

"No," Jaina said. "If he had been, we could both have spared ourselves that sentence."

The driver's eyes dropped at once. The guard's did not. He held his place, which told her something useful about the house. Servants here were trained to absorb pressure without turning to water.

"The estate is occupied with harvest concerns," he said.

"Then he will understand why a Kirin Tor mage has come to ask about his grain."

That landed. His eyes withdrew a fraction, the look people wore when a conversation reached the exact door they had hoped to keep shut.

He stood aside.

"Of course, my lady."

The mare tossed her head once going under the gate. A good horse, disciplined, road-bred, steady around ward hum. The air changed inside the walls. Less wind. More stone. Men moved in the yard with baskets, ledgers, harness, sacks. Workers. Actual workers, not prisoners shoved through an ugly theater. The place ran like an estate that expected to endure winter.

That made it worse.

Jaina dismounted in front of the main house and passed the reins to a stable boy whose sleeves were patched twice at the wrists and clean besides. Another detail filed itself away. Barov money. Barov order. Barov hands still feeding the machine.

The front doors opened before she reached them.

Warmth met her without smoke.

The great hall beyond carried the expensive stillness of a room nobody had to heat by chopping wood. A hearth big enough to roast an ox stood dark and empty, yet the air remained mild against her face. Enchanted stones in the walls, then. Imported work. Old money that had decided visible comfort mattered more than a flame.

Lord Alexei Barov rose from a chair near a low table where tea waited for two.

He was silver-haired, narrow through the shoulders, dressed in dark wool that sat too well to be local tailoring. His hands rested on the chair arms in easy silence. No ink under the nails. No callus at the thumb. A man who signed things and let the world obey.

"Lady Proudmoore," he said, as though she had been invited for precisely this hour. "House Barov is honored."

"I doubt that."

His smile held. "Honored, then, by your frankness."

Jaina did not sit.

"Your grain routes have become very interesting," she said. "Interesting enough to bring me here."

"A regrettable subject." He gestured toward the tea. "Please."

"No."

"As you wish." He folded one hand over the other. "I assume this concerns Andorhal. We have suffered losses there ourselves. Contamination rumors travel faster than wagons. Panic faster than both. Estates our size attract blame because scale attracts blame."

There it was already: injured respectability, clean enough to wear in company.

Jaina let her gaze travel the room. Thick carpet over polished wood. Books shelved by size and binding, not use. No family clutter. No disorder. A receiving room built to tell visitors that taste and money had agreed to live together permanently.

"I came to ask about more than rumor," she said.

"Then ask."

"How long has scholarly work been conducted at Caer Darrow under your protection?"

That was the first true break in him. Tiny. Brief. The sort of pause most men thought invisible because it took less than a breath.

"You are direct," Alexei said.

"You keep saying things I already know."

The smile thinned. "Caer Darrow houses a private academy. Rural patronage of scholarship is not a crime yet, though I admit the larger cities often behave as if it should be."

"What discipline?"

"Comparative magical theory, among others."

"Necromancy."

He did not flinch. Better than the guard.

"A vulgar word," he said mildly. "The sort used by people who believe naming a field coarsely is the same as understanding it."

Jaina almost laughed. Not because it was funny. Because it was polished too well. Somebody in this house had practiced the line.

"Then educate me," she said.

"Gladly," a woman's voice said from the doorway.

Jaina turned.

Jandice Barov wore burgundy robes built out of Dalaran lines and Barov cloth. The seam language was right, the fall of the sleeves right, the weight of the fabric chosen by someone who had watched mages work and cared about where the cloth would catch at the wrist. Her dark hair was pulled back severely. Ink marked the side of one thumb. Reagent scarring crossed the skin between the fingers of her right hand in fine pale tracks.

Peer's hands.

The recognition struck fast enough to feel rude.

Jandice saw it happen. Pleasure moved at the corner of her mouth and settled before it could become a full smile.

"Lady Proudmoore," she said. "I am Jandice Barov. My father was just about to describe our embarrassment with less precision than it deserves. May I rescue him?"

Alexei leaned back at once.

There. The competence sat there.

"Please do," he said.

Jandice crossed the room with measured ease, every line of her posture controlled until she came close enough for the hands to matter again. Ink. Chalk flattening at the thumb. The small burns that came from handling reagents in glass, not reading about them from a safe chair.

"We have students," Jandice said. "Researchers. Archive staff. Some traditionalists find the curriculum distasteful because they mistake prohibition for rigor. That is their burden, not ours."

"And the grain?" Jaina asked.

"The grain," Alexei said, reclaiming one lane with practised smoothness, "is my burden. Theft, spoilage, panic pricing, opportunists along the road. I imagine one begins to look monstrous once enough frightened men put the same noble name in the same sentence."

"You expected scrutiny," Jaina said.

"I expected famine to make everyone ugly."

"You seem very prepared for ugliness."

Jandice's eyes sharpened. Alexei's hands remained still. The house had been waiting for this knock, then. Maybe not for her specifically. For a version of her.

"We can spend the morning fencing politely," Jandice said, "or I can show you the work respectable people keep misnaming."

Jaina looked at her.

Young. About her own age. Old enough for a choice to have hardened into habit. The sort of woman Jaina would have argued with for two hours in a Dalaran archive and only realized afterward that evening had come and both lamps had burned low.

That was dangerous in a different register than the house.

"You would permit that?" Jaina asked.

"Permit?" Jandice glanced at her father, then back. "Lady Proudmoore, if the Kirin Tor's brightest apprentice has come all this way to inspect our provincial little school, I would be a poor host indeed if I showed you only tea."

Alexei's gaze touched his daughter with the weary calm of a man who knew resistance was now decorative.

"Jandice," he said, "mind the presentable route."

"Father." Her answer held just enough amusement to suggest this had happened before.

Jaina caught the exchange, and the fact nested under it. Alexei relaxed when Jandice took the problem in hand. He trusted her speed.

That settled one question.

She let herself sit at last, took the offered cup, and drank the tea because refusing a second time would waste energy she needed elsewhere. It was excellent. Of course it was.

"You keep a lake crossing for your academy," she said.

"A fortress does better on an island," Jandice said. "Even a scholarly one."

"Scholars who expect inspection usually keep records closer to the road."

"Scholars who expect theft keep them behind water."

Jaina set the cup down.

"Then let's go look at them."

The road from the house to the landing cut through ordered ground. Frost lay under espaliered branches. Low stone walls held back terraces where winter gardens slept in strict squares. The lake appeared only at the last turn, dark and almost lightless under a skin of mist.

Jandice led without hurrying. She had changed nothing visible before leaving the house, yet outside her guide's manner sat on her differently, lighter at the edges, more dangerous for that. Indoors she had been presenting a version of herself to an unwelcome visitor. Here she watched Jaina watching the estate and took the measure of each silence.

"You came alone," Jandice said.

"Yes."

"Bold."

"Efficient."

"Those words have never been perfect twins."

The landing held a long narrow boat tied to iron rings sunk into old stone. No boatman waited there. Jandice bent, untied the rope, and stepped in with the ease of repetition.

"You row yourself?" Jaina asked.

"When I want to arrive."

The lake breathed chemical cold. Preservation. Minerals. Old water kept too long in deep stone cisterns. Jaina felt the outer ward before the boat touched it, a faint tightness behind her teeth. Monitoring boundary. A notice, not a wall.

Easy extraction ends here, she thought, and did not need Sylvanas's missing voice to translate the fact.

She stepped into the boat anyway.

Jandice pushed them off. The oars entered the water with almost no sound. Mist dragged low over the surface, swallowing the shore behind them faster than it should have. Jaina looked back once and saw the estate already dimmed to a pale geometry of roof and wall.

"Beautiful, if one has the taste for it," Jandice said.

"Do you?"

"For beautiful things? Constantly. For badly observed ones, never."

The answer came too fast to be rehearsed. Jaina liked her a little for it and distrusted herself at once.

"Your father wasn't surprised enough by me," she said.

"My father believes most crises can be endured if the room is expensive enough."

"And you?"

Jandice's mouth shifted. "I believe most crises improve if the thinking inside them does."

There it was again. The reach in the sentence. Toward, not away.

The ward line took them with a brief green shimmer under the hull. Jaina felt the watchful pressure adjust, mark her, then pass the information inward. No hidden ambush in the act of crossing. Worse than an ambush. They already knew exactly what had entered.

Caer Darrow rose out of the mist by pieces. First the darker shape of the seawall. Then towers. Then the high mass of the keep itself, old stone converted rather than rebuilt, every line of the fortress still meant for defense even after scholarship had been folded into it. Narrow windows on the upper levels. Broad doors at the lower. No lights at the parapets. No banners. A place that wanted its life kept indoors.

Jandice rowed the last stretch in silence.

Students did not cluster on the landing. No bustling little academy greeted the boat with armfuls of notes and bright provincial ambition. A single porter in dark clothes waited with a lantern hooded against the daylight, which made the gesture stranger instead of less so.

Curated, then.

Jaina stepped onto the island and felt the deep pull of the place settle fully into her bones.

The strongest signature still did not come from the visible keep.

It came from under it.

The porter bowed them in through a side entry broad enough for carts and too clean for a ruin. Stone floors, scrubbed. Iron brackets polished. The first corridor ran straight and dry, lit by lamps that burned with steady yellow-white light behind fitted glass. No torch smoke. No drafty theatrics. The air smelled of limewash, ink, lamp oil, and the sharp medicinal taint of preserving compounds worked into the mortar over years.

Institution first. Horror later.

Jandice watched for the shift in her face and did not get what she wanted.

"Disappointed?" she asked.

"No."

"People usually are. They expect screaming crypts. We have lecture schedules."

Two students in dark robes moved ahead of them carrying a stack of slates between them. Neither looked frightened. Neither stared. They glanced once at Jaina's face, once at the staff, and stepped aside with the brisk irritation of people delayed on the way to actual work.

That landed harder than corpses would have.

Jaina walked beside Jandice through a hall where framed anatomical sketches hung between alcoves full of catalogued jars. The labels were neat. The shelves dusted. A preserved human hand lay on one instructor's table farther in, palm down, as casually placed as a pointer waiting for the next lesson.

"You teach openly," Jaina said.

"Within the building? Of course."

"I meant methodology."

"So did I."

They entered a lecture room with tiered benches and a board still marked from the previous class. Someone had drawn the stages of tissue breakdown in a fine decisive hand, then crossed part of the sequence out and replaced it with a better one. The notation in the margin hit Jaina in the chest. Antonidas's seminar marks. His school, carried forward in another hand.

She moved closer before she could stop herself.

"You know the system," Jandice said quietly.

"Yes."

"I thought you might."

The corrections on the board were recent. Chalk dust still clung to the ledge. Jaina read three lines and had to make herself keep breathing evenly. Whoever had written them had not abandoned Kirin Tor rigor. They had taken it somewhere the Council had nailed shut and gone on building with the stolen tools.

Jandice came to stand at her shoulder, near enough that the difference between their hands became unbearable again.

"The provincial caricature disappoints people," she said. "They expect enthusiasm with no discipline. We are spared that particular weakness."

"You were trained in Dalaran?"

"Briefly adjacent to it." A flash of annoyance. "Enough to know what it refuses on principle. More than enough to read what it produces. Come. The archive is more interesting than our classroom scraps."

They crossed two more corridors and a stair, the route turning just enough each time to remind Jaina that she was being shown a path, not a building. Doors stood open on tidy workrooms where assistants copied notes, sorted reagents, checked a rack of labeled vials against ledgers. A girl no older than nineteen argued with a man in a stained apron over measurements at a wash basin. The argument used terms any advanced circle in Dalaran would have recognized. The basin itself was etched with wardwork meant to contain spillage and little else.

Routine use. Routine adaptation. Ordinary bodies moving through an impossible system until the system became furniture.

It would have been easier if the place had wanted to leer.

The archive occupied a long chamber paneled in dark wood gone dry with age. Cabinets lined one wall. Locked cases lined the other. Three large tables filled the center, each under weighted lamps and surrounded by stacks of tied folios. A man with thin spectacles looked up when they entered, saw Jandice, and looked back down without question.

Expected.

Jandice moved to one cabinet, opened it with a key from inside her sleeve, and drew out a leather-bound ledger. She set it before Jaina with a care that was almost reverent.

"Recent circulation from the lower research rooms," she said. "Restricted, but then so are many things worth reading."

The leather was cracked at the hinge and warm from someone's hand.

Jaina opened it.

The first page might as well have been her own under a bad light.

Same notational spine. Same way of crowding the lower margin when a thought outran the original space allowed for it. Same clipped symbols to mark failed branches without wasting half a line on apology. Not identical handwriting. That would have been easier to dismiss. Kin handwriting. Lineage handwriting.

Kel'Thuzad's work or a student formed under him so completely the shapes had merged.

Jaina turned a page.

The theorem there pressed directly against the hole in the farmhouse read. Host response, retention through storage, patterned answer in conditioned tissue. Incomplete, still. The final waking step absent. Yet the thought moving toward it ran in a lane she could follow without stumbling.

Ink and reagent. Dalaran and something fouler laid over it. The smell alone nearly did it. Old leather, metal gall, dust warmed by lamp glass. Home turned on its side.

"You see why I thought the word necromancy was too blunt," Jandice said.

Jaina did not look up. "I see disciplined work."

Silence from Jandice then, taut and bright.

"Disciplined," she repeated.

"You wanted a better word?"

"I wanted an honest one."

"Honest is harder to offer without a fuller sample."

The archivist glanced up again at that, faintly startled, then lowered his eyes so quickly it became its own confession. Nobody spoke to Jandice that way often in this room. Good.

Jandice rested her fingertips on the table edge. "Most visitors stop at disgust."

"Most visitors cannot read it."

That did it. Pleasure showed openly now, quick and naked enough to make her younger.

"No," Jandice said. "They cannot."

Jaina turned another page and found a column of marginal symbols Antonidas had made her memorize at sixteen because Kel'Thuzad had once insisted the older system wasted less time than the fashionable one. Antonidas had taught it with a grim mouth and no story attached. No need. The story had been sitting in the chalk all along.

The lower floors were all through the ledger even when they were not named. References cut short. Samples transferred inward. Pressure readings taken from beneath the archive foundation. One title repeated often enough to matter: master, used the way other institutions wrote chair or rector, ordinary rank over extraordinary work.

Jaina closed the book.

"You keep saying we," she said.

"We do the work here."

"How far down does your we travel?"

Jandice's composure tightened by a degree. Enough to show the wall.

"Far enough."

"Convenient answer."

"A practical one." Jandice tilted her head. "You came in breathing accusation and are now asking real questions. That strikes me as progress."

"I came in following plague grain through noble storehouses."

"And found an academy."

"I found a building using Kirin Tor bones to grow a second body around them."

For the first time Jandice went still in a way that was not cultivated.

Then she laughed softly.

"That," she said, "is much closer."

She took the ledger back and slid it under her hand with proprietary ease. Jaina noticed the small things then. A darkening bruise-shadow at the base of Jandice's fingers, the sort certain reagents left after years. Fine pale scars at the heel of the palm from chalk cuts and knife slips. Workman's damage on a noblewoman's skin. Real enough to trust. Dangerous enough to follow.

"Come," Jandice said. "You've seen the shell. You should at least see how honestly the shell is built."

The next rooms sharpened the method instead of expanding the horror. A preparation chamber where bodies on tables wore linen over the face and numbered tags at the wrist, cleaner than any battlefield dead Jaina had ever seen. A cataloguing room where two copyists worked through observation reports with the relentless dullness of men doing necessary clerical labor. A sealed laboratory where stripped joints and sinew samples sat arranged by stage, every tray numbered, every instrument cleaned and returned precisely to its place.

No chaos. No theatrical depravity. Only intellect with all the softness planed out of it.

At each threshold Jaina felt the density below increase. Not the pleasant layered hum of Dalaran's vaults. Pressure with teeth in it. It thickened under the feet, rose through the calves, touched the roots of her teeth again. The visible route sat over a deeper machine. The building admitted it by weight even while the tour refused it by direction.

Jandice saw her notice the strain in the stone.

"You've been reading down all afternoon," she said.

"You've been expecting me to."

"I was hoping you would."

"Because you want me impressed."

"Because I prefer being understood."

That answer came too quickly to sand smooth.

Jaina stopped beside a narrow window that looked across an inner court to another tower. Two robed researchers crossed below carrying a locked box between them. The box's ward seals were workmanlike and strong. Neither of them looked up. Above, on the opposite wall, a narrow gallery door stood shut under three separate sigils all feeding the same line of power downward.

Omitted route. Private access. Higher clearance than Jandice intended to admit.

"Who teaches beneath us?" Jaina asked.

"Beneath us?" Jandice repeated. "How dramatic."

"The wards are louder there."

"Only to you, perhaps."

"No." Jaina touched the stone beside the window. Cold. Dry. Alive with the drag of contained force. "To the masonry too."

Jandice's mouth changed again. Irritation, then restraint.

"There are private rooms," she said. "Every serious institution has them."

"Private scholarship. Private storage. Private authority. You don't have access to all of it."

"Do you?"

"No."

"Then we are evenly placed for once."

Jaina almost smiled at that. Jandice saw it and brightened visibly, which told Jaina more than the sentence had. Recognition hunger, yes. Not social approval. Not charm. She wanted the click of one mind meeting another and refusing to look away.

That made her easier to read. It did not make her safe.

They returned her, at last, to a guest chamber in an upper residential wing where the windows looked over Darrowmere's black water and made the outside seem farther away than distance warranted. The room was comfortable to the point of insult. Thick rug. Carved chair. Writing desk already supplied with paper and ink. A wash basin steaming gently from an embedded warming charm.

Jandice stopped at the threshold instead of entering fully.

"You should write down whatever version of this you intend to send home," she said. "People always trust paper more than memory."

"Do they?"

"People who like committees do."

There was the Dalaran wound under her own.

"Will I see the library again?" Jaina asked.

"If you ask interestingly." Jandice's gaze dropped to the ledger still in Jaina's hand, and only then did Jaina realize she had carried it out of the archive without thinking. Jandice laughed under her breath. "Keep it for now. I would rather not pretend you didn't mean to."

"You trust me with it?"

"I trust your appetite." The answer warmed with pride before cooling again. "Do not mistake the distinction."

She left.

The door closed softly. No lock slid. No guard planted himself outside with audible boots.

Which meant the room was almost certainly listening.

Jaina stood motionless for a count of ten and let the chamber declare itself. Window. Desk. Ward-thread in the lintel. Another in the lamp base. A finer web hidden under the plaster around the doorframe, passive, delicate, tuned to vibration and voice. A receiver disguised as courtesy.

She sat at the desk anyway, opened the ledger, and read until the light outside thinned.

The same answer kept returning in different language. The body was not passive matter waiting for command from outside. The body learned. Held. Repeated. The outer preparation survived grain, travel, baking, gut passage. After that the work moved inward. The gap remained the same gap she had carried since the farmhouse. What turned conditioned tissue into obedience after death? What woke it as more than spoilage?

By the time the sky over the lake went iron-grey she had three pages of notes, a tighter ache behind the eyes, and one word penciled twice in the margin where it did not yet deserve certainty.

Resonance.

She sat back, rubbed the heel of her hand over her mouth, and looked at the dark water beyond the glass.

Antonidas would tell her to leave with what she had.

He would be right in every way that did not solve the problem fast enough.

The sending stone in her satchel felt heavier for being thought about. She looked at the door, at the web hidden in the plaster, and almost left the stone where it was.

Almost.

She set a muting line round the desk with the smallest, plainest ward she knew, one designed for privacy between scholars in crowded archive rooms, not for this depth of interference. It would not defeat the chamber. It might blur the edges enough to cost an eavesdropper certainty.

Then she laid the stone in her palm and spoke Antonidas's name.

The reply took too long. When it came, the light was weak and blue and crowded with watery distortion.

"Jaina?"

Just that, and the room in Dalaran came through behind it in broken fragments. Ward hum. Somebody speaking too far away to parse. A door. The whole of home reduced to a damaged echo.

"I'm alive," she said at once.

The answering silence carried relief and anger braided so tightly she could not separate them.

"Where are you?"

"Inside the Barov line," she said. Not the whole truth. The chamber had not earned the whole truth and neither had distance. "The route evidence held. There is institutional work here, layered and deliberate. The scale is worse than Andorhal."

"Can you leave now?"

Straight to it. Good.

"With material, yes."

"Then leave with material."

The stone crackled. She could hear the strain on the line now, the heavy pressure between them. Distance did part of it. Wards did more. The building disliked being spoken through.

"The archive uses our notation," she said. "Not imitation. Continuation. Whoever built this place built it out of Kirin Tor method."

Antonidas did not answer for a heartbeat too long.

"I know what that implies," he said.

No qualifiers left. None of the old academic cushioning when the truth already had knives in it.

"The body-model from the outpost was right," Jaina said. "The preparation teaches living tissue to hold the pattern. The grain is the outer carrier only. I still do not have the waking step."

"Then you do not have enough to stay."

She looked at her notes, at the penciled word in the margin.

"I have enough to know the answer is lower."

"Jaina."

The stone dimmed and flared again. Waterlogged distortion roughened his voice until age showed through it harder than usual.

"Record everything you can carry," Antonidas said. "Do not trust memory to be kinder tomorrow than it is tonight. If the building is what you think it is, it has already told its master you are there."

The private room is listening, she thought, and did not say it because saying it would change nothing except his fear.

"The Council?" she asked.

"The Council still prefers proof that can survive a clean table and six chairs."

There it was. Institutional failure in its proper dress.

"Of course they do."

"Jaina."

The second use of her name came differently. Less warning. More plea. Dangerous ground.

"I am documenting everything," she said, because that part was true. "I'll leave safely."

Also dangerous. Also not wholly true.

He heard enough in it to dislike it. Not enough to stop her from a continent away.

"Send what you have the moment the line clears," he said. "And if it does not clear, keep breathing until it does."

The phrasing was so unlike the careful man at the lecture dais that her throat tightened without permission.

"I know."

The blue light shook once. Faded. Returned for half a pulse.

"I should have moved faster," Antonidas said.

Then the connection tore.

The room went back to itself. Dark lake. Warm walls. Thin hidden wards in the plaster. Jaina sat without moving until the muting line under the desk guttered out on its own.

He cannot move at plague speed, she thought.

Neither can the Council. Neither can any body built to survive itself by delay.

The knock on the door came twenty minutes later, light and precise.

"Come in," Jaina said.

Jandice entered carrying a second lamp and no mask worth naming.

"You stayed with the notes," she said, pleased.

"You left me the notes."

"A calculated extravagance." She set the lamp down and crossed to the desk, where her gaze fell at once to the penciled word in Jaina's margin. Her eyes lifted, bright as struck metal. "Did you write that?"

"Twice."

"Good."

The single word sat between them with the latch already lifted.

"Then let's stop pretending you're only here to accuse me," Jandice said quietly.

Jaina leaned back in the chair and let the fatigue sharpen her instead of softening her.

"Fine. The host-body model is elegant. The retention layer through storage and baking is better than anything I expected to find. The missing step remains the same problem it was this morning. Conditioned tissue does not merely retain contamination. It learns toward an answer. I can see that much now. I still cannot see what calls the answer back after death."

Jandice had gone very still. Scholar's stillness. The body gone quiet because the mind had found its exact speed.

"Yes," she said.

"So either the waking trigger sits in a separate external command lattice, which is crude and would not match the rest of the work, or the body is being taught to amplify a pattern it already half contains."

Jandice's hands came up before she caught them.

"Exactly."

The word broke out of her with more naked satisfaction than any smile earlier had managed.

"You're treating death as a threshold of response," Jaina said. "Not as the insertion point."

"Closer."

Jandice stepped to the desk, bent over the notes, and forgot to perform for three glorious dangerous seconds.

"Force from outside is wasteful," she said. "Everybody in Dalaran still thinks in that old brutal direction. Push enough power in, dead matter jerks, congratulations, you've made a puppet. It's childish. The interesting question is what the tissue has already been taught to keep. What it will repeat once enough of the living noise falls away."

Her fingers moved over the page, sketching the shape in the air faster than speech quite held it.

"A struck string answers the note it knows. A room answers a sung pitch if the walls are built for it. Why would flesh be less teachable than wood or plaster if you have months instead of minutes?"

Resonance. There it was earning the word at last without finishing the theorem.

"Patterned amplification," Jaina said.

"Yes." Jandice smiled then, full and sudden and far too young for the room. "Yes, exactly."

"And the body keeps enough of the taught line that death strips resistance instead of stripping the lesson."

"You see it."

Recognition. She wanted it badly enough for the need to show plain in her mouth and eyes.

Jaina should have distrusted that more than she did.

"I see the shape," she said. "I do not yet see the whole answer."

"Neither did Dalaran," Jandice said, and contempt flashed across the old institutional wound. "Dalaran saw the gate and declared the field forbidden so it would never have to learn what lay beyond it."

"Or Dalaran saw what the field did to the people inside it."

That hit. Jandice's face closed, though not all the way.

"Moral disgust is a poor substitute for close reading."

"So is elegance."

For a heartbeat the room sharpened to a knife edge between them. Then, instead of drawing back, Jandice laughed under her breath.

"Good," she said. "I'd wondered."

"About what?"

"Whether you were actually capable of being interesting."

Jaina surprised herself by laughing once. Tiredness had made the sound rough. Jandice liked that too.

"The full model is lower," Jaina said.

"Yes."

"Lower than your access."

"Not entirely."

"Not entirely isn't a useful phrase."

"It's the honest one."

Jandice straightened, folded her hands into her sleeves, unfolded them again. A leak in discipline. Small. Human.

"The master knows you're here," she said.

"I assumed that when the lake announced me."

"He was always going to know. The question was whether he would permit you farther than the curated route."

"And?"

Jandice held her gaze. For the first time all day uncertainty touched the edge of her poise. Hope, maybe. Hope that the answer she wanted from below would match the answer she wanted from Jaina's face.

"I asked," she said. "On the grounds that it would be intellectually wasteful to send you away now."

"That is a memorable defense."

"I thought so." A pause. "He agreed."

There should have been triumph in that. Jaina got none from it. Only the clean hard drop of initiative leaving her hands entirely.

"Why?"

"Because he is curious," Jandice said. "And because curiosity does not always oppose confidence."

That answer chilled her more effectively than threat could have.

"Then let's not keep his curiosity waiting," Jaina said.

Jandice's eyes widened a fraction at the lack of hesitation. Then she smiled again, this time slower.

"No," she said. "Let's not."

The route down began behind a door disguised as paneling at the end of a service hall Jaina had not been shown earlier. Of course it did. Jandice opened it with a small brass key and a spoken syllable that made the hidden ward in the frame fold aside.

Cold dry air moved up from below carrying stone dust, preserving salts, and the faint organic sweetness of long-kept decay.

The stair was older than the upper keep. That showed at once. The walls narrowed. The treads had been worn smooth by centuries of feet before newer traffic laid its own path over the old wear. Lamps had been fixed at careful intervals down the curve, and between their pools of light pale fungi clung to the mortar in thin crescent shelves. As Jaina stepped onto the first turning, the nearest growth gave off a dim blue-green bloom in answer to the magic on her skin.

Jandice glanced back at it with proprietary fondness.

"They like practitioners," she said.

"Reassuring."

"You prefer your wonders hostile?"

"I prefer them comprehensible."

"That may be the same thing by the time we're done."

Below them the ward pressure thickened until it pressed like weather against the face. Jaina kept one hand on the ledger without realizing she had done so until her fingers cramped around the leather spine. She had carried it out of the guest room and down the hidden stair on instinct, the way a soldier carried the map that might keep her alive two turns from now.

She did not put it down.

The first landing opened on a short corridor lined with old burial niches, most sealed, some repurposed behind new iron grilles for storage. The second held a pair of shut doors marked in the same clean hand she had seen in the archive, only more abbreviated here, more certain, as though the writer expected everyone in this level to keep up without help. Somewhere deeper metal rang softly against metal. No screams. No chanting. Work.

Jandice kept descending.

"You understand," she said without turning, "that permission is not the same as protection."

"I had not mistaken them."

"Good."

They reached another turn. The stair bent out of sight again, down toward the pressure at the heart of the place. Behind them, somewhere above, the panel door eased shut. No slam. No lock grinding into place. Just wood meeting stone with the finality of a decision already made elsewhere.

Open route, Jaina thought. Trap all the same.

She tightened her grip on the ledger and followed Jandice lower.
