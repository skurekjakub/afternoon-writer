# Chapter 10: The Estate

Trimmed ditches and fresh labor kept Eastweald too neat for famine country.

Jaina rode through ruts pressed deep into the old road, her Thalassian mare sweating in the cold while orchard walls and trimmed ditches showed fresh labor and the money to keep this land neat. Frost silvered the low grass. Cart tracks crossed the road with the patient logic of harvest work done at scale. Barov grain fed half a province, and the crews here meant to keep it moving.

The wrongness reached her through the reins first, a live tingle under the skin, then thickened as the road bent southeast. Aboveground the estate showed grain, stone walls, labor. Under it lay wards in layers, old work built over newer work, and a denser knot of necromantic pressure beyond the house itself, past the orchards and the estate road, out toward the lake.

Sylvanas would have asked how long everything would take. How long to the gate. How long back out if the servants smiled and the gates closed behind her. Sylvanas would have priced all of it before the estate came into view. Cyndia would have checked the treeline and the service road together. Lyriel would have held the right flank if the world had not chosen otherwise.

Jaina had a horse, copied route scraps, Cyndia's approach notes inside her sleeve, and enough Kirin Tor authority to make a noble family hesitate before lying. She had come alone, with no escort, no bow lane, and no patience left for pretending she could go over a wall like a ranger.

So she rode straight at the front gate.

The Barov crest hung in iron above it, dark against pale stone. Two men in estate colors had been speaking with a wagon driver just inside the arch. They stopped when they saw her. One glance went to her staff, then the horse, then the quality of her robes, and calculation tightened his mouth before he smoothed it away.

Good. Let the gate guards react first.

The nearer man stepped into the road and bowed just enough to be respectful without pretending humility.

"My lady?"

"Jaina Proudmoore," she said. "Protégée of Archmage Antonidas. I require an audience with Lord Barov."

He blinked once at the name, once at the tone. "Is Lord Barov expecting—"

"No," Jaina said. "If he had been, we could both have spared ourselves that sentence."

The driver's eyes dropped at once. The guard's did not. He held his place, which told her something useful. Servants here were trained to take pressure without folding.

"The estate is occupied with harvest concerns," he said.

"Then he will understand why a Kirin Tor mage has come to ask about his grain."

His eyes changed; the door he had hoped to keep shut had just opened anyway.

He stood aside. "Of course, my lady."

The mare tossed her head under the gate, road-bred and steady even with ward hum under her hooves. Inside the walls the wind fell off. Stone held the air close. Men crossed the yard with baskets, ledgers, harness, sacks. They were busy, fed, and sure the estate ran the way it was meant to.

Jaina dismounted before the main house and handed the reins to a stable boy whose sleeves were patched twice at the wrists and clean besides. Money showed here in mended cloth, straight ledgers, and hands kept busy.

The front doors opened before she reached them.

Warmth met her without smoke.

The great hall beyond was too still for a room this warm. A hearth big enough to roast an ox stood dark and empty, yet the air stayed mild against her face. Enchanted stones warmed the walls where a fire should have been.

Lord Alexei Barov rose from a chair near a low table where tea waited for two.

He was silver-haired, narrow through the shoulders, dressed in dark wool that sat too well to be local tailoring. No ink stained his nails, and the thumb on the chair arm bore no callus.

"Lady Proudmoore," he said in a tone polished for expected guests. "House Barov is honored."

"I doubt that."

His smile held, but the corners of his mouth tightened. "Honored, then, by your frankness."

Jaina remained standing. "Your grain routes have become very interesting. Interesting enough to bring me here."

"A regrettable subject." He gestured toward the tea. "Please."

"No."

"As you wish." He folded one hand over the other. "I assume this concerns Andorhal. We have suffered losses there ourselves. Rumors of contamination travel faster than wagons. Panic travels faster still. Estates our size attract blame because scale attracts blame."

"I came to ask about more than rumor," she said.

"Then ask."

"How long has scholarly work been conducted at Caer Darrow under your protection?"

A flicker crossed his face before the composure settled back into place.

"You are direct," Alexei said.

"You keep saying things I already know."

The smile thinned. "Caer Darrow houses a private academy. Rural patronage of scholarship is not a crime, though I admit the larger cities often behave that way."

"What field?"

"Comparative magical theory, among others."

"Necromancy."

He held still, which already put him a step ahead of the guard outside.

"A vulgar word," he said mildly. "The sort used by people who believe coarse naming is the same as understanding."

"Then educate me," she said.

"Gladly," a woman's voice said from the doorway.

Jaina turned.

Jandice Barov wore burgundy robes cut on Dalaran lines and made from Barov cloth. The seam work was right, so was the weight of the fabric, and the sleeves would not catch at the wrist while working. Her dark hair was pulled back severely. Ink marked one thumb. Pale reagent scars crossed the skin between the fingers of her right hand. Those scars came from ink, glass, and live reagents.

Recognition hit fast enough to feel rude. Jandice saw it happen. Her mouth twitched, then stilled.

"Lady Proudmoore," she said. "I am Jandice Barov. My father was about to describe our embarrassment with less precision than it deserves. May I rescue him?"

Alexei leaned back at once.

"Please do," he said.

Jandice crossed the room with measured ease. Ink. Chalk ground flat at the thumb. Small burns from handling reagents in glass, the kind earned at a bench instead of in a lecture hall.

"We have students," Jandice said. "Researchers. Archive staff. Some traditionalists think forbidding a thing is discipline. That burden is theirs."

"And the grain?" Jaina asked.

"The grain," Alexei said, reclaiming one lane with practiced smoothness, "is my burden. Theft, spoilage, panic pricing, opportunists along the road. Put the same noble name in enough frightened mouths and it starts to sound monstrous."

"You expected scrutiny," Jaina said.

"I expected famine to make everyone ugly."

"You seem very prepared for ugliness."

Jandice's eyes sharpened. Alexei's hands stayed still. This was scrutiny backed by rank.

"We can spend the morning fencing politely," Jandice said, "or I can show you the work respectable people keep misnaming."

Jaina looked at her. Young. About her own age. The kind of woman she would have argued with for hours in a Dalaran archive until the lamps burned low.

"You would permit that?" Jaina asked.

"Permit?" Jandice checked her father for objection and found none. "Lady Proudmoore, if the Kirin Tor's brightest apprentice has come all this way to inspect our provincial little school, I would rather have the inspection where I can watch it."

Alexei touched his daughter with one weary look. "Jandice. Mind the presentable route."

"Father." Her answer carried just enough amusement to say this had happened before.

Relief eased his shoulders the moment Jandice took over.

She sat at last, took the offered cup, and drank because refusing a second time would waste energy she needed elsewhere. It was excellent, which figured.

"You keep a lake crossing for your academy," she said.

"A fortress does better on an island," Jandice said. "Even a scholarly one."

"Scholars expecting inspection usually keep records closer to the road."

"Scholars expecting theft keep them behind water."

Jaina set the cup down. "Then let's go look at them."

The road to the landing cut through ordered ground, then gave way at the last turn to a lake dark under a skin of mist.

"You came alone," Jandice said.
"Yes."
"Bold."
"Efficient."

The landing held a long narrow boat tied to iron rings sunk into old stone. Jandice bent, untied the rope, and stepped in with the ease of repetition.

"You row yourself?" Jaina asked.

"When I want to arrive."

The lake carried a chemical cold, mineral-heavy and stale as water kept too long in deep stone cisterns. The outer ward touched the back of Jaina's teeth before the boat reached it, light but deliberate. Enough to mark her. Enough to leave the ward's taste there.

She stepped into the boat anyway.

Jandice pushed them off. The oars entered the water with no sound. Mist dragged low over the surface, swallowing the shore behind them faster than it should have. Jaina looked back once and saw the estate already dimmed to pale roofline and wall.

"Beautiful, if one has the taste for it," Jandice said.

"Do you?"

"For beautiful things? Constantly. For badly observed ones, never."

"Your father wasn't surprised enough by me," she said.

"My father believes most crises can be endured if the room is expensive enough."

"And you?"

Jandice's mouth shifted. "I believe most crises improve if the thinking inside them does."

The ward line took them with a brief green shimmer under the hull. The pressure shifted, marked her, and passed the message inward through the lattice.

Caer Darrow rose out of the mist a piece at a time—the seawall first, then towers, then the keep itself, old stone converted rather than rebuilt, every line of the fortress still meant for defense even after scholarship had been folded into it. Narrow windows above. Broad doors below.

A single porter in dark clothes waited with a lantern hooded against daylight. Someone had cleared the landing before she arrived.

Jaina stepped onto the island and the deep pull of the place settled fully into her bones.

The strongest signature came from under it.

The porter bowed them in through a side entry broad enough for carts and too clean for a ruin. The stone floors had been scrubbed. Iron brackets caught the light. The first corridor ran straight and dry, lit by lamps behind fitted glass. The air smelled of limewash, ink, lamp oil, and preserving compounds worked into the mortar over years.

Jandice watched for a reaction and did not get the one she wanted.

"Disappointed?" she asked.

"No."

"People usually are. They expect screaming crypts. We have lecture schedules."

Two students in dark robes moved ahead of them carrying a stack of slates between them. They glanced once at Jaina's face, once at the staff, and stepped aside with the brisk irritation of people delayed on the way to actual work.

Jaina walked beside Jandice through a hall where anatomical sketches hung between alcoves of labeled jars. Every label was neat. The shelves had been dusted. A preserved human hand lay on one table as casually as a pointer.

"You teach openly," Jaina said.
"Within the building? Of course."
"I meant the work itself."
"So did I."

They entered a lecture room with tiered benches and a board still marked from the previous class. Someone had drawn the stages of tissue breakdown in a fine decisive hand, then crossed part of the sequence out and replaced it with a better one. The notation in the margin hit Jaina squarely in the chest: Antonidas's seminar marks, his school carried forward in another hand.

She moved closer before she could stop herself.

"You know the system," Jandice said quietly.

"Yes."

"I thought you might."

The corrections were recent. Chalk dust still clung to the ledge. Jaina read three lines and had to make herself keep breathing evenly. Whoever had written them had taken Kirin Tor rigor into the place the Council had nailed shut and kept working there with the stolen tools.

"The provincial caricature disappoints people," she said. "They expect enthusiasm without discipline. We are spared that weakness."

"You were trained in Dalaran?"

"Briefly adjacent to it." Irritation flashed. "Enough to know what it refuses. Come. The archive is more interesting than our classroom scraps."

They crossed two more corridors and a stair, the route turning just enough each time to remind Jaina she was being led one way and not shown the whole of the place. Doors stood open on tidy workrooms where assistants copied notes, sorted reagents, checked labeled vials against ledgers. They worked in plain sight. Open benches, spread ledgers, assistants bent over their copying.

Dry dark paneling boxed the archive around three lamp-weighted tables. Cabinets stood along one wall, locked cases along the other. A man with thin spectacles looked up when they entered, saw Jandice, and looked back down without question.

Jandice moved to one cabinet, opened it with a key from inside her sleeve, and drew out a leather-bound ledger. She set it before Jaina with care close to reverence.

"Recent circulation from the lower research rooms," she said. "Restricted, but then so are many things worth reading."

The leather was cracked at the hinge and warm from someone's hand.

Jaina opened it.

The first page might as well have been her own under a bad light: the same notational spine, the same crowded lower margin, the same clipped symbols for failed branches.

Kel'Thuzad's work, or a student shaped by him so completely the forms had merged.

What sat in the ledger ran straight at the hole in the farmhouse read—host response, retention through storage, patterned answer in conditioned tissue. The final waking step was still missing. Even so, the line of thought pointed at the same missing step she had seen in the farmhouse.

The smell nearly did it by itself: old leather, metal gall, dust warmed by lamp glass, ink and reagent, Dalaran with something fouler laid over it.

"You see why I thought the word necromancy was too blunt," Jandice said.

Jaina kept her eyes on the ledger. "I see disciplined work."

"Disciplined," she repeated.

"You wanted a better word?"

"I wanted an honest one."

"Honest is harder to offer without a fuller sample."

The archivist glanced up again, startled, then dropped his eyes.

Jandice rested her fingertips on the table edge. "Most visitors stop at disgust."

"Most visitors cannot read it."

That did it. Pleasure showed openly now, quick and naked enough to make her younger.

"No," Jandice said. "They cannot."

Jaina turned another page and found a column of marginal symbols Antonidas had made her memorize at sixteen because Kel'Thuzad had once insisted the older system wasted less time than the fashionable one. Antonidas had taught it with a grim mouth. She should have recognized the lineage sooner.

Repeated references to inward transfers and pressure readings pointed below the archive. One title repeated often enough to matter: master.

Jaina closed the book.

"You keep saying we," she said.

"We do the work here."

"How far down does your we travel?"

Jandice's mouth flattened, and the warmth went out of her eyes.

"Far enough."

"Convenient answer."

"A practical one." Jandice tilted her head. "You came in breathing accusation and are now asking real questions. That strikes me as progress."

"I came in following plague grain through noble storehouses."

"And found an academy."

"I found Kirin Tor method under all of this, holding up something uglier."

For the first time, Jandice went still and did not recover it at once.

Then she laughed softly. "That is much closer."

She took the ledger back and slid it under her hand with proprietary ease. Bruise-darkness at the base of her fingers. Pale scars at the heel of the palm from chalk cuts and knife slips. Bruise-darkness and chalk cuts marked them as a working mage's hands.

"Come," Jandice said. "You've seen the shell. You should at least see how honestly the shell is built."

Bodies lay on numbered tables under linen in the preparation chamber. In the cataloguing room beyond, two copyists bent over reports. Past them, stripped joints and sinew samples sat arranged by stage, every tray numbered, every instrument cleaned and returned to its place.

Clean instruments and straightened tags were worse than rot would have been.

At each threshold the density below increased until the pressure grew teeth. The visible route sat over a deeper machine. Cracks in the masonry and the tightening wards gave her more than Jandice's route did. Jandice kept turning her away from the pressure below.

Jandice saw her notice the strain in the stone. "You've been reading down all afternoon."
"You've been expecting me to."
"I was hoping you would."
"Because you want me impressed."
"Because I prefer being understood."

Jaina stopped beside a narrow window that looked across an inner court to another tower. Two robed researchers crossed below carrying a locked box between them. The ward seals on it were workmanlike and strong. Neither of them looked up. Above, on the opposite wall, a gallery door stood shut under three separate sigils all feeding the same line of power downward. A private route, then, and clearance above what Jandice had admitted.

"Who teaches beneath us?" Jaina asked.

"Beneath us?" Jandice repeated. "How dramatic."

"The wards are louder there."

"Only to you."

"No." Jaina touched the stone beside the window. Cold. Dry. Alive with the drag of contained force. "To the masonry too."

Jandice's jaw tightened, then eased.

"There are private rooms," she said. "Every serious institution has them."

"Private scholarship. Private storage. Private authority. You don't have access to all of it."

"Do you?"

"No."

"Then we are evenly placed for once."

The corner of Jaina's mouth twitched. Jandice saw it and brightened at once, hungry for recognition.

They returned her to an upper guest chamber overlooking Darrowmere's black water. Thick rug. Carved chair. Writing desk already supplied with paper and ink.

"You should write down whatever version of this you intend to send home," she said. "People always trust paper more than memory."

"Do they?"

"People who like committees do."

"Will I see the library again?" Jaina asked.

"If you ask interestingly." Jandice's gaze dropped to the ledger still in Jaina's hand, and only then did Jaina realize she had carried it out of the archive without thinking. Jandice laughed under her breath. "Keep it for now. I would rather not pretend you didn't mean to."

"You trust me with it?"

"I trust your appetite. Do not mistake the distinction."

She left.

The door closed softly. Wards in the room listened for voices all the same.

Jaina stood still for a count of ten and read what the wards were doing. Window. Desk. Ward-thread in the lintel. Another in the lamp base. A finer web hidden under the plaster around the doorframe, tuned to vibration and voice.

She sat at the desk anyway, opened the ledger, and read until the light outside thinned.

The same answer kept turning up. Repeated entries showed tissue conditioned until it held the pattern and answered to it again. The outer preparation survived grain, travel, baking, gut passage. After that the work moved inward. What turned conditioned tissue into obedience after death?

By the time the sky over the lake went iron-grey she had three pages of notes, an ache behind the eyes, and one word penciled twice in the margin where it did not yet deserve certainty.

Resonance.

She sat back and looked at the dark water beyond the glass. Antonidas would tell her to leave with what she had. He would be right, and still too slow for the problem in front of her.

The sending stone in her satchel grew heavier the moment she considered it. She looked at the door, at the hidden web in the plaster, and weighed leaving the stone where it was.

She set a muting line round the desk with the smallest ward she knew, one meant for privacy between scholars in crowded archive rooms, not for this depth of interference. It would not defeat the chamber. It might blur the edges enough to cost an eavesdropper certainty.

Then she laid the stone in her palm and spoke Antonidas's name.

The reply took too long. When it came, the light was weak and blue and crowded with watery distortion.

"Jaina?"

Just that, and the room in Dalaran came through behind it in broken fragments. Ward hum. Somebody speaking too far away to make out. A door.

"I'm alive," she said at once.

Antonidas said nothing for a beat.

"Where are you?"

"Inside the Barov line," she said. "The route evidence held. There is real work here, layered and deliberate. The scale is worse than Andorhal."

"Can you leave now?"

"With material, yes."

"Then leave with material."

The stone crackled. Distance did part of it. Wards did more. Distance and ward pressure roughened the signal all the way back to Dalaran.

"The archive uses our notation," she said. "The work continues ours. Whoever built this place built it out of Kirin Tor method."

"I know what that implies," he said.

"The model from the outpost was right," Jaina said. "The preparation teaches living tissue to hold the pattern. The grain is only the outer carrier. I still do not have the waking step."

"Then you do not have enough to stay."

She looked at her notes, at the penciled word in the margin. "I have enough to know the answer is lower."

"Jaina."

The stone dimmed and flared again. Distortion roughened his voice.

"Record everything you can carry," Antonidas said. "Do not trust memory to be kinder tomorrow than it is tonight. If the place is what you think it is, its wards have already told the master you are there."

The wards in the room were listening, she thought, and did not say it.

"The Council?" she asked.

"The Council still prefers proof that can survive a clean table and six chairs."

"Of course they do."

"Jaina."

"I am documenting everything," she said. "I'll leave safely."

"Send what you have the moment the line clears," he said. "And if it does not clear, keep breathing until it does."

"I know."

The blue light shook once. Faded. Returned for half a pulse.

"I should have moved faster," Antonidas said.

Then the connection tore.

The room went back to dark lake beyond the glass, warm walls, and hidden wards in the plaster. Jaina sat without moving until the muting line under the desk guttered out on its own.

He cannot move at plague speed, she thought. The Council will be worse.

The knock on the door came twenty minutes later, light and precise.

"Come in," Jaina said.

Jandice entered carrying a second lamp and no mask.

"You stayed with the notes," she said. Her voice quickened on notes.

"You left me the notes."

"A calculated extravagance." She set the lamp down and crossed to the desk, where her gaze found the penciled word in Jaina's margin at once. Her eyes lifted, bright as struck metal. "Did you write that?"

"Twice."

"Good."

"Then stop pretending you're only here to accuse me," Jandice said quietly.

"I'm not pretending anything. The model is elegant. The retention layer through storage and baking is better than anything I expected to find. The missing step remains what it was this morning. Conditioned tissue holds contamination and keeps the pattern alive inside it. I still cannot see what calls it back after death."

"Yes," she said.

"So either the waking trigger sits in a separate command lattice, which is crude and would not match the rest of the work, or your method conditions the body to amplify a pattern it already half contains."

Jandice's hands came up before she caught them. "Exactly."

"You're treating death as a threshold of response," Jaina said. "As the place where it answers."

"Closer."

Jandice bent over the notes so fast the smile left her face for a few seconds.

"Force from outside is wasteful," she said. "Everybody in Dalaran still thinks in that old brutal direction. Push enough power in, dead matter jerks, congratulations, you've made a puppet. It's childish. The interesting question is what the tissue keeps once you've taught it long enough. What it repeats once enough living noise falls away."

"A struck string answers the note it knows. A room answers a sung pitch if the walls are built for it. Why would flesh be less teachable than wood or plaster if you have months instead of minutes?"

Resonance. There it was.

"Patterned amplification," Jaina said.

"Yes." Jandice's mouth pulled into a quick fierce smile. "Yes, exactly."

"And the body keeps enough of the taught line that death strips resistance instead of the lesson."

"You see it."

The words landed. Jandice went still for half a breath, then looked away too late.

"I see the shape," she said. "I do not yet see the whole answer."

"Neither did Dalaran," Jandice said, and contempt flashed across the old institutional wound. "The men who sit on the Council saw the gate and forbade the field so they would never have to learn what lay beyond it."

"Or Dalaran saw what the field did to the people inside it."

Jandice went still and set her mouth hard.

"Moral disgust is a poor substitute for looking closely."

"So is elegance."

For a heartbeat, neither of them moved. Then Jandice laughed under her breath.

"Good," she said. "I'd wondered whether you were actually capable of being interesting."

Jaina surprised herself by laughing once. Tiredness had made the sound rough.

"The full model is lower," Jaina said.

"Yes."

"Lower than your access."

"Partly below it."

"Partly won't do."

"It's honest."

Jandice straightened, folded her hands into her sleeves, unfolded them again.

"The master knows you're here," she said.

"I assumed that when the outer ward marked me."

"He was always going to know. The question was whether he would permit you farther than the curated route."

"And?"

Jandice held her gaze long enough for the polish to slip.

"I asked," she said. "Sending you away now would be a waste."

"That is a memorable defense."

"I thought so." A pause. "He agreed."

Her stomach tightened instead. The master below had already decided.

"Why?"

"Because he is curious," Jandice said. "And curiosity does not always oppose confidence."

"Then let's not keep his curiosity waiting," Jaina said.

Jandice blinked once at the speed of her answer, then the pleased look returned. "No," she said. "Let's not."

The way down opened behind paneling at the end of a service hall Jaina had not seen earlier. That much she had expected. Jandice touched a brass key to the seam and spoke a syllable. The ward in the frame folded aside.

Cold dry air moved up from below carrying stone dust, preserving salts, and the faint sweetness of long-kept decay.

The stair was older than the upper keep. Its walls narrowed, and the treads had been worn smooth by centuries of feet before newer traffic laid its own path over the old wear. Lamps had been fixed at careful intervals down the curve, and between their pools of light pale fungi clung to the mortar in thin crescent shelves. When Jaina stepped onto the first turning, the nearest growth gave off a dim blue-green bloom in answer to the magic on her skin.

Jandice glanced back at it with proprietary fondness. "They like practitioners."

"Reassuring."

"You prefer your wonders hostile?"

"I prefer them comprehensible."

"That may be the same thing by the time we're done."

Below them the ward pressure thickened until it pressed like weather against the face. Jaina kept one hand on the ledger until her fingers cramped around the leather spine. She had carried it out of the guest room and down the hidden stair on instinct, like a mage refusing to lose the only proof in hand.

She did not put it down.

The first landing opened on a short corridor lined with old burial niches, most sealed, some repurposed behind new iron grilles for storage. The second held a pair of shut doors marked in the same clean hand she had seen in the archive, only more abbreviated here. Somewhere deeper, metal rang softly against metal. Only that sound, and someone putting it to use.

Jandice kept descending.

"You understand," she said without turning, "that permission is not the same as protection."

"I had not mistaken them."

"Good."

They reached another turn. The stair bent out of sight again, down toward the pressure at the heart of the place. Behind them, somewhere above, the panel door eased shut with a soft wood-on-stone click.

The stair stayed open behind her. She tightened her grip on the ledger and followed Jandice lower.
