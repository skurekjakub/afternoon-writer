# Chapter 4: The Sick Village

The road south had spent two days teaching Jaina how easily disaster could hide inside good weather.

They rode through fields already cut for harvest, past sheds with doors flung wide for air, past women shaking linens over fences while children ran under the cloth and came out laughing. Carts creaked. Dogs barked. Smoke rose where it ought to. If she had not known what to listen for, the country might have passed for healthy.

The horses felt it before the people did. Weariness first, then resistance. Too many sudden stops. Jaina's mare had started giving the village road a hard eye half a mile out. By the first cottages she was tossing her head at a sweetness the wind kept smearing under bread smoke and wet earth.

Jaina caught it too.

A child crossed the lane with kindling in both arms. Fever had darkened her face, but she was still on her feet. A man at the pump paused to cough into his sleeve, spat into the mud, then put his shoulder back into the handle. Somewhere a baker was knocking loaves from a board. The thump came through the morning noise clean and hard.

Alive, then. Worse for that.

"There," Jaina said, pointing toward the broadest building off the square. "That ought to be where they're keeping the worst."

Sylvanas did not look where she pointed at once. Her gaze took its sweep first: lanes, roofs, alley mouths, the line of the well, the men watching strangers arrive and pretending not to. Cyndia and Lyriel had already spread a little, not enough to threaten the village, only enough to show they were not careless.

"And why," Sylvanas said, "ought it be there?"

"Because if I had six cots and no proper infirmary," she said instead, "I would put them where air can move."

"Useful. Stay here."

The refusal rose so fast it scraped her throat.

"Absolutely not."

That got Sylvanas's eyes on her. Blue-green, steady, unreadably bright in the mild light.

"You were told to stay here."

"And you were told Millhaven told us nothing we can use if the living die before I can read them."

One corner of Sylvanas's mouth twitched.

"You improve," she said. "Keep it."

Then she turned her horse toward the square.

The militia captain met them halfway. Thick through the chest, beard gone iron at the chin, one hand already raised in warning before he had decided whether the newcomers were help or trouble.

"Hold there," he called. "No need for weapons in the middle of my village."

No one had drawn. The mistake crossed his face the moment he understood that. He had expected swagger, or threat, or human men he could push chest to chest if he had to. What he got was an armored elf who sat her horse as if argument were a lesser species, a woman in travel-stained blue too tired to bother with pride, and two silent rangers already seeing more than he wanted seen.

"Your village is coughing," Sylvanas said. "That makes a need."

"A fever run makes work," the captain shot back. "It doesn't make it your business."

Jaina's answer crowded her teeth. Sylvanas used none of it.

"Millhaven is dead," she said.

The captain's jaw tightened. "I've heard stories."

"Then hear one from someone who walked through it. We found tables laid. Doors open. No living voice left in the place but one girl under earth. The dead had already taken the north road."

The square changed around that. Two women at the pump stopped pretending not to listen. The baker's board went quiet.

He looked at Jaina then, perhaps because the robe and staff told him which one might sound more like the kind of answer he trusted.

"You can prove that?"

"I can prove your people are carrying the same wrongness," Jaina said. "Whether you have time left to be offended by that depends on how long you keep me out of your sickroom."

The captain opened his mouth.

Sylvanas cut across him. "You can argue after she's done."

He looked back at her.

"Captain," Sylvanas said, lowering her voice enough that he had to lean a fraction nearer to hear it. "If this is only fever, I leave and you keep your pride. If it isn't, every minute you spend proving your authority costs you people who still might live."

The man exhaled through his nose.

"Dane Hall," he said at last, jerking his chin toward the long building Jaina had marked. "We moved the worst there yesterday. The medic's inside. If you frighten them, that's on you."

"Everything in this village looks ready to frighten itself," Sylvanas said.

Dry enough that Jaina nearly missed the humor. The captain caught it; the line in his face shifted.

He stepped aside.

Jaina swung down before her mare had fully settled.

The hall had been built for weddings, meetings, winter suppers. It smelled of rush mats, stale beer in old wood, lamp grease, boiled cloth, and under all of it the sweet wrongness on sick breath.

Cots had been jammed into every space that would take them, and still it was not enough. Two children lay on pallets between benches. An old woman had been propped upright against the wall because no frame remained. A man sat with a bucket between his boots, bent over it and shaking, while someone who must have been his wife held the back of his neck and stared at Jaina as if she had brought either salvation or insult.

The medic came at her fast. "If you're here to tell me it's marsh fever, I've already heard it from three farmers and a priest—"

"Good," Jaina said. "Then you have practice being wrong."

He stopped dead.

Red eyes. Grey stubble. Shirt sleeves rolled to the elbow. Hands scrubbed raw. He looked from her face to the staff and decided that anything unusual was better than one more argument with the village.

"What do you need?"

"Quiet, as much as you can manage. And whoever knows which of these people ate what."

He blinked. "Ate what?"

"Do you want the long version?"

"No."

"Then I need the room and your memory."

He gave her both.

Jaina let the crystal in her staff wake properly for the first time that morning. Blue-white ran through it, then steadied into a thinner light she could hold without wasting herself. She moved from cot to cot, not touching at first, letting the spell skim above skin, throat, belly, the hollows under the ribs. The pattern showed itself more quickly in the living than it had in Millhaven's aftermath. Alive, it was busier. Greedier. It was working.

Gut first.

She stopped over the man with the bucket. Breath sweet, skin damp, pulse skipping under the jaw. The light drew amber filaments through his middle, a fouled knot tight under the stomach and spreading outward in finer threads.

Not a wound, then. Something taken in, again and again, until the body had started helping it.

"How long?" she asked.

The wife answered. "Three days on the belly. Fever by last night."

"What has he eaten?"

"What does everyone eat?" Suspicion entered before the words were out. "Broth. Bread. What we can keep down."

Jaina moved on.

Same knot in the gut. Worse in some, lighter in others. A boy with sunken eyes and cracked lips carried it only faintly. An old woman who still had enough spite to curse Jaina's cold hands carried it deeper. A child sleeping too hard under a patched blanket made Jaina stop entirely. The contamination sat in her like a banked coal.

Jaina set the end of her staff to the floor and built the ward carefully.

She had no cure. She could only slow a door from opening.

Blue light sank around the child in a thin shell, visible only in the way the air bent and clarified. The little body twitched once, then loosened. Breathing eased. In the corner the mother made a sound into both hands and held it there.

"That buys time," Jaina said. "Nothing more. Do not spend it by giving her more bread."

The medic stared from the child to Jaina's hands. "You can stop it?"

"I can slow some of them. If you want stopping, tell me where it enters."

"You think it's food."

"Their bellies know something the rest of the village hasn't admitted."

His mouth flattened. "We've had foul wells before. A spoiled cask can run through half a street."

"Show me the households that stayed light."

That took him a moment.

He had not expected the question.

"Marta and her boys," he said slowly. "They were sick, but not like this. The widow in the south lane. Two men from the tannery. One of the shepherd families."

"And what do they have in common?"

"How should I know?"

"Because you've been watching them live through it and I have only just arrived."

He scrubbed a hand over his mouth. Thought. Thought properly, to his credit, instead of taking offense.

"The widow keeps oats," he said. "Hates paying mill prices. Marta's eldest broke a tooth and they've been feeding him broth more than bread. The shepherds don't buy much from the baker. They eat what they keep."

There.

Not proof yet. Shape.

Jaina crossed the hall to the shuttered window and pushed it wider with her wrist. The square lay beyond it in slices: captain, frightened villagers, Cyndia at the edge of the pump, Lyriel near the lane mouth, and Sylvanas speaking to an old man in an apron dusted pale to the elbows. Granary keeper, probably. Or baker.

Three short questions from Sylvanas; five useful answers out of the man's face before his mouth caught up.

Jaina looked back at the room.

Bread baskets. Crumbs. A knife left in a loaf on the trestle.

She crossed to the table and pressed two fingers into the cut loaf. Her spell answered at once, brighter than it had over the weaker patients.

"You idiot," she said softly, not to the medic.

He misheard it anyway. "I was trying to keep them alive."

"I know."

She turned the loaf over. Flour dusted her gloves. Warm room, warm bread, village life still going through its motions while every shared meal laid down another layer.

"How many households grind their own?"

"A few. Hardly enough."

"How many buy from the baker?"

He looked at the baskets, the pallets, the answer already sitting in front of him.

"Most."

Jaina braced one hand on the trestle and shut her eyes long enough to fit the pieces together. The gut-first pattern. The households spared the worst because they had eaten differently. The sweetness on infected breath. Millhaven had given her nothing to split apart. Here the living had done that work for her. Repeated feedings, not one bad swallow, until the body took the lesson and carried it further inward.

When the pattern locked, her arm went still all the way to the wrist.

"What?" the medic asked.

Jaina straightened too fast and caught herself on the table edge.

"Bread," she said.

"Bread can carry sickness."

"This bread. This flour. The body takes it in a little at a time and it settles where food settles first. That's why the heavy bread-eaters are failing faster. That's why the lighter cases are the ones living off their own stores."

The medic stared at the loaf, waiting for it to answer.

"Sweet Light," he said.

"No," Jaina said, already moving for the door. "Grain."

Outside, the square had shifted again.

The captain was there, the older man in the apron, a narrow-shouldered woman with grain dust at both cuffs, and Sylvanas with one glove resting lightly on her saddle pommel. Grain dust filmed the glove. Then the faces.

Sylvanas turned as the hall door banged behind her. "Well?"

"It's the grain," Jaina said. "Or what they've done to it before it becomes bread."

The captain swore once, blunt and heartfelt. The older man went white under his flour.

"You say that now," Sylvanas said. No challenge in it. Only: what changed.

"Because now I can earn it." Jaina came down the steps into the square. "The worst cases cluster in the belly first. The people living light on bakery flour are ill, but they are not failing at the same speed. The heavy bread-eaters are."

"And outside the hall," Sylvanas said, "our new grain seller arrived three months ago with lower prices and no one could remember his face five breaths after describing it. Pleasant. Forgettable. Different wagon paint from one week to the next. Same stock. Same route. Millhaven's old man had company here after all."

Jaina stopped in front of her.

Irritation flashed through her. She had been right, but not finished. Magic had given her the carrier. Sylvanas had given her the path. Together they were enough to move on.

The elf's gloves were filmed with grain dust. Jaina's hands still held the last of the ward-light.

"It fits exactly," she said.

"Yes."

Jaina had the oddest urge to laugh. Nothing in the square deserved laughter. Still, hearing Sylvanas say what Jaina had already begun to think made the whole village seem to tilt truer beneath her boots.

Not because the answer had grown kinder. Because it had stopped splitting in two.

She caught herself looking to Sylvanas before she spoke again.

New enough to be unpleasant.

"Where's the granary keeper?"

The dust-cuffed woman swallowed. "Inside. My father—"

"Good," Sylvanas said. "He can open it again."

The captain bristled. "Again?"

"If someone's feeding this through your stores on purpose, captain, then your granary is no longer a storeroom. It's evidence."

"If there's still treated stock there," Jaina said, "I need to see how it's being carried. Mixed, washed, kept apart. If it started in liquid form, it would travel better."

Sylvanas's eyes flicked over her face once, reading strain and deciding whether it mattered.

"Can you stand long enough to prove it?"

"Can you ask a useful question without sounding wounded by success?"

One of Cyndia's eyebrows rose half a line. Lyriel looked away with suspicious care. The captain stared between them.

Sylvanas spared him the study.

"Captain. Two of yours with us. No one else goes near the grain until the mage tells us what part of it wants burning."

Then she was already moving.

The granary sat behind the square with its long side to the road and its doors barred for the afternoon. Jaina smelled it before they reached it: dry grain, old wood, mouse droppings, and under all of it the same sweetness, now stripped of disguise.

The captain's men went ahead with the keeper. One lifted the bar. The other took the lamp by the door and held it shoulder-high as the first plank opened.

Movement broke inside at once.

Too tall for a rat. Too fast for any frightened animal in a grain room.

A young man in work clothes lunged from behind the nearest stack, one hand already on the hanging lamp, the other reaching for a clay jug on the floor at his feet. He had the raw, cornered face of someone who had spent all morning pretending not to hear suspicion sharpening outside his walls and had run out of time.

"No," Jaina snapped.

Sylvanas was quicker.

The arrow left her bow with so little wasted motion Jaina barely caught the draw. It took the young man through the sleeve and into the post behind him before the lamp could complete its swing. He cried out, lost his grip, and the lamp struck the floor in a burst of oil and sparks.

Frost sheeted across the boards under it, thin and sudden. Flame bit once, hissed, died.

The clay jug flew from the man's other hand and shattered against a sack edge instead of the floor.

Sweetness hit the room hard and close.

Concentrated this time. Narrow. Made, not accidental.

Jaina stepped through the doorway before anyone could tell her not to. "Don't touch it."

Nobody else looked eager.

The shards lay in a pale slick that darkened the dust around them. The smell coming off it had none of the village blur she had been reading all morning. This was cleaner. Skilled. Made to ride quietly until the second half woke in flesh.

She knelt and held the crystal over it.

The light hated the stuff. It whitened hard, then broke into fine trembling lines that ran over the spill and back into her hand. Jaina hissed under her breath. Recognition came without a proper name. Warnings overheard in Dalaran corridors after lectures had ended. A branch we do not teach. Methods better left buried.

She knew necromancy. Any apprentice worth the name knew the broad shape of it. This was not the broad shape. This was the handwork of someone who had learned how to make death travel as craft.

"Well?" Sylvanas asked from behind her.

"This isn't hedge poison," Jaina said. "It was made to survive a road, a storehouse, an oven, and still wake when the body gave it enough time. Whoever did it knew exactly what they were building."

The pinned young man had stopped struggling. Terror had left him almost limp.

"I didn't mean to spill it," he said.

The captain rounded on him. "Gods damn you, Tomas. What did you do?"

"Nothing. I watched the loads. That's all. I only watched."

Sylvanas stepped into his sightline and every other voice in the room flattened.

"Who told you to burn it?"

He looked at her as though she had asked who had taught him fear.

"No one."

"Liar."

"I—"

Jaina moved past the spill into the darker back half of the granary. Rows of sacks. Scoops. Ledgers on a narrow desk. Two barrels set apart from the rest and marked with chalk lines already half-rubbed off by hurried hands. Grain dust lay over everything, but not evenly.

On the nearest stack, burned into the rough weave, was a shipping mark she had not seen in Millhaven because Millhaven had left too little intact to read. A block letter over a mill-sign. Another stack bore the same mark, then a second stamp beneath it in darker ink: a warehouse sigil and an abbreviated place-name.

Andorhal.

Route, if not source.

The ledger told the same story in uglier handwriting. Dates. Weights. Lot numbers. Grain in by one road and out by six others. Jaina could not take in the whole page at a glance, only enough to see destinations radiating southwest and back again through the village.

"Sylvanas."

That made the room turn.

Sylvanas came at once and gave no sign she had noticed.

Jaina turned the ledger toward her and touched the stamped sacks with the crystal's light.

"These are recent. The same store-mark keeps turning up, and not local. Southwest route. Andorhal."

The captain took Andorhal harder than Millhaven. Millhaven was horror close enough to smell. Andorhal was scale.

"That's half the kingdom's flour," he said.

"Which is why someone put a watcher in your granary instead of trusting the work to carry itself," Sylvanas said.

The boy on the post made a broken noise.

"I didn't—"

"You are exactly what you look like," Sylvanas said. "A village pair of hands taught to spill a lamp and keep his mouth shut."

His face lost what little blood it had left.

Cyndia and Lyriel moved on him together at a wordless signal. The arrow came out. He folded before he properly understood he was no longer held up by it. Cyndia caught one arm. Lyriel took the other.

Jaina saw Cyndia's hand clamp hard over his jaw at once, thumb forcing his mouth open. Lyriel ran quick fingers through sleeves, belt, and boots. Nothing. Tomas gagged, twisted, and nearly bit Cyndia for the trouble.

"Keep him breathing," Sylvanas said. "Captain, where do you keep drunks or thieves when they need a door between them and the rest of you?"

"Store room off the yard."

"Show us."

The room was little more than a lockable shed behind the hall, fitted with a bench, a ring in the wall, and a smell of old straw. They sat the watcher against the stone and bound his wrists before he found enough spine to throw himself about. Cyndia took the left. Lyriel the door. The captain stayed. The medic arrived because word had outrun them.

Jaina stood just inside the threshold with the ledger still in one hand and the aftertaste of the spill in her mouth.

Sylvanas crouched in front of the prisoner, forearms on her knees, all her stillness gathered into a point sharp enough to enter skin.

"Name."

"Tomas."

"Who hired you?"

"No one hired me."

"Who told you when to watch the granary?"

"I always watched it."

"Who brought the jug?"

His lips pressed together. A child might have done it the same way, if the child had first been taught silence would save his soul.

The captain swore again. "He was on barley counts last winter. My own sister's boy."

That, Jaina thought, was how these things spread best. Through nephews. Through clerks. Through boys told to mind one corner of the work and believe it small.

Sylvanas's next question changed.

"How many villages?"

He stared at the dirt between her boots. "I don't know."

"Lie smaller. It makes you sound brighter."

"I don't know," he said again, and this time Jaina believed at least part of it.

He was not high enough in the thing to know its whole body. Only high enough to keep the lamp near and the jug hidden.

Jaina stepped forward. "Tomas. Look at me."

He did.

"That liquid in the granary was not meant to make people ill for a week and recover. It was meant to fill them slowly until death came easier and did more work after. If you know a name, a road, a place where the next loads are being touched, this is the moment to stop serving the thing that picked you."

His eyes filled so quickly she nearly mistook it for remorse.

"I never touched the loads," he whispered. "They said watch. They said if anyone came asking too much, I was to spoil the stock and keep my mouth shut."

"Who said?"

His throat worked.

Nothing came.

Then he bent forward in a convulsion so sharp Jaina thought for one absurd instant that he was about to vomit up honesty.

Instead foam touched the corner of his mouth.

She was moving before the captain finished shouting. She caught his jaw, forced it open, saw the blood where he had already bitten down on something hidden in the back of his cheek. Crushed kernel. Poison. Quick and thorough.

"No."

She reached for frost because cold was quickest and got a barrier over throat and chest fast enough to trap the seizure in place and steal one more moment than the poison had planned.

Too late.

His eyes went wide at her, then wider still, not with pleading. With surprise, as if even now he had expected a little more time.

The light in her staff fluttered, failed, came back thin.

By the time the medic was kneeling at her shoulder there was nothing left to slow.

Tomas sagged sideways against the wall ring, warm still, foam bright on his lip in the last slant of daylight.

No one spoke for several breaths.

The captain crossed himself once, hard.

Lyriel looked at the body and then at the door, outward already. Cyndia's face gave nothing at all. Her gaze had already moved to the yard, the hall, the lane beyond.

Sylvanas stood. "Then we take what he left."

Jaina let go of the jaw carefully and got to her feet slower than she wanted seen. Fatigue had been stalking her since morning. Now it came in close, helped along by anger.

"The ledger," she said. "The marks on the sacks. The treated barrels. That's enough to move on."

"To Andorhal," Sylvanas said.

"To Andorhal," Jaina agreed.

The captain looked between them. "You mean to leave."

"I mean to keep your village from becoming the next place we save too late," Sylvanas said.

The man flinched because they were true.

Jaina rubbed the heel of one hand across her brow.

"Listen carefully," she said to the medic. "Anyone still walking and only feverish may have time if you cut the bread now. No bakery loaves. No common flour from the granary. Oats if households still have them. Root broth. Anything not milled from the recent stock. The worst cases can be slowed, not healed, if I mark them. The children first. Then anyone who turned bad in the last day."

He stared, face drawn tight around every person he was already counting.

"And if they beg for bread?"

"Say no."

"You make that sound simple."

"It isn't. Do it anyway."

She went back through the hall one more time with the last of her strength parceled meanly. Blue light over one child, then another. A younger man not yet fully taken. The old woman with enough spite to call her a heartless cow when Jaina slapped bread from her hand, which Jaina took as proof she might survive the night.

Outside, Sylvanas had turned the square back into an order.

The granary doors stood open while the captain's men dragged the suspect sacks into a separate line under Lyriel's eye. Cyndia nailed broken shutters over the yard room until someone could decide what the village did with its dead. The baker was weeping over the nearest pile of flour as though grief might bleach it clean.

Sylvanas ignored the grief and used the useful parts.

"Seal the granary," she told the captain. "Nothing in or out without your hands on it. Post men who won't drink and won't steal."

"That leaves me three."

"Then pray three is enough until morning."

"And if the villagers fight me?"

"They will," Sylvanas said. "Tell them their dead north road came from the same sacks as their supper."

The captain looked toward the hall where the coughing had started again, soft and human and not yet lost.

"And if they still don't listen?"

Sylvanas's face did not change. "Then choose whether you want to command a village or bury one."

Jaina came down the hall steps with her staff braced across both palms. The world had narrowed to the next quarter hour: saddle, water, road, the light left in the sky.

Sylvanas read the calculation on her face. "How many did you buy?"

"Some." Jaina swallowed against a throat gone dry from spellwork. "Hardly enough."

"Good. I'd have worried if you claimed more."

It should not have helped. It did.

They mounted in the failing light while the captain set men to the granary door and the medic began the ugly work of explaining to frightened people why the thing they trusted most on the table had to go.

From horseback the village looked ordinary again in patches. A woman crossing a lane with a kettle. A boy carrying split wood. Smoke from a cookfire climbing into the evening. Only now Jaina could see the pattern under it all, quiet and fed and waiting.

"Andorhal first?" she asked.

Sylvanas gathered the reins. "Southwest by the road. Records if we can find them. More watchers if we cannot."

Jaina nodded, then added, "You'll want the order of the sick written down before we lose the shape of this."

Sylvanas turned her head slightly. "I know."

Jaina looked once over her shoulder at the hall, the granary, the square still trying to behave like a square under a sky going to iron.

Millhaven had been aftermath. This was worse. This was the hour before a place understood it had already begun dying.

She drew in cooling bread smoke and dust.

"The body helps it," she said, mostly to herself. "That's why repeated meals matter. The grain carries the first cut, but people finish the work by living on it."

"Then we'll follow whoever keeps sharpening the blade," Sylvanas said.

Jaina glanced at her.

Dry again. Flat enough to pass for simple sense. But useful.

No thaw. No trust spoken aloud. Only the ugly fact that the answer came clearer when the two halves met.

The quartet rode out as the first lamps came up in the cottages behind them.

On the road beyond the village the air cooled fast. Wheels had bitten deep into the mud here in recent days. Grain wagons, Jaina thought, and once the thought would have stayed with the magic of them alone. Now she found herself looking where the ruts forked, which turn took the heavier load, where a man would stop if he meant to watch a village without seeming to.

Ahead, the road bent southwest.

Andorhal waited somewhere beyond the dim fields, warehouse by warehouse, ledger by ledger, with half Lordaeron's bread running through its hands.
