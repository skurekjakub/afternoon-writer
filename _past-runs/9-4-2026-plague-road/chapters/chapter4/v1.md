# Chapter 4: The Sick Village

The road south had spent two days teaching Jaina how easily disaster could hide inside good weather.

They had ridden through fields already cut in strips for harvest, past sheds with doors flung wide for air, past women shaking linens over fences while children ran under the cloth and came out laughing. Carts creaked. Dogs barked. Smoke rose where it ought to. If she had not known what she was listening for, the country might have passed for healthy.

Every ordinary thing carried its own delayed answer.

The horses felt it before the people did. Weariness first, then resistance: the sort that rode up the reins from a field horse's mouth and settled in the rider's wrists. Too many sudden stops. Too many scents that made an animal think twice and then go on because its rider asked it to. Jaina's mare had started giving the village road a hard eye half a mile out. By the first cottages she was tossing her head at a sweetness the wind kept smearing under bread smoke and wet earth.

Jaina caught it too.

A child crossed the lane carrying kindling in both arms. Her face was flushed dark with fever, but she was still on her feet. A man at the pump paused to cough into his sleeve, spat into the mud, then put his shoulder back into the handle. Somewhere a baker was knocking loaves from a board. The thump of them came through the morning noise clean as hammer-strikes.

Alive, then. For the moment.

That made the place worse.

"There," Jaina said, pointing with two fingers toward the broadest building off the square. "That ought to be where they're keeping the worst cases."

Sylvanas did not look where she pointed at once. She let her gaze make its sweep first: lanes, roofs, the mouths of alleys, the line of the well, the men watching strangers arrive and pretending not to. Cyndia and Lyriel had already spread a little, not enough to threaten the village, only enough to make it clear the riders were not careless.

"And why," Sylvanas said, "ought it be there?"

Jaina bit back the answer that arrived first. Because it is the largest building with windows that open and shade enough to keep a fever room from turning into an oven. Because villages repeat themselves in the same practical shapes. Because if she kept having to explain the existence of obvious things, they would lose whatever margin remained.

"Because if I had six cots and no proper infirmary," she said instead, "I would put them where air can move."

"Useful. Stay here."

The refusal rose so fast Jaina felt it in her throat before she heard her own voice.

"Absolutely not."

That finally got Sylvanas's eyes on her. Blue-green, steady, unreadably bright in the mild light. Jaina hated how often she had already learned to recognize the particular stillness that meant the elf was about to decide whether opposition mattered.

"You were told to stay here."

"And you were told last chapter's proof turns to nothing if we reach the living too late."

One corner of Sylvanas's mouth moved. The expression came and went too quickly to count as a smile.

"You improve," she said. "Keep it."

Then she turned her horse toward the square.

The militia captain met them halfway, as predictable as a door latch. Thick through the chest, beard gone to iron at the chin, one hand already raised in warning before he had decided whether the newcomers were help or trouble.

"Hold there," he called. "No need for weapons in the middle of my village."

No one had drawn.

Jaina saw the mistake arrive in his face the moment he understood that. He had expected swagger, or threat, or human men he could push chest to chest if he had to. What he got was an armored elf who sat her horse like she had been born above all argument, another woman in travel-stained blue who looked too tired to bother with pride, and two silent rangers on the edges of the square already seeing more than he wanted seen.

"Your village is coughing," Sylvanas said. "That makes a need."

"A fever run makes work," the captain shot back. "It doesn't make it your business."

Jaina would have answered too quickly. She knew that because she could feel the answer trying to climb out of her teeth: necromantic contamination, bodily spread, proof already north of here, you ignorant man, do you want your dead on the road before noon?

Sylvanas did not use any of it.

"Millhaven is dead," she said.

Nothing theatrical in the line. No weight added. She spoke it the way another person might speak the hour.

The captain's jaw tightened.

"I've heard stories."

"Then hear one from someone who walked through it. We found tables laid. Doors open. No living voice left in the place but one girl hiding under earth. The dead had already taken the north road."

That changed the room the square sat inside. Jaina could feel it happen the way she felt a ward settle when the right word struck it. Two women at the pump stopped pretending not to listen. The baker's board went quiet. Even the captain's horse shifted and stood with one hind leg cocked, waiting.

He looked at Jaina then, perhaps because the robe and staff told him which one might sound more like the sort of answer he trusted.

"You can prove that?"

"I can prove your people are carrying the same wrongness," Jaina said. "Whether you've enough time left to be offended by that depends on how long you keep me out of your sickroom."

The captain opened his mouth.

Sylvanas cut across him before he spent the moment on dignity.

"You can argue with a report after she's done."

He looked back at her. Jaina watched understanding settle in his face faster than she had expected: the elf was not here to own the village, only to force the right door open before the place tipped past use. She had expected command from Sylvanas, perhaps contempt, perhaps one of those cold little displays soldiers liked when civilians inconvenienced them.

What she got instead was quieter. More dangerous for that.

"Captain," Sylvanas said, lowering her voice enough that he had to lean a fraction nearer to hear it. "If this is only fever, I leave and you keep your pride. If it isn't, every minute you spend showing me your authority costs you people who still have enough blood in them to save."

The man exhaled through his nose.

"Dane Hall," he said at last, jerking his chin toward the long building Jaina had marked. "We moved the worst there yesterday. The medic's inside. If you frighten them, that's on you."

"Everything in this village looks ready to frighten itself," Sylvanas said.

It was so dry Jaina only barely caught the humor in it. The captain caught it too, apparently, because the line that came into his face shifted from offense into something stranger and more useful: reluctant recognition.

He stepped aside.

Jaina swung down before her mare had fully settled.

The hall had once been built for weddings, meetings, winter suppers, all the dull communal things villages did to prove they would still exist come spring. It smelled of rush mats, stale beer in old wood, lamp grease, boiled cloth, and under all of it the sweet wrongness on sick breath.

Cots had been jammed in any space that would take them, and still it was not enough. Two children lay on pallets between benches. An old woman had been propped upright against the wall because no frame remained. A man sat with a bucket between his boots, bent over it and shaking, while someone who must have been his wife held the back of his neck and stared at Jaina as if she had either brought salvation or insult and there would be no middle ground.

The medic came at her fast.

"If you're here to tell me it's marsh fever, I've already heard it from three farmers and a priest—"

"Good," Jaina said. "Then you have practice being wrong."

He stopped dead.

The man had the red eyes and grey stubble of someone who had slept in pieces for too many nights. Shirt sleeves rolled to the elbow. Hands scrubbed raw. He looked from her face to the staff and decided, sensibly, that anything unusual was better than one more argument with the village.

"What do you need?"

"Quiet, as much as you can manage. And whoever knows which of these people ate what."

The medic blinked. "Ate what?"

"Do you want me to start with the longer version?"

"No."

"Then the shorter one is that I need the room and your memory."

He gave her the room.

Jaina let the crystal in her staff wake properly for the first time that morning. Blue-white ran through it, then steadied into a thinner light she could hold without wasting herself. She moved from cot to cot, not touching first, letting the spell skim above skin, throat, belly, the hollows under the ribs. The pattern showed itself more quickly in the living than it had in Millhaven's aftermath. Alive, it was busier. Greedier. The wrongness was not sitting in them like poison waiting out its hour. It was working.

Gut first.

She stopped over the man with the bucket. Breath sweet, skin damp, pulse skipping under the jaw. The light drew amber filaments through his middle, a fouled nest tight under the stomach and spreading outward in finer threads.

It was not spreading from a wound. It was settling where use had put it.

"How long?" she asked.

The wife answered. "Three days on the belly. Fever by last night."

"What has he eaten?"

"What does everyone eat?" Suspicion entered the line before the words were out. "Broth. Bread. What we can keep down."

Jaina moved on.

Same nesting in the gut. Worse in some, lighter in others. A boy with sunken eyes and cracked lips had it only faintly, as if the thing had knocked once at his body and found less welcome than it expected. An old woman who still had enough spite to curse Jaina's cold hands carried it deeper, well-fed and settled. A child sleeping too hard under a patched blanket made Jaina stop entirely. The contamination sat in her like a banked coal.

Jaina set the end of her staff to the floor and built the ward carefully.

She had no cure. She could only slow a door from opening. That was not the same thing as building a wall.

Blue light sank around the child in a thin shell. Not visible as a dome, only in the way the air bent and clarified. The little body twitched once, then loosened. Breathing eased. In the corner the mother made a sound into both hands and held it there.

"That buys time," Jaina said without looking at her. "Nothing more. Do not spend the time by giving her more bread."

The medic stared at the child, then at Jaina's hands. "You can stop it?"

"I can slow some of them. If you want stopping, tell me where it enters."

"You think it's food."

"Their bellies know something the rest of the village has not admitted."

The line of his mouth flattened. "We've had foul wells before. A spoiled cask can run through half a street."

"Show me the households that stayed light."

That took him a moment.

He had not expected that question. Jaina had started learning that the useful things were often the ones people forgot to count because they had not seemed dramatic enough at the time.

"Marta and her boys," he said slowly. "They were sick, but not like this. The widow in the south lane. Two men from the tannery. One of the shepherd families."

"And what do they have in common?"

"How should I know?"

"Because you've been watching people live through it and I have only just arrived."

He scrubbed one hand over his mouth. Thought. Thought properly, to his credit, instead of taking offense.

"Widow keeps oats," he said. "Hates paying mill prices. Marta's eldest broke a tooth and they were feeding him broth more than bread. The shepherds don't buy much from the baker. They eat what they keep."

There.

Not proof yet, but shape.

Jaina crossed the hall to the shuttered window and pushed it open wider with her wrist. The square lay beyond it in slices: captain, two frightened villagers, Cyndia at the edge of the pump, Lyriel near the lane mouth, and Sylvanas in the middle of them all as if the village had been built to place her there. She was speaking to an old man in an apron dusted pale to the elbows. Granary keeper, probably. Or baker. Perhaps both in a place this size.

Jaina watched Sylvanas ask three short questions and get five useful answers out of the man's face before his mouth had caught up.

She looked back at the room.

Bread baskets. Crumbs. A knife left in a loaf on the trestle where the medic had probably been forcing food into people who needed strength and did not know he was feeding the thing already chewing through them.

Jaina crossed to the table and pressed two fingers into the cut loaf. Her spell answered at once, brighter than it had over the weaker patients. The bread did not hold the deepest part of the design. It carried the first useful part of it.

"You idiot," she said softly, not to the medic.

He misheard it as accusation anyway. "I was trying to keep them alive."

"I know."

She turned the loaf over. Flour dusted her gloves. Warm room, warm bread, village life still going through its motions while every shared meal laid down another layer.

"How many households grind their own?"

"A few. Hardly enough."

"How many buy from the baker?"

He looked at the baskets, at the pallets, at the answer already sitting in front of him.

"Most."

Jaina braced one hand on the trestle and shut her eyes long enough to fit the pieces together. The gut-first concentration. The households that had been spared the worst because they had eaten differently. The sweetness on infected breath. The grain loud in Millhaven, loud here, loud enough both times to tempt stupidity. Millhaven had given her nothing to compare: everyone there had breathed the same air, eaten from the same stores, died before the pattern could be split apart. Here the living had done that work for her. Repeated feedings, not one bad swallow, until the body took the lesson and began carrying it further inward on its own.

When the pattern locked she felt it physically, all through her arms, a click so clean it seemed to come from the joints.

The medic saw it happen.

"What?"

Jaina straightened too fast and had to catch herself on the table edge.

"Bread," she said.

"Bread can carry sickness."

"No. This bread. This flour. The body takes it in a little at a time and the wrongness settles where food settles first. That's why the heavy bread-eaters are failing faster. That's why your lighter cases are the people living off their own stores."

The medic stared at the loaf as though he expected it to confess.

"Sweet Light," he said.

"Not the Light."

She did not wait to see whether he understood. The hall had gone thinner around the edges from too much magic and too little sleep. If she stood here another minute explaining what his eyes could do for themselves, she would waste the part of the day still worth having.

Outside, the square had shifted.

The captain was there, the older man in the apron, a narrow-shouldered woman with grain dust at both cuffs, and Sylvanas with one glove resting lightly on the pommel of her saddle. Jaina saw the dust on that glove first. Then the set of the group's faces.

Sylvanas turned as the hall door banged behind her.

"Well?"

"It's the grain," Jaina said. "Or what they've done to it before it becomes bread."

The captain swore once, blunt and heartfelt.

The older man went white under his flour.

"You say that now," Sylvanas said. No challenge in it. Only the demand for what had changed.

"Because now I can earn it." Jaina went down the steps into the square. "The worst cases cluster in the belly first. Not the throat, not the blood, not the skin. The people living light on bakery flour are ill, but they are not failing at the same speed. The heavy bread-eaters are."

"And outside the hall," Sylvanas said, "our new grain seller arrived three months ago with lower prices and no one could remember his face five breaths after describing it. Pleasant. Forgettable. Different wagon paint from one week to the next. Same stock. Same route. Your old man in Millhaven had a brother here after all."

Jaina stopped in front of her.

For one bare second irritation went through her, sharp and private. She had been right, but not finished. Magic had given her the carrier. Sylvanas had given her the human path it had taken. Either alone would have been useful. Together they turned sharp enough to act on.

The elf's gloves were filmed with grain dust. Jaina's hands still held the last of the ward-light like cold water.

"It fits exactly," she said.

"Yes."

Jaina heard herself ask, "Where's the granary keeper?"

The dust-cuffed woman swallowed. "Inside. My father—"

"Good," Sylvanas said. "He can open it again."

The captain bristled. "Again?"

"If someone's feeding this through your stores on purpose, captain, then your granary is no longer a storeroom. It's a scene."

Jaina had the oddest urge to laugh. Nothing in the square deserved laughter. Still, hearing Sylvanas say what Jaina had already begun to think made the whole village seem to tilt a degree truer beneath her boots.

She caught herself looking to Sylvanas before speaking again.

New enough to be unpleasant.

"If there's still treated stock there," Jaina said, "I need to see how it's being carried. Mixed? Washed? Stored separate? Something in liquid form would survive transport better than raw casting."

Sylvanas's eyes flicked over her face once, reading strain and deciding whether it mattered.

"Can you stand long enough to prove it?"

"Can you ask a useful question without sounding surprised by your own success?"

One of Cyndia's eyebrows rose half a line. Lyriel looked away very carefully. The captain, who did not yet know enough to stay out of it, stared between them as if trying to determine whether this was how elves cooperated or how they threatened to bite.

Sylvanas spared him the study.

"Captain. Two of yours with us. No one else goes near the grain until the mage says what part of it wants burning."

Then she was already moving.

The granary sat behind the square with its long side to the road and its doors barred for the afternoon. Jaina smelled it before they reached it: dry grain, old wood, mouse droppings, and under all of it the same sweetness now stripped of disguise because she knew what she was standing in.

The captain's men went ahead with the keeper. One lifted the bar. The other took the lamp hanging by the door and held it shoulder-high to throw light across the gap as the first plank opened.

Movement broke inside at once.

Too tall for a rat. Too fast for any frightened animal in a grain room.

A young man in work clothes lunged from behind the nearest stack, one hand already on the hanging lamp, the other reaching for a clay jug on the floor at his feet. He had the raw, cornered face of someone who had spent all morning pretending not to hear suspicion sharpening outside his walls and had just run out of time.

"No," Jaina snapped.

Sylvanas was quicker.

The arrow left her bow with so little wasted motion Jaina barely saw the draw. It took the young man through the sleeve and into the post behind him, pinning cloth and skin together before the lamp could complete its swing. He cried out, lost his grip, and the lamp struck the floor in a burst of oil and sparks—

—not on open grain. On stone, because Jaina's spell got there first.

Frost sheeted across the boards under the lamp, thin and sudden. Flame bit once, hissed, died. The clay jug flew from the man's other hand and shattered against a sack edge instead of the floor.

Sweetness hit the room like a hand over the mouth.

Wrong. Concentrated. Not rot, not spoiled flour, not anything that had come out of a village by accident.

Jaina stepped through the doorway before anyone could tell her not to.

"Don't touch it," she said, though nobody else was mad enough to want to.

The shards lay in a pale slick that darkened the dust around them. The smell coming off it had none of the muddled village sprawl she had been reading all morning. This was cleaner. Narrower. Skilled. Someone had built the poison part and the answering part together so the first would ride quietly until the second could wake in flesh.

She knelt and held the crystal over it.

The light did not like the stuff. It whitened hard, then broke into fine trembling lines that ran over the spill and back into her hand. Jaina hissed under her breath. Recognition without a proper name moved through her. Warnings heard in Dalaran corridors after lectures had ended. Phrases Antonidas's generation used only when the students had already gone: a branch we do not teach, methods better left buried, brilliance turned toward the wrong door.

She knew necromancy. Any apprentice worth the name knew the broad shape of it. This was not that broad shape. This was the handwork of somebody who had learned how to make death travel as craft.

"Well?" Sylvanas asked quietly from behind her.

"This isn't hedge poison," Jaina said. "It was made to survive a road, a storehouse, an oven, and still wake when the body gave it enough time. Whoever did it knew exactly what they were building."

The pinned young man had stopped struggling. Terror had gone through him so hard it had left him almost limp.

"I didn't mean to spill it," he said.

The captain rounded on him. "Gods damn you, Tomas. What did you do?"

"Nothing. I watched the loads. That's all. I only watched."

Sylvanas stepped into his sightline and every other voice in the room flattened under hers at once.

"Who told you to burn it?"

He looked at her as though she had asked who had taught him fear.

"No one."

"Liar."

"I—"

Jaina stood and moved past the spill into the darker back half of the granary. Rows of sacks. Scoops. Ledgers on a narrow desk. Two barrels set apart from the rest and marked with chalk lines already half-rubbed off by hurried hands. Grain dust lay over everything, but not evenly. Some sacks had been disturbed that morning. Others had a finer settled coat that said they had been left alone for days.

On the nearest stack, burned into the rough weave, was a shipping mark she had not seen in Millhaven because Millhaven had not left enough intact to read. A block letter over a mill-sign. Another stack bore the same mark, then a second stamped beneath it in darker ink: a warehouse sigil and an abbreviated place-name.

Andorhal.

Not proof of source. Proof of route.

The ledger told the same story in uglier handwriting. Dates. Weights. Lot numbers. A clerk's running account of grain that had come in by one road and gone out by six others. Jaina could not take in the whole page at a glance, only enough to see the pattern of destinations radiating southwest and back again through the village like veins feeding a hand.

"Sylvanas."

That made the room turn.

She hated how easy the name had come in urgency. Hated it more because she did not have time to retrieve it.

Sylvanas came at once, and if she noticed the name she gave no sign except speed.

Jaina turned the ledger toward her and touched the stamped sacks with the crystal's light.

"These are recent. The same store-mark keeps turning up, and not local. Southwest route. Andorhal."

The word changed the captain differently than Millhaven had. Millhaven was horror close enough to smell. Andorhal was scale.

"That's half the kingdom's flour," he said.

"Which is why someone put a watcher in your granary instead of trusting the work to carry itself," Sylvanas said.

The boy on the post made a broken noise.

"I didn't—"

"You are exactly what you look like," Sylvanas said. "A village pair of hands taught how to spill a lamp and die before speaking."

His face lost what little blood it had left.

Cyndia and Lyriel moved on him together at a wordless signal. The arrow came out. He folded before he properly understood he was no longer held up by it. Cyndia caught one arm. Lyriel took the other. Young, yes. Frightened, yes. But the fear in him had an old instruction under it. Jaina saw that much even before Sylvanas did.

"Keep him breathing," Sylvanas said. "Captain, where do you keep drunks or thieves when they need a door between them and the rest of you?"

"Store room off the yard."

"Show us."

The room was little more than a lockable shed behind the hall, fitted with a bench, a ring in the wall, and a smell of old straw. They sat the watcher against the stone and bound his wrists before he found enough spine to fling himself about. Cyndia took the left. Lyriel the door. The captain stayed because leaving would have looked like weakness. The medic arrived because word had outrun them.

Jaina stood just inside the threshold with the ledger still in one hand and the aftertaste of the concentrated spill in her mouth.

Sylvanas crouched in front of the prisoner, forearms on her knees, all her stillness gathered into a point sharp enough to enter skin.

"Name."

"Tomas."

"Who hired you?"

"No one hired me."

"Who told you when to watch the granary?"

"I always watched it."

"Who brought the jug?"

His lips pressed together. A child might have done it the same way, if the child had first been told silence would save his soul.

The captain swore again. "He was on barley counts last winter. My own sister's boy."

That, Jaina thought, was how these things spread best. Through nephews. Through clerks. Through boys with frightened faces who had been told to mind one corner of the machine and believe it was too small to matter.

Sylvanas saw it too. Jaina knew because the next question changed.

"How many villages?"

He stared at the dirt between Sylvanas's boots.

"I don't know."

"Lie smaller. It makes you sound brighter."

"I don't know," he said again, and for the first time Jaina believed part of it.

He was not high enough in the thing to know its whole body. Only high enough to keep the lamp near and the jug hidden.

Jaina stepped forward.

"Tomas," she said, and the sound of her own voice startled him more than Sylvanas's had. "Look at me."

He did.

"That liquid in the granary was not meant to make people ill for a week and then recover. It was meant to fill them slowly until death came easier and did more work after. If you know a name, a road, a place where the next loads are being touched, now is the moment to stop being useful to the thing that picked you."

His eyes filled so quickly she nearly mistook it for remorse.

"I never touched the loads," he whispered. "They said watch. They said if anyone came asking too much, I was to spoil the stock and keep my mouth shut."

"Who said?"

His throat worked.

Nothing came.

Then he bent forward in a convulsion so sharp Jaina thought for one absurd instant that he was about to vomit up honesty.

Instead foam touched the corner of his mouth.

Jaina was moving before the captain finished shouting. She caught his jaw, forced it open, saw the blood where he had already bitten something hidden in the back of his cheek. Bitter almond, crushed shell, a smell so brief and murderous it barely existed before it was in him.

"No."

She reached for frost because cold was quickest and got a barrier over his throat and chest fast enough to trap the seizure in place, slow it, make his body fight for one more moment than the poison had planned.

Too late.

His eyes went wide at her, then wider still, not with pleading. With the raw surprise of someone who had truly thought he would have longer between the choice and the dark.

The light in her staff fluttered. Failed. Came back thin.

By the time the medic was kneeling at her shoulder there was nothing left to slow.

Tomas sagged sideways against the wall ring, warm still, foam bright on his lip in the last slant of daylight.

No one said anything for several breaths.

The captain crossed himself. Once. Hard.

Lyriel looked at the body and then at the door, outward already, as if the dead boy had only narrowed the map further.

Cyndia's face gave nothing at all. Her gaze had already moved to the yard, the hall, the lane beyond. Watchers died. Routes remained.

Sylvanas stood.

"Then we take what he left."

Jaina let go of the jaw carefully and got to her feet slower than she wanted seen. Fatigue had been stalking her since morning. Now it came in close, helped along by anger.

"The ledger," she said. "The marks on the sacks. The treated barrels. That's enough to move on."

"To Andorhal," Sylvanas said.

"To Andorhal," Jaina agreed.

The captain looked between them. "You mean to leave."

"I mean to keep your village from becoming the next reason we are too late somewhere larger," Sylvanas said.

The man flinched, not from the words but because they were true.

Jaina rubbed the heel of one hand across her brow, buying half a second to order the next work.

"Listen carefully," she said to the medic, and this time she let the full speed into her voice because he had earned the right to keep up. "Anyone still walking and only feverish may have time if you cut the bread now. No bakery loaves. No common flour from the granary. Oats if households still have them. Root broth. Anything not milled from the recent stock. The worst cases can be slowed, not healed, if I mark them. The children first. Then anyone who only turned bad in the last day."

He stared, face drawn tight around every person he was already counting.

"And if they beg for bread?"

"Say no."

"You make that sound simple."

"It isn't. Do it anyway."

She went back through the hall one more time with the last of her strength parceled meanly. Blue light over one child, then another. A younger man not yet fully taken. The old woman with enough spite to call her a heartless cow when Jaina slapped bread from her hand, which Jaina took as proof the woman might survive the night.

Outside, Sylvanas had turned the square into an order again.

The granary doors stood open while the captain's men dragged the suspect sacks into a separate line under Lyriel's eye. Cyndia nailed the broken shutters of the yard room closed over the watcher's body until someone could decide what the village did with its dead now that burial itself had become suspect. The baker's apron-man was weeping openly over the nearest pile of flour as though grief might bleach it clean.

Sylvanas ignored the grief and used the useful parts.

"Seal the granary," she told the captain. "Nothing in or out without your hands on it. Post men who won't drink and won't steal."

"That leaves me three."

"Then pray three is enough until morning."

"And if the villagers fight me?"

"They will," Sylvanas said. "Tell them their dead north road came from the same sacks as their supper."

The captain looked toward the hall where the coughing had started up again, soft and human and not yet lost.

"And if they still don't listen?"

Sylvanas's face did not change.

"Then choose whether you want to command a village or bury one."

Jaina came down the hall steps with her staff braced across both palms. The world had narrowed to the practical range of the next quarter hour. Saddle. Water. Road. The light left in the sky.

Sylvanas saw the calculation on her face.

"How many did you buy?"

"Some." Jaina swallowed against a throat gone dry from spellwork. "Hardly enough."

"Good. I'd have worried if you claimed more."

It should not have helped. It did.

They mounted in the failing light while the captain set men to the granary door and the medic began the ugly work of explaining to frightened people why the thing they trusted most on the table had become the first thing he would take away.

From horseback the village looked ordinary again in patches. That was the cruelty of it. A woman crossing a lane with a kettle. A boy carrying split wood. Smoke from a cookfire climbing into the evening. Only now Jaina could see where the pattern sat beneath it all, quiet and fed and waiting for each body to finish the work the road had started.

"Andorhal first?" she asked.

Sylvanas gathered the reins. "Southwest by the road. Records if we can find them. More watchers if we cannot."

Jaina nodded, then heard herself add, "You'll want the medic's triage order written down before we lose the shape of this one."

Sylvanas turned her head slightly.

"I know."

Agreement, then. Clean and immediate.

Jaina looked once over her shoulder at the hall, the granary, the square still trying to behave like a square under a sky going to iron.

Millhaven had been aftermath. This was worse. This was the moment before a place understood it had already begun dying.

She took a breath that tasted of cooling bread smoke and dust.

"The body helps it," she said, mostly to herself. "That's why repeated meals matter. The grain carries the first cut, but people finish the work by living on it."

"Then we'll follow whoever keeps sharpening the blade," Sylvanas said.

Jaina glanced at her.

Dry again. Flat enough to pass for simple sense. But useful. Load-bearing. The sort of line that turned her own thought half a degree and made it easier to carry.

No thaw. No trust spoken aloud. Only the ugly fact that the answer came clearer when the two halves met.

The quartet rode out as the first lamps came up in the cottages behind them.

On the road beyond the village the air cooled fast. Wheels had bitten deep into the mud here in recent days. Grain wagons, Jaina thought, and once the thought would have stayed with the magic of them alone. Now she found herself looking where the ruts forked, at which turn took the heavier load, at where a man would stop if he meant to watch a village without seeming to.

Ahead, the road bent southwest.

Andorhal waited somewhere beyond the dim fields, warehouse by warehouse, ledger by ledger, with half Lordaeron's bread running through its hands.
