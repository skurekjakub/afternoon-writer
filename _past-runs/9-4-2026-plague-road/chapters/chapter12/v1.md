# Chapter 12: The Necromancer

Kel'Thuzad's sanctum was an office built at the bottom of a grave.

Jaina had expected grandeur. A throne, perhaps. Black candles. Some obvious vulgarity to brace against. The room gave her shelves instead, high and orderly, crowded with stitched folios and labeled jars. A broad desk sat under a bank of slate boards filmed in chalk. Green fungi shone from old stone niches and threw a drowned light across glass, paper, brass weights, and an inkknife laid exactly square to the desk's edge. The air was cold and dry. Preservative salts lived in it. Under that sat the sweeter, older smell that had followed her all the way through Scholomance's upper halls and worsened the lower she went.

Antonidas's hand was on every surface.

His habits, rather. Margin stars. Cross-braced proofs. The little hooked correction mark he used whenever a student had reached the right answer by an ugly route. Jaina had grown up under those signs. She had spent half her life arranging thoughts so they would survive that red pen. Here they covered the deepest room in Scholomance, written by the man who had taken those same lessons and kept going.

At the threshold, Jandice stood with one hand still on the open door. She had gone proud and careful again, all that quick delighted spillage packed back behind a noblewoman's posture. The excitement still showed in the angle of her chin.

Kel'Thuzad did not look up from the page in front of him at once. He turned it, set a paperweight on the finished sheet, then raised his head.

Alive. Human. Grey-haired, gaunt, scholar-thin. His robe still carried the bones of Kirin Tor cut under the darker cloth. He might have been any senior archmage in a restricted study if Jaina ignored where the room sat and what the jars held. That was the first real shock of him. No theatrics. No visible madness. Just a man at his work.

"Mistress Barov," he said, "you have done enough."

Jandice's fingers tightened on the latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore has followed the upper sequence farther than I expected. She caught the false rings almost as soon as--"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad said, and his eyes moved to Jaina at last. Pale. Steady. Interested. "I can see that."

Warmth touched Jandice's face and vanished under discipline. She bowed. "Of course."

Jaina watched the bow more than the words. Respect. Pride. Disappointment at being excluded. None of it belonged to a jailer turning over a prisoner. Jandice looked like a student dismissed from a seminar she desperately wanted to stay in.

The door closed behind her with a thick stone sound. The wards under the floor hummed once, almost below hearing, as if the room had noted the change in numbers and approved it.

Kel'Thuzad folded his hands on the desk. "You have come a long way for the missing step."

Jaina stayed where Jandice had left her, seated across from him at the long table that cut the room in two. A stick of chalk lay near her right hand. She had not noticed picking it up upstairs. It left a dusting across her thumb.

"I came because the body was doing work my field reads could not finish," she said. "Your grain carries the strongest signature because you want it to. Every site answered there first. Every site stalled there too."

"Good." A single word, simple as breath. "Then begin there."

He rose and crossed to the nearest board. The movement was unhurried. He did not pace. He relocated, as if one part of the room served speech better than another. Chalk clicked once against slate.

"The grain is only the carrier," he said.

He drew a circle, then a second around it. Outer shell. False ring. No need to write the words. The structure said them.

"You know that much," Jaina said.

"You know more than that much. You know the treated grain survives storage, travel, even baking, because the preserving work is simple and brutal. You know repeated ingestion matters more than any single meal. You know the host is altered before death. What you lacked was the final relation."

Jaina went still. Host. Altered. He used the words as if they were ordinary furniture, as if they did not drag a village behind them.

Kel'Thuzad wrote a second chain below the first, this one deeper, tighter, each mark nested inside the next.

"The outer shell teaches nothing," he said. "It only arrives intact. The lesson begins in the body."

The chalk rasped softly. Conditioned tissue. Repeated exposure. Pattern retention. His diction stayed on the right side of the world. No modern clinical coldness. He said it all in the language of wards and bindings and prepared mediums, and that made it worse. Jaina could follow every step.

"Not poison," she said.

"No. Poison spends itself."

She heard herself answer before she had decided to. "This settles. Waits. The body carries it farther than the bread can."

His mouth changed. Almost a smile. More dangerous than one.

"Exactly."

Heat climbed the back of her neck. She hated that flicker of pleasure his approval drew from her, hated it because it was honest. Jandice had been fast. Kel'Thuzad was faster. He left room where the next inference belonged and her mind, trained to hate an empty step in a proof, rushed to fill it.

He tapped the deeper chain with the chalk. "Your field work at the farmhouse was close. The body does not merely suffer contamination. It is taught by it. Repetition lays down an answerable shape. Death does not interrupt the sequence. Death releases it."

Jaina looked at the board, then at the specimen jars on the shelf beyond him. A child's hand in one. Two preserved eyes in another. The room wanted to split. The proof wanted one sort of attention and the jars another.

"The body becomes the answering chamber," she said quietly.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head.

The phrase sat between them. It completed too much at once. The villages. The gut-first spread. Why the strongest readings had never yielded the whole thing. Why the dead moved with a rough common pull even before handlers sharpened them into obedience.

She stood without meaning to and crossed to the board. The cold under the room had crept into her knees during the descent. She only felt it now when she straightened. She took the chalk from the ledge and added a branching line under his.

"The first waking is crude," she said. "Because the body has been taught enough to answer, not enough to remain itself."

"Precisely."

She drew another branch, narrower.

"A village fed together dies together," Kel'Thuzad said behind her. "A village that dies together does not rise as a pile of accidents. It rises in relation. Loose relation, in the field case. Appetite. Direction. Response. Enough to muster. Enough to become useful under a competent hand."

Jaina wrote nothing. The board was already too full. Her hand stayed raised anyway.

"The acolytes in the villages," she said. "They do not create that from nothing."

"No."

"They catch it."

"And harden it."

She set the chalk down with great care. White dust marked her fingertips. For a moment she could only look at them.

"Then the dead in those villages never had a chance," she said.

Kel'Thuzad turned the chalk in his fingers. "The villages had many chances. They baked them."

The flatness of the answer struck harder than outrage would have. She turned back to him.

"You speak as if routine were consent."

"I speak as if routine is the strongest force available to any large design. You of all people should respect repetition."

He set the chalk aside and moved to the desk again, not retreating, only changing the room's balance once more. The seminar had shifted from board to table. Jaina felt it happen with the same clarity she might have felt a line of cold on bare skin.

"There is still a difference," she said. "What rises in a village and what you are doing here."

"At last."

He drew one folio nearer and opened it. Notes. Diagrams. A lattice of circles bound around a single central mark. Finer work than the village mechanisms. Costlier. Individualized.

"Your field dead preserve function," he said. "Motion. Appetite. Rough answerability. Their pattern survives in low resolution because low resolution is enough for war. The finer work requires finer structure."

Jaina leaned over the page despite herself. Ink lines. Layered binding. Different words, same concepts underneath them.

"This is what the villages do not receive," she said.

"This is what the villages cannot afford."

The distinction opened under her feet.

Low village dead. The shambling choir. And above them, a second aim. A finer, more exact one.

"Preserved selfhood," she said, and the phrase came out colder than she intended.

Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened with pleasure. "There. Yes."

She stepped back from the desk as if the page had heat in it.

"That is what you want for yourself."

"Of course."

No shame. No evasion. Only mild surprise that the point would need saying aloud.

"You built a kingdom-scale slaughter to prove a private theorem."

"No." He sat again and steepled his fingers. "I built a kingdom-scale apparatus because the theorem was true whether anyone liked it or not. My private interest lies in carrying that truth farther than your teachers permitted."

Jaina stared at him.

The room had gone very quiet. The wards under the floor persisted, low and regular. Somewhere water touched stone in a slow measured drip. The fungi in their wall niches gave the books a green seam at every edge. She could smell chalk. Ink. Preservative salts. The whole place held together with offensive professionalism.

"Dalaran banned necromancy," she said.

"Yes."

"Because it destroys the line between a body and a person."

"Yes."

"Because it collapses consent."

"Yes."

"Because it turns every household, every healer, every grave into a point of violation."

Something like approval warmed his face again. "Much better. You have stopped objecting at the level of taste."

Anger hit clean and fast.

"Do not mistake clarity for agreement."

"I have never required agreement from a serious mind."

He said it gently. That was the obscenity. Another man might have barked, might have gloated. Kel'Thuzad spoke as though he were excusing a promising student for arriving late to the lecture and missing the first premise.

Jaina put both hands on the table. Cold wood under her palms. Smooth from use.

"The fact that the theorem holds," she said, "does not absolve what it does."

"Absolve?" he repeated. "No. That is a priest's word. The work needs no absolution. It needs description."

She wanted to strike him. The thought flashed up whole and stupid. He would have seen it in her shoulders if he cared to look. He did not. He was watching her mind instead.

"Description is not enough when the method consumes whole villages."

"And prohibition is not enough when the method remains sound."

The answer came down neat as a blade.

"Your Council never disproved necromancy," he said. "It forbade it. There is a difference. They found the line where valid method touched living society and became intolerable to them. Persons. Consent. Continuity. Civic trust. They were correct to fear that line. Fear, however, is not refutation."

Jaina opened her mouth. Nothing useful arrived.

Because he was right there too, in the architecture of the argument. Moral rightness never entered it. But the line of the thing held. Dalaran had not called it nonsense. Dalaran had called it forbidden.

Kel'Thuzad saw the moment land and did not press. He let the silence do it. Professor's instinct. Let the student sit with the hard turn before laying the next piece down.

That restraint frightened her more than any sermon could have.

She drew her hands off the table and looked around the room again, forcing the eye outward. The jars had not changed while they spoke. The books had not shut themselves in disgust. A measuring rod lay beside the desk. A tea cup, abandoned to cold. Nothing in the sanctum reacted as if anything monstrous had been said. The room received all of it and kept working.

"That is your answer?" she said. "That because the logic survives the ban, the ban can be ignored?"

"No. My answer is that the ban names the true cost more honestly than the scholars who still invoke it as if it were proof of magical incoherence."

He rose once more and went to one of the shelves, where he took down a bound volume and set it in front of her with both hands. The gesture had the care of librarians and executioners.

"Read the old Dalaran debates, if you like," he said. "They are very pious on the subject of danger and disarmingly frank on the subject of efficacy."

Jaina did not touch the book.

"You think this makes you brave."

"No," Kel'Thuzad said. "Bravery is for men about to do something uncertain. I did something certain and accepted the predictable result."

There it was again. No boasting. No need to inflate himself. Only the calm of a proof already concluded.

Jaina stayed on her feet. Sitting felt too much like compliance now.

"The predictable result," she said, "is famine, plague, and mothers dragged out of their own houses by what used to be their sons."

"Yes."

"You say that as if the word were sufficient."

"It is sufficient to describe what occurred."

"Description is bloodless."

"Description is honest."

She laughed once. It came out without humor and without control.

"You built an atrocity and then taught yourself to narrate it cleanly."

Kel'Thuzad's head tipped, a fraction only. "And your response has been what, exactly? You crossed borders without sanction, pursued a plague no one had authorized you to pursue, and followed your private analysis into this room because you were certain the answer waited below. Shall I call that clean?"

The words struck with almost physical force. Too much of them was familiar.

"Do not compare us."

"I have no need to force the comparison. You made it on your own the moment you entered."

Jaina's hands curled at her sides. Chalk dust marked one cuff where she had brushed it there upstairs. She could feel the old bruised weariness of the last weeks in her bones all at once: the roads, the village rooms, the grain dust, the map tables, Sylvanas's blood on her own fingers at the ridge. She took one breath, then another, and dragged the argument back onto ground she could stand on.

"I crossed a border to stop this."

"Yes."

"You crossed one to spread it."

"Direction matters. I have never denied that."

He said it so easily that her anger stumbled. She had expected sleight there, some self-excusing rhetoric. He gave her none.

"Then what are you claiming?"

"Only this." He touched the desk once with two fingers, like a lecturer marking the true line of a problem. "We were both made by the same institution. We were both taught to follow pattern where it led. We both found that the world outside our study chambers moved too slowly for the work at hand. You call your trespass duty because the object of your conviction flatters your ethics. I call mine scholarship because it flatters my honesty. The underlying habit is the same."

Jaina went cold.

Herself. Fingertips first, then inward.

"No."

"No?" His voice stayed mild. "You left a note and rode north alone because you trusted your reading over procedure. Admirable. So did I, once."

"I did not build a machine for stripping personhood out of a kingdom."

"No. You are earlier in the chain."

The words landed and stayed.

He should have sounded cruel. Instead he sounded exact.

Jaina turned away from him before she could stop herself. The nearest shelf carried catalogued boxes in a careful hand, each labeled by subject and date. One had Antonidas's old sigil for translational instability scratched into the corner and crossed out later, replaced by a cleaner notation Jaina recognized as Kel'Thuzad's own. Same schooling. Same gesture corrected two different ways.

She put her palm against the shelf. The wood was dry and cool. Solid.

"You are wrong."

"About your intentions? I do not doubt them. About your methods? I doubt them less with every minute."

Jaina stared at the books until the titles blurred.

The walls still stood where they had. Yet everything meaningful in the room had drawn closer: the boards, the shelves, the jars, the man behind her. One conversation. One table. One set of habits reflected back at her in a shape she wanted to reject without remainder and could not.

"If I had had another trained mind with me in Andorhal," she said, hearing the edge in her own voice, "the work would have gone faster."

"Would it have gone differently?"

The question came too fast.

She turned.

"Yes."

"Because that second mind would have introduced superior ethics?" He let the absurdity breathe for a heartbeat. "Or because it would have forced you to price time, risk, and consequence against your hunger to complete the pattern?"

Sylvanas rose in her mind then as friction. The way she would cut through a room by asking the one ugly useful question no scholar wanted in the air. How long. How many. Which door. Who pays if you stay another minute.

Jaina had not thought that sentence aloud once since she entered the sanctum. She had not needed to. That was the point. Nothing in here pushed back on the part of her that leaned toward completion for its own sake. Nothing in here priced the dead while the theorem was still unfolding.

She closed her eyes for a single beat.

When she opened them, Kel'Thuzad was watching with fresh interest, as if he had seen a mechanism shift inside a box whose workings pleased him.

"There," he said softly. "Now you are asking the useful question."

"No," Jaina said, and found she meant more than the word itself. "Now I am remembering the human one."

For the first time something sharper entered his face. Offense would have been easier to bear. What touched him sat nearer disappointment, though disappointment in him still looked measured.

"Human concerns are always invoked at the point where disciplined minds become inconvenient."

"Human concerns are the point."

"To governments. To priests. To frightened committees."

"To anyone who has ever buried someone they loved."

The line changed the room.

Only a little. He did not flinch. But the warmth left him for good. The seminar chill moved in.

"Love is an argument from attachment," he said. "Attachment is unstable policy."

"And your policy is to make every body in Lordaeron answerable to an intelligence it never consented to serve."

"Your outrage remains ethical rather than analytical. I understand. It is the one ground on which the old ban can still stand."

"Still stand?" Jaina stepped toward the table again. "That ban stands because once your work touches actual bodies, the category changes. You stop moving dead flesh. You assault continuity. You turn a person into an instrument without their leave."

"Only because you insist on treating continuity as sacred at the point where it becomes technically interesting."

"Technically interesting." The phrase left her tasting iron. "That is what villages are to you."

"The villages are proof of concept," Kel'Thuzad said.

There was no emphasis in it. No savoring. Just the unbearable honesty of a man who could not imagine why the plain term would need softening.

Jaina's stomach pulled hard. The board work, the chalk, the clean sequence of it all tried to stay intact in her mind and the sentence kept splitting it with images from the road: sick children in a hall, a dog too weak to bark, grain dust on a dead man's cuff.

"You think that makes you fearless," she said.

"No. It makes me accurate."

She stopped moving. The air between them held.

Accuracy. There, stripped bare, was the real obscenity. Worse than delight in cruelty. A willingness to call cruelty by a clean operational name and go on building.

"Dalaran did not lose its nerve," she said. "It found the place where your method became a civic atrocity and named it correctly."

Kel'Thuzad regarded her for a long moment. Then he nodded once, almost formal.

"That is the strongest version of the argument," he said. "Antonidas never put it so cleanly."

The mention of her teacher hit low and deep.

"Do not speak as though you knew him better than I do."

"I knew him before you had a staff to hold."

His voice did not rise. It did not need to.

"Antonidas was generous," he said. "Brilliant in a bounded way. He loved order enough to mistake it for wisdom. The Council as a whole was worse. They were willing to study only until study reached flesh. At that point they called the result impure and congratulated themselves on virtue."

Jaina wanted the line ready. Wanted the clean rebuttal. It did not come.

Because Antonidas had drawn that line. Because Jaina now understood why. Because some part of her had spent the last quarter hour proving Kel'Thuzad's underlying mathematics instead of disproving it.

"He taught you the same discipline he taught me," Kel'Thuzad said. "The difference is that I accepted where it led."

"No," Jaina said, quieter now. "The difference is that he understood the cost where you chose not to."

Kel'Thuzad studied her with an expression too calm to read as triumph.

"Perhaps," he said. "And perhaps he understood the cost only because he lacked the will to bear it."

The line should have enraged her. It did. It also lodged beside a dozen smaller failures she had spent the road north trying not to name: the Council's delay, the methodology reviews, the long careful language that arrived after the dead were already moving.

Anger at Dalaran and fear of herself struck at once and refused to separate.

She went back to the table because standing still felt too much like surrender. The chair scraped when she pulled it and the sound cracked the room at last. She sat.

Kel'Thuzad remained standing for another beat, then took his own chair again as if the shared seated posture mattered. Equals, the arrangement implied. Or near enough to tempt him.

He folded one page over another and exposed a new diagram. A wider structure. Multiple chains descending toward one marked point. The sight of it told her the chapter of the conversation had turned before he spoke.

"You came for mechanism," he said. "You have it. You came for scale as well, though you did not know that when you entered."

Jaina stared at the new diagram.

"Say what sits above you," she said.

"Not above me," Kel'Thuzad said. "Above the work."

He placed one fingertip on the uppermost mark in the chain.

"A will in Northrend," he said. "Bound. Vast. Learned in ways your libraries only gesture toward. I did not derive this design alone, Jaina. No mortal mind in Lordaeron could have derived it alone. The patronage came first. The revisions after."

The word he chose lodged as hard as the content.

"Patronage."

"What else should I call it? I was given a larger frame than Dalaran ever offered me. A mind capable of answering the questions your teachers banned at the point of discomfort. A power equal to the labor required."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the table edge.

"Its name."

He met her eyes.

"The Lich King."

The room did not change. The fungi still shone. The wards still hummed. Her body changed instead. The new name moved through the conversation backward, illuminating what had already been said. Patron. Northrend. Voice in the dark. Scholar and power meeting in a frozen place far outside any Council's jurisdiction.

"King of what," she asked.

"Of the dead, eventually." He gave the smallest shrug. "Titles arrive before they are fully earned. Mortal rulers are not unique in that."

Jaina did not smile.

"You say that as if I should find it amusing."

"I say it because accuracy does not forbid proportion."

She looked down at the diagram again. One local plague. One necromantic academy. One continent already too slow. Behind them, farther north, a named intelligence feeding revisions into the work. The answer she had come for kept opening into larger rooms.

"The plague is logistics," she said, more to herself than to him.

"Yes."

"Preparation."

"Yes."

"For an invasion."

That earned another degree of warmth. "You are quick when you stop arguing for the room."

She hated how much she understood the chain before he confirmed it.

"The Scourge is preparation," he said. "The force above it is older and far less patient."

He did not wait this time for her to ask the name. He gave it the way a man gives coordinates to someone who finally deserves the map.

"The Burning Legion."

Jaina sat very still.

Two names now. Two shocks. The second did not strike like the first. It widened. The floor under the whole war dropped away and showed depth where she had been reading only spread. Plague villages. Grain roads. Scholomance. And beyond them something using all of it as groundwork.

"You expect me to accept that on your word."

"You have accepted harder things on my word in the last hour."

She looked up sharply.

"Do not become careless enough to mistake me for your only source of truth," he said. "I offer you scale because scale is already bearing down on your province. Whether your institutions can digest the names in time is another matter."

The cruelty of it lay in the fact that he was probably right there too. Antonidas would hear the name Lich King and understand too much. The Council would hear it and convene. The second name would do even worse to them. Burning Legion. Too large, too distant, too impossible-sounding for any chamber built on procedure.

"How much of this design is yours?" she asked.

"Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only exact one available. I built the mortal apparatus. Grain. Cult. Instruction. Deployment. I refined the host work because mortal scholarship is still useful when properly guided. Some edges belong to higher authorship. Side effects. Certain convergences in the field. The line between patronage and ownership interests only bureaucrats."

Jaina thought of all the villages that had already died inside that line.

"It interests anyone trying to stop you."

"Then stop the machine you can touch. Leave the cosmology for later."

He said it almost kindly. Advice from enemy to investigator. The worst part was that it might even be sound advice.

She pushed back from the table and crossed once more to the shelves, unable to stay still while the room widened around her. The fungi in the niches cast green bars along the floor. She paced through them and stopped at the far wall where the stone carried a colder thrum than the rest. Ward lines. Old ones. Stronger. Beneath them the whole sanctum felt less like a buried office than a chamber built inside a larger instrument.

"Why tell me any of this?" she asked.

"Because you came this far."

"That is vanity."

"Partly." He did not deny it. "Mostly it is economy. There is no point withholding names once the pressure has already reached the threshold where names become unavoidable."

Jaina turned back.

"And if I carry them out?"

"You will carry out a more accurate understanding of your defeat."

The answer should have sounded triumphant. It didn't. Only settled.

He reached for the final folio on the desk and opened it with care that bordered on reverence. The diagram inside was smaller than the rest. Tighter. Personal. Its lines nested toward a single center and held there without dispersal.

"There remains one distinction you have not fully accepted," he said.

Jaina came back to the desk in spite of herself.

"You mean the difference between your villagers and your own plans."

"Yes."

He touched the central mark.

"The village dead are rough continuities. Useful enough for the field. The self remains only in outline. For them, recurrence is low and cheap."

He said recurrence, and the word landed in her stomach with a colder force than resurrection would have. Plainer. More worked-through.

"For you," she said.

"For me, the preserving structure is individualized. Costly. Deliberate. Designed to hold memory, intellect, priority, will."

Jaina looked at the page and understood before she wanted to.

He had already priced his own death.

As transition inside a continuity he trusted. Sacrifice and risk had nothing to do with it.

"You think this solves mortality."

"It solves enough of it."

"Enough," she repeated, and heard how thin the word sounded against the certainty in his voice.

"You still frame this as faith," he said. "It is not. I have no interest in crude village survival for myself. The village choir proved what it needed to prove. The finer question was always whether the self could be carried intact through a prepared threshold."

"And you intend to be your own proof."

"Every serious scholar should prefer first-hand verification where possible."

Jaina's mouth went dry.

The line was monstrous. It was also delivered with the same cool professional mildness another archmage might use to defend experimental controls. She stared at him and saw, all at once, the full shape of the thing that made him worse than a zealot. Zealots burned hot enough to break their own language. Kel'Thuzad had arranged his future corpse with the serenity of a man packing for travel.

"You made villages into rehearsal for your private continuation."

"The villages proved one branch of the theorem. My own work proves another. Do not collapse scales that differ in purpose and requirement merely because outrage finds compression convenient."

Jaina should have answered. Instead she looked back down at the folio and the understanding came over her in one clean, intolerable sweep.

Carrier. Host. Answering chamber. Low recurrence. High continuity. Pattern surviving death by degree rather than category.

There it was.

The beauty of the structure.

Formal beauty, never moral. The kind that lived in a proof whose parts locked so cleanly the mind wanted to admire the fit before judgment could arrive and strike it apart.

For one heartbeat she followed it all the way through.

The body taught to hold its ending. The threshold approached and crossed. The self not extinguished, only transferred along a prepared line. The gradient between a shuffling village dead and a preserved intelligence narrowing from impossibility to method. One heartbeat. No longer.

The ward under the floor rippled.

Jaina's hands flattened on the desk.

She felt Kel'Thuzad notice the exact instant the movement in her face changed, because of course he would notice. He had been waiting for comprehension since she entered the room. He had his proof now.

"Yes," he said quietly.

The word broke the spell of it.

Jaina jerked back from the desk so hard the chair struck the stone behind her. Air hit her lungs too fast. The room swam once, sharply, then steadied.

"Do not," she said.

Kel'Thuzad did not move.

"I said no such thing."

"You did not need to."

Her voice sounded wrong in her own ears. Thin where she wanted steel.

"Understanding is not surrender," he said.

"No," Jaina said, and forced breath into a shape that held. "But it is enough to wound."

That, at least, felt like the truth of it.

She understood the structure. She hated the structure. The hatred did not erase the understanding. Nothing would erase it now. The theorem had crossed the line into her and would stay there with everything else she had learned on this road that could never be made harmless again.

The sanctum's banality became unbearable.

The tea cup still sat cold by his elbow. The ink still shone wet at the edge of one unfinished note. The chair under her hand remained only chair, wood polished by years of use. The room did not register her recoil. It had held too many explanations like this already.

Jaina made herself sit because her legs were unreliable for a moment and she refused to let him see more than he already had.

He watched her, not kindly, not cruelly. Evaluatively. The way Antonidas watched when a lesson bit deeper than expected and he was deciding whether to speak or leave the student alone with it.

That resemblance hurt almost more than the rest.

"You said Antonidas lost his nerve," she said.

"I said he stopped where the work offended the room."

"He stopped where it touched bodies."

"As though bodies were not always where the work was heading."

"They were people."

"Yes." This time the answer came with unmistakable impatience, brief and real. "That is exactly what made the question worth asking."

Jaina stared.

The impatience vanished so quickly she might have imagined it, if her own nerves were not flayed open enough to trust the reading. There. At last. A place where his calm had to work to cover appetite. The appetite was for the question itself. He had wanted to know what would happen to persons, and the wanting had overruled every claim of decency before the first experiment ever ended.

"That is the line," she said.

"That is one line."

"It is the only one that matters."

Kel'Thuzad looked at her for a long time. When he spoke again his voice had lost the last warmth entirely.

"It matters to you because someone remained in your life long enough to make it matter."

The words hit low because some part of them rang against the absence she had only just named.

"You have a partner," he said. "A friction your mind did not produce. I had a Council. They preferred order to inquiry. The outcomes differ."

Jaina did not answer. She could not. The thought of Sylvanas belonged in this room only as lack, and suddenly lack filled it. Every place the conversation had been permitted to go. Every inference she had supplied a beat too quickly. Every moment no one had interrupted to ask what the elegant answer would do in a village with actual ovens and actual dead.

Kel'Thuzad mistook the silence for opportunity.

"Do not romanticize your distinction from me," he said. "Understand it precisely. Intelligence is morally inert. Method is morally inert. What governs them is relation. Attachment. Context. You still possess those constraints. I chose a different economy."

Jaina sat with that because refusing too quickly would have felt like cowardice. Acceptance never entered it.

He had named the danger cleanly in one respect at least. The mind that followed pattern had no built-in mercy. Mercy came from elsewhere or not at all.

And if it came from elsewhere, then the lie she had been living on since Dalaran cracked a little wider.

Being right alone had never been enough. Worse. Being right alone had always carried this shadow inside it.

Neither of them spoke for a while.

The sanctum settled around the silence. Wards cycling under stone. Water sounding somewhere beyond the wall, deep in the lower crypt. The faint scratch of fungus-light along the spines of books. Jaina became aware of a stiffness in her shoulders, of how hard she had been holding them in place for the last hour or more. The lamp on Kel'Thuzad's desk had burned lower. A skin had formed over the cold tea. Chalk dust marked the heel of one hand where she had planted it on the board and forgotten it there.

She had the answer now.

The complete mechanism. The body as answering chamber. The distinction between crude recurrence and preserved selfhood. The reason Dalaran had banned the discipline without ever disproving its inner logic. The names above the plague. All of it sat in her head at once, too large and too sharp.

Kel'Thuzad seemed satisfied by that alone.

He had wanted understanding alone. He had it. The lack of triumph in him made the thing uglier. This had never been a theatrical contest he hoped to win. It had been a lesson, and he had judged it successful.

Jaina looked at the unfinished notes near his elbow.

Her own copied pages from the farmhouse lay there too, where he had set them beside his work as if the hand that made them belonged naturally in the same stack. Oilcloth edges. Her cramped field script. The sight of them in this room made something inside her recoil harder than any specimen jar had.

"You kept those."

Kel'Thuzad followed her gaze. "They were worth keeping."

The compliment landed like contamination.

Jaina sat back in the slow aftershock that comes when the body realizes the blow has already landed and no further brace will help. She rested one hand flat against the table to feel something plain and built and real. Wood. Grain. Dust at the seam.

"If I leave here with this," she said, "and speak it exactly, no one in Dalaran will know what to do with the truth."

"That is very likely."

"Antonidas might."

"Antonidas will understand it. Understanding and action have never been identical."

No argument there either. Just another neat cut laid exactly where it belonged.

She thought of the sending stone going thin and blue with distance. Thought of Antonidas's voice stripped of everything but urgency. Thought of the Council compressing horror into procedure. Thought of the roads south where grain still moved whether any chamber believed in the names attached to it or not.

Then, because the thought had nowhere else left to go, she thought of Sylvanas.

The interruption. The drag on her sleeve at the exact wrong brilliant moment. The question asked from the door while Jaina still had the proof half-open in her hands. How long. How many. What does it do outside this room. The partnership had never been comfort. It had been correction. An abrasive one. Field reality scraping the scholar's blind spots raw before they could harden into habit.

Understanding the mechanism had cost her enough. Understanding that cost almost as much.

She pulled her hand off the table and wiped the chalk from her fingers onto her sleeve. White against blue.

Kel'Thuzad's head turned before hers did.

It was a small motion. Barely there. Attention lifting, not alarmed, simply redirected.

Jaina felt the wards a beat later.

The floor answered first, before the boards or shelves. A pulse. Then another, farther out, taking the sanctum's old protections in sequence as something moved through the upper levels with relentless exactness. No fumbling at locked geometry. No blundering magical pressure. Each threshold answered, shivered, passed the disturbance downward.

Kel'Thuzad stood.

"Interesting," he said.

Jaina was already on her feet.

The next ripple came clearer. Stair. Landing. Stair again. The building registering a descent that wasted nothing. No false starts. No panicked burst of speed where a turn narrowed. Whoever was coming knew how to move through hostile ground without letting the ground choose the pace.

Kel'Thuzad looked not at the door but at her.

She understood why a moment later. The change had shown on her face before she could master it.

"Someone expected?" he asked.

Jaina did not answer.

Another pulse. Closer now. The wards she had half-felt on the way down answering in layered sequence to a body driving through them with tactical precision. A pause at one choke point. Assessment. Then movement again, cleaner than before.

Kel'Thuzad's eyes narrowed in sharpened curiosity. "You know the approach."

She did.

She knew it by the shape pressure took when it crossed a defended space and priced every angle on the way through. No wasted motion. No magical show. The building itself was telling her what kind of mind was in it now.

Above them, something heavy struck stone. Then silence. Then the next ward answered.

Relief hit so hard it almost buckled her knees. It came braided with dread at once, because whatever entered this room next would not enter the conversation she had actually had. It would enter the aftermath of it. The wound of comprehension, the chair knocked back, the chalk on her hands, Kel'Thuzad alive and calm and wholly in possession of the scene he had built.

Too late to stop the moment.

Early enough to see all of it and read it wrong.

Kel'Thuzad took one step toward the door and stopped. He was listening now in earnest.

"Remarkable," he said.

Jaina heard the next ripple and knew.

Sylvanas.
