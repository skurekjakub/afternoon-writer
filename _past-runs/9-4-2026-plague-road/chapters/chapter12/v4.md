# Chapter 12: The Necromancer

Kel'Thuzad's sanctum was an office at the bottom of a grave.

Jaina had expected vulgarity. A throne. Black candles. Something obvious to brace against. The room gave her shelves instead, crowded with folios and labeled jars, a broad desk under chalk-filmed slate, green fungi washing glass and brass in drowned light. The air was cold, dry, salted against decay. Under that sat the sweeter smell that had followed her down through Scholomance.

Antonidas's hand was on every surface.

His habits, rather. Margin stars. Cross-braced proofs. The little hooked correction mark he used when a student reached the right answer by an ugly route. Jaina had spent half her life arranging thoughts for that red pen. Here those marks covered the deepest room in Scholomance, written by the man who had taken the same lessons and kept going.

At the threshold, Jandice stood with one hand still on the door. She had gone proud and careful again, all her delighted spillage packed back behind a noblewoman's posture. The excitement still showed in the angle of her chin.

Kel'Thuzad turned a page, set a paperweight on it, and raised his head.

Alive. Human. Grey-haired, gaunt, scholar-thin. His robe still carried the bones of Kirin Tor cut under darker cloth. In another room he might have been any senior archmage at work.

"Mistress Barov," he said, "you have done enough."

Jandice's fingers tightened on the latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore followed the upper sequence farther than I expected. She caught the false rings almost as soon as--"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad said, looking at Jaina at last. Pale eyes. Steady. Interested. "I can see that."

Warmth touched Jandice's face and vanished. She bowed. "Of course."

Jaina watched the bow more than the words. Respect in it, pride too, and a quick flash of disappointment at being sent out. Jandice looked like a student dismissed from a seminar she wanted to stay in.

The door shut with a thick stone sound. The wards under the floor hummed once, almost below hearing.

Kel'Thuzad folded his hands on the desk. "You have come a long way for the missing step."

Jaina stayed where Jandice had left her, seated across from him at the long table that cut the room in two. A stick of chalk lay near her right hand. White dust marked her thumb.

"I came because the body was doing work my field reads could not finish," she said. "Your grain carries the strongest signature because you want it to. Every site answered there first. Every site stalled there too."

"Good." A single word, plain as breath. "Then begin there."

He rose and crossed to the nearest board. Chalk clicked once against slate.

"The grain is only the carrier," he said.

He drew a circle, then a second around it. Outer shell. False ring. No need to write the words. The pattern made them obvious.

"You know that much," Jaina said.

"You know more than that much. You know the treated grain survives storage, travel, even baking, because the preserving work is simple and brutal. You know repeated ingestion matters more than any single meal. You know the host is altered before death. What you lacked was the final relation."

Jaina went still. Host. Altered. He used the words like furniture.

Kel'Thuzad wrote a second chain below the first, deeper and tighter, each mark tucked inside the next.

"The outer shell teaches nothing," he said. "It only arrives intact. The lesson begins in the body."

The chalk rasped softly. Prepared tissue. Repeated exposure. Pattern retention. He said it all in the language of wards and bindings and prepared mediums, and Jaina could follow every step.

"Not poison," she said.

"No. Poison spends itself."

She answered before she had decided to. "This settles. Waits. The body carries it farther than the bread can."

His mouth changed. Almost a smile.

"Exactly."

Heat climbed the back of her neck. She hated the flicker of pleasure his approval drew from her. Jandice had been fast. Kel'Thuzad was faster.

He tapped the deeper chain with the chalk. "Your field work at the farmhouse was close. The body does not merely suffer contamination. It is taught by it. Repetition lays down an answerable shape. Death does not interrupt the sequence. Death releases it."

Jaina looked at the board, then at the specimen jars on the shelf beyond him. A child's hand in one. Two preserved eyes in another.

"The body becomes the answering chamber," she said quietly.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head.

The phrase completed too much at once. The villages. The gut-first spread. Why the strongest readings had never yielded the whole thing.

She stood and crossed to the board. Cold had settled into her knees during the descent. She took the chalk from the ledge and added a branching line under his.

"The first waking is crude," she said. "Because the body has been taught enough to answer, not enough to remain itself."

"Precisely."

She drew another branch, narrower.

"A village fed together dies together," Kel'Thuzad said behind her. "A village that dies together does not rise as a pile of accidents. It rises in relation. Appetite. Direction. Response. Enough to muster. Enough to become useful under a competent hand."

Jaina wrote nothing. The board was already too full. Her hand stayed raised anyway.

"The acolytes in the villages," she said. "They do not create that from nothing."

"No."

"They catch it."

"And harden it."

She set the chalk down with great care. White dust clung to her fingertips.

"Then the dead in those villages never had a chance," she said.

Kel'Thuzad turned the chalk in his fingers. "The villages had many chances. They baked them."

The flatness of the answer struck harder than outrage. She turned back to him.

"You speak as if routine were consent."

"I speak as if routine is the strongest force available to any large design. You of all people should respect repetition."

He set the chalk aside and moved back to the desk. The shift from board to table felt deliberate, the way a lecture changes shape when the proof is done and the argument begins.

"There is still a difference," she said. "What rises in a village and what you are doing here."

"At last."

He drew one folio nearer and opened it. Notes. Diagrams. A lattice of circles bound around a single central mark. Finer work than the village mechanisms. Costlier. Individualized.

"Your village dead preserve function," he said. "Motion. Appetite. Rough answerability. Their pattern survives in outline because outline is enough for war. The finer work requires finer structure."

Jaina leaned over the page despite herself. Ink lines. Layered binding. Different words, same concepts underneath.

"This is what the villages do not receive," she said.

"This is what the villages cannot afford."

The distinction opened under her feet.

Low village dead. The rough answerables. And above them, a second aim.

"Preserved selfhood," she said.

Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened with pleasure. "There. Yes."

She stepped back from the desk.

"That is what you want for yourself."

"Of course."

Mild surprise that the point would need saying aloud.

"You built a kingdom-scale slaughter to prove a private theorem."

"No." He sat and steepled his fingers. "I built a kingdom-scale apparatus because the theorem was true whether anyone liked it or not. My private interest lies in carrying that truth farther than your teachers permitted."

Jaina stared at him.

"Dalaran banned necromancy," she said.

"Yes."

"Because it destroys the line between a body and a person."

"Yes."

"Because it collapses consent."

"Yes."

"Because it turns every household, every healer, every grave into a point of violation."

Approval warmed his face again. "Much better. You have stopped objecting at the level of taste."

Anger hit clean and fast.

"Do not mistake clarity for agreement."

"I have never required agreement from a serious mind."

He said it gently. That was the obscenity.

Jaina put both hands on the table. Cold wood. Smooth from use.

"The fact that the theorem holds," she said, "does not absolve what it does."

"Absolve?" he repeated. "No. That is a priest's word. The work needs no absolution. It needs to be named."

She wanted to strike him. The thought flashed up whole and stupid.

"Naming it is not enough when the method consumes whole villages."

"And prohibition is not enough when the method remains sound."

"Your Council never disproved necromancy," he said. "It forbade it. There is a difference. They found the line where valid method touched living society and became intolerable to them. Persons. Consent. Continuity of self. Civic trust. They were correct to fear that line. Fear, however, is not refutation."

Jaina opened her mouth. Nothing useful arrived.

Because he was right there too, in the shape of the argument. Dalaran had not called it nonsense. Dalaran had called it forbidden.

Kel'Thuzad saw the moment land and did not press. That restraint frightened her more than any sermon could have.

"That is your answer?" she said. "That because the logic survives the ban, the ban can be ignored?"

"No. My answer is that the ban names the true cost more honestly than the scholars who still invoke it as if it proved magical incoherence."

He rose once more, took down a bound volume, and set it in front of her with both hands.

"Read the old Dalaran debates, if you like," he said. "They are pious on the subject of danger and disarmingly frank on the subject of what works."

Jaina did not touch the book.

"You think this makes you brave."

"No," Kel'Thuzad said. "Bravery is for men about to face the unknown. I did something certain and accepted the result."

"The predictable result," Jaina said, "is famine, plague, and mothers dragged out of their own houses by what used to be their sons."

"Yes."

"You say that as if the word were sufficient."

"It is sufficient to name what occurred."

"Naming it is bloodless."

"Naming it is honest."

She laughed once.

"You built an atrocity and then taught yourself to say it cleanly."

Kel'Thuzad tipped his head a fraction. "And your response has been what, exactly? You crossed borders without sanction, pursued a plague no one had authorized you to pursue, and followed your private analysis into this room because you were certain the answer waited below. Shall I call that clean?"

"Do not compare us."

"I have no need to force the comparison. You made it on your own the moment you entered."

Jaina's hands curled at her sides. Chalk dust marked one cuff. The last weeks pressed in her bones: roads, village rooms, grain dust, Sylvanas's blood on her own fingers at the ridge. She dragged the argument back onto ground she could stand on.

"I crossed a border to stop this."

"Yes."

"You crossed one to spread it."

"Direction matters. I have never denied that."

He said it so easily that her anger stumbled.

"Then what are you claiming?"

"Only this." He touched the desk once with two fingers, like a lecturer marking the true line of a problem. "We were both made by the same institution. We were both taught to follow pattern where it led. We both found that the world outside our study chambers moved too slowly for the work at hand. You call your trespass duty because the object of your conviction flatters your ethics. I call mine scholarship because it flatters my honesty. The underlying habit is the same."

Jaina went cold.

"No."

"No?" His voice stayed mild. "You left a note and rode north alone because you trusted your reading over procedure. Admirable. So did I, once."

"I did not build a machine for stripping personhood out of a kingdom."

"No. You are earlier in the chain."

Jaina turned away from him before she could stop herself. The nearest shelf carried labeled boxes in a careful hand, each marked by subject and date. One had Antonidas's old sigil for translational instability scratched into the corner and crossed out later, replaced by a cleaner notation she recognized as Kel'Thuzad's own. Same schooling. Same gesture corrected two different ways.

She put her palm against the shelf. Dry wood. Cool. Solid.

"You are wrong."

"About your intentions? I do not doubt them. About your methods? I doubt them less with every minute."

Jaina stared at the books until the titles blurred.

"If I had had another trained mind with me in Andorhal," she said, hearing the edge in her own voice, "the work would have gone faster."

"Would it have gone differently?"

The question came too fast.

She turned.

"Yes."

"Because that second mind would have introduced superior ethics?" He let the absurdity breathe for a heartbeat. "Or because it would have forced you to price time, risk, and consequence against your hunger to complete the pattern?"

The questions arrived in a voice that was not in the room. How long. How many. Which door. Who pays if you stay another minute. Jaina had not asked any of them here. Nothing in this sanctum priced the dead while the theorem was still unfolding.

She closed her eyes for a single beat.

When she opened them, Kel'Thuzad was watching with fresh interest.

"There," he said softly. "Now you are asking the useful question."

"No," Jaina said, and found she meant more than the word itself. "Now I am remembering the human one."

For the first time a sharper look entered his face. Not offense. Something nearer disappointment.

"Human concerns are always invoked at the point where disciplined minds become inconvenient."

"Human concerns are the point."

"To governments. To priests. To frightened committees."

"To anyone who has ever buried someone they loved."

That reached him. The last warmth went out of him.

"Love is an argument from attachment," he said. "Attachment makes poor policy."

"And your policy is to make every body in Lordaeron answerable to an intelligence it never consented to serve."

"Your outrage remains ethical rather than analytical. I understand. It is the one ground on which the old ban can still stand."

"Still stand?" Jaina stepped toward the table again. "That ban stands because once your work touches actual bodies, the category changes. You stop moving dead flesh. You assault the self. You turn a person into an instrument without their leave."

"Only because you insist on treating the self as sacred at the point where it becomes worth studying."

"Worth studying." The phrase left iron in her mouth. "That is what villages are to you."

"The villages are proof," Kel'Thuzad said.

Jaina's stomach pulled hard. The clean sequence on the board kept splitting under images from the road: sick children in a hall, a dog too weak to bark, grain dust on a dead man's cuff.

"You think that makes you fearless," she said.

"No. It makes me honest."

A willingness to call cruelty by a clean name and keep building.

"Dalaran did not lose its nerve," she said. "It found the place where your method became a civic atrocity and named it correctly."

Kel'Thuzad tapped one finger on the folio. Then he nodded once, almost formal.

"That is the strongest version of the argument," he said. "Antonidas never put it so cleanly."

"Do not speak as though you knew him better than I do."

"I knew him before you had a staff to hold."

"Antonidas was generous," he said. "Brilliant in a bounded way. He loved order enough to mistake it for wisdom. The Council as a whole was worse. They were willing to study only until study reached flesh. At that point they called the result impure and congratulated themselves on virtue."

Jaina wanted the line ready. Wanted the clean rebuttal. It did not come.

Because Antonidas had drawn that line. Because Jaina now understood why. Because some part of her had spent the last quarter hour proving Kel'Thuzad's underlying mathematics instead of disproving it.

"He taught you the same discipline he taught me," Kel'Thuzad said. "The difference is that I accepted where it led."

"No," Jaina said, quieter now. "The difference is that he understood the cost where you chose not to."

Kel'Thuzad studied her with an expression too calm to read as triumph.

"Perhaps," he said. "And perhaps he understood the cost only because he lacked the will to bear it."

The line should have enraged her. It did. It also lodged beside the Council's delays and their long careful language arriving after the dead were already moving.

She went back to the table because standing still felt too much like surrender. The chair scraped when she pulled it. She sat.

Kel'Thuzad remained standing another beat, then took his own chair again.

He folded one page over another and exposed a new diagram. A wider structure. Multiple chains descending toward one marked point. She saw the diagram and knew the conversation had turned before he spoke.

"You came for mechanism," he said. "You have it. You came for scale as well, though you did not know that when you entered."

Jaina stared at the new diagram.

"Say what sits above you," she said.

"Not above me," Kel'Thuzad said. "Above the work."

He placed one fingertip on the uppermost mark in the chain.

"A will in Northrend," he said. "Bound. Vast. Learned in ways your libraries only gesture toward. I did not derive this design alone, Jaina. No mortal mind in Lordaeron could have derived it alone. The patronage came first. The revisions after."

The word he chose lodged as hard as the content.

"Patronage."

"What else should I call it? I was given a larger frame than Dalaran ever offered me. A mind capable of answering the questions your teachers banned at the point of discomfort. A power equal to the labor required."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the table edge.

"Its name."

He met her eyes.

"The Lich King."

Her body changed before the room did. The new name threw light backward through the whole conversation. Patron. Northrend. Voice in the dark.

"King of what," she asked.

"Of the dead, eventually." He gave the smallest shrug. "Titles arrive before they are fully earned. Mortal rulers are not unique in that."

Jaina did not smile.

"You say that as if I should find it amusing."

"I say it because proportion need not die with accuracy."

She looked down at the diagram again. One local plague. One necromantic academy. One continent already too slow. Behind them, farther north, a named intelligence feeding revisions into the work.

"The plague is logistics," she said, more to herself than to him.

"Yes."

"Preparation."

"Yes."

"For an invasion."

That earned another degree of warmth. "You are quick when you stop arguing for the room."

"The Scourge is preparation," he said. "The force above it is older and far less patient."

He did not wait this time for her to ask the name.

"The Burning Legion."

Jaina sat very still.

The second widened. Plague villages. Grain roads. Scholomance.

"You expect me to accept that on your word."

"You have accepted harder things on my word in the last hour."

She looked up sharply.

"Do not become careless enough to mistake me for your only source of truth," he said. "I offer you scale because scale is already bearing down on your province. Whether your institutions can digest the names in time is another matter."

Antonidas would hear the first name and understand too much. The Council would hear the second and slow to the crawl of classification. Either way, the roads would keep moving grain.

"How much of this design is yours?" she asked.

"Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only honest one available. I built the mortal apparatus. Grain. Cult. Instruction. Deployment. I refined the host work because mortal scholarship is still useful when properly guided. Some edges belong to higher authorship. Side effects. Certain convergences in the field. The line between patronage and ownership would interest only bureaucrats."

Jaina thought of all the villages that had already died inside that line.

"It interests anyone trying to stop you."

"Then stop the machine you can touch. Leave the cosmology for later."

He said it almost kindly. The worst part was that it might even be sound advice.

She pushed back from the table and crossed to the shelves, unable to stay still. Green bars from the fungi lay across the floor. She stopped at the far wall where the stone carried a colder thrum than the rest. Ward lines. Old ones. Stronger.

"Why tell me any of this?" she asked.

"Because you came this far."

"That is vanity."

"Partly." He did not deny it. "Mostly it is economy. There is no point withholding names once the pressure has already reached the threshold where names become unavoidable."

Jaina turned back.

"And if I carry them out?"

"You will carry out a truer understanding of your defeat."

He reached for the final folio on the desk and opened it with care that bordered on reverence. The diagram inside was smaller than the rest. Tighter. Personal. Its lines nested toward a single center and held there.

"There remains one distinction you have not fully accepted," he said.

Jaina came back to the desk in spite of herself.

"You mean the difference between your villagers and your own plans."

"Yes."

He touched the central mark.

"The village dead are rough continuities. Useful enough for the field. The self remains only in outline. For them, recurrence is low and cheap."

He said recurrence, and the word landed in her stomach with a colder force than resurrection would have. Plainer. More worked through.

"For you," she said.

"For me, the preserving structure is individualized. Costly. Deliberate. Designed to hold memory, intellect, priority, will."

Jaina looked at the page and understood before she wanted to.

He had already priced his own death.

"You think this solves mortality."

"It solves enough of it."

"Enough," she repeated, hearing how thin the word sounded against the certainty in his voice.

"You still frame this as faith," he said. "It is not. I have no interest in crude village survival for myself. The rough answerables proved what it needed to prove. The finer question was always whether the self could be carried intact through a prepared threshold."

"And you intend to be your own proof."

"Every serious scholar should prefer first-hand verification where possible."

Jaina's mouth went dry. The line was monstrous, delivered with the mildness another archmage might use to defend experimental controls. Kel'Thuzad had arranged his future corpse with the serenity of a man packing for travel.

"You made villages into rehearsal for your private continuation."

"The villages proved one branch of the theorem. My own work proves another. Do not collapse scales that differ in purpose and requirement merely because outrage finds compression convenient."

Jaina looked back down at the folio and the understanding came over her in one clean, intolerable sweep.

Carrier. Host. Answering chamber. Low recurrence. High preservation.

The beauty of the structure. Formal beauty, never moral.

For one heartbeat she followed it all the way through.

The body taught to hold its ending. The threshold approached and crossed. The self not extinguished, only transferred along a prepared line. The gradient between a shuffling village dead and a preserved intelligence narrowing from impossibility to method. One heartbeat. No longer.

The ward-line under the floor shivered with the chair's blow against stone.

Jaina's hands flattened on the desk. Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened.

"Yes," he said quietly.

Jaina jerked back from the desk so hard the chair struck stone behind her. Air hit her lungs too fast. The room swam once, sharply, then steadied.

"Do not," she said.

Kel'Thuzad did not move.

"I said no such thing."

"You did not need to."

Her voice sounded wrong in her own ears. Thin where she wanted steel.

"Understanding is not surrender," he said.

"No," Jaina said, and forced breath into a shape that held. "But it is enough to wound."

She understood the structure. She hated the structure. The hatred did not erase the understanding. Nothing would erase it now.

Cold tea sat by his elbow. Ink still shone wet at the edge of an unfinished note. Under her hand the chair was wood, polished smooth by years of use.

Jaina made herself sit because her legs were unreliable for a moment and she refused to let him see more than he already had.

He watched her the way Antonidas watched when a lesson bit deeper than expected. That resemblance hurt almost more than the rest.

"You said Antonidas lost his nerve," she said.

"I said he stopped where the work offended the room."

"He stopped where it touched bodies."

"As though bodies were not always where the work was heading."

"They were people."

"Yes." This time the answer came with unmistakable impatience, brief and real. "That is exactly what made the question worth asking."

Jaina stared. There. At last. His composure slipped and showed appetite beneath. She could see, at last, how badly he had wanted that question itself.

"That is the line," she said.

"That is one line."

"It is the only one that matters."

Kel'Thuzad turned one page corner between his fingers. When he spoke again his voice had lost the last warmth entirely.

"You have a partner," he said. "A friction your mind did not produce. I had a Council. They preferred order to inquiry. The outcomes differ."

Jaina did not answer. The silence showed her the same absence more clearly than any argument had: no one here had cut through elegance to ask what it did in a village with ovens and actual dead.

Kel'Thuzad mistook the silence for opportunity.

"Do not romanticize your distinction from me," he said. "Understand it precisely. Intelligence answers whatever hand directs it. Method does the same. What governs either is relation. Attachment. The bonds that still pull at you. I cut mine elsewhere."

Jaina sat with that because refusing too quickly would have felt like cowardice. Acceptance never entered it. Her mind ran toward pattern by default. Mercy required someone else in the room. And if mercy came from elsewhere, then the lie she had been living on since Dalaran cracked a little wider.

Neither of them spoke. Wards cycled under stone. Beyond the wall, water. On Kel'Thuzad's desk the lamp had burned lower, a skin forming over the cold tea.

The complete mechanism. The distinction between crude recurrence and preserved selfhood. The names above the plague. Kel'Thuzad settled back, satisfied by that alone.

Jaina looked at the unfinished notes near his elbow.

Her own copied pages from the farmhouse lay there too, where he had set them beside his work as if the hand that made them belonged naturally in the same stack. Oilcloth edges. Her cramped field script. The sight of them in this room made her recoil harder than any specimen jar had.

"You kept those."

Kel'Thuzad followed her gaze. "They were worth keeping."

The compliment fouled the air.

Jaina sat back and rested one hand flat against the table. Wood. Grain. Dust at the seam.

"If I leave here with this," she said, "and speak it exactly, no one in Dalaran will know what to do with the truth."

"That is very likely."

"Antonidas might."

"Antonidas will understand it. Understanding and action have never been identical."

She thought of the sending stone going thin and blue with distance. Thought of Antonidas's voice stripped of everything but urgency. Thought of the Council compressing horror into procedure. Thought of the roads south where grain still moved whether any chamber believed in the names attached to it or not.

Then the thought turned, as it always did now, toward the missing correction she had left outside this room. The interruption. The drag on her sleeve at the exact wrong brilliant moment. How long. How many. What does it do outside this room. Correction, not comfort.

She pulled her hand off the table and wiped the chalk from her fingers onto her sleeve. White against blue.

Kel'Thuzad's head turned before hers did.

It was a small motion. Barely there. Attention lifting, redirected.

Jaina felt the wards a beat later.

The floor answered first, before the boards or shelves. A pulse. Then another, farther out, taking the sanctum's old protections in sequence as something moved through the upper levels with relentless exactness. No fumbling at the ward locks. No blundering magical pressure. Each threshold answered, shivered, passed the disturbance downward.

Kel'Thuzad stood.

"Interesting," he said.

Jaina was already on her feet.

The next ripple came clearer. Stair. Landing. Stair again. The wards took the descent cleanly and sent it downward. Whoever was coming knew how to move through hostile ground and kept their own pace through it.

Kel'Thuzad looked not at the door but at her.

She understood why a moment later. The change had shown on her face before she could master it.

"Someone expected?" he asked.

Jaina did not answer.

Another pulse. Closer now. The wards she had half-felt on the way down answered in layered sequence to a body driving through them with tactical precision. A pause at one choke point. Assessment. Then movement again, cleaner than before.

Kel'Thuzad's eyes narrowed in sharpened curiosity. "You know the approach."

She did.

She knew it by the shape pressure took when it crossed a defended space and priced every angle on the way through. She read the descent for what it was and knew what kind of mind had entered.

Above them, a heavy blow struck stone. Then silence. Then the next ward answered.

Relief nearly folded her knees. Dread came with it at once. Whoever entered next would not enter the conversation she had actually had. They would enter the aftermath: the chair knocked back, chalk on her hands, Kel'Thuzad alive and calm at the center of the scene.

Kel'Thuzad took one step toward the door and stopped. He was listening now in earnest.

"Remarkable," he said.

Jaina heard the next ripple and knew.

Sylvanas.
