The road south smelled of wet leather, horse sweat, and grain dust that clung to Sylvanas even out of sight of the wagons.

They had left the sick village at dusk. By dawn the horses were dark with dried sweat where the tack had rubbed, and Cyndia's mare was laying her ears back at every loaded cart. The mage rode with her hood down, hair loose from its tie, eyes on every crossing.

Counting choices.

Sylvanas had seen scouts do the same over hostile ground.

Their first stop was an inn that doubled as a weigh house where three feeder lanes met. Low beams. Smoke in the rafters. Two teamsters were still eating. The innkeeper was pretending not to resent four armed riders tracking mud across his floor.

"Ledgers," Sylvanas said.

He blinked. "For what?"

"For grain."

"That's not a reason."

"It is if you want me to stop there."

She did not point with the bow in her hand. He looked at it anyway, then at Jaina's staff, then at Cyndia and Lyriel taking opposite ends of the room.

"You're from Silvermoon."

"You noticed."

He produced the ledgers.

The work was uglier than any battlefield map. Weights, dates, mill marks, room charges beside wagon crews, little slashes showing sacks spoiled or shorted. Sylvanas read the road the way she read a patrol report: who arrived heavy, who left light, which teams slept inside, which pushed on in bad weather and had been paid enough to make the risk worth it.

Jaina built her own picture beside hers. Not the same picture. That made it useful.

"These two villages," Jaina said, touching the margin of one page. "Leave them."

Sylvanas did not look up. "Why?"

"Their deliveries are too recent."

"Recent means closer."

"Recent means there hasn't been time yet." Jaina turned back two pages. "If the sickness worsens by repeated eating, then the cleaner read is older stock, not nearer stock. This road. Then this one."

North-west first, not south.

"You were sure of the other road yesterday."

"Yesterday I had one village. Today I have three inns, one tally, and the knowledge that your kingdom writes everything down if you charge enough copper for it."

Sylvanas shut the ledger and pushed it back. "West fork first, then."

The innkeeper frowned. "That's not the direct road."

"Good."

Outside, Lyriel had already loosened girths a notch. Cyndia studied the yard, eyes on wheel ruts hardened in last night's damp.

"West first?" Cyndia asked.

"The mage wants older stock."

Cyndia nodded once. "The heavier teams turned that way. More wear in the outer rut."

"Good."

They rode.

The west fork gave them a baker's wife offended to be questioned, a clerk who kept answering with prices instead of dates, and a square where no one yet knew enough to be afraid. Chickens under carts. Children with flour on their sleeves. An old man coughing into a rag and tucking it away.

Jaina touched a sack seam in the storehouse and drew her hand back with that still look she got when a thought hardened.

"Lighter here," she said. "Not clean. Just lighter."

"How much lighter?"

"Enough that if we stay, we lose time. The next stop matters more."

The clerk heard only that he was not the center of catastrophe and tried to swell back into himself.

"Our main supplier comes through the south road exchange twice monthly—"

"Names, then," Sylvanas said.

"I gave them."

"You gave me what you call them. I want the marks on the wagons, the men who sign, and which team asks for locked storage."

He hesitated.

Jaina had already moved to the far shelf, crystal lit low, reading across sack cloth and tally sticks. "The contamination sits stronger in the older stitching than in the fresh deliveries. Whoever's doing this isn't loading every stop equally. They're spreading it wide and deep at once."

The clerk's face went blank.

Sylvanas stepped closer. "Marks first."

He told her.

By noon the investigation was hooves.

Ride. Stop. Read. Ask.

The road bucked under them where wagons had cut it apart. At one yard a depot mistress with an ink-black thumb refused them the books until Sylvanas repeated the request in a ranger's tone. At another they found a half-drunk merchant who swore the flour from one route baked sweeter and kept longer, and Jaina's head snapped up.

"Which route?"

"Southwest," he said. "By the Andorhal road, mostly. Best grain in the region until lately."

"Until lately how?"

He spread his hands. "More people sick after. Not enough to ruin trade. Enough to make wives complain."

Jaina was already shaking her head. "Not wives. Households. Track the households."

Sylvanas looked at her.

"If the complaint starts at the table, the route isn't broad enough yet. If it starts in the big orders, we're behind it. This one matters. The next depot after this matters more."

"South or west?"

"South. We were wrong on west."

Sylvanas turned in the saddle at once. "South, then."

Lyriel wheeled her horse before the word had fully landed. Cyndia fell in on Sylvanas's left.

"Third change since dawn," Cyndia said.

"Complain later."

"Wasn't going to."

The south road ran harder and straighter. Better used. Grain wagons had bitten paired grooves in the clay, one line heavier where teams had been overburdened. The countryside around it was still carrying on with insulting competence. Laundry on lines. Boys on fence rails. A priest on a mule with mud to the knees.

At the last depot before evening they found books enough to make the scale visible.

The yard master was a broad man with a missing thumbnail and the harried look of someone accustomed to too much movement and too little praise. He had six carts loading, one unloading, a boy asleep on a flour sack, and no patience for strangers taking up floor.

"I can give you weights," he said. "I can give you routes. I can't give you miracles."

"Try routes first," Sylvanas said.

The office stank of old paper and grain heat. Shelves bowed under books. Wax had crawled down the necks of three lamps though only one was lit. Jaina spread her notes on the desk without asking leave. Sylvanas took the depot books. Andorhal stopped looking like a destination and started looking like a knot.

Same route marks. Same exchange signs. Same named carriers touching one village, then another, then five more beyond it. Some settlements had only weak sickness reports attached, enough to pass for bad flour. Others had men gone from work and too many sick customers at once.

Not one chain. A web.

Jaina laid two pages side by side. "This one repeats."

"They all repeat."

"No. This order." She tapped the entries. "The heavier deliveries go where the early sickness has time to turn worse. Not the nearest places. The most dependent ones."

"Show me."

She did. Swiftly. No simplifying now unless the books forced it. Here, this line. There, that village's dates. This gap mattered. That repetition mattered more.

Sylvanas checked the same route against the wagon tallies and found the military equivalent immediately: feed a static line long enough and the line becomes yours.

Somebody was turning food into siege work.

She looked at the map again. Villages she had never heard named. Inns where they had watered horses without thinking. Granaries. Side roads. More of them every time the records widened.

Regional. Not local.

Jaina had gone still beside the desk, one hand braced on the wood.

"It's larger than Millhaven," Jaina said.

"Yes. Larger."

"Larger than the villages we've seen."

"Larger than those too."

"If Andorhal is only the visible knot—"

"Then somebody built this to survive notice."

The yard master had been watching their faces instead of the books.

"How bad?" he asked.

Sylvanas shut the ledger. "Bad enough that if I were you, I'd remember who asked for books they had no business needing and who paid extra to move after dark."

He swallowed.

"There was another depot south of here," he said. "Closer to the main exchange road. Smaller. Mostly transfer work. They've been stripping volume through it all month."

"Name it."

He gave it.

Jaina was already gathering papers. "If they know the route is visible at all, that depot is next."

"Or already gone."

"There were."

The smaller depot sat where the south road thickened toward the main exchange, and the horses were moving on discipline more than appetite.

Something was wrong before Sylvanas put a hand on the gate.

No yard noise. No grain dust hanging where late work should have stirred it. No teamsters swearing at wheels. The place had the shape of a working depot and nothing alive enough to prove it.

"Perimeter," she said.

Cyndia peeled left at once. Lyriel right. Jaina dismounted without waiting to be told and came up the track with the staff already in hand.

The gate opened on a yard too clean to trust.

Not abandoned. Stripped.

Cart ruts everywhere, but none fresh enough to still hold shine in the clay. Swept patches where loose grain should have caught in cracks. One loading frame shoved aside and left where haste had dropped it. The warehouse doors stood open, and the dark inside looked arranged.

"They moved in a hurry," Lyriel said from the right wall. "One team left loaded. Southbound. Deep cut at the gate, still damp underneath."

"No stable hands," Cyndia said from the opposite side. "No lanterns. Tack room open. Two horses left in the back pen and nobody to claim them."

Sylvanas crossed the yard first. Jaina came in half a step behind her, staff light kept low enough not to spoil night sight. The warehouse shelves had been cleared in strips. Ledger hooks on the office wall stood empty. The floor near them had been overswept, broom lines still visible in the dust.

"There were books here," Jaina said.

"There were."

"Recently."

"Recent enough."

The office door was half shut.

Inside sat a man old enough that death ought to have looked less surprising on him than it did. Broad desk. Tall chair. Cup knocked on its side. One hand still resting on the blotter beside a pen.

His chair had been straightened after he slumped. The cup had spilled wrong for the angle. One drawer hung open an inch, then closed again. Sylvanas touched the man's sleeve. Warm still.

"Not long," she said.

Jaina moved nearer, light shifting blue-white over desk, hand, throat. Her face altered as the spell deepened — less expression, more attention.

"No visible wound," Jaina said.

"I can see that."

"Wait."

Sylvanas waited.

The light thinned until it was almost nothing. Jaina touched two fingers to the dead overseer's jaw, then the skin below the ear, then held the crystal over the mouth.

"There," she said softly.

"What is it?"

"Residue. Buried under the rest of it. Deliberate. Thin enough to pass if nobody went looking."

"Necromancy."

"Yes. Not enough to raise him. Enough to stop him cleanly and make it look ordinary."

Sylvanas looked at the body again and hated how much that helped.

Targeted. Not rot loosed in bulk. Somebody had reached into one office, one man, one moment, and closed a mouth before it opened.

"He held the books," she said.

"Yes."

"And lost them."

"Or gave them up and died for it anyway."

Sylvanas opened the drawer the inch it had already given her. Empty except for wax crumbs and one broken tally peg. The desk held smears where stacks had been dragged off too fast. In the brazier under the window, ash had been stirred, then smothered. She crouched and touched it.

Warm.

"They burned what they couldn't carry," she said.

Jaina came down beside her. "Can you get anything out of it?"

"If they were sloppy."

"They were hurried," Jaina said. "That's better."

Sylvanas tipped the brazier carefully onto the stone. Ash fanned across the floor. Beneath the grey lay heavier scraps that had not finished dying before someone killed the fire. She separated them with the point of her knife.

Route fragment. Two lines only. A warehouse mark. Half a place-name.

And a clot of wax that had fallen without fully melting. Red so dark it read black in the lamplight, one edge stamped before it tore free.

Jaina saw it when she did.

"That's not local," Jaina said.

"No."

Sylvanas turned it over on her blade tip. The sigil was partial but recognizable: formal, expensive, made for hands that paid other hands.

Barov.

Not guilt. Not yet. Only reach.

Outside, a door banged once in the night air. Lyriel's boots crossed the warehouse threshold a heartbeat later.

"Found a rear tally board," she said. "Most of it's blank. They scraped off what mattered."

"Bring it."

Lyriel set the board against the desk. Scraped, yes. But not well enough. The knife had taken the chalk, not the pressure score cut into the wood beneath repeated entries. Numbers lingered as shallow grooves. So did abbreviated route names in slant light.

Jaina angled the crystal. Sylvanas tilted the board. Between them, the erased marks began to reappear.

"Andorhal," Jaina said.

"Again."

"And this fork." She touched a set of almost-gone slashes. "South feeder. Not the east line."

"Why not the east."

"Because they stopped using it here before the scrape. This one continues."

Sylvanas looked to the yard through the open door. Southbound. Lyriel's fresh cut at the gate. The loaded team. The cleaned office. The dead overseer.

Active enemy, then. A trail being swept ahead of them by hands that knew how far behind they were.

Cyndia appeared in the doorway with a torn sheet held flat between both hands.

"Found this under the outside rail," she said. "Caught on a nail."

Paper, stiff with damp and grain dust. Torn from a larger route bundle. One edge bore the same dark wax. The center held three surviving words and part of a fourth:

...Barov holding...

...storehouse line...

...Andor...

Sylvanas read it twice anyway. Making certainty out of scraps had killed better people than this overseer.

"Barov paper, at least," she said.

"Not Barov intent," Jaina said at once.

Sylvanas glanced up. "Agreed."

"It could mean ownership. Lease. Factors moving goods under a house seal. It could mean infiltration of a route already in place."

"So far."

"It could mean guilt. It doesn't mean proven guilt."

"Write that in your own blood if you need to remember it," Sylvanas said. "I have no interest in accusing a house because somebody died with the wrong seal in his brazier."

One corner of Jaina's mouth moved. Almost a smile.

"Then we're agreed."

"On that point."

Outside, one of the pen horses whickered, sharp and lonely.

"They're reacting to us," Jaina said behind her.

"They are."

"Which means someone is still close enough to react fast."

Sylvanas looked toward the black square of the yard and the darker road beyond it. "If I were running this, I wouldn't need eyes on us all the time. Only enough men on the route to know when the wrong questions were asked."

"And if you're not?"

"Then we have a worse problem."

Jaina considered that. "I dislike your answer."

"It wasn't for your comfort."

She went back out into the yard. Night had settled fully now, leaving the depot under a thin moon and Cyndia's shuttered lamp.

"We're done here," Sylvanas said. "Take the board. Take the sheet. Leave the body for whatever human officer gets here too late to matter."

Lyriel nodded once and went for the horses.

Cyndia stayed near the rear rail, still scanning the south road. "If they cleared this much in one day, the next stop will be cleaner."

"Probably. Cleaner, at least."

"Then we stop expecting books."

"Yes. No more books."

Jaina came out of the office carrying the half-burned fragments between two ledger covers to keep them from blowing apart. Her hair had fully escaped its tie.

"One thing," she said.

"Say it."

"The residue on the overseer wasn't broad plague work. It was hidden handwork. Deliberate silencing."

"Yes."

"Whoever did it knew enough to bury the sign."

"Also yes."

She looked back once toward the office. "I would like to meet them in a room with the door shut."

Sylvanas put a boot in the stirrup. "Get in line."

They did not chase the dark road blind. Two miles east they found a coaching house with a stable for the four horses and an upper room with a bolt on the door and a table broad enough for evidence.

The innkeeper, half out of bed, took one look at their faces and stopped trying to overcharge.

The room smelled of tallow, damp wool, and old onion stew. Cyndia and Lyriel took first and second watch without discussion. By the time the bolt settled from Lyriel's last pass, Sylvanas and Jaina had already covered the table with route fragments, copied tallies, and the lamp turned low.

They worked in silence for a while.

Jaina copied fast. Not carelessly. Fast the way some swordsmen drew fast, because wasted motion offended them. She had stopped simplifying hours ago. Now she spoke into the page or the air between them and assumed Sylvanas would keep up unless proved otherwise.

"The older routes hit hardest where the villages depend on bought flour," she said, making another mark on the map. "The mixed lines don't. Home milling blunts it."

"Which means?"

"Which means the broad road isn't enough by itself. They need dependence. Habit. Regular purchase. If you want a village to sicken deep, you don't poison it once. You feed it."

"Siege work," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

There was ash under Jaina's left thumb from the office brazier. Grain dust at one cuff. Purple exhaustion under both eyes.

Sylvanas pushed the Barov wax toward the light. "House paper enters here. Not before."

"Visible house paper," Jaina corrected.

"Fine. Visible house paper enters here."

Jaina did not waste time pretending not to have more. "If Barov holdings keep turning up across the grain road, then Andorhal matters more than before. Not because the Barovs are automatically responsible. Because the road narrows there. Ownership, storehouses, transfer hands, all of it. The depots after this are paper. The weight is farther south."

"And the enemy knows we know enough to head there."

"Yes."

Sylvanas leaned over the map, one palm on the boards. Candlelight made the inked roads look like cuts. Jaina shifted her papers aside before Sylvanas's arm could crush them, not even glancing up.

Used to the motion already. Or trusting it.

"This fork," Sylvanas said. "Why keep it active and scrape the east line away."

"Because the east line is too hot now. Or too visibly sick. Or because the south road is feeding something more important." Jaina glanced at the copied tallies, then at the route scrap. "If I had to choose, they still trust the south feeder."

Sylvanas marked the feeder road with the point of her knife. "That sends us near Strahnbrad."

"Yes."

"And where Arthas is fighting."

"Also yes."

Across from her, Jaina was already revising the route list to match it.

Sylvanas tapped one copied tally with a fingernail. "This merchant note. Empty wagon. North road seller."

Jaina looked up. "You think of him now too."

Not the old man. Not the merchant. Him.

Sylvanas let her eyes rest on the page instead of on Jaina's face. "I think of anything that repeats."

"He did."

"Yes."

The memory came back harder for being incomplete. Not a face. Never the face. An empty wagon when grain ought to have weighed it down. Cheap civility. The wrong kind of ease. The faint sense that she had been offered something ordinary and let it go.

She hated having reason to keep it now.

"What do you actually have," Jaina asked, "besides irritation."

"Empty wagon. Newer trader. Price cuts. Witnesses remember his usefulness more clearly than his face." Sylvanas looked at the candle flame until the old road scene half-built itself behind it. "And a farewell."

"What farewell."

"May the stars keep your road."

Jaina's brows drew in a fraction. "Odd."

"Yes."

"Important?"

"Not yet."

Jaina nodded and went back to the map. "Keep him in mind and move on."

"Good," Sylvanas said.

They worked on.

Below them, somebody laughed in the common room and was told to shut up. Rain touched the shutters once, then thought better of it. On the table the roads narrowed toward one thing.

Sylvanas reached across for the tally sheet Jaina had just finished marking and found, a beat too late, that the name in her head had already changed.

Not the mage.

Jaina had marked this poorly.

No thunder under it. No revelation. Only the useful fact of a hand she now knew by work alone — sharper pressure in the downstrokes when Jaina was tired, neat corrections when a line had to move, the loss of softness in her script once she understood Sylvanas needed none.

Jaina looked up because Sylvanas had stopped with the page half-lifted. "What."

"Your mark's wrong," Sylvanas said, tapping the route. "This tally belongs to the south feeder, not the east branch. The scoring under the chalk cut left."

Jaina leaned in at once. Hair slid forward. Her shoulder brushed Sylvanas's forearm by accident and stayed only as long as the correction required.

"You're right," she said. No defensiveness. "I copied the scrape, not the grain cut."

After a breath: "Thank you."

Sylvanas pushed the paper back. "Then fix it."

Jaina fixed it.

The last question was the road itself.

Sylvanas laid the surviving evidence in one line — Barov wax, torn sheet, copied tally, scraped board notes, the memory of village sickness breaking worse on one road than another, the loaded team cutting south out of the cleaned depot. Jaina set her own beside it — heavier contamination on the older dependency routes, weaker spread where bought flour mattered less, the south feeder still carrying weight while the east line went quiet.

Then Cyndia knocked once and came in without waiting for permission. "Road report. Three troop strings passed south in the last watch. Lordaeron colors. Fast enough to be dispatch, not patrol. Same road also took two grain teams before moonrise. Loaded both times."

Lyriel appeared a moment later behind her. "East fork's quieter than it should be. One abandoned cart at the branch and no fresh wheel score after it. South road still carries both traffic types."

Not whim. Not convenience. Evidence, hooves, the road turning south.

Sylvanas looked at Jaina.

Jaina looked back, then at the table, then at the south line they had both already known would win if the facts kept holding.

"South," Jaina said.

"South," Sylvanas agreed.

Cyndia gave the barest nod and withdrew. Lyriel followed, already turning the choice into horseflesh and distance.

Sylvanas gathered the papers into two bundles — one for the saddle case, one for her own hand until dawn. Jaina corked the ink, flexed her cramped fingers once, and sat back stiffly, like someone whose mind had forgotten the body too long.

"Sleep if you can," Sylvanas said.

Jaina gave her a look that said no.

"You first," she said.

"I can sleep in a saddle."

"That barely counts as sleep. Only cavalry would call it that."

"And mage study by candle stub is better."

"Considerably."

She left Jaina with the map and opened the shutters a crack. The yard below was black, the horses pale where stable light caught their flanks. Beyond the coaching house the road bent south through fields silvered by moonlight and vanished into the dark country where grain wagons and human soldiers were both being drawn toward the same tomorrow.

Strahnbrad first, most likely. Then the deeper road to Andorhal.

Behind her, paper rustled. Jaina correcting one more line before surrendering the page.

By dawn the room had cooled and the candle had died in its own grease.

They went down without ceremony.

Bread, watered ale, horses saddled in the yard while mist still held low over the road. Cyndia checked girths. Lyriel tightened the second pack on Jaina's mare without being asked and moved on before thanks could become a subject. The coaching-house keeper brought out the copied route page wrapped in oilcloth because Jaina had paid him for drier ink before sunrise. Bought and therefore reliable.

Sylvanas mounted first and looked south. "Same order. South feeder. Fast until the horses tell us otherwise. If troop traffic thickens, we leave the main spine and parallel it where we can. No heroics if Lordaeron uniforms decide to be curious."

"Understood," Cyndia said.

"Yes, Ranger-General," Lyriel said.

Jaina settled into the saddle and tucked the oilcloth packet inside her cloak. "If we pass fresh grain teams, I want the sacks read before the drivers know what I'm doing."

"Then don't make it look magical."

"I never do."

Sylvanas looked at her.

Jaina looked back, as solemn as a priest at bad theatre.

"Hardly ever," she amended.

Sylvanas turned her horse onto the road. "Ride."

The quartet moved south as the mist broke around the horses' knees and the feeder road took them toward Strahnbrad, Andorhal, and whatever had started cleaning the world ahead of them before they could reach it.
