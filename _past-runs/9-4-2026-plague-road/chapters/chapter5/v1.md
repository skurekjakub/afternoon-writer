The road south smelled of wet leather, horse sweat, and grain dust that existed now for Sylvanas whether the wagons were in sight or not.

They had left the sick village at dusk. By dawn the horses were carrying the night in their flanks, dark with dried sweat where the tack had rubbed, and Cyndia's mare had started laying her ears back whenever they passed a loaded cart. The mage rode with her hood down despite the cold, hair half out of its tie, eyes on the road whenever the road crossed another road. Not looking at scenery. Counting choices.

Sylvanas had seen scouts do the same over hostile ground.

The first stop was an inn that doubled as a weigh house where three feeder lanes met. Human place. Low beams, smoke in the rafters, one wall furred with nailed-up notices about livestock, missing tools, and a boy who had gone east with a pedlar and not come back. Two teamsters were still eating. An innkeeper with sleeves rolled to the elbow was pretending not to resent four armed riders tracking mud across his floor.

"Ledgers," Sylvanas said.

He blinked at her. "For what?"

"For grain."

"That's not a reason."

"It is if you want me to stop there."

She did not point with the bow in her hand. She did not need to. He looked at it anyway, then at Jaina's staff, then at Cyndia and Lyriel taking opposite ends of the room without fuss. The innkeeper's mouth tightened.

"You're from Silvermoon."

"You noticed."

He produced the ledgers.

The work was uglier than any battlefield map because battlefields at least admitted they were trying to kill you. This was weights, dates, mill marks, room charges written beside wagon crews, little slashes showing sacks shorted or spoiled, three months of ordinary commerce hiding inside itself. Sylvanas turned pages with clean fingertips and read the road the way she read a patrol report: who arrived heavy, who departed light, which teams slept inside, which pushed on in bad weather and had been paid enough to make the risk worth it.

The mage built her own picture beside hers. Not the same picture. That was what made it useful.

"These two villages," Jaina said, touching the margin of one page. "Leave them."

Sylvanas did not look up. "Why?"

"Their deliveries are too recent."

"Recent means closer."

"Recent means there hasn't been time yet." Jaina was already turning back two pages. "If the sickness worsens by repeated eating, then the cleaner read is older stock, not nearer stock. This road. Then this one."

Sylvanas followed the line with her eyes. Different fork than the one she had chosen. North-west first, not south.

"You were sure of the other road yesterday."

"Yesterday I had one village. Today I have three inns, one tally, and the knowledge that your kingdom writes everything down if you charge enough copper for it."

Dry, despite the hour. Useful.

Sylvanas tore the relevant route names into memory, shut the ledger, and pushed it back.

"We're taking the west fork first," she said.

The innkeeper frowned. "That's not the direct road."

"Good," Sylvanas said, and stood.

Outside, the horses were tied under a lean-to roof that did not keep out much except falling leaves. Lyriel had already loosened girths a notch. Cyndia was studying the yard, eyes on wheel ruts hardened in last night's damp.

"West first?" Cyndia asked.

"The mage wants older stock."

Cyndia gave one quick nod. "The heavier teams turned that way. More wear in the outer rut."

"Good."

They rode.

The west fork gave them a village where the baker's wife looked offended to be questioned, a storehouse clerk who kept trying to answer with prices instead of dates, and a square where no one yet knew enough to be afraid. Chickens under carts. Children with flour on their sleeves. One old man coughing into a rag and then tucking it away because work still had to be done.

Jaina touched a sack seam in the back room of the storehouse and drew her hand away with the look she got when a thought sharpened under it.

"Lighter here," she said. "Not clean. Just lighter."

"How much lighter?"

"Enough that if we stay, we lose time. The next stop matters more."

The clerk, hearing only that he was not presently the center of catastrophe, tried to swell back into himself. "I've already told you, our main supplier comes through the south road exchange twice monthly—"

"Names," Sylvanas said.

"I gave them."

"You gave me what you call them. I want the marks on the wagons, the men who sign, and which team asks for locked storage."

He hesitated.

Jaina had moved to the far shelf, crystal lit low, reading across sack cloth, tally sticks, the scales themselves. She did not speak for the clerk's benefit. "The contamination sits stronger in the older stitching than in the fresh deliveries. Whoever's doing this isn't loading every stop equally. They're seeding breadth and depth together."

The clerk's face went blank in the way human faces often did when they understood none of the words but all of the danger.

Sylvanas stepped closer. "Marks."

He told her.

By noon the investigation had turned into a thing with hooves.

Ride. Stop. Read. Ask. Ride again.

The road bucked under them where wagons had cut it apart. At one yard a depot mistress with an ink-black thumb refused them the books until Sylvanas repeated the request in the same tone she might have used for a ranger who had failed to string a bow. At another they found a half-drunk merchant who swore the flour from one route baked sweeter and kept longer, and Jaina's head snapped up so fast Sylvanas changed the next question before she'd finished hearing the answer.

"Which route?"

"Southwest," he said. "By the Andorhal road, mostly. Best grain in the region until lately."

"Until lately how?"

The merchant spread his hands. "More people sick after. Not enough to ruin trade. Enough to make wives complain."

Jaina was already shaking her head.

"Not wives," she said. "Households. Track the households."

Sylvanas looked at her.

"If the complaint starts at the table, the route isn't broad enough yet. If it starts in bulk buyers, we're behind it." Jaina tucked stray hair behind one ear with the back of her wrist, leaving a line of dust at the temple. "This one matters. The next depot after this matters more."

"South or west?"

"South. We were wrong on west."

No apology in it. No vanity either. Just the answer changing shape under new weight.

Sylvanas turned in the saddle at once.

"South," she said.

Lyriel was nearest enough to hear the change in real time. She wheeled her horse before the sentence had fully landed and was already checking the road mouth by the time Cyndia fell in on Sylvanas's left.

"Third change since dawn," Cyndia said.

"Complain later."

"Wasn't going to."

The south road ran harder and straighter. Better used. Grain wagons had bitten it deep enough to leave paired grooves in the clay, one line riding heavier than the other where teams had been overburdened. The countryside around it was still carrying on with insulting competence. Women hanging laundry. Boys running fence lines. A priest on a mule with mud clear to the knees. Every village ordinary until it wasn't. That was beginning to feel like the point.

At the last depot before evening they found books enough to make the scale finally visible.

The yard master was a broad man with a missing thumbnail and the harried look of someone accustomed to too much movement and too little praise. He had six carts loading, one unloading, a boy asleep on a flour sack in the shade, and no patience for four strangers taking up floor.

"I can give you weights," he said. "I can give you routes. I can't give you miracles."

"Try routes first," Sylvanas said.

The office stank of old paper and grain heat. Shelves bowed under books. Wax dripped down the necks of three lamps though only one was lit. Jaina spread her own notes on the desk without asking leave. Sylvanas took the depot books. Together, they made Andorhal start to look less like a destination and more like a knot.

Same route marks. Same exchange signs. Same named carriers touching one village, then another, then five more beyond it. Some settlements had only weak sickness reports attached to them, enough to be filed as bad flour or stomach complaint. Others had men gone from work and bakeries complaining of too many sick customers at once. Not one chain. A web.

Jaina laid two pages side by side and traced them with one finger.

"This one repeats."

"They all repeat."

"No. This order." She tapped the entries. "The heavier deliveries go where the earlier sickness later worsens. Not the nearest places. The most dependent ones."

"Show me."

She did. Swiftly. Not translating downward anymore unless the books forced her to. Here, this line. There, that village's dates. This gap mattered. That repetition mattered more. Sylvanas followed, then checked the same route against the wagon tallies and found the military equivalent immediately: feed a static line long enough and the line becomes yours.

Somebody was turning food into siege work.

The understanding landed ugly and clean.

She looked at the map again. Villages she had never heard named. Inns where they had watered horses without thinking. Granaries. Side roads. More of them every time the records widened.

Regional. Not local.

Jaina had gone still beside the desk, one hand braced on the wood. That was her tell, Sylvanas was learning. Not when she was uncertain. When the pattern clicked hard enough to make motion unnecessary.

"It's larger than Millhaven," Jaina said.

"Yes."

"Larger than the villages we've seen."

"Yes."

"If Andorhal is only the visible knot—"

"Then somebody built this to survive notice."

The yard master had been watching their faces instead of the books, which was sensible. Records meant nothing to most people until a mouth worth trusting changed shape over them.

"How bad?" he asked.

Sylvanas shut the ledger. "Bad enough that if I were you, I'd remember who asked for books they had no business needing and who paid extra to move after dark."

He swallowed.

"There was another depot south of here," he said. "Closer to the main exchange road. Smaller. Mostly transfer work. They've been stripping volume through it all month."

"Name."

He gave it.

Jaina was already gathering papers. "If they know the route is visible at all, that depot is next."

"Or already gone."

"Yes."

No softness. Just agreement.

Sylvanas looked once through the office window at the yard outside. Cyndia at the north gate, easy in the saddle and not easy at all if you looked twice. Lyriel on the road approach, talking to a driver while pretending not to. Their horses blown. The light falling.

They would ride anyway.

By the time they reached the smaller depot the day had folded toward dark and the horses were moving on discipline more than appetite.

Something was wrong before Sylvanas put a hand on the gate.

No yard noise. No grain dust hanging where late work should have stirred it. No teamsters swearing at wheels. The place had the shape of a working depot and the silence of an emptied chapel.

"Perimeter," she said.

Cyndia peeled left at once. Lyriel right. Jaina dismounted without waiting to be told and came up the track with the staff already in hand.

The gate opened on a yard too clean to trust.

Not abandoned. Stripped.

Cart ruts everywhere, but none fresh enough to still hold shine in the clay. Swept patches where loose grain should have caught in cracks. One loading frame overturned not by storm or negligence but because it had been shoved aside and then left where haste had dropped it. The warehouse doors stood open, and the dark inside looked arranged.

"They moved in a hurry," Lyriel said from the right wall. "One team left loaded. Southbound. Deep cut at the gate, still damp underneath."

"No stable hands," Cyndia said from the opposite side. "No lanterns. Tack room open. Two horses left in the back pen and nobody to claim them."

Useful. Different kinds.

Sylvanas crossed the yard first. The mage came in half a step behind her, not crowding, staff light kept low enough not to spoil night sight. The warehouse shelves had been cleared in strips. Not all of them. Just enough to make absence visible. Ledger hooks on the office wall stood empty. The floor near them had been overswept, broom lines still showing in the dust.

"There were books here," Jaina said.

"Yes."

"Recently."

"Yes."

She did not need the answer twice. Neither did Sylvanas. The point was rhythm now. One woman naming the magical or hidden part of the damage, the other the physical. The pieces met in the middle and stayed there.

The office door was half shut.

Inside sat a man old enough that death ought to have looked less surprising on him than it did. Broad desk, tall chair, cup knocked over on its side, one hand still resting on the blotter as if he had been reaching for a pen when his heart decided it had done enough. The first read was almost tidy.

Too tidy.

His chair had been straightened after he slumped. The cup had spilled, but not where it should have from that angle. One drawer hung open an inch, then closed again. Sylvanas touched the man's sleeve. Still some warmth under the cloth.

"Not long," she said.

The mage moved nearer, light shifting blue-white over the desk, the hand, the throat. Her face altered as the spell deepened — less expression, more attention, the way a hound's ears change when it catches a distant horn.

"No visible wound," Jaina said.

"I can see that."

"Wait."

Sylvanas waited.

The light thinned until it was almost nothing. Jaina touched two fingers to the dead overseer's jaw, then to the skin just below the ear, then held the crystal over the mouth where no breath would ever touch it again.

"There," she said, very softly.

"What there."

"Residue." Her eyes stayed on the corpse. "Buried under the rest of it. Deliberate. Thin enough to pass if nobody went looking."

"Necromancy."

"Yes. Not enough to raise him. Enough to stop him cleanly and make the stop look ordinary."

Sylvanas looked at the body again and hated that the explanation improved it.

Targeted. Not rot loosed in bulk. Not village filth. Somebody had reached into one office, one man, one moment, and closed a mouth before it could open.

"He held the books," she said.

"Yes."

"And lost them."

"Or gave them up and died for it anyway."

Both plausible. Neither useful yet.

Sylvanas opened the hanging drawer the inch it had already given her. Empty except for wax crumbs and one broken tally peg. The desk top held smears where stacks had been dragged off too fast. In the brazier under the window, ash had been stirred, then smothered. She crouched and put two fingers into it.

Warm.

"They burned what they couldn't carry," she said.

Jaina turned from the corpse and came down beside her. "Can you get anything out of it?"

"If they were sloppy."

"They were hurried," Jaina said. "That's better."

Dry. Tired. Right.

Sylvanas tipped the brazier carefully onto the stone. Ash fanned across the floor in a dark spill. Beneath the grey lay heavier scraps that had not finished dying before someone killed the fire. She separated them with the point of her knife.

Route fragment. Two lines only. A warehouse mark. Half a place-name.

And a clot of wax that had fallen without fully melting. Red so dark it read black in the lamplight, one edge stamped before it tore free. Not enough for a crest entire. Enough for a house sign.

Jaina saw it at the same time she did.

"That's not local," the mage said.

"No."

She turned it over on her blade tip. The sigil was partial but recognizable: formal, expensive, made for people who did not touch their own freight if they could pay others to do it.

Barov.

Not guilt. Not yet. Only reach.

Outside, a door banged once in the night air. Lyriel's boots crossed the warehouse threshold a heartbeat later.

"Found a rear tally board," she said. "Most of it's blank. They scraped off what mattered."

"Bring it."

Lyriel set the board against the desk. Scraped, yes. But not well enough. The knife work had taken the chalk. It had not taken the pressure score cut into the wood beneath repeated entries. Numbers lingered as shallow grooves. So did abbreviated route names if you caught them in slant light.

Jaina angled the crystal. Sylvanas tilted the board. Between them, the erased marks began to reappear by insult and persistence.

"Andorhal," Jaina said.

"Again."

"And this fork." She touched a set of almost-gone slashes. "South feeder. Not the east line."

"Why not the east."

"Because they stopped using it here before the scrape." Her finger moved to another groove. "This one continues."

Sylvanas looked to the yard through the open door. Southbound. Lyriel's fresh cut at the gate. The loaded team. The cleaned office. The dead overseer.

Active enemy, then. Not a trail lying where it had fallen. A trail being swept ahead of them by hands that knew exactly how far behind they were.

Cyndia appeared in the doorway with a torn sheet held flat between both hands.

"Found this under the outside rail," she said. "Caught on a nail."

Paper, stiff with damp and grain dust. Torn from a larger route bundle. One edge bore the same dark wax. The center held three surviving words and part of a fourth:

...Barov holding...

...storehouse line...

...Andor...

Enough.

No more than that.

Sylvanas read it twice anyway. The instinct to make certainty out of fragments had killed better people than depot overseers.

"Barov paper," she said.

"Not Barov intent," Jaina said at once.

Sylvanas glanced up.

"Yes."

"It could mean ownership. Lease. Factors moving goods under a house seal. It could mean infiltration of a route already in place."

"Yes."

"It could mean guilt," Jaina said. "It doesn't mean proven guilt."

Good. The correction deserved to stay.

"Write that in your own blood if you need to remember it," Sylvanas said. "I have no interest in accusing a house because somebody died with the wrong seal in his brazier."

One corner of Jaina's mouth moved. Not a smile. More like recognition of a clean answer.

"Then we're agreed."

"On that point."

She rose and crossed back to the corpse. Office man. Keeper of the transfer depot. Dead before he could speak, dead neatly enough to slow suspicion, not neatly enough to fool a mage who had spent the last week putting her hands into everything death-adjacent she could reach.

Outside, one of the pen horses whickered, sharp and lonely.

"They're reacting to us," Jaina said behind her.

"Yes."

"Which means someone is still close enough to react fast."

"Yes."

"Close enough to watch?"

Sylvanas looked toward the black square of the yard and the darker road beyond it.

"If I were running this," she said, "I wouldn't need eyes on us all the time. Only enough men on the route to know when the wrong questions were being asked."

"And if you're not?"

"Then we have a worse problem."

Jaina considered that. "I dislike your answer."

"It wasn't for your comfort."

She went back out into the yard. Night had settled fully now, leaving the depot under a thin moon and Cyndia's shuttered lamp. The stripped place looked smaller from outside. More frightened.

"We're done here," Sylvanas said. "Take the board. Take the sheet. Leave the body for whatever human officer gets here too late to matter."

Lyriel nodded once and went for the horses.

Cyndia stayed near the rear rail, still scanning the south road. "If they cleared this much in one day, the next stop will be cleaner."

"Probably."

"Then we stop expecting books."

"Yes."

Jaina came out of the office carrying the half-burned fragments between two ledger covers to keep them from blowing apart. Her hair had fully escaped its tie now. She looked as if the day had been trying to abrade her down to bone and she had decided not to give it the satisfaction.

"One thing," she said.

"Say it."

"The residue on the overseer wasn't broad plague work. It was hidden handwork. Deliberate silencing."

"Yes."

"Whoever did it knew enough to bury the sign."

"Also yes."

She looked at the office once more. "I would like to meet them in a room with the door shut."

Sylvanas put a boot in the stirrup.

"Get in line."

They did not chase the dark road blind. Even Sylvanas was not in the mood for that kind of stupidity. Two miles east they found a coaching house with a stable large enough for the four horses and an upper room whose chief virtues were a bolt on the door and a table broad enough to lay evidence on.

The innkeeper, roused half from bed, took one look at their faces and stopped trying to overcharge them.

The room smelled of tallow, damp wool, and the onion stew somebody had cooked downstairs too long ago to matter.

Cyndia and Lyriel took first and second watch without discussion. Habit. Cyndia checked the yard and stable. Lyriel checked the road and stairs. By the time the bolt settled from Lyriel's last pass, Sylvanas and the mage had already covered the table with route fragments, copied tallies, and the coaching-house keeper's lamp turned low.

Silence held for a while, but it was working silence, not the kind that asks to be filled.

Jaina copied fast. Not carelessly. Fast the way some swordsmen drew fast — because wasted motion offended them. She had stopped simplifying hours ago. Now she spoke into the page or into the air between them and assumed Sylvanas would keep up unless proved otherwise.

"The older routes spike hardest where the villages depend on bought flour," she said, making another mark on the map. "The mixed lines don't. Home milling blunts it."

"Which means?"

"Which means the broad road isn't enough by itself. They need dependence. Habit. Regular purchase. If you want a village to sicken deep, you don't poison it once. You feed it."

"Siege work," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

There was ash under Jaina's left thumb from the office brazier. Grain dust at one cuff. A purple exhaustion under both eyes. She looked young then, not in any soft way, only in the way fatigue stripped some layers off and showed how much force it actually took to keep up with the pace of her own mind.

Sylvanas pushed the Barov wax toward the light.

"House paper enters here," she said. "Not before."

"Visible house paper," Jaina corrected.

"Fine. Visible house paper enters here."

She waited for the rest of it. Jaina did not waste time pretending not to have more.

"If the Barov holdings are in the grain road at scale, then Andorhal matters more than before." She drew one line, then another, joining routes already on the table. "Not because the Barovs are automatically responsible. Because the road narrows there. Ownership, warehousing, transfer, something. The depots after this are clerk work and waystations. The weight is farther south."

"And the enemy knows we know enough to head there."

"Yes."

Sylvanas leaned over the map, one palm on the boards. Candlelight made the inked roads look like cuts. Jaina shifted her own papers aside before Sylvanas's arm could crush them, not even glancing up. Used to the motion already. Or trusting it.

That thought ought to have caught.

It did not.

"This fork," Sylvanas said. "Why keep it active and scrape the east line away."

"Because the east line is too hot now. Or too visibly sick. Or because the south road is feeding something more important." Jaina glanced at the copied tallies, then at the route scrap. "If I had to choose which surviving road still carries the operation's confidence, it would be the southern feeder."

No pause over the answer. No pretty uncertainty.

Sylvanas marked the feeder road with the point of her knife.

"That sends us near Strahnbrad."

"Yes."

"And Arthas's current theatre."

"Also yes."

Human war-country, then. Grain traffic and soldiery using the same road, one ignorance crossing another.

The thought should have irritated her more than it did. Instead she found herself reordering distances, counting rations, recalculating how hard they could push the horses before dawn and still arrive with something left in the animals for flight if a prince's men decided foreigners on the road were an inconvenience.

Across from her, Jaina was already revising the route list to match the same conclusion.

No explanation offered. None needed.

Sylvanas tapped one copied tally with a fingernail.

"This merchant note. Empty wagon. North road seller."

Jaina looked up.

"You think of him now too," she said.

Not *the old man*. Not *the merchant*. Him.

Sylvanas let her eyes rest on the page instead of on Jaina's face.

"I think of anything that repeats."

"He did."

"Yes."

The memory came back harder for being incomplete. Not a face. Never the face. Just details refusing to die properly: an empty wagon when grain ought to have weighed it down, cheap civility, the wrong kind of ease, the sense afterward that she had been offered something ordinary and filed it where ordinary things went.

Annoying.

She hated being given reason to remember what she had not yet had reason to keep.

"What do you actually have," Jaina asked, "besides irritation."

"Empty wagon. Newer trader. Price cuts. Witnesses remember his usefulness more clearly than his face." Sylvanas looked at the candle flame until the old road scene half-built itself behind it. "And a farewell."

"What farewell."

"May the stars keep your road."

Jaina's brows drew in a fraction. "Odd."

"Yes."

"Important?"

"Not yet."

She said it and knew at once it was true. Sharpened, not solved. A pebble in the boot, not the road itself. Worth keeping. Not worth turning aside for while live depots were still being emptied ahead of them.

Jaina nodded and returned to the map. "Then we file him harder and keep moving."

File him harder.

Dry enough to be funny if either of them had the spare flesh for laughter.

"Good," Sylvanas said.

They worked on.

Below them, somebody laughed in the common room and was told to shut up. Rain touched the shutters once, then thought better of it. The coaching house timbers settled around the room in small, tired sounds. On the table the roads narrowed, simplified, stopped pretending to be separate things.

At some point Sylvanas reached across for the tally sheet Jaina had just finished marking and found, a beat too late, that the name in her head had already changed.

Not the mage had marked this poorly.

Jaina had marked this poorly.

No thunder under it. No revelation. Only the useful fact of a hand she now recognized by work alone — the sharper pressure in the downstrokes when she was tired, the neat corrections when a line had to be moved, the way she abandoned softness in her script the same way she had abandoned it in speech once she understood Sylvanas did not need coddling through her own thoughts.

Jaina looked up because Sylvanas had stopped with the page half-lifted.

"What."

"Your mark's wrong," Sylvanas said, and tapped the route. "This tally belongs to the south feeder, not the east branch. The scoring under the chalk cut left."

Jaina leaned in at once. Hair slid forward. Her shoulder brushed Sylvanas's forearm by accident and stayed there only as long as the correction required.

"You're right," she said. No defensiveness. "I copied the scrape, not the grain cut."

Then, after a breath: "Thank you."

Simple. Meant.

Sylvanas pushed the paper back.

"Fix it."

Jaina fixed it.

The last question was the road itself.

Sylvanas laid the surviving evidence in one line — Barov wax, torn sheet, copied tally, scraped board notes, the remembered timing of village sickness, the loaded team cutting south out of the cleaned depot. Jaina added her own line beside it — heavier contamination on older dependency routes, weaker spread where bought flour mattered less, transfer pattern favoring the southern feeder, silence in the east line where scraped entries had stopped.

Then Cyndia knocked once and came in without waiting for permission. Field habit again.

"Road report," she said. "Three troop strings passed south in the last watch. Lordaeron colors. Fast enough to be dispatch, not patrol. Same road also took two grain teams before moonrise. Loaded both times."

Lyriel appeared a moment later behind her.

"East fork's quieter than it should be," she said. "Too quiet for a main trade line. One abandoned cart at the branch and no fresh wheel score after it. South road still carries both traffic types."

There it was.

Not author whim. Not convenience. Evidence, hooves, and the road itself choosing for them.

Sylvanas looked at Jaina.

Jaina looked back, then at the table, then at the south line they had both already known would win if the facts kept holding.

"South," Jaina said.

"South," Sylvanas agreed.

Cyndia gave the barest nod and withdrew. Lyriel followed, already turning the choice into horseflesh and distance in her head.

The room felt smaller after the decision. Cleaner. Not better.

Sylvanas gathered the papers into two bundles — one for the saddle case, one for her own hand until dawn. Jaina corked the ink, flexed her cramped fingers once, and sat back in the chair with the stiffness of someone whose mind had forgotten the body for too long and was being reminded of it all at once.

"Sleep if you can," Sylvanas said.

Jaina gave her a look that suggested sleep had become a problem she had no current interest in solving.

"You first," she said.

"I can sleep in a saddle."

"That isn't sleep. That's cavalry lying to itself."

Unexpected. Accurate.

Sylvanas felt one corner of her mouth threaten movement and denied it the satisfaction.

"And mage study by candle stub is better."

"Considerably."

Flat enough between them to keep from becoming anything else.

She left Jaina with the map and opened the shutters a crack. The yard below was black, the horses pale ghosts where stable light caught their flanks. Beyond the coaching house the road bent south through fields silvered by moonlight and then vanished into the dark country where grain wagons and human soldiers were both being drawn toward the same tomorrow.

Strahnbrad first, most likely. Then the deeper road to Andorhal.

Behind her, paper rustled. Jaina correcting one more line before surrendering the page.

By dawn the room had cooled and the candle had died in its own grease.

They went down without ceremony.

Bread, watered ale, horses saddled in the yard while mist still held low over the road. Cyndia checked girths. Lyriel tightened the second pack on Jaina's mare without being asked and moved on before thanks could become a subject. The coaching-house keeper brought out the copied route page wrapped in oilcloth because Jaina had paid him to fetch drier ink before sunrise. Human efficiency. Bought and therefore reliable.

Sylvanas mounted first and looked south.

"Same order," she said. "South feeder. Fast until the horses tell us otherwise. If troop traffic thickens, we leave the main spine and parallel it where we can. No heroics if Lordaeron uniforms decide to be curious."

"Understood," Cyndia said.

"Yes, Ranger-General," Lyriel said.

Jaina settled into the saddle and tucked the oilcloth packet inside her cloak. "If we pass fresh grain teams, I want the sacks read before the drivers know what I'm doing."

"Then don't make it look magical."

"I never do."

Sylvanas looked at her.

Jaina looked back, as solemn as a priest at bad theatre.

"Hardly ever," she amended.

That was better.

Sylvanas turned her horse onto the road.

"Ride."

The quartet moved south as the mist broke around the horses' knees and the feeder road took them toward Strahnbrad, Andorhal, and whatever had started cleaning the world ahead of them before they could reach it.
