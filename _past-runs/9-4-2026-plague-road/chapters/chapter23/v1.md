# Chapter 23: The Throne Room

The service door gave under Antonidas's softened lock with hardly any sound at all. One breath of blue-white Kirin Tor light touched the jamb, then the magic withdrew and left Jaina in the dark of the palace passages with Sylvanas already ahead of her, bow low, shoulders angled to a corridor that smelled of lye, old grease, and stone scrubbed so often the surface had gone slick in the middle. Behind them the latch settled back into place. No tower. No archmage. No witness who would matter if the room ahead went wrong.

Only the two of them.

Jaina stopped for one heartbeat and let the palace come through her.

Cold in the walls. Not morning cold, not cellared stone, not the ordinary damp chill of a big old building. This ran the wrong way. Up through the floor, sideways through mortar, thin at first and then stronger as the corridor bent inward. Frostmourne was in the building like rot in a beam, not visible until you put your hand on the wood and found how far the softness had spread.

The bread-taste came right behind it.

Yeast. Salt. Warm crust torn open at table height. Wrong enough in Northrend, unbearable here, in corridors where kitchen boys had probably spent half the morning carrying trays toward people dressing for court. Jaina swallowed against it and got nothing but more of it coating the back of her tongue.

Ahead, Cyndia leaned in from a side turn, one hand up. Two fingers. Then she pointed: left branch clear, right branch occupied deeper in.

Sylvanas touched the bowstring once and shifted that way. "How many?"

"Two," Jaina whispered. The answer had come before her eyes found them. Two dead hollows in armor, standing where living guards would have shifted weight or muttered to each other or scratched beneath a gorget because human men always found a way to itch inside polished steel. These did none of it. "Not breathing."

Cyndia's mouth flattened. The ranger at her shoulder eased an arrow from the quiver. The other two had already drifted back toward the service stair they were meant to hold.

"We keep the lane," Cyndia said quietly.

That was all. No promise. No dramatics. Her sweep widened once, catching Jaina where a missing partner might once have stood, then moved on.

Sylvanas looked over one shoulder. "With me."

Jaina went.

The two sentries stood before a narrower door banded in iron. From twenty paces away they could have passed for palace men on a dull assignment. At ten the trick broke. The stillness was wrong. The skin at the edge of the gorgets was too pale in a way that had nothing to do with winter. The hum from the blade answered through them, little splinters of the larger note.

Sylvanas held up one finger. Wait.

Then she moved.

Fast enough that Jaina's eye lost the first part of it. One instant the Ranger-General was a lean shadow against the wall. The next she had crossed the corridor and driven a long knife up under the nearer helm. Metal clicked against teeth. The dead guard folded without even the courtesy of surprise. Jaina sent frost into the second man's joints as he started to turn. Not a lance. Not a flourish. Just a hard lock through knee and elbow, enough for the halberd to snag on its own shaft and for Sylvanas to put the knife through his throat seam before the dead nerves could learn a new order.

Armor hit stone. Once. Twice.

No cry. No alarm.

Only the palace going on around them.

Somewhere beyond two walls, crockery rattled. Someone laughed. A servant's shoes scuffed over tile, careless, alive, completely ignorant of the thing sitting in the center of the kingdom with a sword across his knees.

Jaina pressed the heel of one hand to her mouth and breathed through her nose. Bread. Bread and metal. The locked door in front of them. The dead weight at their feet. The wrongness in the palace had moved past invasion into occupation. Arthas had not merely returned. He had arranged himself inside the kingdom's habits. Servants still carried dishes. Guards still held doors. The dead stood watch in the seams where ceremony turned to labor and no one important ever looked.

Sylvanas wiped the blade on a dead man's tabard. "Still with me?"

"Yes."

"Good. Because I'm not carrying you until after."

It might have been funny on another road. Here it landed exactly where she meant it to: a hand under the jaw, keeping Jaina's head pointed the right way.

They moved deeper into the passage network. Service stairs. Linen shelves. A bend where someone had left a mop bucket and a broken crate because palaces, like ships, were all dignity in the front rooms and clutter anywhere the real work got done. Jaina let the working nouns anchor her. Bucket. Shelf. Door latch. Torch bracket. If she stayed with those, the pressure in the walls was only another problem. If she let herself think about the room beyond them, the king beyond that room, the prince beyond the king, her reserves would start going before the spell ever did.

The corridor widened at a crossing. That was where they nearly ran into Uther.

Not fully. Not close enough for the whole disaster to change shape. He came around the far turn in worn plate with one palace functionary hurrying beside him and stopped so hard the man behind him took half a step into his back before catching himself. Big hands. Travel dirt on the hem of the surcoat. Face worn down past anger into something leaner and worse.

"By the Light," the functionary started, then took in Sylvanas's knife, Jaina's staff, the two women dressed for the kind of work no court should ever need, and went white.

Uther understood faster. His eyes found Jaina first, then the line of the corridor behind her, then the cold shivering out of the stones. "You made it in."

"Barely," Jaina said.

"The king?"

"We don't know."

The functionary found his voice. "Lord Uther, you cannot simply—"

"Be quiet." Uther did not look at him. He was already looking past them, toward the center of the palace, toward the source of the wrongness he had spent days being politely denied. "How close?"

Sylvanas answered. "Close enough to smell it."

That won one grim line from his mouth that might once have been approval. Or despair. With him the two had started to look alike.

The functionary hissed, "You cannot let armed—"

Uther turned on him then, and the man shut up without needing the end of the sentence. "Run to your captain," he said. "Tell him the palace is compromised and if he has any sense left in him he will get his men out of the ceremonial halls and start checking the interior routes."

"My lord, I—"

"Run."

The man ran.

For one thin second the corridor belonged to people who actually knew what it held.

Uther looked at Jaina again. Her face must have given away more than she meant, because his voice changed when he said, "Can it be done?"

Not saved. Not fixed. Done.

"I don't know," she said. No point lying to him. "I know where the blade turns. I know what the sequence has to be. I don't know what the room will let me keep."

He took that without flinching. "Then you'll do it anyway."

"Yes."

Something old and father-shaped moved behind his eyes and was gone before she could bear it. "Good."

The question hung there after that. Use him or let him go. Keep the one man in the kingdom whose testimony might still mean something in the room, or send him away to gather an Order that had already proved itself slower than catastrophe.

Jaina heard the answer in the walls before she formed it in words. The rhythm from Frostmourne had sharpened. Not louder. Nearer. The blade had gone from pressure to event. Whatever was happening in the throne room was already underway.

"You can't make it in time by the front," she said.

The answer reached Uther as soon as it reached her. That was the misery of him. He was never stupid enough to need the truth softened. "No."

Sylvanas's gaze flicked once toward the nearest service branch. Then back. "If you bring the Order, bring them for what comes after."

Uther's jaw worked. Not in uncertainty. In refusal forced to swallow itself because the seconds would not stretch for his grief. "And if there is no after?"

"Then you've lost nothing by not arriving to watch it happen."

The words landed. The old paladin took them anyway because he had spent a lifetime taking blows that would have dropped gentler men, and perhaps because he knew she was right.

He stepped in once, closer, his voice dropped under the torch crackle. "If I reach the chamber too late, they will say I wasn't there. They will say I hated him for Stratholme and came wanting the rest."

The bread-taste turned bitter in her mouth. "I know."

"Then live long enough to say otherwise."

He went. Heavy steps swallowed by the passage turns, moving fast for a man wearing that much iron and that much hopelessness.

When he was gone, the corridor widened around the space he had left.

Sylvanas set a hand against the service door ahead of them. Not the chamber yet. The last turn before it. Jaina could hear the room now through stone and wood and distance.

Voice.

Male. Young. Familiar in the worst possible way.

Arthas.

Not all the words. Only cadence, flattened by masonry into something formal and lightless. Another voice answered, older, thinner, carrying the brittle edge of a man speaking from a throne because he was too surprised to stand.

Terenas.

Alive.

For now.

Jaina's nose started bleeding before the room even opened.

Warm wetness touched her lip. She wiped it with the back of her hand and found red in the torchlight. The blood brought the bread-smell higher. Salt, iron, yeast. Her fingers shook once around the staff.

Sylvanas took that in. Said only, "Can you still cast?"

"Yes."

"Then stop looking at your own blood."

The service door opened on a breath of cold clean air and the Imperial Chamber beyond.

White marble. She had known it would be white. The sort of white kings build when they expect distance to do half the ruling for them. Columns in two long rows. Gallery shadows above. The great golden seal of Lordaeron spread across the floor where the octagonal window threw late light down in a sheet so bright it hurt. At the far end, under the dome and before the throne, Arthas stood with Frostmourne bared in his hand.

Prince-shaped. That was the worst part. The profile she knew. The blond hair, now duller, the broad shoulders inside plate gone dark around the joints with something that looked like frost and did not melt. The line of the jaw she had once touched in rooms with no witnesses and no kingdoms in them.

Only the stillness had changed.

No human being stood that still with a sword in hand.

Terenas was half-risen from the throne. Crown crooked already from one sharp movement. His face sat in the final moments before comprehension, when the world is still trying to decide whether what stands in front of you can possibly be what your eyes insist it is.

Jaina found the seam at once.

There. In the blade's cycle. The hitch between taking and throwing. So close now she could have put a fingertip on it if fingertips worked that way. Her body locked around the knowledge. Frost first. Hold the turn. Arcane through the opening. Fire while it stayed bare.

Reach the sword.

Keep breathing.

Do not let him see you first.

Arthas said, perfectly level, "Succeeding you, Father."

Then he drove the blade in.

Terenas made a sound Jaina would have spent the rest of her life failing to name if the rest of her life had room for it. Frostmourne did not cut the way other weapons cut. There was no wet struggle, no human hitch of resistance. The sword drank. Surprise froze the king from the inside out.

The crown fell first.

Gold rang on marble and rolled through the seal's inlaid lines, bright and absurd and public. The sound bounced under the dome and came back smaller.

Sylvanas was already moving.

Her first arrow flew before Terenas finished collapsing. Jaina barely caught the draw. One heartbeat the Ranger-General was shadow and cloth behind a column. The next the shaft crossed the room in a black line and took Arthas clean through the throat.

Any living man would have died at once.

Arthas rocked half a step. Blood welled dark around the wound. Darker than it should have been, thicker, already pulling wrong. Then the flesh closed. Not all at once. Worse than that. A slow gathering. The body remembered its ordered shape and returned to it.

His head turned toward them.

Concealment ended there.

"I wondered," he said.

His voice crossed the chamber without strain. It did not need to rise. The dome did the work for him, laying the words along stone and returning them whole. Jaina had spent too long remembering warmth in that voice. The cold version found every place those old memories still sat and laid ice into them.

Sylvanas had another arrow nocked. "And still you turned your back on the door."

"You were never the door I feared."

The lie was casual. That made it sting harder.

Terenas slid from the sword and struck the steps beneath the throne. The body hit in folds, old man first, king second. Blood moved slowly across the marble, bright at the edges where the sunlight caught it. The hand lay palm-up beside the signet ring, fingers still trying to close after the rest of him had gone slack.

Move, she told herself.

Her feet obeyed a fraction late. She and Sylvanas split without needing to speak. Left and right, using the first row of columns, dragging his sight line wide. Jaina kept the staff low, not hidden, not flaunted. She needed one clean opening and a sword that believed for half a second it was dealing with only one threat.

Arthas stepped clear of the throne dais.

The chamber changed around that step.

Cold rolled low over the floor, not as mist but as pressure. The blood on the marble skinned over black-red and then darkened beneath a rind of ice. Jaina's teeth hurt. Every shard of light from the octagonal window turned hard-edged. The air lost its softness. It turned into the moment before glass breaks.

He looked at the king's body once, as though checking the work, then back to them. "You came all this way to stop a coronation?"

"I came," Jaina said, "to end the thing carrying his face."

His mouth changed. Not enough to call it a smile.

Sylvanas loosed.

The second arrow took Arthas high in the chest. The third came so fast behind it the fletching of one nearly clipped the shaft of the other. He moved for that one at last, faster than a man in plate had any right to move, and the arrow meant for his eye glanced off a burst of rime that blew outward from Frostmourne's guard. Ice shattered against the marble in a bright hiss.

That was all the invitation the room needed.

He came down the center aisle with the sword low and the old careful training of his footwork still visible under whatever now occupied him. Jaina hated that most of all. Not the dead calm. Not the strength. The remnants of the prince inside the monster's shape. The familiar weight shifts. The way he saved distance on the turn.

Sylvanas met him first because of course she did.

She broke from behind the column, loosed at near point-blank range, and was already sideways by the time the arrowhead sparked against his pauldron. Frostmourne tore through the place she had been with enough force to shear a thumb-deep slice from the marble base behind her. Chips stung Jaina's cheek. Sylvanas dropped, slid, came up on one knee, knife in her off hand now, bow still in the other, keeping both range and steel.

"Jaina!" she snapped.

The seam. Yes.

Jaina drove frost at the blade.

Not at Arthas. The sword. The place inside the cycle where taking became casting. Ice ran along the fuller in a pale line. For one suspended instant the hunger-note in the room changed pitch. Not broken. Caught. A gear with iron grit in it.

Arthas flinched. His eyes found her completely then, no longer dividing attention with Sylvanas, and the violence of that gaze told her the theory had been right.

Good.

She hit the opening with arcane force before it could close.

No grand sigil. No floating architecture of light. There was no time for beauty in it. She rammed pure blue pressure through the stalled turn, a wedge where the blade expected flow, and the sword answered with a crack like winter lake ice taking weight.

Arthas shouted.

Not loud. Not human. The sound came from farther back than his throat.

Fire, Jaina thought, already reaching.

Too slow.

He tore the blade free of the lock with brutal strength, the frost she had fixed there exploding into needled shards. Frostmourne swung and the backlash from the broken turn hit her square in the chest. Jaina flew backward into a column hard enough to empty her lungs. Pain burst white along her spine. She slid, caught herself on one knee, and only then found her staff gone skidding away over the marble.

The whole room tilted.

Bread in her mouth. Blood in her nose. No breath.

Arthas was on her before the second inhale came.

Sylvanas saved it.

The Ranger-General shot him from eight paces and then from six and then from four, walking the arrows into joints, neck seam, visor slit, any place where motion had to pass through something smaller than plate. The shafts hit hard enough to turn him, hard enough to buy Jaina the half-seconds her lungs needed. Arthas chopped one arrow out of the air. Let another bury in the meat above his hip and kept moving.

"Up," Sylvanas said.

Jaina got up because there was nothing else to do.

She snatched her staff with a pull of arcane force and nearly lost her balance doing it. Arthas crossed the space between them in three strides. Sylvanas met him at the fourth, too close for the bow to matter now. She caught Frostmourne on the long knife's spine for one shrieking instant that sent sparks and white fire across her knuckles, then spun off the contact before the blade could take more than metal. The knife snapped at the midpoint anyway. Half the broken weapon spun away under the columns.

That near, the wound in Arthas's throat was still knitting around the first arrow's torn path.

Not invulnerable. Not untouchable. Just held together by a thing with more appetite than mercy.

She slammed a fan of ice across the floor between his feet.

The marble whitened. Arthas's leading boot lost purchase for a fraction. That fraction was all Sylvanas ever asked from the world. She stepped inside his reach, drove the broken knife into the gap beneath his arm, and kicked off his cuisse to throw herself clear as Frostmourne ripped a blue-white wake through the air where her ribs had just been.

He turned on her.

That was part of the plan. It had always been part of the plan. Seeing it happen still made something inside Jaina seize.

Sylvanas drew him across the chamber in a running diagonal that used every column base, every shift in light, every line where the floor inlay gave a heel more or less trust. She never retreated in a straight line. Never offered him the center of her back. She made him earn each pace with a cut, a pivot, a violent correction, while loosing from impossible angles and keeping him turned away from where Jaina needed to stand.

Buy her the seconds, the memory ledger had said in its dry little phrasing.

This was what those words cost in a room full of marble.

An arrow glanced from his cheek and left a furrow that instantly blackened. Another buried in the joint behind his knee. He went down once, not fully, only enough to brace a gauntlet against the floor while Frostmourne punched cold through the tiles in a starburst around him. Sylvanas used the heartbeat to clear distance and reach a new angle. Jaina used it to rebuild her own.

Frost.

Hold the turn.

Arcane.

Open the wound wider.

Fire.

But timing in a practice chamber and timing while the dead prince tried to cut your ally in half were not the same creature.

Arthas rose and threw something with his free hand.

Not ice. Not spellwork she had a name for. A black-red surge like clot and shadow together, torn straight from the field around the sword. It struck the column Sylvanas had just passed and bit through marble the way teeth go through soaked bread. The pillar groaned. Dust showered. A crack ran up through carved laurel and disappeared into the vaulting.

The room gave that sound back to them all at once.

Sylvanas's glance flicked upward. Only a glance. Enough.

Arthas closed.

He backhanded her with the guard of Frostmourne before the blade even came around. Metal hit flesh with a sound that rang in Jaina's own teeth. Sylvanas went sideways, struck a column, and dropped to one hand. The bow skidded from her grip. She pushed up immediately and almost made it. The second cut opened from shoulder height toward the neck.

Jaina caught the blade in ice three inches short of Sylvanas's throat.

Everything in her locked at once. Arms. Jaw. Spine. The spell took so much from so little left that her vision freckled black at the edges. Frost crawled up Frostmourne in bright branching veins. The seam flashed beneath it. There. There. Sylvanas rolled away under the arrested cut with blood on one side of her face and murder still intact in both eyes.

"Now!" Jaina shouted.

She drove arcane power into the revealed hitch so hard her hands went numb around the staff.

This time the sword truly cracked.

Not broken. Not yet. But the rhythm tore open. The field around the blade spasmed outward in violent pulses, taking from the chamber and throwing it back wrong. Light dimmed, flared, dimmed again. The king's spilled blood on the steps steamed, then froze, then steamed. Jaina tasted hot copper and cold bread together until her stomach lurched.

Arthas staggered.

For the first time since entering the room, he looked uncertain of his footing.

Sylvanas did not waste uncertainty.

She came off the floor with blood at her temple and no bow in hand, only another arrow gripped like a short spear. Her strike was ugly, practical, meant for the face. Arthas turned from it and took the arrowhead through the ruined seam of his cheek instead of the eye. The shaft snapped across his helm. That bought Jaina one more opening and cost Sylvanas the last clean retreat in that lane.

Frostmourne tore free of the ice with a scream like metal dragged across a cathedral bell.

The backswing caught Sylvanas under the ribs.

Not the edge. Thank every buried god not the edge. The guard or the flat, Jaina could not tell through the speed of it, only that the impact lifted Sylvanas clear of the floor and threw her into the base of the nearest column hard enough for her to stay down a breath too long afterward.

The breath was enough to turn Jaina's bones watery.

"Sylvanas!"

Wrong name to spend air on. She recognized it as soon as it left her. Arthas heard the attachment inside it and moved for Jaina at once.

Good, another part of her answered with feral clarity. Better me. Better me while she breathes.

He came with no speech now. No prince left performing for the throne room. Plate ground over plate. Frost smoked from the edge of the sword. The chamber around him had stopped looking like a royal hall and started looking like a place a storm had chosen to remember.

Jaina backed toward the seal at the center of the floor because the arc lines gave her cleaner footing and because she needed the distance between him and Sylvanas more than she needed safety. The great golden sigil spread under her boots, sunburst and wheat and crown, all the cheerful weight of a kingdom that had once believed symbols could hold. Arthas stepped onto it after her and blackened the gold with rime.

She cast while retreating.

Short work. Mean work. Blue lances at the visor slit. Ice spikes from the floor where the joints bent. A shove of force at the wounded knee Sylvanas had already marked with an arrow. Nothing dignified. Nothing pretty. Every spell chosen for drag, not glory.

He cut them out of the air or wore through them.

The seam kept flashing at the turn of the sword's hunger. Every time it did, her body tried to commit to the full sequence and every time the next step of survival broke the thought apart again. She needed one held instant. One.

Arthas raised his free hand.

She read the timing a breath too late.

The cold hit like a body-sized hammer.

It smashed into her chest and face and drove her skidding over the seal until her hip struck the first step of the throne dais. Ice flowered along the stone around her. The staff flew from her hand again. This time it spun almost to Terenas's body before clattering to a stop.

She could not feel three fingers on her left hand.

Arthas advanced through the wreck of his own spell, slow now, certain now, sword dragging a line of white across the marble. Jaina shoved herself backward with her palms. Her heel slid in the king's blood where it had frozen and thawed and frozen again.

The dead king lay at her side.

His eyes were open.

She had known Terenas as a presence more than a man, a throne at the end of a room, a voice above councils, a weight behind Arthas's name. Death had stripped all that off him in the time it took to fall. He looked only old now. Old and betrayed in his own hall.

Arthas mounted the steps.

Jaina reached for the staff with arcane pull and got a stuttering twitch in the wood across the floor. Too far. Not enough strength. Her chest burned where the last blast had gone through her. The bread-taste had vanished at last, replaced by straight blood and the mineral bite of fear.

Then Sylvanas's voice cut the room in half.

"Here."

Hoarse. Furious. Alive.

Arthas turned.

The Ranger-General stood below the columns with one hand clamped over her side and the other drawing the longbow back to the ear on an arrow fletched black and grey. Not a broadside war shot. Something narrower. Built for piercing.

She had one clean line to him through two columns and the air above the king's blood.

He read the line. The sword began to come around.

That was the second she had needed all along.

Jaina planted one palm flat on the marble step and dragged the chamber's heat toward her.

There was not much left. Winter held the room in both hands. But sunlight still fell through the octagonal window in a bright shaft over the central seal, and the braziers along the walls had not yet fully died, and a dead king's blood was warm under its skin of ice, and Jaina still had enough magic in her to call fire if she did not care what it burned on the way through.

She did not care.

Frost first.

She struck the seam dead center. Not with a broad wash this time. With a needle of killing cold, precise as spite. The blade locked on the turn.

Arcane next.

She rammed power into the caught hitch until the chamber lights doubled and her teeth rang. The crack in Frostmourne opened like a split lip, small and bright and catastrophic.

Fire.

Jaina gave it everything left.

The flame ran white-blue at the base where magic fed it, gold at the edges where the room's surviving heat answered. It did not leap from her staff. She had no staff. It went from her hands, ugly and direct, a stream dragged straight through the opened seam. For one blinding moment Frostmourne became transparent to itself. Down the blade's length ran pure motion: the constant taking, the constant throwing, the trapped scream between the two.

The scream got out.

It burst through the chamber in a soundless wave that hit skin, eyes, teeth, every old scar and every recent bruise. Arthas jerked as though the sword had tried to pull his arm off with itself. The blackened frost on his armor blew outward in scales. His mouth opened.

Sylvanas loosed.

The arrow went through his eye and out the back of the helm.

No miracle marked it. No banner of light. No heavenly choir descending over murdered kings and ruined sons.

Arthas simply stopped.

He stood there with the shaft buried to the fletching and Frostmourne still in his grip and all the terrible motion suddenly absent from the room. The silence after it hurt worse than the noise had. It was like stepping into a space where a waterfall had stood for months and finding the cliff bare.

Then his knees went.

He hit the steps hard enough to jar the crown farther across the floor below. One gauntleted hand smacked the marble and slid in his father's blood. The sword dropped with him but did not leave his grasp. Jaina smelled burned iron. Burned wool. Burned meat under the armor seams.

He looked up.

For one impossible instant she had him.

Not recovered. Not saved. Not the prince returned to himself in some merciful final reconciliation the world had not earned. Only a face emptied enough by pain and shock that the old lines showed through. Boyhood around the eyes. Recognition. Her name maybe, or maybe only the shape of regret trying to form in a mouth that no longer belonged to it.

Then the soul-echo hit.

It came out of the opened sword and the opened man together.

Not ghosts in any orderly sense. Not apparitions that could have been greeted or questioned. Fragments. A father's disappointment. A child's hunger for praise. The stink of Stratholme smoke. Snow over Northrend graves. The bright sick exultation of power first taken into exhausted hands. Uther at a training yard. Muradin laughing somewhere the sun still worked. Her own younger self turning toward Arthas because he had made some sharp remark under his breath during a council and she had needed not to laugh and failed.

All of it slammed through her at once with no mercy for sequence.

Jaina folded over on the step and gagged.

Tears came from pure bodily force before sorrow had room to arrive. Her skull felt split. Her ears rang with voices that were not voices, memory stripped raw of context. She caught one last image before it tore apart: a little blond boy in a window alcove, wooden sword across his knees, looking down into the court and waiting for a father who was late again.

Then even that was gone.

The chamber rocked back into itself.

Arthas lay dead on the steps beneath the throne.

Frostmourne did not.

The sword was still singing.

Quieter. Wounded. But active, and furious now in a way it had not been while held. Cracks veined the blade near the seam. Thin light leaked through them and then sucked back in. The hilt twitched once against the marble, hungry for new hands.

"Don't touch it," Jaina said, though Sylvanas was nowhere near foolish enough to try.

The Ranger-General had dropped to one knee again below the columns, one arm around her ribs. Blood had soaked her leathers dark under the left breast. Her face had gone that waxy color Jaina hated seeing on the living. Still, she answered, "I wasn't planning to."

Jaina got one foot under herself. Then the other. The room had become all distance and angles. Terenas on the floor to her left. Arthas in front of her. Crown below. Sword half between. Sylvanas across the chamber, breathing shallow. Somewhere in the palace at large, men were finally beginning to understand that something had broken.

Boots. Distant. Many.

Too many.

Jaina looked for a clean ending and found none.

If they ran, they would have to leave the throne room by the same service lanes they had used to enter, dragging one wounded ranger and passing whatever panic had already sealed those passages. If they stayed and tried to explain with sword, corpse, and dead king arranged around them, explanation would drown under first sight. Two armed women in a throne room. The prince fallen. The king butchered. Bow in hand. Mage reeking of burnt spellwork. Ranger in blood. There was no version of that first glance that turned kindly.

Sylvanas read the same arithmetic from across the marble. She pushed upright against the column and almost hid the wince. "Can you move?"

"Yes."

"Far?"

Jaina measured her own body and hated the answer. "Not usefully."

The boots were no longer distant. Voices now. Commands breaking over one another at the far doors, then one clear shout that carried through the chamber galleries.

"Open!"

Uther, Jaina thought. No. Too young a voice. Palace captain maybe. One of the men the functionary would have found. Men late to the only room where lateness mattered.

She took one step toward Sylvanas anyway. Then another. The floor dipped oddly under her, and she understood the real trouble: her left leg had gone numb from hip to ankle where the cold blast had hit. She kept moving.

Frostmourne gave a sharp, insectile note.

Both women stopped.

The sword dragged half an inch over the marble, no hand on it. Light leaked through the crack again. For a sick instant Jaina thought it would rise. That it would need no wielder now. That everything they had spent themselves to accomplish would harden into some worse shape before the witnesses even arrived.

Instead the blade shuddered and went still.

Still, but not dead.

"We need it contained," Jaina said.

"Do you have a containment spell left in you?"

"Not one I'd trust."

Sylvanas's laugh was small and raw. "Then let the paladins do one useful thing today."

The great side doors boomed inward.

Armed men flooded the threshold and then checked so hard the first rank almost collided with the second. They had expected violence. The formation showed it, the angled shields, the white faces readying themselves against an ordinary kind of horror.

This was not ordinary enough.

The chamber took them one sight at a time. The king on the steps. The prince down below the throne. The sword between. Blood. Ice. A cracked column. The black-feathered arrow jutting from Arthas's eye. Jaina in scorched blue and silver, half on the dais. Sylvanas with bow in hand and blood down her side.

The captain found his voice first. "Drop your weapons!"

Jaina nearly said something useless, something like look at the room or we killed him or you're standing in the answer. None of it would have reached them. Their fear had already chosen its shape.

More boots sounded in the corridor. Different rhythm. Heavier. Ordered.

The Silver Hand.

Uther came through the press of palace guards bareheaded, hair damp against his temples, warhammer in his fist and three paladins at his back. He took in the king, and whatever in him had remained held together solely because action was still possible broke in one silent visible place across his face.

Then his gaze found Arthas.

Not alive. Not rising. Dead at last on the steps below the throne.

His expression changed again. Not relief. Relief would have been cleaner.

He took one stride into the chamber before the palace captain caught his arm. "My lord, stand back. These women—"

Uther shook him off hard enough to stagger the man. His eyes lifted to Jaina. Then to Sylvanas. Then to Frostmourne, and there the old discipline slammed back into place because the sword was still the largest danger in the room whether anyone else understood it or not.

"No one touches the blade," he said.

The authority in it landed like a hammer. Even the captain obeyed, if only because he heard enough command to recognize he had found someone ranking above his fear.

Jaina tried to speak and coughed blood instead. Not much. Enough.

Uther was at the foot of the dais in three strides. Close enough now for her to see the red rims of his eyes, the wetness he had not let fall, the way his gaze skidded once toward Terenas and then locked back on her because duty, cruel and faithful, still had the reins.

"Report," he said.

He asked it like a commander because if he asked it like anything else he would never get through the asking.

Jaina swallowed iron. "Terenas was dead before we entered." Another breath. "Sylvanas's first arrow failed. The sword closed the wound. We hit the seam. Frost, arcane, then fire. She took the final shot."

Uther looked at Arthas again, at the arrow buried through helm and skull, then at the cracked sword. "And the blade?"

"Damaged. Not finished."

"Can it move?"

"I don't know."

That answer cost her. He took it and hated the world for leaving him no better option.

Behind him the captain had recovered enough certainty to be dangerous again. "My lord, by all appearances—"

"By all appearances," Uther said without turning, "you arrived after the king's murder, after the prince's death, and after these two spent what remained of themselves stopping whatever that sword was doing to your palace." His voice roughened on the last word. "So I would advise great caution with appearances."

The captain stiffened. "Then they are to be treated as witnesses?"

Jaina almost laughed. It came out as another cough.

Uther looked back at her, and the truth sat between them in full armor. Not witnesses. Never that simple. Not with Terenas dead. Not with Arthas dead by an arrow. Not with Sylvanas Windrunner in the throne room of Lordaeron, her very existence enough to start a civil riot in half the kingdom. Not with the old accusations from Stratholme waiting only a body count and a frightened court to rise again.

If he declared for them outright in this room, half the palace would hear betrayal. If he let the captain take the lead, panic would turn to chains and probably blades.

He chose the narrow ledge between.

"They are under my authority until this chamber is secured," he said. "Disarm them. Carefully. Then put them in custody."

Sylvanas's shoulders went still across the room.

The archers at the door tightened their lines at once. Palace men, not rangers. Their fear smelled sharp as tallow smoke.

Jaina forced herself down the last step of the dais. Her staff turned suddenly absurd in her hand, a piece of carved wood pretending the world still sorted itself into useful tools and honest outcomes. She set it on the marble. The sound of it landing was small. Final enough anyway.

Sylvanas looked at her.

Not questioning. Measuring.

Jaina gave the faintest nod.

Not because she trusted the room. Not because surrender tasted honorable. Because neither of them had enough left to cut through thirty men and a circle of paladins while a damaged soul-drinking sword lay twitching at their feet. Because living long enough to speak later, as Uther had told her in the corridor, was the only work left that might still matter.

Sylvanas unstrung the bow with steady hands and laid it down beside her.

Only then did the palace guards advance.

They came in too fast despite the order for care. Men fresh to terror always did. Two spears leveled at Sylvanas before the first gauntlet even reached for her wrists. Another pair closed on Jaina with all the brittle bravado of soldiers who had just found a woman standing over a prince's corpse and needed her helpless before their courage ran out.

Uther checked one of them with a forearm. "Mind the mage."

The guard muttered an apology he clearly did not mean. Jaina let them take her hands anyway. The iron around her wrists was common restraint, not magework, laughably insufficient if her reserves had been anything but rags. Cold metal touched skin already numbed by colder things. The tremor in her fingers started only after the cuffs closed.

Across the chamber, Sylvanas did not offer the guards even that much to work with. She stood upright while they bound her, blood soaking steadily through her leathers, face blank as drawn bark. One man jerked the cord too hard. She looked at him once, and he went grey around the mouth without understanding why.

Paladins moved around Frostmourne in a wary crescent. No one touched it. Good. One began praying under his breath. Another set the hammer-head of his weapon against the marble, ready to strike if the sword so much as twitched. The chamber had become a ruined altar to several incompatible truths.

Jaina turned her head enough to find Terenas one last time.

They had already draped a cloak over him. Blue velvet. Wrong color for the blood beneath it. The crown lay on its side three steps below, one point bent.

Arthas had no covering.

She could not decide whether that was justice or only shock.

The captain, recovering his importance by degrees, pointed toward the side exit. "Take them below."

Below.

Cells, then. Or old holding rooms under the palace where courtiers never went.

Jaina's legs nearly failed on the first step. One of the guards hauled her upright more roughly than necessary. Sylvanas clocked it. The look she gave him promised excellent future reasons for regret if either of them ever walked free again.

Uther fell in beside them for the first stretch to the door. Not close enough to be called comfort. Close enough that no frightened man would decide a bound prisoner needed the back of a spear in motion.

He did not look at Jaina when he said, very low, "I will get the sword sealed."

"It isn't done," she whispered.

"I know."

"The crack."

"I know."

That was all the corridor gave them.

The chamber passed out of sight behind her. Marble, dome, throne, blood, crown, son, father, sword. The room narrowed to service stone again almost at once. Palaces hid disaster in the walls the moment the doors shut.

The bread-taste came back faintly as they descended.

Not from the sword this time. From the actual kitchens somewhere below, where dough would already be rising for the evening meal because kingdoms, even broken ones, kept feeding themselves while they decided whom to blame.

Jaina walked into custody with her wrists bound, Sylvanas bleeding beside her, and the whole kingdom above them tilting toward whatever story frightened men would tell first.
