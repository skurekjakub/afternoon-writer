# Chapter 23: The Throne Room

The service door gave under Antonidas's softened lock with hardly any sound. Blue-white light touched the jamb, then withdrew, leaving Jaina in palace dark with Sylvanas already ahead of her, bow low, shoulders angled toward a corridor that smelled of lye, old grease, and stone scrubbed slick by years of traffic. Behind them the latch settled. No tower. No archmage. No witness who would matter if the room ahead went wrong.

Jaina stopped one heartbeat and let the palace come through her.

Cold ran in the walls, wrong-way cold, rising through the floor and sideways through the mortar. Frostmourne sat somewhere ahead like rot in a beam. The bread-taste followed: yeast, salt, warm crust torn open at table height. Wrong enough in Northrend. Here it made her swallow against air.

Ahead, Cyndia leaned from a side turn, one hand raised. Two fingers. Then she pointed: left branch clear, right branch occupied deeper in.

Sylvanas shifted that way. "How many?"

"Two," Jaina whispered. "Not breathing."

Cyndia's mouth flattened. The ranger beside her eased out an arrow. "We keep the lane."

That was all. No promise. No dramatics.

Sylvanas turned back once. "With me."

Jaina went.

The sentries stood before a narrow iron-banded door. At twenty paces they could have passed for weary palace men. At ten the trick broke. They held too still. Skin showed pale above the gorgets, waxy in the torchlight. Frostmourne's hum answered through them in splinters.

Sylvanas held up one finger.

Then she moved.

One instant she was a lean shadow against the wall. The next she had crossed the corridor and driven a knife up under the nearer helm. Metal clicked against teeth. The corpse folded. Jaina locked the second guard's joints with frost as it started to turn, halberd snagging on its own shaft, and Sylvanas put the knife through the throat seam before the dead nerves found a new order.

Armor hit stone. Once. Twice.

No cry. No alarm.

Somewhere beyond two walls crockery rattled. Someone laughed. A servant's shoes scuffed over tile, careless and alive. Arthas had arranged the dead inside the kingdom's habits and left the living to carry trays around them.

Sylvanas wiped the blade on a tabard. "Still with me?"

"Yes."

"Good. I'm not carrying you until after."

It might have been funny on another road. Here it set Jaina's head straight.

They moved deeper into the passage network. Service stairs. Linen shelves. A bend where someone had abandoned a mop bucket and a broken crate; front rooms stayed polished while clutter gathered where the work got done. Jaina stayed with the nouns. Bucket. Shelf. Latch. Torch bracket. If she did that, the pressure in the walls remained a problem instead of a prophecy.

At the next crossing they nearly ran into Uther.

He came around the far turn in worn plate with a palace functionary hurrying behind him and stopped so hard the man almost struck his back. Big hands. Travel dirt on the surcoat hem. A face worn down past anger into hard lines.

"By the Light," the functionary started, then saw Sylvanas's knife and Jaina's staff and went white.

Uther understood faster. He took in Jaina, the corridor behind her, then the cold shivering through the stones. "You made it in."

"Barely."

"The king?"

"We don't know."

"Lord Uther, you cannot simply—"

"Be quiet." He was already looking toward the center of the palace. "How close?"

Sylvanas answered. "Close enough to smell it."

That won one grim line from his mouth.

The functionary tried again. "You cannot let armed—"

Uther turned on him and the rest died in the man's throat. "Run to your captain. Tell him the palace is compromised and if he has any sense left, he'll get his men out of the ceremonial halls and start checking the interior routes."

"My lord, I—"

"Run."

The man ran.

No one in the corridor needed anything explained.

Uther faced Jaina again. "Can it be done?"

Done.

"I know where the blade turns," she said. "I know the sequence. I don't know what the room will let me keep."

He took that without flinching. "Then you'll do it anyway."

"Yes."

His eyes tightened for a blink. "Good."

Keep the one man in the kingdom whose word might still matter in the room, or send him away to gather an Order that had already proved slower than catastrophe.

Jaina heard the answer in the walls before she put words on it. Frostmourne's rhythm had sharpened. Nearer. Whatever was happening in the throne room had already started.

"You can't make it in time by the front," she said.

"No."

Sylvanas checked the nearest service branch, then faced him again. "If you bring the Order, bring them for what comes after."

Uther's jaw worked. "And if there is no after?"

"Then you've lost nothing by not arriving to watch it happen."

He said nothing.

He stepped closer, voice low under the torch crackle. "If I reach the chamber too late, they'll say I wasn't there. They'll say I hated him for Stratholme and came wanting the rest."

The bread-taste went bitter. "I know."

"Then live long enough to say otherwise."

He went, heavy steps swallowed by the turns.

Sylvanas set a hand against the next service door. The last one before the chamber. Jaina could hear voices now through stone and wood and distance: one young, flattened by masonry into cold cadence; one older, thinner, carrying the brittle edge of a man speaking from a throne because he was too startled to rise.

Arthas.

Terenas.

Alive for the moment.

Jaina's nose started bleeding before the door opened. Warm wetness touched her lip. She wiped it with the back of her hand and found red in the torchlight.

Sylvanas saw the blood on Jaina's hand. "Can you still cast?"

"Yes."

"Then stop looking at your own blood."

The service door opened on a breath of cold clean air and the Imperial Chamber beyond.

White marble stretched between the service door and the throne. Columns in two long rows. Gallery shadows above. The golden seal of Lordaeron spread across the floor where the octagonal window threw late light in a bright sheet. At the far end, under the dome and before the throne, Arthas stood with Frostmourne bared in his hand.

Prince-shaped. Blond hair gone dull. Broad shoulders in plate dark around the joints with frost that did not melt. The line of the jaw she had once touched in rooms with no witnesses and no kingdoms in them.

Only the stillness had changed.

No living man stood that still with a sword in hand.

Terenas was half-risen from the throne, crown already crooked. His face was still blank with surprise.

Jaina found the seam at once.

There. In the blade's cycle. The hitch between taking and throwing. Frost first. Hold the turn. Arcane through the opening. Fire while it stayed bare.

Reach the sword.

Keep breathing.

Do not let him see you first.

Arthas said, perfectly level, "Succeeding you, Father."

Then he drove the blade in.

Terenas made a sound Jaina would have spent the rest of her life failing to name. Frostmourne did not cut the way other weapons cut. The sword drank. Surprise froze the king from the inside out.

The crown fell first.

Gold rang on marble and rolled through the inlaid lines of the seal, bright and absurd and public.

Sylvanas was already moving.

Her first arrow flew before Terenas finished collapsing. One heartbeat she was shadow and cloth behind a column. The next the shaft crossed the room in a black line and took Arthas through the throat.

Any living man would have died there.

Arthas rocked half a step. Blood welled dark around the wound. Then the flesh pulled itself closed, slow and obscene, reshaping around the wound.

His head turned toward them.

Concealment ended there.

"I wondered," he said.

His voice crossed the chamber without strain. Jaina had spent too long remembering warmth in it. The cold version found every old memory still lodged in her and iced it over.

Sylvanas had another arrow nocked. "And still you turned your back on the door."

"You were never the door I feared."

Terenas slid from the sword and struck the steps beneath the throne. Blood spread across the marble, bright where the sunlight caught it. His hand lay palm-up beside the signet ring, fingers still curling closed.

Move, she told herself.

Her feet obeyed a fraction late. She and Sylvanas split without speaking, left and right through the first row of columns, widening his sightline. Jaina kept the staff low. She needed one clean opening and a sword fixed on one enemy for half a second.

Arthas stepped clear of the dais.

Cold rolled low over the floor, pressure more than mist. The king's blood skinned over black-red and iced. Jaina's teeth hurt. Light from the high window turned hard-edged. The air took on the brittle feel of glass about to break.

He checked Terenas once, then turned back to them. "You came all this way to stop a coronation?"

"I came," Jaina said, "to end the thing carrying his face."

A thin smile pulled at his mouth.

Sylvanas loosed.

The second arrow took him high in the chest. The third came so fast behind it the fletching nearly clipped the first shaft. He moved for that one at last, faster than a man in plate had any right to move, and the arrow meant for his eye glanced from a burst of rime blown outward from Frostmourne's guard.

Then he came down the center aisle.

Jaina hated the remnants of the prince in that most of all. The careful footwork under the monster. The saved distance on the turn. The old training still visible beneath whatever now occupied him.

Sylvanas met him first because of course she did.

She broke from behind a column, loosed at near point-blank range, and was already sideways when Frostmourne tore through the space she had just left, shearing a thumb-deep slice from the marble base. Chips stung Jaina's cheek. Sylvanas dropped, slid, came up on one knee, knife in her off hand, bow still in the other.

"Jaina!"

The seam. Yes.

Jaina drove frost at the blade.

Not at Arthas. At the turn in the cycle where taking became casting. Ice ran along the fuller in a pale line. For one suspended instant the hunger-note changed pitch, caught.

Arthas flinched. Then he turned on her fully, his face hardening with violence, and she knew the theory had been right.

Good.

She hit the opening with arcane force before it could close.

She rammed blue pressure through the stalled turn, a wedge where the blade expected flow, and the sword cracked like lake ice taking weight.

Arthas shouted.

The sound came from farther back than his throat, low and inhuman.

Fire, Jaina thought, already reaching.

Too slow.

He tore the blade free of the lock with brutal strength. Frost exploded into needled shards. Frostmourne swung and the backlash from the broken turn struck Jaina square in the chest. She flew backward into a column hard enough to empty her lungs. Pain burst white along her spine. She slid to one knee and found her staff gone, skidding over marble.

Arthas was on her before the second inhale came.

Sylvanas bought it.

She shot him from eight paces, then six, then four, walking arrows into joints, neck seam, visor slit, any gap narrower than plate. The shafts hit hard enough to turn him and buy Jaina half-seconds. Arthas chopped one arrow out of the air, took another above the hip, and kept moving.

"Up," Sylvanas said.

Jaina got up because there was nothing else to do.

She yanked the staff back with arcane force and nearly lost her balance doing it. Arthas crossed the space between them in three strides. Sylvanas met him at the fourth, too close for the bow to matter now. She caught Frostmourne on the spine of the long knife for one shrieking instant that threw sparks across her knuckles, then spun off before the blade could take more than metal. The knife snapped anyway. Half of it spun away under the columns.

The wound in Arthas's throat was still knitting around the first arrow's path.

Held together by appetite with no mercy in it.

Jaina slammed a fan of ice across the floor between his feet. Arthas's leading boot lost purchase for a fraction. That was all Sylvanas ever asked from the world. She stepped inside his reach, drove the broken knife into the gap beneath his arm, and kicked off his cuisse to throw herself clear as Frostmourne ripped a blue-white wake through the air where her ribs had been.

He turned on her.

Seeing it happen made Jaina's stomach seize even though it had always been part of the plan.

Sylvanas drew him across the chamber in a running diagonal that used every column base, every shift in light, every line in the floor inlay that gave a heel more or less trust. She never retreated straight. Never gave him the center of her back. She made him earn each pace with a cut, a pivot, a violent correction, all while loosing from impossible angles and keeping him turned away from the lane Jaina needed.

An arrow glanced off his cheek and left a furrow that blackened at once. Another buried in the joint behind his knee. He went down once, not fully, only enough to brace a gauntlet against the floor while Frostmourne punched cold through the tiles in a starburst around him. Sylvanas used the heartbeat to clear distance. Jaina used it to rebuild her own.

Frost.

Hold the turn.

Arcane.

Open it wider.

Fire.

But practice-chamber timing and throne-room timing were different beasts.

Arthas rose and threw his free hand out.

A black-red surge tore from the field around the sword and struck the column Sylvanas had just passed. Marble gave under it the way soaked bread gives under teeth. The pillar groaned. Dust showered. A crack ran up through the carved laurel and vanished into the vaulting.

The room gave the sound back to them.

Sylvanas glanced upward. Only a glance. Enough.

Arthas closed and backhanded her with Frostmourne's guard before the blade came around. Metal hit flesh with a sound that rang in Jaina's own teeth. Sylvanas went sideways, struck the column, and dropped to one hand. The bow skidded from her grip. She pushed up and almost made it. The second cut opened from shoulder height toward her neck.

Jaina caught the blade in ice three inches short of Sylvanas's throat.

Everything in her locked at once—arms, jaw, spine. Frost crawled up Frostmourne in bright branching veins. The seam flashed under it.

There.

Sylvanas rolled away under the arrested cut with blood on one side of her face and murder still intact in both eyes.

"Now!" Jaina shouted.

She drove arcane power into the hitch so hard her hands went numb around the staff.

This time the sword truly cracked.

The blade stayed whole, but the rhythm tore open. The field around the blade spasmed outward in ugly pulses, taking from the chamber and throwing it back wrong. The king's spilled blood steamed, froze, steamed again. Jaina tasted hot copper and cold bread together until her stomach lurched.

Arthas staggered.

For the first time since entering the room, he looked unsure of his own footing.

Sylvanas did not waste uncertainty.

She came off the floor with blood at her temple and another arrow gripped as a short spear. The strike was ugly and practical, meant for the face. Arthas turned from it and took the arrowhead through the ruined seam of his cheek instead of the eye. The shaft snapped across his helm. That bought Jaina one more opening and cost Sylvanas the last clean retreat in that lane.

Frostmourne tore free of the ice with a cathedral-bell scream.

The backswing caught Sylvanas under the ribs.

Flat or guard, Jaina could not tell through the speed of it, only that the impact lifted Sylvanas clear of the floor and threw her into the base of the nearest column hard enough for her to stay down one breath too long.

"Sylvanas!"

Wrong name to spend air on. Arthas heard the attachment in it and turned for Jaina at once.

Good, another part of her answered. Better me while she breathes.

He came without speech now, plate grinding over plate, frost smoking from the edge of the sword. The chamber had stopped looking like a royal hall and started looking like a place gone storm-struck.

Jaina backed toward the seal at the center of the floor because the arc lines gave cleaner footing and because she needed the distance between him and Sylvanas more than she needed safety. Arthas stepped onto the gold after her and blackened it with rime.

She cast while retreating. Short work. Mean work. Blue lances at the visor slit. Ice spikes where the joints bent. A shove of force at the knee Sylvanas had already marked with an arrow. Nothing pretty. Every spell chosen to slow him.

He cut them out of the air or wore through them.

The seam kept flashing at the turn of the sword's hunger. Every time it did, her body tried to commit to the full sequence, and every time the next demand of survival broke the thought apart again. She needed one held instant.

Arthas raised his free hand.

She read the timing one breath too late.

The cold hit full-force.

It smashed into her chest and face and drove her skidding over the seal until her hip struck the first step of the dais. Ice flowered along the stone around her. The staff flew from her hand again and spun almost to Terenas's body before stopping.

She could not feel three fingers on her left hand.

Arthas advanced through the wreck of his own spell, slow now, certain now, sword dragging a white line across the marble. Jaina shoved herself backward with her palms. Her heel slid in the king's blood where it had frozen and thawed and frozen again.

The dead king lay at her side.

His eyes were open.

Death had stripped throne and title off him in the time it took to fall. He looked only old now. Old and betrayed in his own hall.

Arthas mounted the steps.

Jaina reached for the staff with arcane pull and got only a stuttering twitch in the wood. Too far. Too little strength. Her chest burned where the last blast had gone through her. The bread-taste had vanished at last, replaced by blood and the mineral bite of fear.

Then Sylvanas's voice cut the room in half.

"Here."

Hoarse. Furious. Alive.

Arthas turned.

The Ranger-General stood below the columns with one hand clamped over her side and the other drawing the longbow back to her ear on an arrow fletched black and grey. Narrow-headed. Built to pierce.

She had one clean line to him through two columns and the air above the king's blood.

He read the line. The sword began to come around.

That was the second Jaina had needed all along.

She planted one palm on the marble step and dragged the chamber's heat toward her.

There was little left. The room sat deep in winter cold. But sunlight still fell through the high window in a bright shaft over the seal, the braziers along the walls still carried embers, and a dead king's blood stayed warm under its skin of ice. Jaina still had enough magic to call fire if she did not care what it burned on the way through.

She did not care.

Frost first.

She struck the seam dead center, no broad wash this time, only a needle of killing cold precise as spite. The blade locked on the turn.

Arcane next.

She rammed power into the caught hitch until the chamber lights doubled and her teeth rang. The crack in Frostmourne opened, small, bright, ragged.

Fire.

Jaina gave it everything left.

The flame ran white-blue at the base where magic fed it, gold at the edges where the room's surviving heat answered. She had no staff. It went from her hands, ugly and direct, dragged straight through the opened seam. For one blinding moment Frostmourne became transparent to itself. Down the blade's length ran pure motion: the constant taking, the constant throwing, the trapped scream between them.

The scream got out.

It burst through the chamber in a soundless wave that hit skin, teeth, old scars, fresh bruises. Arthas jerked as if the sword had tried to pull his arm off with itself. Blackened frost blew off his armor in scales. His mouth opened.

Sylvanas loosed.

The arrow went through his eye and out the back of the helm.

Arthas simply stopped.

He stood there with the shaft buried to the fletching, Frostmourne still in his grip, and all the terrible motion gone from the room.

Then his knees went.

He hit the steps hard enough to jar the crown farther across the floor. One gauntleted hand struck marble and slid in his father's blood. The sword dropped with him but did not leave his grasp. Jaina smelled burned iron. Burned wool. Burned meat under the armor seams.

He looked up.

She had him.

Only emptied enough by pain and shock that the old lines showed through. Boyhood in his face, twisted by pain.

Then the soul-echo hit.

It came out of the opened sword and the opened man together.

Only fragments. A father's disappointment. A child's hunger for praise. Stratholme smoke. Snow over Northrend graves. The sick exultation of power first taken into exhausted hands. Uther in a training yard. Muradin laughing somewhere the sun still worked. Her own younger self turning toward Arthas after some muttered remark in council and failing not to laugh.

All of it slammed through her at once.

Jaina folded over on the step and gagged.

Tears came by force before sorrow found room to arrive. Her skull felt split. Her ears rang with voices that were not voices, memory skinned raw of context. She caught one last image before it tore apart: a little blond boy in a window alcove, wooden sword across his knees, waiting for a father who was late again.

Then even that was gone.

Stone and echo settled around her.

Arthas lay dead on the steps beneath the throne.

Frostmourne did not.

The sword was still singing. Quieter. Wounded. Furious in a new way. Cracks veined the blade near the seam. Thin light leaked through them and sucked back in. The hilt twitched once against the marble.

"Don't touch it," Jaina said, though Sylvanas was nowhere near foolish enough to try.

The Ranger-General had dropped to one knee below the columns, one arm around her ribs. Blood soaked her leathers dark under the left breast. Her face had gone wax-pale. Still she said, "I wasn't planning to."

Jaina got one foot under herself, then the other. The room had become all distance and angles: Terenas on the floor to her left, Arthas in front of her, crown below, sword between, Sylvanas across the chamber breathing shallow. Somewhere beyond the chamber, men were shouting orders at last.

Boots sounded in the corridors.

Too many.

If they ran, they would have to leave by the same service lanes they had used to enter, dragging one wounded ranger through whatever panic had already sealed those passages. If they stayed and tried to explain with a dead king, a dead prince, a soul-drinking sword, and their own blood all over the room, first sight would drown any explanation.

Two armed women in a throne room. The king butchered. The prince down. Bow in hand. Mage reeking of burnt spellwork. There was no first glance that turned kindly.

Sylvanas read the same arithmetic from across the marble. She pushed upright against the column and almost hid the wince. "Can you move?"

"Yes."

"Far?"

Jaina measured her body and hated the answer. "Not usefully."

Commands were breaking over one another at the far doors now. Then one shout carried through the chamber galleries.

"Open!"

Palace captain, she thought. Too young for Uther.

She took one step toward Sylvanas anyway. Then another. The floor dipped oddly under her and she understood the real trouble: her left leg had gone numb from hip to ankle where the cold blast had hit.

Frostmourne gave a sharp insectile note.

Both women stopped.

The sword dragged half an inch over the marble with no hand on it. Light leaked through the crack again. For one sick instant Jaina thought it would rise by itself. Instead the blade shuddered and went still.

Still alive.

"We need it contained," Jaina said.

"Do you have a containment spell left in you?"

"None I'd trust."

Sylvanas's laugh was small and raw. "Then let the paladins do one useful thing today."

The great side doors boomed inward.

Armed men flooded the threshold and then checked so hard the first rank almost collided with the second. They had expected violence. They took it in one piece at a time. The king on the steps. The prince below the throne. The sword between. Blood. Ice. A cracked column. The black-feathered arrow through Arthas's eye. Jaina in scorched blue and silver half on the dais. Sylvanas with bow in hand and blood down her side.

The captain found his voice first. "Drop your weapons!"

Jaina nearly said anything at all—look at the room, we killed him, you're standing in the answer. None of it would have reached them. They were already looking at her and Sylvanas, not the sword.

More boots sounded in the corridor. Heavier this time. Ordered.

The Silver Hand.

Uther came through the press of guards bareheaded, hair damp at his temples, warhammer in his fist and three paladins at his back. He took in the king, and whatever in him had remained held together only because action was still possible broke in one silent visible place across his face.

Then he turned to Arthas.

Dead at last on the steps below the throne.

He took one stride into the chamber before the palace captain caught his arm. "My lord, stand back. These women—"

Uther shook him off hard enough to stagger the man. He checked Jaina, then Sylvanas, then Frostmourne, and his face hardened back into command because the sword was still the largest danger in the room whether anyone else understood it or not.

"No one touches the blade," he said.

Jaina tried to speak and coughed blood instead. Enough.

Uther was at the foot of the dais in three strides. Close enough now for her to see the red rims of his eyes and the wetness he had not let fall.

"Report," he said.

He asked it like a commander because asking it any other way would have broken him.

Jaina swallowed iron. "Terenas was dead before we entered. Sylvanas's first arrow failed. The sword closed the wound. We hit the seam. Frost, arcane, then fire. She took the final shot."

Uther took in Arthas again, the arrow buried through helm and skull, then the cracked sword. "And the blade?"

"Damaged. Still active."

"Can it move?"

"I don't know."

Behind him the captain had recovered enough certainty to be dangerous. "My lord, by all appearances—"

"By all appearances," Uther said without turning, "you arrived after the king's murder, after the prince's death, and after these two spent what remained of themselves stopping whatever that sword was doing to your palace. So I would advise caution with appearances."

The palace captain's jaw set. "Then they are to be treated as witnesses?"

Jaina almost laughed. It came out as another cough.

Uther turned back to her. Terenas lay dead at her side, Arthas below the throne with an arrow through him, and Sylvanas Windrunner stood bleeding across Lordaeron's throne room. If he declared for them outright, half the palace would hear betrayal. If he left the captain to panic, fear would turn to chains and blades.

"They are under my authority until this chamber is secured," he said. "Disarm them. Carefully. Then put them in custody."

Sylvanas's shoulders went still across the room.

The archers at the door tightened at once.

Jaina forced herself down the last step of the dais. Her staff turned useless in her hand. She set it on the marble. The sound was small. Final enough anyway.

Sylvanas measured her across the room.

Measuring.

Jaina gave the faintest nod.

Because neither of them had enough left to cut through thirty men and a ring of paladins while a damaged soul-drinking sword twitched at their feet. Because living long enough to speak later, as Uther had told her in the corridor, was the only work left that might still matter.

Sylvanas unstrung the bow with steady hands and laid it down beside her.

Only then did the guards advance.

They came in too fast despite the order for care. Two spears leveled at Sylvanas before the first gauntlet reached for her wrists. Another pair closed on Jaina with the brittle bravado of soldiers who needed her helpless before their courage ran out.

Uther checked one with a forearm. "Mind the mage."

The guard muttered an apology he did not mean. Jaina let them take her hands anyway. The iron around her wrists was common restraint, laughable if her reserves had been anything but rags. Cold metal touched skin already numbed by colder things. The tremor in her fingers started only after the cuffs closed.

Across the chamber, Sylvanas stood upright while they bound her, blood soaking steadily through her leathers, face blank as bark. One man jerked the cord too hard. She looked at him once, and he went grey around the mouth.

Paladins moved around Frostmourne in a wary crescent. No one touched it. One began praying under his breath. Another set the hammer-head of his weapon against the marble, ready to strike if the sword twitched again.

Jaina turned her head enough to find Terenas one last time.

They had already draped a blue velvet cloak over him. Wrong color for the blood beneath it. The crown lay on its side three steps below, one point bent.

Arthas had no covering.

She could not decide whether that was justice or only shock.

The captain pointed toward the side exit. "Take them below."

Cells, then. Old holding rooms under the palace where courtiers never went.

Jaina's legs nearly failed on the first step. One of the guards hauled her upright more roughly than necessary. Sylvanas saw it. She turned her head enough to let him know she would remember him if either of them ever walked free again.

Uther fell in beside them for the first stretch to the door. Close enough that no frightened man would decide a bound prisoner needed the back of a spear while moving.

He did not look at Jaina when he said, very low, "I will get the sword sealed."

"It isn't done," she whispered.

"I know."

"The crack."

"I know."

That was all the corridor gave them.

The chamber passed out of sight behind her. Marble, dome, throne, blood, crown, son, father, sword. The walls turned to service stone almost at once.

The bread-taste came back faintly as they descended.

From the actual kitchens somewhere below, where dough would already be rising for the evening meal.
