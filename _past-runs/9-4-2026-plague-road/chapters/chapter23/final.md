# Chapter 23: The Throne Room

The latch settled behind them. Antonidas's blue-white light had already gone.

Jaina stood in palace service dark with Sylvanas ahead of her, bow low, shoulders angled toward a corridor that smelled of lye, old grease, and stone scrubbed slick by years of traffic. If the room ahead went wrong, no one behind that door would reach it in time.

Jaina stopped. Cold pushed up through her boots and into her teeth.

Frostmourne pulled from deeper in the passage, a cold knot under the mortar ahead. The bread-taste followed: yeast, salt, warm crust torn open at table height. Wrong enough in Northrend. Here it turned her stomach.

Ahead, Cyndia leaned from a side turn, one hand raised. Two fingers. Then she pointed: left branch clear, right branch occupied deeper in.

Sylvanas shifted that way. "How many?"

"Two," Jaina whispered. "Not breathing."

Cyndia's mouth flattened. The ranger beside her eased out an arrow. "We keep the passage."

No promise. No dramatics.

Sylvanas turned back once. "With me."

Jaina went.

The sentries stood before the last iron-banded service door into the Imperial Chamber's flank. At twenty paces they could have passed for weary palace men. At ten the trick broke. They held too still. Skin showed pale above the gorgets, waxy in the torchlight. Frostmourne's hum answered through them in splinters.

Sylvanas held up one finger.

Then she moved.

One instant she was a lean shadow against the wall. The next she had crossed the corridor and driven a knife up under the nearer helm. Metal clicked against teeth. The corpse folded. Jaina locked the second guard's joints with frost as it started to turn, halberd snagging on its own shaft, and Sylvanas put the knife through the throat seam.

Armor hit stone. Once. Twice.

No cry. No alarm.

Somewhere beyond two walls scullery crockery rattled. Someone laughed. A servant's shoes scuffed over tile, careless and alive. The dead held the inner hall while the living still carried trays around them.

Sylvanas wiped the blade on a tabard. "Still with me?"

"Yes."

"Good. I'm not carrying you until after."

On another road it might have been funny. Here it steadied her.

They moved deeper into the passage network, past service stairs, linen shelves, and a bend clogged by a mop bucket and a broken crate.

At the next crossing they nearly ran into Uther.

He came around the far turn in worn plate with a palace functionary hurrying behind him and stopped hard. Travel dirt on the surcoat hem. A face worn down past anger.

"By the Light," the functionary started, then saw Sylvanas's knife and Jaina's staff and went white.

Uther took in Jaina, the corridor behind her, then the cold ticking through the stones. "You made it in."

"Barely."

"The king?"

"We don't know."

"Lord Uther, you cannot simply—"

"Be quiet." He was already looking toward the center of the palace. "How close?"

"Close enough to smell it," Sylvanas said.

The functionary opened his mouth again. Uther cut him off without looking at him. "Run to your captain. Tell him the palace is compromised and if he has any sense left, he'll pull his men out of the ceremonial halls and check the interior routes."

The man ran.

Uther faced Jaina again. "Can it be done?"

Jaina wiped her upper lip with the back of her wrist. "I know where the blade turns. I know the sequence. I don't know what survives the cast."

"Then you'll do it anyway."

"Yes."

"Good."

Cold quickened in the wall stones. Arthas was already in the chamber with the king.

"You can't make it in time by the front," she said.

"No."

"If you bring the Order," Sylvanas said, "bring them for what comes after."

Uther's jaw worked. "And if there is no after?"

"Then you've lost nothing by not arriving to watch it happen."

He stepped closer, voice low. "If I reach the chamber too late, they'll say I wasn't there. They'll say I hated him for Stratholme and came wanting the rest."

The bread-taste went bitter. "I know."

"Then live long enough to say otherwise."

He went.

Sylvanas set a hand against the next service door.

Voices came through stone and wood now: one young, flattened by masonry into cold cadence; one older, thin and brittle from a throne.

Arthas.

Terenas.

Alive, by the sound of him.

Jaina's nose started bleeding before the door opened. Warm wetness touched her lip. She wiped it with the back of her hand and found red in the torchlight.

Sylvanas saw it. "Can you still cast?"

"Yes."

"Then stop looking at your own blood."

The service door opened on cold clean air, the hush of ceremony, and the Imperial Chamber beyond.

White marble stretched between the service door and the throne. Columns in two long rows. Heavy curtains banked along the service flank. Spectator galleries shadowed above. The golden seal of Lordaeron spread across the floor where the octagonal window threw late light in a bright sheet. At the far end, under the dome and before the throne, Arthas stood with Frostmourne bared in his hand.

Prince-shaped. Blond hair gone dull. Broad shoulders in plate dark around the joints with frost that did not melt.

Only the stillness had changed.

Terenas was half-risen from the throne dais, crown already crooked, one hand knotted on the throne arm.

Jaina found the seam at once.

There. In the blade's cycle. The hitch between taking and throwing. Frost first. Hold the turn. Arcane through the opening. Fire while it stayed bare.

Reach the sword.

Keep breathing.

Arthas said, perfectly level, "Succeeding you, Father."

Then he drove the blade in.

Terenas made a sound Jaina would have spent the rest of her life failing to name. Frostmourne fed through the wound. Surprise froze the king from the inside out.

The crown fell first.

Gold rang on marble and rolled through the inlaid lines of the seal, bright and absurd and public.

Sylvanas was already moving.

Her first arrow flew before Terenas finished collapsing. The shaft crossed the room in a black line and took Arthas through the throat.

Blood welled dark around it, and still he did not fall.

Arthas rocked half a step. Then the flesh pulled itself closed, slow and obscene, reshaping around the wound.

His head turned toward them.

Concealment ended there.

"I wondered," he said.

His voice crossed the chamber without strain. The old warmth she remembered from it dropped out of her so fast her grip slipped on the staff.

Sylvanas had another arrow nocked. "And still you turned your back on the door."

"You were never the door I feared."

Terenas slid from the sword and struck the steps beneath the throne. Blood spread across the marble, bright where the sunlight caught it.

Move, she told herself.

She and Sylvanas split without speaking, left and right through the first row of columns, widening his sightline. Jaina kept the staff low. She needed one clean opening and a sword fixed on one enemy for half a second.

Arthas stepped clear of the dais.

Cold rolled low over the floor. The king's blood skinned over black-red and iced. Jaina's teeth hurt.

"You came all this way to stop a coronation?"

"I came," Jaina said, "to end the thing carrying his face."

A thin smile pulled at his mouth.

Sylvanas loosed.

The second arrow took him high in the chest. The third came so fast behind it the fletching nearly clipped the first shaft. He moved for that one at last, faster than a man in plate had any right to move, and the arrow meant for his eye glanced from a burst of rime blown outward from Frostmourne's guard.

Then he came down the center aisle over the golden seal.

What turned her stomach was the familiar economy of it—the saved step, the shoulder drop before the cut—still sitting inside that dead cold.

Sylvanas met him first because of course she did.

She broke from behind a column, loosed at near point-blank range, and was already sideways when Frostmourne tore through the space she had just left, shearing a thumb-deep slice from the marble base. Chips stung Jaina's cheek. Sylvanas dropped, slid, came up on one knee, knife in her off hand, bow still in the other.

"Jaina!"

The seam. Yes.

Jaina drove frost at the blade.

Not at Arthas. At the turn where taking became casting. Ice ran along the fuller in a pale line. For one suspended instant the hunger-note changed pitch, caught.

Arthas flinched. Then he turned on her fully, fast and violent, as the caught note dragged his attention off Sylvanas and onto the blade.

Good.

She hit the opening with arcane force before it could close.

Blue pressure rammed through the stalled turn, a wedge into the channel, and the sword cracked like lake ice taking weight.

Arthas shouted.

Fire, Jaina thought.

Too slow.

He tore the blade free of the lock. Frost exploded into needles. The backlash caught Jaina square in the chest and threw her into a column hard enough to empty her lungs. Her staff skidded over marble.

Arthas was on her before the second inhale came.

Sylvanas bought the breath with arrows—eight paces, then six, then four—turning him one hard hit at a time.

"Up," Sylvanas said.

Jaina got up. She yanked the staff back with arcane force as Arthas crossed the space between them. Sylvanas met him too close for the bow to matter, caught Frostmourne on the spine of the long knife for one shrieking instant, and lost the knife anyway.

Jaina slammed a fan of ice across the floor between his feet. Arthas slipped. Sylvanas stepped inside it, drove the broken knife into the gap beneath his arm, and kicked clear before Frostmourne ripped through the air where her ribs had been.

He turned on her.

Jaina's stomach seized as Sylvanas gave him the diagonal, one column base at a time, buying Jaina's opening with her own body.

An arrow furrowed his cheek. Another buried in the joint behind his knee. He went down once, only enough to brace a gauntlet against the floor while Frostmourne punched cold through the tiles in a starburst around him.

Frost.

Hold the turn.

Arcane.

Open it wider.

Fire.

Arthas rose and threw his free hand out.

A black-red surge tore from the field around the sword and struck the column Sylvanas had just passed. Marble gave. The pillar groaned. Dust showered. A crack ran up through the carved laurel and vanished into the vaulting.

Sylvanas looked up once.

Enough.

Arthas closed and backhanded her with Frostmourne's guard before the blade came around. Metal hit flesh with a sound that rang in Jaina's own teeth. Sylvanas went sideways, struck the column, and dropped to one hand. She pushed up and almost made it. The second cut opened toward her neck.

Jaina caught the blade in ice three inches short of Sylvanas's throat.

Everything in her locked at once—arms, jaw, spine. Frost crawled up Frostmourne in bright branching veins. The seam flashed under it.

There.

Sylvanas rolled away under the arrested cut with blood on one side of her face and murder still intact.

"Now!" Jaina shouted.

She drove arcane power into the hitch so hard her hands went numb around the staff.

This time the sword truly cracked.

The blade stayed whole, but the rhythm tore open. The field around it spasmed outward in ugly pulses, taking from the chamber and throwing it back wrong. The king's spilled blood steamed, froze, steamed again. Jaina tasted hot copper and cold bread together until her stomach lurched.

Arthas staggered.

Sylvanas did not waste it.

She came off the floor with blood at her temple and another arrow gripped as a short spear. The strike was ugly and practical, meant for the face. Arthas turned from it and took the arrowhead through the ruined seam of his cheek instead. The shaft snapped across his helm.

Frostmourne tore free of the ice with a cathedral-bell scream.

The backswing caught Sylvanas under the ribs.

Flat or guard, Jaina could not tell, only that the impact lifted Sylvanas clear of the floor and threw her into the base of the nearest column hard enough for her to stay down one breath too long.

"Sylvanas!"

Wrong name to spend air on. Arthas heard the attachment in it and turned for Jaina at once.

Good, another part of her answered. Better me while she breathes.

The gold seal was rimed black, one column was cracked to the laurel, and frost smoke dragged low across the marble.

Jaina backed toward the seal because she needed the distance between him and Sylvanas more than she needed safety. Arthas stepped onto the gold after her and blackened it with rime.

She cast while retreating. Short work. Mean work. Blue lances at the visor slit. Ice spikes where the joints bent. A shove of force at the knee Sylvanas had already marked with an arrow. Nothing pretty. Every spell chosen to slow him.

He cut them out of the air or wore through them.

The seam kept flashing at the turn of the sword's hunger. Each flash dragged her hands toward frost, arcane, fire. Then the next cut, the next slip of footing, tore the order apart again.

Arthas raised his free hand.

She read the timing one breath too late.

The cold hit full-force.

It smashed into her chest and face and drove her skidding over the seal until her hip struck the first step of the dais. Ice flowered along the stone around her. The staff flew from her hand again and spun almost to Terenas's body.

She could not feel three fingers on her left hand.

Arthas advanced through the wreck of his own spell, slow now, certain now, sword dragging a white line across the marble. Jaina shoved herself backward with her palms. Her heel slid in the king's blood where it had frozen and thawed and frozen again.

The dead king lay at her side.

His eyes were open.

Death had stripped throne and title off him in the time it took to fall. He looked only old now. Old and betrayed in his own hall.

Arthas mounted the steps.

Jaina reached for the staff with arcane pull and got only a stuttering twitch in the wood. Too far. Too little strength. Her chest burned where the last blast had gone through her. The bread-taste had vanished at last, replaced by blood and the mineral bite of fear.

Then Sylvanas's voice cut the room in half.

"Here."

Hoarse. Furious. Alive.

Arthas turned.

The Ranger-General stood below the columns with one hand clamped over her side and the other drawing the longbow back to her ear on an arrow fletched black and grey. Narrow-headed. Built to pierce.

She had one clean line to him through two columns and the air above the king's blood.

He read the line. The sword began to come around.

That was the second Jaina had needed all along.

She planted one palm on the marble step and dragged the chamber's heat toward her.

There was little left. The room sat deep in winter cold. But sunlight still fell through the high window in a bright shaft over the seal, the braziers along the walls still carried embers, and a dead king's blood stayed warm under its skin of ice. Jaina still had enough magic to call fire if she did not care what it burned on the way through.

She did not care.

Frost first.

She struck the seam dead center, no broad wash this time, only a needle of killing cold precise as spite. The blade locked on the turn.

Arcane next.

She rammed power into the caught hitch until the chamber lights doubled and her teeth rang. The crack in Frostmourne opened, small, bright, ragged.

Fire.

Jaina gave it everything left.

Light ran inside the blade. Cracks flared along the fuller, and something trapped in the steel hit the chamber hard enough to rattle her teeth.

The scream got out.

It burst through the chamber in a soundless wave that hit skin, teeth, old scars, fresh bruises. Arthas jerked as if the sword had tried to pull his arm off with itself. Blackened frost blew off his armor in scales. His mouth opened.

Sylvanas loosed.

The arrow went through his eye and out the back of the helm.

Arthas simply stopped.

Then his knees went.

He hit the steps hard enough to jar the crown farther across the floor. One gauntleted hand struck marble and slid in his father's blood. The sword dropped with him but did not leave his grasp. Jaina smelled burned iron, burned wool, burned meat under the armor seams.

He looked up.

She had him.

Only emptied enough by pain and shock that the old lines showed through. Boyhood in his face, twisted by pain.

Then the soul-echo hit.

It came out of the opened sword and the opened man together.

Fragments slammed through her: Uther's voice carrying across a training yard, Stratholme smoke in her nose, Northrend glare off snow, a child's hands slick on a wooden practice sword, Muradin laughing against stone, her own younger self turning at some half-muttered council remark and failing not to laugh.

Jaina folded over on the step and gagged.

Tears came by force before she had room for sorrow. Her skull felt split. Her ears rang with voices that were not voices, memory skinned raw of context.

Then even that was gone.

Stone and echo settled around her.

Arthas lay dead on the steps beneath the throne.

Frostmourne still answered on the marble.

The sword was still singing. Quieter. Wounded. The note kept snagging on the crack near the seam. Cracks veined the blade there. Thin light leaked through them and sucked back in. The hilt twitched once against the marble.

"Don't touch it," Jaina said.

The Ranger-General had dropped to one knee below the columns, one arm around her ribs. Blood soaked her leathers dark under the left breast. Her face had gone wax-pale.

"I wasn't planning to."

Jaina got one foot under herself, then the other. Terenas lay to her left, Arthas in front of her, the crown below the steps, the sword between them, Sylvanas across the chamber breathing shallow.

Boots sounded in the corridors.

Too many.

If they ran, they would have to leave by the same service lanes they had used to enter, dragging one wounded ranger through whatever panic had already sealed those passages. If they stayed, first sight would drown any explanation.

Across the marble, Sylvanas pushed upright against the column and almost hid the wince. "Can you move?"

"Yes."

"Far?"

Jaina measured her body and hated the answer. "Not usefully."

Commands were breaking over one another at the far doors now. Then one shout carried through the chamber galleries.

"Open!"

Palace captain, she thought. Too young for Uther.

She took one step toward Sylvanas anyway. Then another. The floor dipped oddly under her and she understood the real trouble: her left leg had gone numb from hip to ankle where the cold blast had hit.

Frostmourne gave a sharp insectile note.

Both women stopped.

The sword dragged half an inch over the marble with no hand on it. Light leaked through the crack again. For one sick instant Jaina thought it would rise by itself. Instead the blade shuddered and went still.

Still alive.

"We need it contained," Jaina said.

"Do you have a containment spell left in you?"

"None I'd trust."

Sylvanas's laugh was small and raw. "Then let the paladins do one useful thing today."

The great side doors boomed inward.

Armed men flooded the threshold and then checked so hard the first rank almost collided with the second. They had expected violence. The chamber handed them a verdict: the king on the steps, the prince below the throne, the sword between them, blood, ice, a cracked column, the black-feathered arrow through Arthas's eye.

The captain found his voice first. "Drop your weapons!"

Jaina nearly said anything at all—look at the room, we killed him. None of it would have reached them. They were already looking at her and Sylvanas, not the sword.

More boots sounded in the corridor. Heavier this time. Ordered.

The Silver Hand.

Uther came through the press of guards bareheaded, hair damp at his temples, warhammer in his fist and three paladins at his back. He took in the king, then Arthas.

He took one stride into the chamber before the palace captain caught his arm. "My lord, stand back. These women—"

Uther shook him off hard enough to stagger the man. He checked Jaina, then Sylvanas, then Frostmourne, and his face hardened back into command.

"No one touches the blade," he said.

Jaina tried to speak and coughed blood instead.

Uther was at the foot of the dais in three strides. Close enough now for her to see the red rims of his eyes and the wetness he had not let fall.

"Report," he said.

Jaina swallowed iron. "Terenas was dead before we entered. Sylvanas's first arrow failed. The sword closed the wound. We hit the seam. Frost, arcane, then fire. She took the final shot."

Uther took in Arthas again, the arrow buried through helm and skull, then the cracked sword. "And the blade?"

"Damaged. Still active."

"Can it move?"

"I don't know."

Behind him the captain had recovered enough certainty to be dangerous. "My lord, by all appearances—"

"By all appearances," Uther said without turning, "you arrived after the king's murder, after the prince's death, and with that sword still active between them. So I would advise caution with appearances."

The palace captain's jaw set. "Then they are to be treated as witnesses?"

Jaina almost laughed. It came out as another cough.

Uther turned back to her. The captain's men already had spearpoints up between the dais and the doors.

"They are under my authority until this chamber is secured," he said. "Disarm them. Carefully. Then put them in custody."

Sylvanas's shoulders went still across the room.

The archers at the door tightened at once.

Jaina forced herself down the last step of the dais. Her staff turned useless in her hand. She set it on the marble.

Across the room, Sylvanas measured her.

Jaina gave the faintest nod.

Sylvanas unstrung the bow with steady hands and laid it down beside her.

Only then did the guards advance.

They came in too fast despite the order for care. Two spears leveled at Sylvanas before the first gauntlet reached for her wrists. Another pair closed on Jaina with brittle bravado.

Uther checked one with a forearm. "Mind the mage."

The guard muttered an apology he did not mean. Jaina let them take her hands anyway. The iron around her wrists was common restraint, laughable if her reserves had been anything but rags. Cold metal touched skin already numbed by colder things. The tremor in her fingers started only after the cuffs closed.

Across the chamber, Sylvanas stood upright while they bound her, blood soaking steadily through her leathers, face blank as bark. One man jerked the cord too hard. She looked at him once, and he went grey around the mouth.

Paladins moved around Frostmourne in a wary crescent. No one touched it. One began praying under his breath. Another set the hammer-head of his weapon against the marble, ready to strike if the sword twitched again.

Jaina turned her head enough to find Terenas one last time.

They had already draped a blue velvet cloak over him. Wrong color for the blood beneath it. The crown lay on its side three steps below, one point bent.

Arthas had no covering.

The captain pointed toward the side exit. "Take them below."

Cells, then. Old holding rooms under the palace where courtiers never went.

Jaina's legs nearly failed on the first step. One of the guards hauled her upright more roughly than necessary. Sylvanas saw it. She turned her head enough to let him know she would remember him if either of them ever walked free again.

Uther fell in beside them for the first stretch to the door. Close enough that no frightened man would decide a bound prisoner needed the back of a spear while moving.

He did not look at Jaina when he said, very low, "I will get the sword sealed."

"It isn't done," she whispered.

"I know."

"The crack."

"I know."

That was all the corridor gave them.

The chamber passed out of sight behind her. Marble, dome, throne, blood, crown, son, father, sword. The walls turned to service stone and kitchen lye almost at once.

The bread-taste came back faintly as they descended.

From the actual kitchens somewhere below, where dough would already be rising for the evening meal.
