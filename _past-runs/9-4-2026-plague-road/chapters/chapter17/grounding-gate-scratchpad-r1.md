# Grounding Gate Scratchpad — Chapter 17, Pass r1

## KEEP Decisions (survivals from deeper scrutiny)

### KEEP 1 — "chose motion over thought" (line 15)
**Category:** Inference language  
**Excerpt:** "One guard looked at Uther, then at Arthas, then at Falric already moving, and chose motion over thought."  
**Defense:** Borderline but survives. The physical sequence (look at A, look at B, look at C) grounds the decision chain. "Chose motion over thought" is Sylvanas's tactical inference — she reads that look-sequence as a man who stopped thinking and started following. The inference is earned by the concrete eye-tracking that precedes it.  
**Survived because:** Physical evidence presented before the inference. Sylvanas is a commander; reading decision patterns in men is her established skill.

### KEEP 2 — "the smallest pause Sylvanas had heard in years" (line 69)
**Category:** Temporal hyperbole  
**Excerpt:** "Another handful followed after the smallest pause Sylvanas had heard in years."  
**Defense:** Scrutinized hard. "In years" is retrospective, and "heard" a pause is technically synesthetic. But this is Sylvanas's interior voice — a ranger-commander who measures hesitation like she measures distance. The pause between following Uther and following Arthas IS audible (bootfalls stop, then resume). "In years" registers as her professional calibration, not narrator editorializing.  
**Survived because:** Interior voice of a professional measuring professional hesitation. The "hearing" of a pause is defensible from a character whose survival depends on reading gaps in sound.

### KEEP 3 — "Correct. / Monstrous." (lines 169-171)
**Category:** Assessment language  
**Excerpt:** Two one-word paragraphs.  
**Defense:** Already kept in pass 0. Re-examined. These arrive AFTER three paragraphs of concrete observation (districts, teams, ordered cuts, doors in rhythm). They are Sylvanas's judgment, not emotional labels. The chapter plan demands she hold tactical coherence and moral horror simultaneously. These two words are the minimal possible expression of that dual read.  
**Survived because:** Assessment earned by evidence. Removing them would lose the chapter's central tension.

### KEEP 4 — "Never enough to make a man out of. Only command." (line 223)
**Category:** Abstraction in observation  
**Excerpt:** "A glimpse of white-blond at a crossing. The flash of a plate shoulder through smoke. A mounted figure at the center of a knot of runners. Never enough to make a man out of. Only command."  
**Defense:** The preceding three details are distance-appropriate: hair color, armor flash, mounted silhouette. "Never enough to make a man out of" is Sylvanas's reading of her own visual evidence — she's noting that Arthas has become fragmentary, reduced to glimpses. "Only command" is the tactical conclusion: the man has been absorbed into the operation. This is a commander reading another commander at distance, not narrator psychology.  
**Survived because:** Evidence-first, inference-second. The abstraction summarizes concrete observations already on the page.

### KEEP 5 — "the ugly small noises of a city after human voices had been worked out of it" (line 225)
**Category:** Potential emotional label ("ugly")  
**Excerpt:** Full line.  
**Defense:** "Ugly" is borderline, but "small noises" and "human voices had been worked out of it" do physical work. "Worked out" reads as process (the city was systematically cleared of people), not metaphor. The sounds described are timber settling, wind, fire — mechanical sounds replacing organic ones. Sylvanas, as a military observer, would categorize the acoustic shift. "Ugly" is her judgment, not the narrator's.  
**Survived because:** The metaphor resolves into a concrete acoustic observation. "Worked out" carries both the violence and the systematicity.

### KEEP 6 — "a gesture sharp enough to cut air" (line 259)
**Category:** Dead metaphor  
**Excerpt:** "gathered the last of his officers with a gesture sharp enough to cut air"  
**Defense:** Borderline cliché. However, "sharp enough to cut air" at least conveys speed and precision of a physical arm movement, and it arrives at the chapter's final beat where Arthas is defined by his efficiency. The alternative — describing the exact arm motion — would slow the exit when the chapter needs to close.  
**Survived because:** Speed-of-close defense. The chapter needs to end on Arthas walking away, not on anatomical description of his beckoning arm.

### KEEP 7 — "Districts. Pieces a commander could work." (line 163)
**Category:** Tactical shorthand  
**Excerpt:** "Districts. Pieces a commander could work. Teams moving in ordered cuts through streets already divided in some commander's head."  
**Defense:** Fragment-style tactical read from Sylvanas's professional register. "Pieces a commander could work" is how she thinks. "In some commander's head" — she can see the grid pattern imposed on streets and infers that someone planned it. The inference stays at a level consistent with her expertise.  
**Survived because:** Professional inference from visible evidence. The grid is on the ground; the mind behind it is reasonable deduction.

### KEEP 8 — "put the city down before dusk could hand it to the dead" (line 73)
**Category:** Personification (dusk "handing")  
**Excerpt:** End of the passage about Arthas mounting.  
**Defense:** Borderline. "Dusk could hand it to the dead" personifies a time of day. But this is Sylvanas's tactical framing — nightfall IS the operational deadline when the plague's mass-turn becomes unstoppable. In military planning, time windows are routinely spoken of as "giving" the enemy advantage. This reads as ranger shorthand, not literary ornament.  
**Survived because:** Military idiom. Rangers talk about windows, deadlines, and what the enemy gains if you miss them. "Dusk handing it to the dead" is how Sylvanas would price the timeline.
