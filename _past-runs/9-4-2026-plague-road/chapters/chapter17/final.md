# Chapter 17: The Gate

No.

Jaina's answer rang beneath Stratholme's southern crestwork, and the line under the main arch locked where it stood. The prince in road-dusted plate. Uther with his hammer low beside the saddle. Guards packed along the iron leaves and the chain-winch. Beyond them the broad throat of the city opened toward Market Row, with carts still rattling somewhere inside as if an ordinary bell could still sort the hour.

Arthas's face emptied.

Sylvanas knew anger in a body. Anger had heat, waste, movement. This was cleaner. He set his mouth and became all edge.

He turned enough for Falric to hear.

"Seal the gates once the column is through. Nobody leaves without my order."

Falric moved before the sentence cleared the arch. Men ran for the chains. Others heaved a brewer's dray out of the lane. One guard looked at Uther, then at Arthas, then at Falric already moving, and chose motion over thought.

Jaina stood where she had given the answer, one hand still grey with warehouse flour. Ash marked the cuff of her sleeve. Her knuckles were raw from the staff. Smaller without the magic. Harder hit.

Arthas faced the city.

"Every man who has eaten the grain is already lost," he said, voice level enough to carry farther than a shout. "If we wait, the dead take the whole of it. We go in now. Street by street. House by house. Kill the infected before they turn."

Alonsus Chapel's bells were going.

Uther stepped forward. No rush. No uncertainty. The hammer head hung low, but final.

"No."

Arthas looked at him then.

"They're dead already," he said.

"They are alive until you kill them."

That moved through the line faster than any speech. Boots shifted on the gate mud. A paladin at Uther's shoulder tightened his gauntlet on the reins. A soldier by the winch went pale under the road dirt.

Arthas kept his voice level.

"Then stand aside."

Jaina found hers in the same instant. That ruined hand closed once on the staff.

"I won't help you do this."

His face stayed blank.

"I didn't ask."

Arthas looked from one refusal to the next and cast both off. "Those who won't act can leave the field. Falric."

"My prince."

"Take the first companies through Market Row. Burn what you must. Any house that resists goes to the torch."

Falric stayed stone-faced. "Yes, my prince."

Uther drew the line where everyone could see it, under the crest of the city that had founded his order.

"Then I take every knight and soldier who still knows the difference with me."

The men at the gate felt that one. Leather scraped. Mail shifted. A horse sidled into the iron leaf and rang it once.

Arthas never slowed.

"Take them."

He gave the order and moved before the words finished clearing the arch.

Uther took it without bending. He wheeled his horse instead. A handful went with him at once. Another handful followed after the smallest pause Sylvanas had heard in years.

Jaina made no move toward either man.

Arthas climbed back into the saddle. His mount sidestepped once, catching the charge in him. He put it under himself with the same cold control that had once looked like princely training and now looked like something meaner. A man pared down to one task: put the city down before dusk could hand it to the dead.

He looked through the arch toward the streets beyond the formal approach the way she studied ground when the kill lines finally showed.

Then he rode in.

Companies followed. Boots. Hooves. Orders cut short and obeyed faster. They went under the crest and into Stratholme by files.

Sylvanas caught Jaina's elbow, hard enough to get bone.

"Move."

Jaina came, jaw locked, because Sylvanas pulled and she did not resist.

Cyndia was already hauling a horse broadside across the lane, using its bulk to break the first surge shoving away from the gate. "Service side," she said. "Now."

Sylvanas did not look back for Uther.

* * *

Handcarts jammed wheel to wheel under the wall. Dropped sacks bled flour into the mud. An open barrel lay on its side and people stumbled over it without seeing it. The smell was bread, wet burlap, horse piss, broken wood. Under all of it, the city's sweet-sour wrongness.

Cyndia shoved the horse ahead long enough to make a moving wall. Sylvanas took the gap it opened, one hand on Jaina's sleeve to keep her in the crush. Jaina stumbled once on a spilled sack, caught herself on the horse's flank, kept going without a word.

Behind them, deeper in the city, the bells kept ringing. An ordinary hour still trying to announce itself over the first impact sounds.

"Left here," Cyndia called.

She had the route by instinct. Service lane, feed yard, wall stair, all from the grade underfoot and the warehouse backs built to shed carts.

The lane bent between warehouse rows and outbuildings. Dye vats under plank covers. A baker's side yard stacked with split kindling and empty flour bins. A woman came out of one door with a basket still covered in cloth and froze at the traffic running the wrong way.

Nobody stopped for her.

The sound inside the city changed as they ran. Commands first. Doors being hit. Horses checked too hard on stone. Battle hit a beat later. The first human scream came after that, sharp enough to cut through the rest and vanish into it.

Jaina flinched and kept moving.

At the next turn a wagon blocked half the lane, empty bed chalked white with old flour. Its owner fought the traces loose with hands that shook too hard for the buckles.

"Leave it," Sylvanas said.

He jerked upright, took in the bow, the armor, Jaina's filthy hands, whatever was still on all three of their faces.

"Don't go by the dye sheds," he blurted. "Service yard's gone bad there. Men dropping where they stand. East stair's still open if you take the tallow cut. Up past the wheelwright and the old feed crane. It'll put you on the inner wall above Market Row."

Cyndia had already turned to mark the line. "Tallow cut. Wheelwright. Feed crane."

The man dragged one sleeve across his mouth. "Keep clear of the lower yard. Don't let the horse smell the blood. He'll plant."

"Go," Sylvanas said.

He backed toward the wagon, caught himself, shame crossing his face for one blink.

"May the stars keep your road," he said, the words falling out of him with the ease of habit.

The fifth time.

The lane stayed the same. Flour in the ruts. Bells over the roofs. A grain carter with fear in his mouth said an old impossible thing in Common and meant only luck by it.

No room to turn and pin him to the wagon bed and ask who had taught it to him, or why people kept saying that phrase on roads where dead things were never far away. Sylvanas locked it away and moved on.

"Which road?"

His hand shot up. "That one. Past the broken press. You'll smell the tallow before you see it."

Cyndia was already moving again. Sylvanas shoved Jaina after her.

They hit the tallow cut at a run. The smell arrived first, rank fat gone soft in the day's heat, vats stacked under a crooked awning. Two chandlers had abandoned a handcart half-loaded with candles. One still burned where it had tipped, wax spilling into the muck.

The lane narrowed there. Wall to one side. Workshops to the other. Good for defense. Bad for crowds.

So Sylvanas kept Jaina in front of her and within reach.

At the wheelwright yard, another sign of what Arthas had chosen came clear. Panic would have looked different. This had drill behind it. A file of soldiers cut across the far end of the opening and vanished between houses at a run, shields up, two torches lit. Shouting stayed clipped. Men held their lanes. Someone below was assigning streets and holding lines.

Jaina saw it too. Her hand tightened on the staff.

"Don't stop," Sylvanas said.

They found the feed crane where the carter promised. Old oak post, rusted hook, rope black with grease. Beside it rose the stair, service stone built into the inner wall. Steep. Narrow enough that two could not climb abreast.

Halfway up, the city opened under them in pieces.

Rooftops first. Then the warehouse district falling toward the market quarter. Smoke climbed in separate columns. Torch lines showed even in daylight where men carried fire into streets that had not yet caught.

From below came doors being broken in rhythm. Method, not panic.

Sylvanas stopped with one hand on the rail and looked down.

Districts. Pieces a commander could work. Teams moving in ordered cuts through streets already divided in some commander's head.

What she saw below was plain enough to turn her stomach.

Kill the living before the dead can claim them. Deny one great rising by breaking the city into manageable deaths.

Correct.

Monstrous.

Behind her, Jaina had stopped too. Cyndia came up two steps below, took one look over Sylvanas's shoulder, and said nothing.

They finished the climb in silence.

* * *

The inner wall gave them height, not distance.

The sounds came up clear. Boots on cobbles. Orders. A child crying somewhere too far east. Timber catching. Men coughing in smoke. Near enough that she wanted to answer.

Sylvanas laid both hands on the parapet and took the quarter below piece by piece.

Market Row first. Of course. Squads moved along its broad spine in clean intervals, one line driving inward while another fell back dragging bodies, sacks, and salvage. Fires choked exits, cleared doorways, burned stock still piled high enough to matter. Bread ovens that had fed the city that morning vented black instead.

Beyond that, the square around Alonsus Chapel had become a holding knot. Horses. Runners. A cluster of shields around a point Sylvanas could not see and did not need to. Reserve there. Direction there. Falric's kind of work.

Crusaders' Square sat just off it, now serving as a staging lane for the men helping unmake the city.

Farther west, where houses narrowed and rose close, movement slowed. That was where the screaming lasted longest. Door by door. Family by family. Soldiers disappeared into homes and came back out alone or with fewer people than had been inside moments before.

Jaina stood at the parapet beside her, still enough that Sylvanas kept marking her in the corner of her eye. Flour remained in the seams of her skin. Frost had bitten two knuckles raw. She never fixed the loose strand of hair sticking to the damp at her temple.

Cyndia took the other crenel and worked outward. Exit lanes. Roof access. The nearest intact stair if they had to move. The next if that one went.

There were turns. A man who got three steps out of a side street with bread still in his hand before his legs went out and his spine bowed wrong. A woman held by two neighbors until they understood too late what was changing under their palms. Children pulled behind soldiers and then not pulled back fast enough. If Arthas had waited, the whole quarter below them would have gone at once and no line on earth would have held it.

He had chosen to kill the city in sections.

Sylvanas had spent her life pricing roads, walls, supply lines, kill zones, retreat lanes. She knew the feel of a correct military answer. She also knew when a man had rotted himself by choosing one.

It showed in the order forced onto the quarter below: merchant streets cut into pieces under a prince's will, lines rotating with siege discipline while a baker's stall burned blue-white and a dog ran blind through the smoke.

Below the wall, one squad halted at a crossing while another cleared the lane ahead. Shields high. Torch to door. Door to blade. Blade to body. Body to cart. Cart to burn pile.

A wild slaughter could pass for collapse. This kept its lanes, torches, and handoffs all the way through.

The bells stopped.

Only when they stopped did Sylvanas taste the hole they left in the city.

After that the sounds came farther apart.

A scream. Then two streets of nothing. A roof beam giving way. Hooves. Someone retching on the lane below the wall. A captain's voice, steady, naming the next turn. Fire settling into its work.

The silence between one noise and the next grew longer.

Every time Sylvanas shifted along the parapet, Jaina shifted too. When one crenel ran black with soot, Cyndia yielded the cleaner angle. When a gust blew sparks high enough to sting, Sylvanas put one forearm out on instinct and found Jaina already ducking into the same narrow sheltering space.

On the wall, she lost track of the hours. Sun moved. Smoke thickened, then thinned. One district was cleared. Then the next. Then aftermath: teams dragging, checking, putting fire where fire served, killing anything that still moved the wrong way.

Arthas showed only in pieces until the end. A glimpse of white-blond at a crossing. The flash of a plate shoulder through smoke. A mounted figure at the center of a knot of runners. Never enough to make a man out of. Only command.

Timber settled in the heat, and the ugly small noises of a city after human voices had been worked out of it went on.

Movement gathered below the southern gate. Lines reforming toward the same formal approach they had used to enter.

Sylvanas straightened from the parapet.

There.

Arthas came out under the archway he had ridden through not long ago.

He was on foot now.

His gauntlets were gone. His hands were bare.

Sylvanas saw that first.

Pale in the braziers' fire at the gate, the skin clean to the wrists. Too clean for accident. Too clean for proximity. Water found. Time made. Blood removed.

He had washed before coming out.

He lifted his head.

At that distance, Sylvanas could not make out the true color of his eyes, yet knew them wrong on sight. Fever-bright would have been easier. Wild would have been easier. This was emptied out. Whatever heat had driven him into the city was gone now, spent down to something flatter and colder.

He looked straight up at the wall.

At them.

Jaina did not move.

Neither did Sylvanas.

They stayed still long enough for the pause to turn sharp.

Then Arthas turned, gathered the last of his officers with a gesture sharp enough to cut air, and walked away from the gate like a man already late to the next necessity.

Behind him Stratholme burned in ordered pockets.

On the stone beside Sylvanas, Jaina's hand stayed flat on the parapet, white at the knuckles, until even the last of the column had passed from sight.
