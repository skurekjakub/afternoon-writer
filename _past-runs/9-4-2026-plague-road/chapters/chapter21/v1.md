# Chapter 21: Frostmourne

Daylight made the harbor honest.

What the night had given them in flashes and silhouettes stood bare now under a low white sky: the black ribs of ships frozen into the bay, dock timbers hacked apart for hurried repairs and never finished, whole stretches of shoreline trampled flat by too many boots that had not cared what they crushed. The Wolfheart rode at anchor behind them, intact by the simple luck of having arrived after the burning. All the hulls that might have carried men home had gone up weeks earlier. Their char had sunk into the place so deep that the cold could not scour it out. When the wind turned, Sylvanas still tasted old pitch and wet ash.

She stood on a rise above the strand with her hood back and read the bay the way she would have read a battlefield after arrows stopped flying.

Cyndia moved below with the rangers, checking the stripped camps one by one. They did not call much to each other. Nothing on that shore needed debate. Broken crate hoops lay under rime. Packing straw had frozen in the mud where it spilled and been ground under heels until it looked like yellow hair caught in ice. Somebody had stacked ration casks beside a half-collapsed lean-to, then split them open to the dregs and left the wood where it fell. The place spoke of neither retreat nor orderly resupply, only a force driven through a stopping place without allowing itself to stop.

Jaina came up the rise two breaths slower than she should have. Her face had color again after the sea, but the sea had not given back what it took. She held a heel of dwarven bread in one hand and forgot to eat it while she looked inland. The smell of it rode the air between them, warm yeast under smoke and salt and cinder.

"I was right last night," she said.

Her voice came thin from the cold, used hard rather than weakened by it.

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the harbor. "That is why we came ashore."

"No." Jaina lifted her free hand a little, then stopped with her fingers spread, listening through her skin. "It is narrower now. Less like a pull. More like a line."

Sylvanas followed that gesture past the burned docks and the rise of snow beyond, out toward the inland white where the coast gave way to harder country. The physical trail had already shown itself. From the edge of the largest stripped camp, deep bootprints ran north and west in a band too broad for scouts and too ugly for a disciplined column. Sled runners had carved parallel grooves under the new frost. Men had hauled weight through here fast enough to break harness buckles and abandon pieces without turning back to collect them.

Late, perhaps not fatally, but enough that the arithmetic had teeth.

Dagny Ironhull came off the Wolfheart in a fur-lined coat too heavy for grace and crossed the ice with care that did not touch fear. She stopped below the rise, glanced once at the wreckage, then at the track running inland.

"I can keep the ship ready two days," she said. "Three if your rangers stop glaring at my rigging and help my people break ice."

"Two," Sylvanas said.

Dagny's mouth tightened in approval, or contempt, or the place where the two met. "Then come back breathing before the bay tries to close."

She went down to the strand again without waiting for blessing. Sensible.

Cyndia mounted the rise at last, carrying a scorched strip of canvas between two fingers. The old device on it had burned almost clean away. Enough silver thread remained to show Lordaeron's colors once.

"No burial pits," she said. "No attempt at them, either. They took what they could drag and left the rest to the cold. Camp edges are full of butchered harness. Two masts chopped down for firewood. He burned the fleet and made the survivors march."

The word he did not need a name.

Jaina bit bread at last as if reminded that her body existed. Chewed once. Stopped. Her gaze had gone to the inland trail again, to the matching of the visible line under snow and the invisible one only she could hear.

"Same direction," she said around the mouthful. "Exactly the same."

There should have been comfort in that. Two methods, one answer. Sylvanas found none. A trail this blunt meant obsession, not command. No scouts laid false lines. No wagons had circled for defense. No party had doubled back to hide numbers. Arthas had driven straight through the bay like a spear thrown from too near the target.

She dropped from the rise and crouched by the first prints. The outer edges had softened, filled, frozen again. Old enough to hurt. Fresh enough that the pattern still held. Men marching overloaded. Men stumbling. One drag mark deeper than the others where a sled had overturned and been righted without unloading. A prince who no longer counted the cost of the hands behind him.

When she straightened, Jaina was watching her instead of the trail.

"What?"

"You are already thinking about how he broke them."

"He saved me the labor of guessing."

Jaina took another bite, smaller this time. "If he did this before he reached the source, what do you think he looks like now?"

The question landed hard and clean. It was measurement, not fear.

Sylvanas looked past her to the harbor, to the splintered docks and burned hulls, and old anger stirred with an edge she had not let herself name. She had put this prince on the road to Northrend. Pride, necessity, vengeance—it scarcely mattered which word she chose now. The road remained.

"Like a man in a hurry," she said. "Move."

They left the shore in a disciplined file, the rangers spreading enough to keep eyes on both flanks while still reading the broken track. Behind them Daggercap Bay sank back into its own white glare, the Wolfheart a single dark shape among the bones of ships that had carried no one home.

The trail rose out of the harbor country and into ground that had forgotten softness.

Hard crust broke under boots with a sound like thin pottery snapping. Every drift hid stone or old ice or the kind of shallow hollow that twisted an ankle and cost the line ten heartbeats to fix. The world had no near color in it. Snow, sky, glacier, the grey of distant ridges. Even the dead brush that showed through in patches looked leeched to bone. Sylvanas had known winters in Quel'Thalas sharp enough to flay breath, but this was another thing. Northrend did not merely endure cold. It enforced it.

They kept Farstrider pace anyway.

Cyndia took the left. One ranger ranged farther right where the ground broke into hummocks and buried stone. The other two worked behind and ahead by turns, checking for spoor, movement, anything still living enough to hunt. Sylvanas held the center front with Jaina beside rather than behind her. On better ground she might have argued for a different place in the file. Here the mage's body said enough. Jaina was tired down to the joints, and the further inland they pushed, the more often her hand rose unbidden, fingers writing in the air and stopping before the shape finished.

By noon her lashes had gathered white at the tips.

"Drink," Sylvanas said.

Jaina obeyed without the old flare of irritation, which troubled Sylvanas more than argument would have. She took the skin, swallowed, coughed once when the cold fought the motion, and passed it back.

"It is louder," she said.

"The wind?"

"No."

She did not need to specify. Her gloved thumb rubbed along the seam of the flask, hunting burrs that were not there. Then her hand lifted again, tracing three short marks, a hooked turn, a pause. Figures. No letters in them, and not quite spell-work either. She frowned at nothing Sylvanas could see.

"What does louder mean?"

"Closer." Jaina lowered her hand. "And... cleaner. The plague always had rot in it. Motion where the pattern should have held. This doesn't blur. It repeats."

Sylvanas heard no more than wind worrying the flat reaches ahead. Yet her ward-sense, blunt compared to Jaina's craft, had begun to complain hours ago, warning of a place where the shape of power itself had gone wrong. Each mile inland tightened that complaint across the back of her teeth.

The land helped. It seemed built to strip a force down to pace and hunger and the next place to put its feet. Shelter existed only as insult: a ridge too low to break the wind, a scatter of boulders rimed so slick they could not be trusted, the carcass of some colossal beast buried under drifts until only a curve of ribs showed like a wrecked fence. Once they passed a frozen pool skinned over so thick with blown snow that one ranger stepped onto it thinking solid ground lay beneath. The ice gave with a long glassy moan before she sprang back.

No one laughed.

Toward afternoon the broad military spoor sharpened again. More bootprints, deeper now where men had begun to labor uphill. The remnants of a snapped axle protruded from a drift. A sack of grain had burst and frozen in a yellow arc across the lee of a rock, each kernel encased in its own bead of ice. Somebody had tried to light a cookfire there and failed. The half-burned tinder still sat in its ring of blackened stones like a confession.

Arthas had not slowed even for that.

Jaina stumbled once.

She caught herself before Sylvanas could reach for her, which would have been worse, and kept walking. The stumble had not come from the footing. Her head had turned sharply to the north, toward a line of low bluffs where the snow packed deeper.

"There," she said.

Sylvanas signaled halt. The column froze where it stood.

"What did you hear?"

Jaina's nostrils flared. She listened a moment longer, lips parted, not to air but to some inner pressure answering it. "Nothing new. Just—" She stopped, dissatisfied. "The line bends. We are not chasing a wide field anymore. We are approaching something anchored."

Anchored. The word sat badly.

Cyndia came in from the flank at a trot light enough that she barely marked the crust. "Wall smoke," she said, pointing ahead. "Low and banked. Timber line looks wrong. Too straight for drift."

A camp, then. Or a fort. Local survivors if fortune remembered them at all.

Sylvanas glanced at Jaina. The mage had gone still in that peculiar way that meant thought outran breath. She was no use kept standing in the open while the cold chewed her down to the nerve.

"We move," Sylvanas said.

They found the camp at the end of a shallow rise where the ground folded just enough to make defense possible.

Dwarven work announced itself from a distance even under snow. The palisade was not pretty, not meant to be. Timber trunks stood shoulder to shoulder, banded in iron where a human would have trusted rope, their tops cut to ugly angles that denied easy purchase. Snow had crusted white along the outer face, but smoke slid from vents behind the wall in controlled, stingy ribbons. Somebody within understood siege fuel. Figures moved on the walkway above—stocky shapes wrapped in fur and mail, crossbows leveled with a steadiness born of practice and exhaustion alike.

"Hold there!" a voice barked from the wall. "Another step and ye answer to me first, then the dead if ye happen to be less troublesome than ye look."

Cyndia's hand drifted toward an arrow. Sylvanas lifted two fingers without looking at her. Not yet.

"We are not with the dead," Sylvanas called. "Though we have been walking in their country since dawn."

That earned no laugh, but one of the shapes on the wall shifted.

"Name yourselves."

"Sylvanas Windrunner. Jaina Proudmoore. Farstriders out of Quel'Thalas and a shipmaster waiting at Daggercap Bay. We are tracking Arthas Menethil."

Silence followed. Hard silence. Men repeating the name inwardly and finding it matched too much of what they had seen.

Then another voice, rougher and closer, called from behind the gate. "Open the damned thing. If the prince's old friends have come this far north on purpose, I want to see what kind of fools they are."

The bar lifted. The gate parted just wide enough to admit one body at a time before being hauled shut again behind the last ranger.

Heat did not meet them. Warmth would have been too generous a word. The camp gave back only the absence of killing wind, and even that seemed precious.

It had the look of a dig turned fort because there had been no time to choose between them. Trenches cut into the packed ground led between low shelters roofed in hide and plank. Iron tools hung beside weapons as if their owners had reached for whichever the hour required. Cookfires burned under windbreaks of stacked timber and ore crates. Men turned to watch the arrivals with the same expression the wall guards had worn: relief fighting suspicion, each refusing to yield.

At the far end of the yard a dwarf ducked out of the largest shelter and came toward them with a stride too hard for his size to diminish.

Muradin Bronzebeard looked like the camp itself: built to take weather, patched where weather had landed, unwilling to stop because stopping invited worse. Frost had crept into his beard and stayed there around the rings braided through it. His coat showed recent mending at one shoulder. A hammer rode his back with the comfortable weight of a limb.

He took one look at Jaina and stopped swearing with his face by force.

"Lady," he said, surprised into courtesy.

Jaina's smile came tired and brief. "Muradin."

Then he looked at Sylvanas, at the rangers behind her, at the order in which they had entered and spread and chosen corners with clear sightlines. His eyes narrowed, not in distrust this time but in recognition of a pattern.

"Well," he said. "That's a machine if ever I saw one. Two of ye at the center, everything else turning clean around it."

Jaina's mouth flattened around a reply she chose not to speak.

Sylvanas said, "If you are done measuring us, tell me how long ago Arthas passed through."

Muradin bared his teeth in something near approval. "Aye. Better inside."

He led them into the long shelter. The air there tasted of hot metal, old wool, damp timber, and the sour edge of men living too close under strain. Better than outside. Barely. A brazier glowed near the center. Somebody shoved cups into their hands without asking and filled them from a pot of dark ale heated almost to boiling.

The first swallow bit like forge steam. Sylvanas took it anyway.

Muradin did not sit. Neither did Sylvanas. Jaina leaned one hip against a table scarred by knife cuts and map weights and kept the cup in both hands for the heat. Cyndia and the rangers spread near the walls, not eavesdropping so much as refusing not to hear.

"He came through two days ago," Muradin said. "Less, maybe. Hard to tell exact in this wind. Came half-starved, half-mad, and mean enough to make the rest of his men mad along with him. Wouldn't hear caution. Wouldn't hear wait. Only wanted the glacier."

"For the blade," Jaina said.

Muradin's stare snapped to her. "You know the name, then."

"Only from the line I'm following." She set the cup down untouched after the second swallow. Her fingers had begun moving again, drawing shapes over the tabletop that left no mark. "Not the story. Tell it."

The dwarf rubbed his beard once, rings clicking under his knuckles. "There's a cave north and east from here, cut into the glacier face. Old warning carved by some poor soul who knew enough to fear what lay inside and not enough to bury it proper. Frostmourne. Blade with a curse on it. We found whispers of it months back, old camp talk, runes, a guardian spirit if ye trust that kind of tale. I trusted the part where every trail pointed away from sense. Tried to keep Arthas off the path."

"Failed," Sylvanas said.

"Aye." No self-pity in it. The word fell like an axe. "He'd already turned by then. He was not yet whatever waits at the end of this, but he was far enough gone. He'd spend men like coal. Speak fair one moment and order a death march the next. Sleep less. Eat less. Look past whoever stood in front of him like the thing calling north had settled beyond their skulls."

That matched the harbor.

Muradin braced both hands on the table and glanced between Sylvanas and Jaina again, perhaps measuring whether either of them needed the prince defended. Neither offered him the burden.

"He passed through here with survivors and left with fewer," Muradin went on. "Took only the ones still quick enough to climb. Said he'd return armed to finish the campaign. Said a great many things. The only true one was that he meant to reach that cave before any man beside him could think better."

"How far?" Sylvanas asked.

"Three days by supply column. Two if ye're a light force and willing to break bodies on the march."

"And he left two days ago."

"Aye."

The arithmetic hung over the brazier with the smoke.

Jaina stopped tracing shapes long enough to press thumb and forefinger hard against her brow. Concentration had been driven until it hurt. When she spoke again, the words came faster, as if speech had to catch up to something already arranged in her head.

"The plague in Lordaeron wasn't one thing. It presented as one thing because flesh is easier to notice than architecture. But underneath it there was a repeating structure—containment, propagation, compulsion, release. This has the same family marks."

Muradin frowned. "Speak dwarf."

She drew a tight breath and obeyed at once. "The sword and the plague were built by the same hand. Or by hands taught the same craft. Not identical. Related."

Muradin went very still.

"You're sure."

"As sure as I can be without touching it, and I'm not touching it." Jaina's hand resumed its blind notation in the air, one hook, two short lines, a twist Sylvanas could not follow. "The field repeats too cleanly for natural corruption. That is why I could follow it from the coast. That is why it feels wrong in a different way."

Relief crossed Muradin's face so quickly another eye might have missed it. The comfort lay in language, not in the kinship itself.

"Months," he said, almost to himself. "Months I've been telling any soul who'd listen that the thing was cursed beyond old stories and none of them had words for me except superstition."

"Now you do," Sylvanas said. "Can those words stop Arthas?"

"Not if he's already laid hands on the bastard."

The brazier popped. Nobody moved.

Muradin's gaze returned to the pair at the center of the room. He looked at their spacing, Jaina's restless fingers, the way Sylvanas had set herself half a pace between the mage and the door without planning it.

"You two have done this before," he said.

"We crossed an ocean," Sylvanas answered.

"That's not what I mean."

He tapped the tabletop with one thick finger. "I've watched companies drill for twenty years and seen less trust than the two of ye spend on a single turn of the head. She listens, ye move. You move, she changes the ground under it. No wasted shouting. No one proving who's cleverest while arrows are flying."

Jaina looked into the brazier instead of at either of them. Color had risen along her cheekbones from heat and fatigue together. She said, very dry, "We can discuss our teamwork later if you prefer."

Muradin barked a laugh that ended without mirth. "If there is a later."

There it was. The only true shape of the room.

He reached for the cup near him, drank, and set it down hard. "I'm coming."

"No," Jaina said at once.

"Aye."

"If your camp still stands, it stands because you stayed in it."

"And if I stay in it now, a cursed blade walks out into the world with the prince you've come to stop wrapped around the hilt. I've the route. I've the last sight of him before the cave. I know where the glacier shelves and where the snow hides crevasses. Unless one of you has turned dwarf since breakfast, I'm faster use with ye than behind a wall."

Sylvanas had already decided the same. She did not enjoy hearing it spoken.

"How many?" she asked.

Muradin looked past her to the rangers and made the numbers as quickly as she would have. "Too many slows us. Too few dies quiet. Me. You. The lady. Your captain and maybe two more to hold the way clear."

"One more," Cyndia said from the wall. "Not maybe."

Muradin's beard twitched. "Fine. One more."

Jaina stared at the moving shapes her hand kept drawing and did not seem aware she was doing it. "If he has the blade already, going faster may only let us witness it sooner."

"Then we witness," Sylvanas said. "And if witness is all that remains, we make it useful."

Jaina lifted her eyes. The exhaustion in them had not softened anything essential. It rarely did.

"You think we're late."

"I think lateness does not excuse stillness."

For a beat the room held only fire-noise and breath. Then Jaina nodded once, abrupt as a lock turning.

"Give me snow goggles if you have them," she said to Muradin. "And spare gloves. My left hand's too stiff."

Muradin grinned without pleasure. "Now that's a useful answer."

They were out of the gate before the camp's next meal could be called.

Muradin ran the way only a dwarf who had spent months on bad ground could run: low, compact, all labor spent on forward motion and none on display. Cyndia took point when the trail narrowed, one ranger behind her, another on rear watch. The last kept near Muradin for the stretches where the snow broke deceptive and local knowledge mattered more than sharp eyes. Jaina stayed beside Sylvanas as long as she could, then half a pace behind when the country steepened. She complained exactly once, which let Sylvanas know the strain had become serious.

Night never fully fell. The sky simply thickened from white to iron and back again while they climbed through broken country toward the glacier.

The cold altered with altitude. On the lower flats it had gnawed. Here it cut. Breath iced in cloth before a sentence could cross it. Ice glazed the inside of Sylvanas's scarf where her own breath struck and froze. Muradin led them over a shelf of wind-packed snow so hard it rang under boots, down into a ravine where old blue ice showed through, then up again between stone shoulders that leaned close enough to force the line single file.

Twice Jaina caught herself with one hand on Sylvanas's arm and dropped away at once, annoyed less by the stumble than by the proof that it existed. Sylvanas did not look at her when it happened. Courtesy, of a kind.

The ward-pressure intensified until it stopped feeling like pressure and started feeling like instruction. Leave. Turn back. The message came without words from the older, fouler thing beneath them.

At the crest of the last rise Muradin threw up a fist.

Everyone dropped.

Below them the glacier wall opened in a dark seam big enough to swallow a hall. Wind had scoured the approach almost clean. Tracks ran to the mouth in a disorder that became more telling the longer Sylvanas studied it: boots in, many of them, none out. Along the left side of the approach, half-buried in driven snow, lay a warhammer with its head still faintly lit from within, a warm gold sunk under blue frost.

Muradin made a sound so raw it barely counted as speech. "Light's Vengeance."

Jaina's breath caught in impact rather than grief. Her hand rose with no conscious order and spread toward the cave mouth as though heat radiated from within. The air around her wrist trembled once.

"I hear it," she said.

She was not reaching anymore. Receiving.

Even to Sylvanas the cave registered wrong, colder and cleaner than danger. She knew the feel of ambush, trap-runes, cursed groves, places where old hatred had steeped into stone. This was something else. The threshold seemed to reject the body before the body crossed it, as though the geometry inside had been laid down for something other than flesh.

"If we leave now," one ranger murmured.

"We won't," Sylvanas said.

No one answered. They rose together and descended.

Light's Vengeance lay where Arthas had discarded it, the leather of its grip rimed white, the gold at its head dulled under sheets of frost and still stubbornly present beneath. Sylvanas spared it one glance. No hand free. No time. The tracks around it were churned deep where men had stopped, hesitated, then gone on anyway.

Muradin touched two fingers to the hammer as he passed and kept moving.

Inside, the cave narrowed first, forcing them into a file between walls of ancient blue ice that held shadows in their depths like things trapped alive. Then it opened without warning into a chamber large enough to swallow the whole camp below. Glacial light bled through the ceiling from no source Sylvanas could trust. It cast everything in blue-white: the stone ledges, the ice-bound floor, the men standing in a broken half-ring before the far wall.

At a glance they were soldiers still. Lordaeron plate dulled by frost. Cloaks stiff with ice. Hands on weapons. At a second glance the lie failed. They stood too still, not with discipline but with vacancy. One man's cheek had split from cold and bled black down into his beard. Another's gaze pointed slightly wrong, fixed on nothing in the chamber. No one shivered.

Arthas stood among them with a sword in his hand.

Frostmourne did not need introduction. The blade made the chamber around it into commentary. Its edge shone with a pale inner light that never warmed the air. Rime crawled from the steel down over Arthas's gauntlet, over the leather at his wrist, over the plates of his armor, eager as floodwater finding a ditch. He looked thinner than the prince who had sailed north. Stripped, not lesser. The handsome softness had gone out of his face, and with it every hesitation that once might have slowed the decision from thought to deed.

His eyes found Jaina first. Then Sylvanas. Recognition crossed his features without changing them.

"You should not have followed," he said.

Muradin stepped past Sylvanas before anyone could stop him. "Ye damned fool."

Arthas did not even look at the hammer on Muradin's back. "I found what I came for."

"You found a curse."

"I found the strength to finish what weak men could not."

The soldiers at his back turned their heads as one slow fraction, not toward Muradin but toward the sound of the exchange, as though the blade had tugged a cord through them. Jaina saw it too. Sylvanas knew because the mage's left hand clenched once, hard, then opened flat.

"They're inside the field," Jaina said, too quickly. "Don't let them bracket us. And don't cast across the center lane unless I tell you."

Orders. Good.

Sylvanas was moving before the last word cleared the chamber.

Her first arrow left at the draw's high point and took Arthas under the collar where plate gave way to mail. A killing shot on any man whose body still obeyed old agreements. The impact rocked him a half step. Frost spread from the wound faster than blood could. He looked down at the shaft protruding from his chest with mild interest, caught it near the fletching, and pulled it free.

The arrow clattered on stone.

There. The decisive mismatch. Ordinary lethal standards gone.

"Left shelf!" Sylvanas snapped.

The rangers broke as ordered, one going high along the stone lip to create an angle on the enthralled soldiers, the others widening the chamber so Arthas could not take them in one straight rush. Cyndia drove an arrow through the throat of the nearest guard before he fully moved. He staggered backward without falling until Muradin's hammer took him across the helm and dropped him in a spray of frost.

Jaina planted her boots and thrust both hands forward. The cast was hard and ugly, built for speed rather than display. Blue-white force sheared across the floor toward Arthas—

—and burst six feet short of him.

The air there took it, split it, threw the fragments sideways in a glittering fan that slammed into the walls and sent shards rattling down.

Jaina inhaled through her teeth. "There."

Still measurement. Her fingers changed shape mid-motion, writing over the failed cast, mapping the place where the field had caught and turned it.

Arthas moved.

No mortal training bridged ground like that. One instant he stood at the chamber's heart. The next he had crossed half the distance to Muradin with Frostmourne low and ready, the sword dragging cold behind it like a wake. Muradin met him with the hammer two-handed. Steel and enchanted stone struck together. The blow should have rebounded both men apart.

Muradin skidded three body lengths and nearly lost his footing. Arthas took one step and came on.

Sylvanas loosed twice more at knee and eye. The knee shot cracked off plate rimed too thick with unnatural frost. The eye shot Arthas caught on the guard of Frostmourne without seeming to see it arrive. He turned that same motion into a cut aimed not at Sylvanas but at the floor between them.

"Back!" Jaina shouted.

Too late for perfect obedience.

Frostmourne bit stone. The chamber answered like a struck bell. Cracks raced through the ice-veined floor in white threads, then the whole left wall blew outward where Jaina's first spell had weakened it. Shards the size of knives and shields scythed across the room.

Sylvanas hit the ground and rolled behind a jut of stone. One ranger cried out. Cyndia dragged her clear by the harness before the next spray fell. Muradin turned at the shout, exactly the wrong instinct and the right one, and took the largest shard high under the collarbone where armor opened at the joint. The impact spun him sideways and slammed him down against the cave wall hard enough that his helmet rang.

He did not rise.

The chamber changed shape at once, tactically rather than physically.

Sylvanas saw it complete itself in a glance: one ranger bleeding but mobile, Muradin down, enthralled soldiers closing from the flanks again, Arthas advancing through the glittering ruin without hurry because he no longer needed it. Jaina saw it too. She had one hand locked around her own wrist to steady it and the other extended toward the killing field between them, fingers drawing the pattern she needed and failing to finish it before the pattern shifted under Frostmourne's hum.

"Can you break it?" Sylvanas called.

"Not here."

Answer enough.

One of the enthralled men reached the fallen ranger. Cyndia cut his hamstring. He went down and kept crawling until a second arrow pinned his throat to the ice. No one had breath for horror. They had passed that threshold when Arthas pulled the first shaft from his own chest.

Arthas stopped ten paces away. Frost crawled around his boots in petals. His face held no triumph. That would have required the old human scale of victory.

"You cannot stop this," he said.

Jaina laughed once, breathless and furious. "I know."

Her hands snapped inward. Instead of throwing power at the field again, she cast across the chamber mouth behind them. Ice surged from the floor in a ribbed barrier that narrowed the entry lane to two bodies abreast and broke the immediate rush of the enthralled soldiers. Clever. Not enough to win, but enough to leave.

Sylvanas was already at Muradin's side. The shard had gone in above the mail seam and broken off deep. Blood came dark and sluggish around the ice. He breathed. Unconscious, then. Better than dead. Worse than useful.

"Carry!" she barked.

The word cracked the chamber back into order.

Two rangers went to Muradin at once. Cyndia took rear guard. Jaina backed toward the mouth without turning, one hand maintaining the narrowing wall, the other still twitching small furious notations as if part of her refused to stop working even now. Sylvanas covered the withdrawal, arrows aimed not at Arthas anymore but at joints, eyes, knees—anything that bought half-seconds from the men around him.

Arthas did not rush the barrier. He watched them.

That unsettled Sylvanas more than pursuit would have. He had already measured the exchange and found no threat in a second round conducted on their terms. Frostmourne's light bled around his silhouette until it looked as though the chamber itself had chosen a shape and given it his face.

Then Jaina's wall failed in a rain of brittle fragments, and the retreat became speed.

They cleared the throat of the cave with Muradin dragging between two elves, boots carving twin trenches in the snow. Light's Vengeance still burned where it had fallen, a gold accusation in the drift. Sylvanas looked at it once and kept going. No hand free. No world left in which sentiment outranked the living weight over their shoulders.

Wind struck them full in the face outside, raw and immense after the chamber's dead cold. It helped. Motion returned to bodies that had begun to lock around the cave's wrongness.

"Count," Sylvanas said.

"Here."

"Here."

"Here."

Cyndia's answer came last, roughened by exertion, and the wounded ranger answered after her with a hiss through clenched teeth. Good. All seven.

They descended the glacier at a brutal half-run, Muradin's weight shifting between the two carriers whenever the ground slanted wrong. Once one of them slipped and nearly took all three down. Sylvanas caught Muradin's harness strap, braced, shoved them upright, and kept moving before the stumble could become a stop. Above and behind, the cave mouth dwindled to a dark cut in white stone. No pursuit came out of it.

Which meant Arthas had already turned his attention elsewhere.

That, more than the battle itself, settled the chapter of him in Sylvanas's head. She had come north to catch a prince in time. The creature in the cave had worn the prince's face and voice, but those were scraps, not substance. The real enemy had been in his hand, humming through the chamber, bending spell-work, lending his body impossible speed, reaching through Jaina hard enough that the mage kept hearing it even with wind screaming in her ears now.

Jaina walked with her gloves off despite the cold, because she could not stop her fingers otherwise. They shook as they moved, tracing invisible turns in the air between strides. Hook. Line. Break. Return. Once she nearly collided with Cyndia because she was following a pattern no one else could see.

"Eyes up," Sylvanas said.

Jaina blinked, focused, nodded, and kept writing.

"I heard where it cycles," she said.

The words came ragged from the climb and the retreat, but the thought inside them held. "Not a weakness. A seam. It repeats there."

"Can you use it?"

"Not yet."

There was no despair in it. Jaina was never that easy.

Sylvanas glanced back once more toward the glacier and found, to her own surprise, that blame did not rise in her—not for Jaina's failed strike, not for the march, not for the fact that they had reached the chamber late and lost cleanly to an enemy built outside the measures they had prepared for. She would not have changed the approach, the spacing, the timing, or the partner.

The recognition went down hard, like medicine swallowed without water, and steadied something in her rather than loosening it.

"Good," she said.

Jaina gave her a quick, startled look, as if the word itself required translation. Then the mage's mouth tightened with something fiercer than gratitude, and she returned to the invisible work between one step and the next.

By the time the camp's palisade showed below them through the whitening dark, Muradin had groaned once and gone limp again. Enough. Alive enough for handoff. The rangers carried him through the gate feet first while dwarves shouted for a surgeon, cloth, hot water, clean iron, all the practical prayers of people who had no use for helplessness.

Sylvanas did not follow immediately.

She stood one moment in the yard with the wind still clawing at her cloak and watched Jaina stop beside the shelter wall, stare at nothing, and draw the same invisible figure again with shaking bare fingers. It was work, not panic.

Inside the cave they had lost the match.

What came after would not be another version of the same attempt. That much, at least, the blade had given them.
