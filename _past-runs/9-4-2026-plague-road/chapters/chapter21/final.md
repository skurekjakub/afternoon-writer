# Chapter 21: Frostmourne

In daylight, the harbor lay bare.

The wrecks had been shapes in the dark. Morning gave them ribs and scars. Black hulls jutted from the bay ice. Dock timbers had been hacked apart for repairs that never finished. The *Wolfheart* rode behind them, intact only because it had come after the burning. Every ship that might have carried men home had gone up weeks earlier. When the wind shifted, Sylvanas tasted wet ash.

She stood above the strand and read the bay like a battlefield after the arrows stopped.

Cyndia and the rangers moved through the stripped camps below. Crate hoops under frost. Straw frozen into mud and ground under heels. Split ration casks beside a collapsed lean-to. Nothing on the strand suggested retreat or resupply. A column had come through here too hard to feed itself properly and too driven to care.

Jaina climbed up beside her two breaths slower than she should have. Colour had returned. Strength had not. She held a heel of dwarven bread in one hand and forgot to eat it while she stared inland. Warm yeast drifted up through smoke and cinder. The old rot-taste moved beneath it. She ate anyway.

"I was right last night," she said.

Sylvanas kept her attention on the harbor. "That is why we came ashore."

"No." Jaina crushed the bread heel. "It is narrower now. Not all through the bay. It is running with that north-west line. With the sled grooves."

Sylvanas followed the gesture past the burned docks to the sweep of snow beyond. The trail showed already. From the largest stripped camp, deep bootprints ran north-west in a band too broad for scouts and too ugly for discipline. Sled runners had carved grooves under the new frost. Men had dragged weight through here fast enough to break harness and leave the pieces.

In reach.

Dagny Ironhull came off the *Wolfheart* in a fur-lined coat too heavy for grace and crossed the ice with steady care. She took in the wreckage once, then the line running inland.

"I can keep the ship ready two days," she said. "Three if your rangers stop glaring at my rigging and help my people break ice."

"Two," Sylvanas said.

Dagny's mouth tightened. "Then come back breathing before the bay closes."

She turned back toward the strand without waiting for an answer.

Cyndia mounted the rise with a scorched strip of canvas between two fingers. Enough silver thread remained to show Lordaeron's colors.

"No burial pits," she said. "They took what they could drag and left the rest to the cold. Camp edges are full of butchered harness. Two masts cut down for firewood. He burned the fleet and marched the survivors."

*He* needed no name.

Jaina bit the bread at last, chewed once, then stopped again. Her attention fixed on the deepest sled groove cutting north-west out of the camp.

"Same direction," she said. "Exactly the same line."

Only a man past caring left that trail.

Sylvanas dropped from the rise and crouched beside the first prints. Their edges had softened, filled, frozen again. Old enough to hurt. Fresh enough to read. Men marching overloaded. Men stumbling. One drag mark deeper than the others where a sled had tipped and been righted without unloading. A commander who no longer counted the cost behind him.

When she straightened, Jaina was watching her instead of the trail.

"What?"

"You are already thinking about how he broke them."

"He saved me the guesswork."

Jaina took another bite, smaller this time. "If he was this far gone before he reached the source, what is he now?"

Sylvanas looked back at the splintered docks and burned hulls. Old anger stirred, edged with the fact she had not let herself name. She had helped put Arthas on this road. Pride, necessity, vengeance—the motive no longer mattered. The road remained.

"Like a man in a hurry," she said. "Move."

They left the shore in disciplined file, rangers spread just far enough to keep both flanks honest while still reading the broken track. Behind them Daggercap Bay sank into its own white glare, the *Wolfheart* one dark hull among ships that had carried no one home.

Dragonblight above the harbor had gone hard. Crust snapped under boots. Every drift hid stone, old ice, or a hollow deep enough to cost the line heartbeats. Snow. Sky. Glacier. The grey of distant ridges.

They kept Farstrider pace anyway.

Cyndia took the left. One ranger ranged wide right where the ground broke into buried stone. The other two traded front and rear. Sylvanas held centre with Jaina beside her. The mage was tired down to the joints, and the farther inland they pushed, the more often her hand rose unbidden, drawing shapes in the air and stopping before they finished.

By noon white clung to her lashes.

"Drink," Sylvanas said.

Jaina obeyed without the old flash of annoyance. That worried Sylvanas more than protest would have. She drank, coughed once against the cold, and gave the skin back.

"It is louder," she said.

"The wind?"

"No."

She worked her thumb along the seam of the flask until the metal bit red into her skin. Then her hand lifted again: three short marks, a hooked turn, a pause. No letters in it. Not quite spellwork either.

"What does louder mean?"

"Closer." Jaina lowered her hand. "The plague always tasted foul before it landed. Rot, grave-damp, spoiled grain. This keeps catching between breaths. Same catch in the mouth. Same twist under the ribs. Every time."

Only wind moved across the flats ahead. The ache across the back of Sylvanas's teeth had been tightening for hours. Skin drew tighter over her knuckles with every mile inland. Leave. Turn back.

Cold, distance, and bad ground stripped a marching force down to pace, hunger, and the next place to put its feet. Shelter arrived only as insult: a ridge too low to break the wind, boulders too slick with rime to trust, the buried curve of a dragon rib showing through the drifts. A little farther on, a Lordaeron soldier lay frozen on one knee beside the snapped axle of a supply cart, one gauntlet still caught in a wheel spoke. Once a ranger stepped onto what looked like hard-packed snow and found a frozen pool beneath.

By afternoon the spoor sharpened. More prints. Deeper now, where men had begun to climb. A snapped axle jutted from a drift. A burst sack of grain lay frozen in a yellow arc beside a rock, every kernel trapped in ice. Someone had tried to start a cookfire there and failed. The half-burned tinder still sat inside its blackened ring.

Arthas had not slowed even for that.

Jaina stumbled once.

She caught herself before Sylvanas could reach for her and kept moving. Her head had turned north toward a line of low bluffs.

"There," she said.

Sylvanas signaled halt. The line froze.

"What changed?"

Jaina listened, lips parted, not to the air but to the pressure inside it. Her chin had already turned toward the low bluffs where the boot spoor kinked around a drift and vanished into the lee. "Nothing new. Just..." She cut herself off. "The spoor kinks there. The pull stops smearing across the flats and starts dragging everything into one place."

More trap in it.

Cyndia came in from the flank at a light trot. "Wall smoke," she said, pointing ahead. "Low and banked. Timber line too straight for drift."

Sylvanas looked once at Jaina. The mage had gone very still in the way that meant her mind had outrun her breath. No use leaving her in the open while the cold worked on her hands.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

They found the camp at the end of a shallow rise where the ground folded just enough to defend.

Even under snow, the position was plainly dwarven. A rough circle of stacked ice-blocks and packed snow sat in the fold of the rise, ugly as a field repair and solid enough to trust. Smoke slid from forge vents and hide-roofed shelters inside in thin ribbons. Stocky figures moved along the wall walk above with crossbows leveled and exhaustion held under discipline.

"Hold there!" a voice barked from the wall. "Another step and ye answer to me first, then the dead if ye happen to be less troublesome than ye look."

Cyndia's hand drifted toward an arrow. Sylvanas lifted two fingers without looking at her.

"We are not with the dead," Sylvanas called. "Though we've been walking in their country since dawn."

"Name yourselves."

"Sylvanas Windrunner. Jaina Proudmoore. Farstriders out of Quel'Thalas. A ship waiting at Daggercap Bay. We are tracking Arthas Menethil."

No one answered at once. A murmur passed along the wall, then cut off.

Then another voice, rougher and closer, came from behind the gate. "Open the damned thing. If the prince's old friends have come this far north on purpose, I want to see what kind of fools they are."

The bar lifted. The gate opened just wide enough to admit one body at a time and slammed shut after the last ranger crossed.

Inside, the killing wind was gone.

Trenches cut through packed snow toward low shelters roofed in hide and plank. Tools hung beside weapons. Men turned to watch the arrivals with the same expression the wall guards had worn: relief and suspicion set hard together.

At the far end of the yard a dwarf ducked out of the largest shelter and came toward them with a stride too hard for his size to soften.

Muradin Bronzebeard was cut from the camp itself. Frost had settled into his beard around the iron rings braided through it. A hammer rode his back with the ease of long use.

He took one look at Jaina and surprise forced courtesy through him.

"Lady."

Jaina lifted a hand no higher than her cup. "Muradin."

Then he took in Sylvanas, the rangers, and the order in which they had entered and spread.

"Well," he said. "I've seen twenty-year companies with less trust than the two of ye spend on a turn of the head."

Jaina did not dignify that with an answer.

"If you are done measuring us," Sylvanas said, "tell me how long ago Arthas passed through."

Muradin showed his teeth in grim approval. "Inside."

The shelter smelled of hot metal and wet wool. A brazier glowed at the centre. Someone shoved cups into their hands and filled them from a pot of dark ale heated almost to boiling.

The first swallow bit with forge-steam heat. Sylvanas took it anyway.

Muradin stayed standing. So did she. Jaina leaned one hip against the table and held the cup in both hands. Cyndia and the rangers took the walls without pretending not to listen.

"He came through two days ago," Muradin said. "Less, maybe. Hard to tell exact in this weather. Half-starved, half-mad, mean enough to drag the rest of his men after him. Wouldn't hear caution. Wouldn't hear wait. Only wanted the glacier."

"For the blade," Jaina said.

Muradin snapped his head toward her. "You know the name."

"Only from the line I'm following." She set the cup down after a second swallow. Her fingers were already moving again, drawing shapes over the tabletop that left no mark. "Tell it."

Muradin rubbed his beard once, rings clicking. "Cave north-east of here, cut into the glacier face. Old warning carved near the thing. Frostmourne. Cursed blade. We found scraps of it months back—camp talk, runes, enough old records to know no sensible man should want it. I tried to keep Arthas off that road."

"Failed," Sylvanas said.

"Aye." No self-pity in it. "He'd already turned by then. Far enough gone to burn men for fuel. Speak fair one breath and order a death march the next. Sleep less. Eat less. Look through whoever stood in front of him toward whatever was calling north."

"He passed here with survivors and left with fewer," Muradin went on. "Took only the men still quick enough to climb. Said he'd return armed to finish the campaign. The only true thing he said was that he meant to reach the cave before anyone beside him could think better."

"How far?"

"Three days by supply column. Two if ye're light and willing to break bodies on the way."

"And he left two days ago."

"Aye."

Across the brazier, no one answered.

Jaina pressed thumb and forefinger to her brow. When she spoke again the words came faster, speech racing a thought already built.

She dragged one fingertip through the ale someone had slopped across the table and laid three short strokes in the wet grain of the wood.

"The plague in Lordaeron kept turning the same way once it got into a body," she said. "Bodies were easier to read than the work driving them. Same turns. Same steps underneath. This has the same hand on it."

Muradin braced both palms on the table. "Speak dwarf."

She obeyed at once. "The sword was made by whoever made the plague."

Muradin's palms flattened hard against the table.

"You're sure."

"As sure as I can be without touching it, and I am not touching it." Her fingers resumed their blind notation. "Every time I catch what sits under it, it turns the same way. Same catch. Same pull. Same maker."

Relief crossed his face before he could stop it.

"Months," he said. "Months I've been telling anyone who'd listen the thing was worse than an old ghost story, and all they gave me back was superstition."

"Now you have better words," Sylvanas said. "Can it stop Arthas?"

"Not if he's already laid hands on the bastard."

The brazier popped. No one moved.

"You two have done this before," he said.

"We crossed an ocean."

"Not what I mean." He jerked his chin toward Jaina's cup still steaming in both hands and to Sylvanas already on her feet for the door. Along the walls, the rangers shifted when either woman so much as glanced their way. "I've seen pairs miss easier handoffs in drill yards. She speaks, ye move. Ye move, she changes the ground under it."

Jaina studied the brazier. "We can discuss our teamwork later if you prefer."

Muradin gave a laugh that ended flat. "If there is a later."

He drank and set the cup down hard. "I'm coming."

"No," Jaina said at once.

"Aye."

"If your camp still stands, it stands because you stayed in it."

"And if I stay in it now, the prince you've come north to stop walks out into the world carrying a cursed blade." He spread both hands. "I've the route. I've the last clear sight of him before the cave. I know where the glacier shelves and where the snow hides crevasses. Unless one of you turned dwarf since breakfast, I'm more use with ye than behind this wall."

Sylvanas had already made the same reading. She did not enjoy hearing it aloud.

"How many?" she asked.

Muradin looked past her to the rangers and counted as quickly as she would have. "Too many slows us. Too few dies quiet. Me. You. The lady. Your captain and two more."

"One more," Cyndia said from the wall. "Two."

Muradin's beard twitched. "Fine. Two."

Jaina stared at the shapes her hand kept drawing, only just noticing them. "If he already has the blade, going faster may only get us there in time to watch."

"Then we witness," Sylvanas said. "And if witness is all that's left, we make it useful."

Jaina went still. Exhaustion had not blurred her expression. Focus still held it sharp.

"You think we're late."

"I think lateness does not excuse stillness."

Fire and breathing held the room for one beat. Then Jaina pushed off the table.

"Snow goggles," she said to Muradin. "If you have them. And spare gloves. My left hand's too stiff."

Muradin showed a grim flash of teeth. "Now that's a useful answer."

They were out of the gate before the next meal could be called.

Muradin ran low and compact, every ounce spent on forward motion. Cyndia took point when the trail narrowed. One ranger stayed with her. Another watched the rear. The last kept close to Muradin when local ground mattered more than sharp eyes. Jaina held beside Sylvanas while she could, then fell half a pace behind when the climb steepened.

Night never fully arrived. The sky only thickened from white to iron and back again while they climbed.

Higher up, the cold cut instead of gnawing. Breath froze in cloth before a sentence could finish crossing it. Muradin led them over a shelf of wind-packed snow so hard it rang under boots, down through a ravine of old blue ice, then up again between stone shoulders close enough to force the line single file.

Twice Jaina caught herself on Sylvanas's arm and pulled away at once. Sylvanas did her the courtesy of not looking.

The pressure in the wards grew until it resolved into one instruction: leave. Turn back. The older thing under the glacier had no interest in whether she understood the message.

At the crest of the last rise Muradin threw up a fist.

Everyone dropped.

Below them the glacier opened in a dark seam wide enough to swallow a hall. Wind had scoured the approach almost clean. Tracks ran to the mouth in a churned band and ended there. Many boots in. None out. Along the left side of the approach, half-buried in blown snow, lay a warhammer with its head still faintly lit from within, warm gold under blue frost. To the right of the mouth, old warning runes had been cut into blue ice and half-scoured by drift. Muradin raked a glove across one groove. Beneath it, in Common, one half-buried line still read clear enough to land: SO MUST POWER SCAR THE SPIRIT.

"Light's Vengeance."

Jaina's breath broke on impact rather than grief. Her hand rose of its own accord toward the cave mouth. The air around her wrist trembled.

"I hear it," she said.

Her hand fell still.

Even to Sylvanas the cave was wrong. Colder and cleaner than danger. She knew ambush, trap-runes, cursed groves, places where old hatred had steeped into stone. The cave pushed the living back before the living crossed it. At the threshold the cold turned hard against her skin. Bow leather went rigid under her glove. A ward lay across the mouth and pushed living flesh back.

"If we leave now," one ranger said.

"We won't," Sylvanas said.

No one argued. Boots bit, slipped, corrected on the ice, and they went down.

Light's Vengeance lay where Arthas had discarded it, leather grip rimed white, gold dulled under frost and still stubbornly present beneath. Sylvanas spared it one glance. No hand free. No time.

Muradin touched two fingers to the hammer as he passed and kept moving.

The cave narrowed first, forcing them into file between ancient blue walls. Then it opened into a chamber large enough to swallow the camp below. Glacial light bled through the ceiling from no source Sylvanas trusted. It washed everything blue-white: stone ledges, ice-bound floor, the men standing in a broken half-ring before the far wall.

At first glance they were soldiers still. Lordaeron plate gone dull with frost. Cloaks stiff with ice. Hands on weapons. At second glance the lie broke. They stood too still, not with discipline but vacancy. One man's cheek had split from the cold and bled black into his beard. No one shivered.

Arthas stood among them with a sword in his hand.

Pale light moved inside the steel without warming the air. Rime crept from it over Arthas's gauntlet and wrist. He looked thinner than the prince who had sailed north. The softness had gone from his face.

He turned to Jaina first, then Sylvanas.

"You should not have followed," he said.

Muradin stepped past Sylvanas before anyone could stop him. "Ye damned fool."

Arthas did not so much as glance at the hammer on Muradin's back. "I found what I came for."

"You found a curse."

"I found the blade to finish the campaign."

The soldiers behind him turned their heads together toward the sound, not toward Muradin himself but toward the exchange. Frost webbed thicker over the nearest man's pauldrons when he moved, as if the motion belonged to the sword before it belonged to him. Her left hand clenched once and opened flat.

"They're standing where the blade catches it," Jaina said, too fast. "Don't let them bracket us. And don't cast straight through the middle unless I tell you. The cave will kick it sideways."

Sylvanas moved on the last word.

Her first arrow left at full draw and took Arthas under the collar where plate gave way to mail. Any normal body would have signed its death there. The impact rocked him half a step. Frost spread from the wound faster than blood. He closed a hand around the shaft and pulled it free.

The arrow rang on stone.

"Left shelf!" Sylvanas said.

The rangers broke as ordered, one taking the stone lip for angle, the others widening the chamber so Arthas could not sweep them in one straight rush. Cyndia put an arrow through the throat of the nearest enthralled guard before he fully moved. He staggered backward without falling until Muradin's hammer took him across the helm and dropped him in a spray of frost.

Jaina planted her boots and threw both hands forward. The cast was ugly with haste, built to strike. Blue-white force ripped across the floor toward Arthas—

—and shattered six feet short of him.

The air took it, split it, flung the fragments sideways in a glittering fan that smashed into the walls and sent shards rattling down.

Jaina inhaled through her teeth. "There."

Her right hand sliced back through the glittering air at a tighter angle. She tracked the exact place where the spell had broken sideways. Two fingers hooked, flattened, hooked again, redrawing the turn before the shard-fall finished ringing off the walls.

Arthas moved.

He came low and fast, sabatons shaving frost from the floor in white spray. One stride ate half the blue light between him and Muradin. Frostmourne rode low enough to hiss over the ice before Muradin met him two-handed. Hammer and enchanted steel struck hard enough to split the chamber's sound.

Muradin skidded three body lengths and nearly lost his footing. Arthas took one step and came on.

Sylvanas loosed twice more, one shot for the knee, one for the eye. The first cracked off plate furred thick with unnatural frost. The second Arthas caught on Frostmourne's guard without seeming to see it. He turned the same motion into a cut at the floor between them.

"Back!" Jaina shouted.

Too late for perfect obedience.

Frostmourne bit stone. The chamber rang. Cracks raced through the ice-veined floor. Then the left wall blew outward where Jaina's first spell had weakened it. Shards the size of knives and bucklers scythed through the room.

Sylvanas hit the ground and rolled behind a stone outcrop. A ranger cried out. Cyndia hauled her clear by the harness before the next spray came down. Muradin turned toward the sound and took the largest shard high under the collarbone where armour opened at the joint. The impact spun him sideways and slammed him into the wall hard enough to ring his helm.

He did not rise.

Sylvanas read it in one sweep: one ranger bleeding and still on her feet, Muradin down, enthralled soldiers closing from both flanks, Arthas advancing through the glittering ruin without hurry because he no longer needed it. Jaina had read it too. One hand locked around her own wrist to steady it. The other stayed out toward the open space between them. She drew the same two hooked motions into the air and watched them die short in front of her fingertips.

"Can you break it?" Sylvanas said.

"Not here."

Enough.

One enthralled soldier reached the fallen ranger. Cyndia cut his hamstring. He went down and kept crawling until a second arrow pinned his throat to the ice.

Arthas stopped ten paces away. Frost ran out around his sabatons in thin white spokes. He watched them.

"You cannot stop this," he said.

Jaina laughed once, breathless and furious. "I know."

Her hands snapped inward. Instead of throwing power straight through the middle again, she cast at the chamber mouth behind them. Ice surged from the floor in a ribbed wall that narrowed the exit to two bodies abreast and broke the first rush of the enthralled soldiers. Clever. Enough to leave.

Sylvanas was already beside Muradin. The shard had gone in above the mail seam and broken off deep. Blood came dark around the ice. He was breathing. Unconscious, then. Better than dead. Worse than useful.

"Carry!" she said.

Two rangers went to Muradin at once. Cyndia took rear guard. Jaina backed toward the mouth without turning, one hand holding the narrowing wall, the other still twitching small furious notations. Sylvanas covered the retreat, aiming not at Arthas anymore but at joints and eyes and knees—anything that bought heartbeats from the men around him.

Arthas did not rush the barrier. He watched them.

That unsettled her more than pursuit would have. Frostmourne hung easy in his hand. No lunge at the narrowing gap. No need.

Then Jaina's wall broke in a rain of brittle fragments and retreat became speed.

They burst out through the cave throat with Muradin dragging between two elves, his boots carving twin trenches in the snow. Light's Vengeance still burned where it had fallen, gold under drift and half-rimed over. Sylvanas looked at it once and kept moving. No hand free. No room left in the world where sentiment ranked above the living weight on their shoulders.

Outside wind hit them full in the face.

"Count," Sylvanas said.

"Here."

"Here."

"Here."

Cyndia answered last, rough with exertion. The wounded ranger answered after her through clenched teeth. Good. All seven.

They went down the glacier at a brutal half-run, Muradin's weight shifting between the carriers whenever the slope turned treacherous. Once one ranger slipped and nearly took all three down. Sylvanas caught Muradin's harness strap, braced, shoved them upright, and kept them moving. Above them the cave mouth shrank to a dark cut in white stone. Nothing came out after them.

The arrow still rang in her memory when it struck stone instead of killing him. Muradin's blood had frozen black around the shard buried at his collar. She stopped thinking prince. Whatever had stood in that chamber wearing Arthas's face had left the prince behind.

Jaina walked with her gloves off because she could not stop her fingers any other way. They shook as they moved, tracing invisible turns between strides. Hook. Line. Break. Return. Once she nearly ran into Cyndia following a pattern nobody else could see.

"Eyes up," Sylvanas said.

Jaina blinked, focused, nodded once, and kept drawing.

"I heard the hitch in it," she said, rubbing numb fingers together once before they flew apart again. "There's a split where it catches the cast. Right there. It catches the spell and throws it sideways. Same crooked turn each time."

"Can you use it?"

"Not yet."

Jaina did not run that way.

Muradin's harness strap had already carved a burning line into Sylvanas's palm. Jaina was still beside her with bare, shaking hands, working the problem while she walked. Sylvanas turned once toward the glacier and found only the hard blank where blame should have been. Not the road. Not the woman beside her.

For one step her jaw locked. Then she steadied.

"Good," she said.

Jaina blinked, startled. Then she went back to the invisible work between one step and the next.

By the time the rough white ring of Muradin's camp showed below them through the whitening dark, Muradin had groaned once and gone limp again. Alive enough for handoff. The rangers carried him through the gate feet first while dwarves shouted for a surgeon, hot water, clean iron, cloth.

Sylvanas did not follow them inside at once.

She stood one moment in the yard and watched Jaina stop by the shelter wall, stare at nothing, and draw the same invisible figure again with bare shaking fingers. The lines matched every time.
