# Chapter 20: The Voyage

Seastone Harbor stayed hidden until the last turn of the switchback.

Wet stone, slipping hooves, Cyndia riding ahead with one hand out to judge the width of the path. Then the coast opened below them: six berths of dock, a net yard gone pale with salt, the bronze dish of the last signal cairn catching the light at the harbor's eastern lip, houses crouched under the cliff, and beyond them the North Sea, flat and hard under a darker sky.

The cold changed first. Road cold carried damp and old inland smoke. This came clean off the water and found the gaps in Jaina's gloves at once.

A team of harbor hands hauled an empty fish cart uphill along the inner edge of the path. Wheels skidded. One of the men swore under his breath. The smell reached her a moment later: tar, kelp, old nets, and the raw mineral bite the wind had carried since dusk.

Below, the *Wolfheart* rode at the outer berth.

A hard-water schooner. Dark hull. Patched canvas. Rigging repaired more often than replaced. Two fishing boats sat farther in against the quay, their decks cluttered with crab pots and split cork floats. Nothing else in the cove looked capable of reaching another continent.

Good, Jaina thought.

Then: too small.

By the time they reached level ground, her thighs had begun to shake. Harbor mud took the horses' shoes with a wet suck. Cyndia had already turned in the saddle, counting doors, boats, men, the distance from stable to dock. Sylvanas only raised two fingers. The rangers broke without a word.

Jaina dismounted and nearly missed the ground.

She kept one hand on the mare's neck, let the animal's warmth soak into her palm, then straightened. The sending-stone sat cold under her coat. She left it there.

"Captain?" one of the harbor hands called toward the schooner. "Riders."

A woman came down the gangplank hitching a weather-stiff coat over one shoulder, broad through the chest, braid gone iron-grey at the temples. She planted her boots with the certainty of long years at sea. She took in horses, bows, mud, staff.

"North run?" she asked.

Jaina opened her mouth.

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

The woman turned back to Jaina. "You're the mage."

Jaina had lost patience for being anyone's most expensive tool weeks ago. "If the crossing needs one."

"Everything north needs one." The captain spat into the harbor mud. "Dagny Ironhull. *Wolfheart's* mine. Northrend costs triple this late in the season, and that's if the sea's merely bad."

She named a number.

Jaina felt the number in the purse strap biting deeper into her shoulder. Triple passage meant counting out enough stamped gold to fill Dagny's palm twice over.

Sylvanas said, "Done."

Dagny's brows drew together a fraction. Jaina said nothing. By the time the old objection reached her tongue, Sylvanas was already turning toward the horses.

"Half now," Dagny said. "Half on landfall, if landfall happens. Afternoon tide. Horses stay here. I won't have them breaking their legs on my deck when the weather turns."

Jaina turned toward the line of mares, steam barely rising from their flanks in the harbor cold. The mares' coats had dried white at the harness seams, and the leather under Jaina's hand was still warm. From here the chase had to balance on wet planks and Dagny's tide math.

The mare nudged her sleeve, searching for the apple she did not have.

Jaina put a hand on the white stripe above the animal's nose. "I'm aware," she murmured.

Dagny snorted. "Tell the horse if you like. Stable fee still stands."

That won a dry sound from Sylvanas.

Saddles off. Bedrolls cut down to what could travel without killing someone in heavy weather. Arrow bundles rewrapped in oilcloth. Jaina's reagent pouches sorted for salt exposure and quick reach. The dock boards sweated under every boot. Nets on tall poles slapped wetly when the wind shifted. Somewhere nearby, fish guts hit a bucket with the sound of stones dumped into a basin.

At the harbor's eastern lip stood the last Farstrider signal cairn before the sea: stone post, bronze dish, the chain's final bright point. A runner arrived while Jaina was crouched beside her staff case, brass relay cylinder still warm from his hand. Route, vessel, destination, departure hour. Cyndia took the note, left one in return, and sent him uphill before the tide changed.

Cyndia checked the runner's seal, folded the slip once, and tucked it under the thong at her bracer. Harbor. Vessel. Destination. Departure hour. Nothing in it that could bring help.

The sending-stone knocked once against Jaina's ribs when she rose.

The temptation had burned out somewhere between Stratholme and the coast. Still she touched it through the wool. Smooth surface. Dead chill.

Sylvanas came up beside her with a waterskin in one hand. "You're squinting at a rock."

"An expensive rock."

"Leave it in your coat, then. We're boarding."

She took the skin, drank, and by the time she lowered it Sylvanas was already halfway to the gangplank.

The horses went last.

Stable hands led them uphill two by two toward the cliff-path livery. Hooves rang once where the stone rose through the mud. Jaina watched until her mare vanished around the first bend and the moving tail gave way to bare rock and a turning net frame.

Then she stepped onto the *Wolfheart*.

The deck shifted under her at once. Wet planks flexed differently than road or stone. The whole vessel flexed with the water in small muscular adjustments her knees resented on principle. Sailcloth smelled of old salt and drying mildew. Tar line. Wet rope. A bucket tapped wood in a rhythm too irregular to forget.

Crew moved around them without wasting motion. Two at the halyards. One at the stern line. Another already up in the rigging, hands black with tar and cold. Cyndia and the rangers turned shipboard as cleanly as they had turned roadward at every other threshold in this pursuit. One took the bow side. One the aft lane. Cyndia paused just long enough to read the narrowing around the mainmast, then chose a place with the gangplank and the hatch in view.

Jaina set her staff by the rail and tightened the leather wrap around its head. The gangplank thudded under her boots. Behind her, stable hands were already hauling the mares toward the cliff path while the deck shifted under her knees with the tide.

Dagny's voice cut across the planks. "Cast off."

Line splash. A sailor's grunt. Pole thudding against dock timber.

Jaina looked up in time to watch the quay begin to slide away.

Slowly. The ship drew away by inches. The distance to the net yard opened by a foot, then another. The houses under the cliff drew together. A barefoot boy on the dock shaded his brow to get a better view of the fools sailing north in weather like this.

The cairn held longest. Bronze catching one dull smear of light above the eastern lip. After that the breakwater stakes. After that the cliff path itself, pale against the rock. After that only cliff line.

Jaina took the sending-stone out then.

The amber weight of it lay in her palm, dull as old resin. She turned it once while the breakwater slipped past. Then she shoved it deep into her coat again.

Sheltered chop gave way to a longer roll that lifted the schooner from beneath instead of slapping at her sides. Rigging strain replaced the last shore sounds. Gulls vanished behind them. Spray salted the rail. The last harbor smell went out of the air. All she had left was wet rope in one hand, canvas snapping overhead, and the deck lifting under her boots.

Only then did the northward pull in Jaina's head clear.

No crowd of roads around it now. No ward-stones. No city smoke. One line rose out of the horizon, harder than before. An ache set in behind her teeth.

"Well?" Dagny called from the helm.

"North."

Dagny barked a laugh. "Useful."

The first tack went clean. So did the second.

On the third, the wind failed in a way weather should not fail. The fore-and-aft canvas snapped wrong. The *Wolfheart* lost her easy slice and shuddered broadside to the swell hard enough that the bucket tore free and skidded across the deck.

One sailor trapped it with a boot.

Dagny hauled the helm over. "Again."

They came about. Canvas filled. For six breaths the schooner held her line.

On the seventh the bow stopped climbing and slammed back into the swell. Canvas cracked. The boom jerked hard enough to wrench the schooner sideways. Jaina felt it across skin and spell alike: the same tooth-ache and eye-pressure the ward-stones had given her, only bigger now, with no wall or roof to blunt it. The schooner heeled under her boots. The man in the rigging nearly lost his grip. Rope sang through a block with a high raw note that set her teeth on edge.

Dagny turned her head. "That's no sea wind."

No.

Jaina braced a hand on the rail and let herself look with the part of her mind she had spent weeks refusing to indulge.

Cold struck first. Then distance. Cold needled behind her eyes. The ache in her teeth turned bright. The line north had been clean at the harbor. Out here it thickened into resistance. Pressure layered on pressure. Deliberate. Every time she leaned north, it tightened and shoved back. She tasted old bread at the back of her tongue so sharply it flooded her mouth with saliva.

She swallowed and reached.

A narrow probe, frost-thin, slipped into the wind the way she might test a ward seam with two fingers.

The headwind hit at once.

It sheared sideways and down. Sailcloth cracked. The boom lurched. The vessel lost the line altogether and slewed across the swell, spray blowing over the bow hard enough to sting her face.

Dagny was already bellowing for reef points and line hands, but she spared one glare for Jaina. "That's you."

"Yes."

"Can you stop doing it?"

"Not if we mean to find what's causing it."

"Splendid."

The crew needed hands. Jaina caught the line thrown toward her and hauled with the sailor beside her. The rope bit through wet gloves. The *Wolfheart* fought every correction. Each time the prow nosed north, the pressure stiffened and the canvas snapped back against them.

By full dark the bow won a little on one tack and lost half of it on the next. The line hands grunted on every correction, and Jaina's wrists had started shaking whenever the rope came round again.

They could still make headway, but only if Jaina worked with the ship instead of against it, lending force where Dagny chose instead of trying to drive her own line through resistant water. When the schooner came about, Jaina laid frost under the windward canvas to stiffen its shape for a heartbeat while the line hands hauled. When a gust hit broad where it should have struck quartering, she shouldered it aside just enough to save the mast the worst of it and let the tack complete. Anything larger brought the headwind back harder.

Reef at Dagny's shout. Release at the next. Brace knees with the roll. Duck wet line. Mind the hatch combing. Learn where the planks grew slickest under the scuppers. Learn which sailor cursed before trouble and which after. Learn the pump-handle rhythm below when spray found a seam.

The harbor vanished within an hour. By midnight the coast was gone. Outside the lantern glow there was only black water, white rips where crests tore, and spray blowing flat off the tops. Sylvanas appeared only in intervals: one gloved hand on a shroud, posted where the footing turned worst, watching the deck with the same patience she used for ambush ground.

Somewhere in the longest watch, Dagny shoved a mug into Jaina's hand. It steamed weakly and smelled of boiled leaves and iron kettle.

"Drink."

Jaina obeyed because the mug was already there. Bitter. Too hot. Wonderful.

Dagny kept her attention on the sail. "You push when I call for it. No heroics between calls."

"I hadn't planned any."

"You've got the face for them."

Jaina took another swallow. "You don't know me."

"I know mages. Close enough." Dagny jerked her chin northward. "Whatever's ahead notices when you knock. From here on you knock only when I tell you the hull can bear the answer. Understood?"

Jaina bit the inside of her cheek and kept her answer to one word. "Understood."

"Good. Lee rail. Half a glass. You look ready to disgrace yourself."

Nothing came up over the lee rail. The nausea stayed lower, dense and ugly under the ribs, helped along by cold and exhaustion and a horizon sliding with the ship. She leaned there until the sourness backed off.

"You keep choosing environments that hate you."

Sylvanas had come to stand beside the rail stanchion and wait there. Salt shone on the leather at her shoulders. Her bow was wrapped high to keep the string dry. She held another waterskin.

Jaina took it, drank, handed it back. "I would have accepted a warmer one."

"Then we should have boarded a better ship."

"On whose purse?"

"The captain's, ideally."

What escaped Jaina came close enough to a laugh to sting.

The hull climbed another swell. She set her hand harder on the rail.

"If you lock your knees," Sylvanas said, "the ship will throw you."

"Thank you. I had mistaken this for stable ground."

"You mistake many things for things that will obey you."

That should have been bait. Instead Sylvanas's attention stayed on sail, helm, and the sailor crossing behind them with a coil of wet line on one shoulder. She said it in the same tone she had used for the waterskin and the warning about locked knees, then went back to watching the sail and the wet line crossing behind them.

Jaina rested her forehead against the cold air a moment, then said, "You have become free with your opinions."

"You've become expensive to transport."

Closer this time. The near-laugh scraped out of her.

Dagny raised a hand from the helm. "Now, mage."

Jaina set the mug down by her boot and stepped clear of the rail.

She did it properly after that.

She gathered cold from the spray blowing over the deck and from the skin of the sea itself, drew it through the ache in her hands, and set it where the sail needed spine for one heartbeat longer than canvas could hold alone. Frost feathered over the line she chose and vanished as the strain changed. The *Wolfheart* climbed the next swell without yawing off. Dagny hauled them through the tack. Crew shouted, answered, hauled. Jaina released before the answering pressure hit full force.

The headwind shoved back anyway.

Bad angle. Dagny's order. Jaina's correction. Rope burn through wet gloves. Salt drying on her mouth and being replaced by more. Lantern light swinging gold over faces narrowed into the same stripped concentration. A sailor on pump duty changed out with another; the cough was different. Cyndia appearing once from the hatch with a blanket around one of the younger rangers' shoulders, then vanishing below after making certain the captain needed nothing she could steal or threaten from the ship.

Near dawn the sea worsened.

Meaner.

The swells lost their broad patience and came at the hull crosswise. Spray iced along the forward rail. One crewman went down on the foredeck and fetched up against a cleat hard enough to split his lip. Dagny wasted no curse on him. She only pointed, and another sailor was there with a rag before the blood finished thinning into saltwater.

Jaina's shoulders had begun to burn in a steady band. Every spell lodged there instead of leaving cleanly. The next one dragged under her shoulder blades before it answered. Her hands were slower to unclench afterward, and the burn stayed where it was.

Daylight returned as diluted pearl through cloud. It improved nothing except visibility.

With the deck fully seen, the labor looked harsher. Hands reddened raw despite gloves. Knots retied one-handed because the other hand was busy keeping a body upright. Canvas patched in three places old enough to count as original. Buckets set under two separate drips below the aft hatch.

Jaina found herself respecting Dagny against inclination.

The captain wasted no movement. She knew which complaint belonged to weather, which to rigging, which to fear in a green hand's face. Eyes on the patched canvas. Hand on the mast when she listened. Head turning before each order left her mouth.

Around midmorning Dagny crossed to where Jaina stood with one palm braced on the mainmast.

"Another day of this, if the sea stays cruel," she said.

"Has it ever not been?"

"No. That's why sailors stop asking." Dagny squinted north. "Can you buy me more speed without drawing whatever's out there straight down on us?"

Jaina weighed the pressure ahead, the lashback every time she reached too far, the ache already building behind her eyes. "Some."

"A real some, then," Dagny said.

"Better." Dagny tipped her chin aft. "Do it from the stern. Keeps you clear of my line hands."

Jaina moved back. A cross-sea tried to slide her toward the scupper and she caught the rail with more urgency than grace. Two paces away, Sylvanas's shoulders had gone still and her hand had tightened on the shroud.

From the stern, the wake stretched behind them in a white mangled seam and vanished into grey. No land. No birds. Only the stern lantern, the white wake, and the next wall of grey water lifting toward them.

Jaina planted her staff beside her boot and set both hands on the worn wood.

Brute force would only bring the wind back harder.

She found the ship's motion first: lift under the bow, drag along the hull, the split-second lag as canvas took instruction from rope and mast and hand. Then she slid her magic into the same motion, shaving the worst edge off the opposing drag where it struck. Frost laced the stern rail, crawled up the staff head, melted down her gloves. In, brace, release. In, brace, release.

For a while it worked.

The *Wolfheart* lengthened her stride through the water. No miracle. A grudging gain. Enough for the sailors at the after lines to trade one sharp glance. Dagny altered course by degrees and spent the gain immediately.

Jaina kept going.

Cloud sank until noon looked like late dusk. The watch glass turned, the lanterns stayed lit, and Jaina stopped trusting the sky to tell her the hour. Cold settled into the joints. Once, when she blinked hard to clear her sight, the deck kept tilting after her eyes opened again, the fluid inside her skull sloshing on its own.

Sylvanas appeared at her left with a strip of salted fish and a heel of bread wrapped in cloth.

"Eat."

Jaina stared at the bread.

The old taste surged up so fast it nearly folded her. Mold where there was none. Sickness in the flour. Cold, motion, and deck labor had kept it distant all morning. One look at the crumb shoved it forward again, rancid and intimate.

"No."

Sylvanas's gaze dropped to the untouched food and rose again. "You need it."

"I said no."

Too sharp. A sailor two paces off gave them one look and moved away.

Sylvanas did not. Wind snapped the hood string against her throat. "Then broth when they've got it hot."

Jaina wanted to apologize and had no use for apologies that changed nothing. "Later," she said.

Sylvanas held the bread one heartbeat longer, reading the refusal without asking it to explain itself, then handed the bundle to a ranger. "Later."

The next answering gust hit during a tack. It drove the bow off course despite Dagny's correction, and Jaina pushed too hard in reply.

The wind hit her at once.

On the line of spell she had run out past prudence. Cold rammed back along it like iron through a sheath. Her teeth clacked. White flared across her sight. The deck went out from under her, or she went out from under it, and for one blank instant there was only the certainty of falling with both hands still locked around the staff.

The fall stopped at the shoulder.

Sylvanas had the back of her coat.

Sylvanas dragged her into the lee of the stern ladder, where a body could go down without being trampled. Jaina's knees hit planks hard enough to promise bruises. Her staff clattered beside her with a thin, humiliating sound.

She sucked air that would not go deep enough.

"Again!" Dagny roared from somewhere forward. "Bring her around, damn you, again!"

Jaina tried to rise.

Sylvanas shoved her back down with one hand on the ladder rung. "No."

"I can still—"

"No."

Spray shot under the ladder and struck them both. Jaina pushed wet hair out of her face with an unsteady hand. The deck swung and settled and swung again.

"We lose time," she said.

"If you go over the rail, we lose more."

"I wasn't going over."

"You were one bad roll from it."

Jaina looked up.

Sylvanas had crouched so the exchange would stay theirs. Water ran from the point of her jaw in irregular drops. Her expression had gone spare with concentration, the way it did before an arrow left the string or an order left her mouth.

"Dagny can carry the ship," she said. "Your work is to remain someone who can stand when she needs you again."

Jaina let out one ugly half-laugh. "Since when do you care about captains' margins?"

"Since one agreed to take us north."

That would have been enough. But Sylvanas's attention flicked to the hand Jaina had braced on the deck.

"And since you keep mistaking damage for usefulness."

Jaina had no answer for that. The deck rose under her knees and made speech irrelevant.

She was tired enough to obey.

Sylvanas reached to a hook by the stern companion and took down a heavier oilskin gone stiff with old salt. She wrapped it around Jaina's shoulders with the brisk economy of covering freight and stood before the gesture could gather more meaning than warmth required.

"Count fifty," she said. "Then decide whether your magic has improved."

Jaina did not trust herself to answer.

So she counted.

At seventeen her pulse stopped trying to leave through her throat. At thirty the reeling in her inner ear eased enough for the planks to become one surface again. At forty-two she realized Sylvanas had not gone back to her old post. She had taken station three paces off, where she could watch the stern approach and Jaina both.

At fifty Dagny's voice cut through the deck noise again.

"Mage."

Jaina rose.

This time she went with the ship's need instead of her own appetite to prove endurance.

Less reach. Cleaner timing. When Dagny called, Jaina answered. Between calls she conserved what remained. The gain was small and ugly. Rope burn stung through wet gloves, and her soaked sleeves chilled harder each time the wind found them.

Blankets smelled of brine. Hair dried stiff with it. The skin at the corners of Jaina's mouth cracked when she spoke. Below deck, where she finally allowed herself to sit for part of one watch, the hull worked at every seam under the swell, timber answering timber while the lantern swung over stacked crates, spare line, a barrel of lamp oil, and the narrow shelf where the rangers had wedged themselves to sleep without rolling to the floor.

Cyndia passed her a tin cup of broth with grease cooling on top.

Jaina drank it because liquid was easier than bread. Salt. Onion boiled to surrender. A scrap of fish too soft to argue with. The warmth spread through her chest and stopped there.

"Still upright," Cyndia said.

"By rumor alone."

"Good enough." Cyndia hooked a thumb toward the ladder. "Your ranger is glaring holes in the hatch every time someone takes too long coming below."

Jaina held the cup in both hands. "My ranger?"

Cyndia's mouth twitched. "If you like, I can call her someone else's."

Jaina set the cup down before she spilled it. "You sound cheerful for a woman on a ship."

"I enjoy surviving in places that ought to kill other people."

"Comforting."

"You'll live."

Cyndia said it dry as inventory and reached for the cup before the deck could tip it into Jaina's lap.

When the cup was empty, Jaina let herself lean once against the lee cloth rigged over the shelf and shut her eyes. Tar seam. Lamp smoke. Hull-knees creaking under the lift. The ugly thought arrived there, in the dark built for sleeping and not sleep at all: Kel'Thuzad had broken with Dalaran because he had been certain. Arthas had broken with everything because he had been certain. She had left on certainty too. She saw Sylvanas's hand on the ladder rung again and heard the flat no over the deck noise. The thought stayed because her body had obeyed before her pride had caught up.

When Jaina climbed back to the deck, night had thinned without breaking. A pallor ahead suggested cloud reflecting off something brighter than cloud. The cold had sharpened beyond sea-cold into a cleaner knife. Spray striking the rail no longer only froze. It crusted.

Northrend.

The pressure she had been following no longer lived only in magic. Spray crusted on the shrouds. The air burned colder in her nose. Even the dawn light came off the coast like metal.

She stood beside the mainmast with the oilskin still on her shoulders and watched the eastern dark turn metallic blue. The crew spoke less now, not from peace but from depletion. Each order had been pared to the fewest words that would move the right body. Dagny's voice had gone raw enough to scrape, but every order still landed clean.

Sylvanas came up beside her, one glove trailing once over a frost-rimed shroud, and gave the coast a glance north.

"You were right," Jaina said before she could stop herself.

"Usually."

"About the ship. About stopping." She rubbed cracked salt from one thumb joint. "I keep thinking that if I can still stand, I can still spend."

"Poor measure."

Yesterday she might have bristled at it. Now it only sounded exact.

Jaina watched the water thicken from black to gunmetal. "Antonidas always preferred proof purchased dearly. So did I."

Sylvanas turned her head. "And?"

The words formed and died there. Too neat for cracked lips, cold shrouds, and a deck still moving under her feet.

What came out instead was, "I can know better and still keep pushing."

"Then teach it another one."

Jaina tipped her chin once.

Under the growing light, shapes emerged ahead that had been hiding inside the horizon: first a pallor in the world, then the first broken pan of ice rolling past the hull with a dull knock. Another followed. Then three together, blue-white at the edges.

Dagny swore with feeling. "Forward watch!"

A sailor went to the bow with pole and gaff. Speed bled away. The *Wolfheart* changed from hard passage to careful entry, every sound aboard suddenly more deliberate because careless wood on hidden ice would kill them more efficiently than any haunted wind had managed.

Jaina reached for the pressure ahead and found it unchanged in direction, changed in scale. Broader now. Near enough that the pull split: one drag along the shore path, another deeper inland, a third pooled cold around the bay mouth.

Land shaped itself at last from the pallor.

Black rock first. Then snow in the seams. Then a low shoulder of coast under cliffs furred with old ice. No welcoming harbor line. No neat approach channel. Northrend stood ahead in the sea.

"Daggercap Bay," Dagny said, and spat over the side.

They edged in on soundings and shouted corrections. One sailor with a pole at the bow. Another heaving lead and calling depth. Dagny reading current, rock color, the crease of water over what it concealed. Jaina kept her magic to herself unless the hull needed one nudge off a drift line. Even that rang louder here.

Beyond a hooked point of stone, they saw the bay: a narrow inner cove under sharp walls, one side built out with newer pale timber atop older dwarven trade docks blackened by weather. At the far end of the bay, three hulls stood black in the ice, ribs charred bare above the frozen waterline. Closer in, a burned longboat lay canted among black rocks above the water mark, one side opened to naked ribs. The burn pattern had taken weather.

Jaina straightened.

The second proof stood farther in: new pilings driven along the inner cove, rough and hurried, their tops still pale where the cuts had not greyed. Crates under hide covers near the shore path. A collapsed cookfire ring. Snow and frozen mud packed by many boots across the dock heads and hardened again in layers.

No people.

Where there had been labor there should have been stragglers, guards, someone too tired or too injured to keep up. Instead the bay held gull-less silence, dark water under ice, and the remains of use after the users had gone.

"How long?" Cyndia asked behind her.

Dagny swept the shoreline once. "Long enough to cut more dock, land stores, and move on. More than days."

Weeks, Jaina thought.

Jaina read the timing from the wood, the burned hull, the emptied camp edge, and the crust over old boot marks.

Arthas had been here long enough to cut dock, land stores, and move inland.

The pull inland had widened. It no longer pricked from one point; it dragged through the whole bay mouth and up the shore path beyond.

The *Wolfheart* touched dock with a muted bump.

Lines went over. Crew jumped to make fast. No one aboard mistook landfall for comfort.

Jaina bent for her staff and had to pause halfway because her hands had begun shaking again.

The dock held steady under her boots, but her hands still shook when she looked past the crates toward the inland trail.

Sylvanas came alongside, her attention moving over shore, burned boat, crude cache, inland tracks the wind had failed to erase.

"They're ahead," Jaina said.

"Yes."

Jaina drew in air so cold it scoured the lungs on the way down. Tar and salt had gone out of it. Ice. Char. Old camp grease. Spruce somewhere inland under the harder mineral smell of the coast. Another continent. Another stage of the pursuit. Too late for comfort. Still time to keep after Arthas.

She stepped onto the dock.
