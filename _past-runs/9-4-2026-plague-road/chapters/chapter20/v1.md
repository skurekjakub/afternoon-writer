# Chapter 20: The Voyage

Seastone Harbor hid under the cliffs until the last turn of the switchback.

One moment there was only wet stone, hooves slipping and catching, Cyndia ahead with one hand out from the saddle to judge the width where the path narrowed. The next the coast opened below them in a hard grey notch: six berths of dock, a net yard gone pale with salt, houses crouched low against the wind, and beyond all of it the North Sea, flat as hammered lead under a sky the same color and a shade darker.

The cold changed first. Road cold had weight and damp and the old smoke of inland fires in it. This came clean off the water and found the gaps in glove seams at once.

Jaina pulled the mare back as a team of harbor hands hauled an empty fish cart uphill on the inner edge of the path. Wheels slid. One of the men swore softly without looking up. The smell reached her a breath later: tar, kelp, old nets, and the raw mineral bite the wind had been carrying since dusk on the coast road. Sea before sight. Now sight matched it.

Below, the *Wolfheart* rode at the outer berth.

Not large. Not graceful. A working schooner with dark hull planks, patched canvas, and rigging that looked more repaired than replaced. Her masts leaned into the harbor wind like things accustomed to argument. Two fishing boats sat farther in against the quay, their decks cluttered with crab pots and split cork floats. Nothing else in the cove looked remotely capable of reaching another continent.

Good, Jaina thought, and then immediately: too small.

The descent took long enough for her thighs to start shaking. Too much saddle time over too few days. The motion eased when the path leveled under the cliff. The ghost roll stayed in her knees anyway, the body's complaint at having one more mode of travel demanded of it. Harbor mud took the horses' shoes with a wet suck. Cyndia had already turned in the saddle, counting doors, boats, men, distances from stable to dock. Sylvanas did not need to look at her to know the count was happening. She only raised two fingers. The rangers broke without a word.

Jaina dismounted and almost missed the ground.

Not enough to fall. Enough to know how close she had been. She kept one hand on the mare's neck a moment longer than dignity asked for and let the animal's heat soak through the damp mane into her palm. The sending-stone sat cold under her coat when she straightened. She left it there.

"Captain?" one of the harbor hands called toward the schooner. "Riders."

No bell. No ceremony. A woman came down the gangplank hitching on a weather-stiff coat, plainly dragged from some more useful task. Broad-shouldered. Thick braid gone iron-grey at the temples. She moved like someone who had spent enough years at sea to distrust dry land on principle. Her gaze passed over Jaina, stalled on Sylvanas for half a beat, then kept moving over horses, bows, the road-caked state of all of them, and back to Jaina's staff.

"North run?" she asked.

Jaina opened her mouth.

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

The woman looked at her, then at Jaina again. "You're the mage."

Not a question. Jaina had lost patience for being anybody's most expensive piece of equipment weeks ago. She only said, "If the crossing needs one."

"Everything north needs one." The captain spat into the harbor mud, narrowly missing her own boot. "Name's Dagny Ironhull. *Wolfheart's* mine. Northrend costs triple this late in the season, and that's if the sea's merely bad."

She named a number.

Jaina felt it like another headwind. Enough gold to buy a house in a small town, or a year's patience from the sort of magistrate who liked being paid to discover he had always agreed with you.

Sylvanas did not even blink. "Done."

Dagny's brows came together a fraction. Perhaps she had expected argument for the ritual of it. Jaina would've argued on reflex a month ago. Show the arithmetic, shave the edges, prove the price excessive. There was no room left for that kind of correctness.

"Half now," Dagny said. "Half on landfall if landfall happens. You board on the afternoon tide. Horses stay here. I won't have them panicking on my deck when the weather turns."

Horses.

That landed harder than the gold. Jaina turned before she meant to, looking over the line of mares standing with steam barely lifting from their flanks in the sea cold. They had carried Stratholme out of the body, chapter by chapter, road by road. The bay waited beyond them, and beyond the bay a ship, and beyond the ship something colder than any road she had ever mapped. From here forward pursuit would depend on planks and rope and one captain's appetite for danger.

The mare nudged her sleeve, searching for the apple she did not have.

Jaina put a hand on the white stripe above the animal's nose. "I'm aware," she murmured.

Dagny's eyes flicked down. "Talk to the horse all you like. Doesn't change the stable fee."

That got a dry sound from Sylvanas that might, in another country, have passed for amusement.

Work swallowed the next hour.

Saddles off. Bedrolls cut down to what could travel aboard ship without getting everyone killed. Arrow bundles rewrapped in oilcloth. Jaina's reagent pouches sorted for salt exposure and immediate reach. The dock boards sweated under every boot sole. Nets hanging on long poles beside the yard slapped wetly when the wind shifted. Somewhere close by, fish guts hit a bucket with the sound of somebody emptying a bowl of stones.

At the harbor's eastern lip, a Farstrider signal cairn stood on a shelf of rock above the water: stone post, bronze dish, the chain's last bright point before the sea took over and the network ceased to exist. A runner arrived while Jaina was crouched by her staff case, brass relay cylinder warm from his hand, not with new orders but with the old world's final habits still trying to maintain themselves. Cyndia took the note, left one in return, and the runner went back uphill at a trot before the tide changed.

No rescue hidden in it. No second thought from Dalaran or Silvermoon. Just route, vessel, destination, departure hour. The sort of clean useful facts institutions could still carry once the power to alter them had already failed.

The sending-stone knocked once against Jaina's ribs when she rose from the crate.

That, more than the cairn, did the work.

Not because she was tempted to draw it. She wasn't. The temptation had burned out on the road north, or in the camp south of Stratholme, or in the silence after she'd made herself place it back inside her coat instead of into Antonidas's waiting hand through impossible distance. She touched it once through wool all the same. Smooth surface. Dead chill. No answer. Merely an object now, and still she carried it.

Sylvanas came up beside her with a waterskin in one hand. "You're squinting at a rock."

"An expensive rock."

"Leave it in your coat, then. We're boarding."

There was care in the line only because Jaina had learned where to look for it. Not softness. Not invitation. A direct correction applied at the right moment. She took the skin, drank, and nearly missed the fact that Sylvanas had already moved on toward the gangplank.

The horses went last.

Stable hands led them uphill two by two toward the cliff path livery, hooves ringing once where the stone rose through mud. Jaina watched until her mare disappeared around the first bend and the sight of the tail was replaced by nothing but grey rock and one net frame turning slowly in the wind.

Then she turned and stepped onto the *Wolfheart*.

The deck gave under her at once. Not much. Enough. Wet planks flexed differently than road or stone, the grain slick beneath her boots, the whole vessel answering the water in tiny muscular shifts that her knees resented on principle. Sailcloth smelled of old salt and drying mildew. Tar line. Wet rope. A bucket rolling a finger-width inside its brace and tapping wood in a rhythm too irregular to become background.

Crew moved around them without making a performance of it. Two at the halyards. One at the stern line. Another up in the rigging already, hands black with tar and cold. Competence had a look. It looked like people too busy to be dramatic.

Cyndia and the rangers turned shipboard in the same instant they had turned roadward at every other threshold in this pursuit. One took the bow side. One the aft lane. Cyndia herself paused only long enough to read where the deck narrowed around the mainmast before claiming a position with a view of both gangplank and hatch.

Jaina set her staff beside the rail, crouched to retie the leather wrap around the head, and felt the harbor moving around her in separate dissolves. Dock underfoot becoming ship underfoot. Cliff above becoming mast. Stable path taking the horses upward while the tide would take them north. No one moment of departure broad enough to stand inside. Only accretion, piece by piece, until there was no unmaking it.

Dagny's voice cut across the deck. "Cast off."

Line splash. A sailor's grunt. The thud of a pole against dock timbers.

Jaina looked up in time to see the quay begin to slide.

Not fast. That was the cruelty of it. The harbor did not break away dramatically. It loosened. Boards drifting from boards. The net yard giving back a foot of distance, then another. The houses on the cliff notch drawing together as perspective took over. A boy on the dock, barefoot despite the cold, shading his eyes to get a better look at the fools sailing north in weather like this.

The cairn remained visible longest. Bronze dish catching one smear of bad light. After that the cliff path itself, a pale switchback scored into the rock. After that only cliff line.

Jaina took the sending-stone out then.

No spell. No last attempt. Merely the object in her palm, dull amber, not lighting even enough to pretend. She turned it once while the breakwater slipped by and thought of nothing at all for the length of three breaths because thinking would have made the thing larger than it deserved.

When she put it back, she put it deep.

Past the harbor mouth, the sea changed.

The sheltered chop gave way to a longer roll that lifted the schooner from beneath rather than merely slapping its sides. Rigging strain replaced the last shore sounds. Gulls vanished behind them one white wing at a time. Cold took over from smell. Even the light altered, flattened by open water until everything looked reduced to rope, sail, plank, hand, wave.

Only then did the northward pull in her head clear.

No crowd of roads around it now. No ward-stones. No city smoke. Just one line, harder than before, rising out of the horizon and fastening somewhere behind her teeth with a steadiness that made her close her eyes against it.

"Well?" Dagny asked from the helm.

Jaina opened them again. "North."

Dagny barked a laugh with no humor in it. "Useful."

The first tack went clean. So did the second.

On the third, Jaina felt the change a heartbeat before the crew did. Not because the wind strengthened. Because it refused the angle it should have taken. The sailcloth snapped wrong. The schooner lost the easy slice she'd been building and shuddered broadside to the swell hard enough that the bucket broke free of its brace and went skidding across the deck.

One sailor caught it with a boot and swore.

Dagny hauled the helm over. "Again."

They came about. Canvas filled. For six breaths the *Wolfheart* held her line. On the seventh the headwind hit, not as gust but as answer.

It slammed flat into the bow.

Not a storm front. Not the shifting bad temper of northern weather. A shut door. Jaina felt it across skin and spell alike, the same family of wrongness she had been reading since the ward-stones, only vast now, stripped of all the little carriers and local disguises. The schooner heeled. The man in the rigging nearly lost his grip. Rope sang through a block with a high raw note that set her teeth on edge.

Dagny looked straight at Jaina.

"That's no sea wind."

No. It wasn't.

Jaina braced a hand on the rail and let herself look with the part of her mind she'd spent weeks refusing to indulge.

Cold struck first, then distance, then that peculiar sensation of pattern becoming visible because you had touched it in exactly the place it did not want to be touched. The line north had been clean at the harbor. Out here it thickened into resistance. Pressure layered on pressure, not random, not weather, not any natural quarrel of temperature and sea. A response. She tasted old bread at the back of her tongue so sharply it made saliva flood her mouth.

She swallowed against it and reached.

Not with a full spell. Just a narrow probe, a filament of frost-thin attention slipped into the wind the way she might test a ward seam with two fingertips.

The answer came instantly.

The headwind sheared sideways and down. Sailcloth cracked like a whip. The boom lurched. One of Dagny's crew shouted as the vessel lost the line altogether and slewed ugly across the swell, spray blowing over the bow in a burst cold enough to sting Jaina's face.

Dagny was already bellowing for reef points and line hands, but she cut the next order short long enough to spit, "That's you."

Jaina wiped salt from her mouth. "Yes."

"Can you stop doing it?"

"Not if we mean to find what is causing it."

"Splendid."

There was no room on deck for shame. The crew needed motion, not apology. Jaina grabbed the line thrown toward her, hauled when the sailor beside her hauled, and learned at once how little raw arm did against canvas and sea. The rope bit wet into her gloves. The schooner fought every correction. North was no longer merely direction. North had become notice.

By full dark they had settled into the terms of it.

The terms were bad.

They could make headway, but only if Jaina worked with the ship instead of ahead of it, lending force to what Dagny chose rather than forcing her own line through water that rejected the attempt. When the *Wolfheart* came about, Jaina laid frost under the windward canvas to stiffen shape for a breath. When a gust hit broad where it should have hit quartering, she shouldered it aside just enough to keep the mast from taking the whole complaint. Small interventions. Timed ones. Anything grander called the answer back harder.

That lesson established itself with bruising speed.

Reef at Dagny's shout. Release at the next. Brace knees with the roll. Duck the sweep of wet line. Mind the hatch combing when the deck pitched. Learn where the planks grew slickest under spill from the scuppers. Learn which sailor cursed before trouble and which after. Learn the rhythm of the pump handle below when spray found a seam and bilge wanted tending.

Travel ceased to mean motion through landscape. It became labor in place.

The sea erased the harbor within an hour. By midnight it had erased even the idea of coast. Nothing remained outside the lantern circles except black water showing its teeth in brief white rips and the intermittent pale of breaking crests. Jaina saw the deck because it kept trying to throw her. She saw the crew because each body had become a working part of the vessel. She saw Sylvanas at intervals only, posted where the worst footing met the most exposed lanes, one gloved hand on a shroud, hair stripped nearly white by spray, watching the whole arrangement with the hard patience she used for ambush country.

Somewhere in the longest watch, Dagny thrust a mug at Jaina that steamed weakly and smelled of boiled leaf and iron kettle.

"Drink."

Jaina obeyed because the mug was already in her hand. Bitter. Too hot. Wonderful.

Dagny kept one eye on the sail above them. "You push when I call for it. No heroics between calls."

"I hadn't planned any."

"You've got the face for them."

Jaina took another swallow. "You don't know me."

"I know mages. Close enough."

The captain jerked her chin toward the north. "Whatever's ahead, it notices when you knock. That means from here on you knock only when I tell you the hull can bear the answer. Understood?"

The old reflex rose in Jaina at once. I understand the magic here better than you do. The variables are not all visible to you. It would have been true and useless.

She said, "Understood."

Dagny's mouth twitched around the edge of surprise, then flattened again. "Good. Take the lee rail for half a glass. You've got the color of someone deciding whether to be sick."

Jaina discovered, with annoyance, that the assessment was exact.

At the lee rail she got nothing up. The nausea stayed lower, a dense miserable turning under the ribs, helped along by cold and exhaustion and the way the horizon refused to hold still long enough for the body to negotiate terms with it. She breathed through her teeth until the sourness backed off.

"You keep choosing environments that hate you."

Sylvanas had come to stand with one boot braced against the rail stanchion, not crowding, not touching. Only present. Salt gleamed on the leather at her shoulders. Her bow was wrapped and slung high to keep the string dry. She held another waterskin.

Jaina took it, drank, handed it back. "I would have accepted a warmer one."

"Then we should have boarded a better ship."

"On whose purse?"

"The captain's, ideally."

The absurdity of it almost produced a laugh. What came out was smaller and died in the wind before it could embarrass her.

Below them the hull struck a swell and climbed. The deck tilted under her boots. She set her hand harder on the rail.

Sylvanas watched the knuckles whiten and said, "If you lock your knees, the ship will throw you."

"Thank you. I had mistaken this for stable ground."

"You mistake a great many things for things that will obey you."

Jaina looked sideways at her.

That should have been bait. The tone had room for it. Instead Sylvanas's attention was on the sail, on Dagny at the helm, on a sailor crossing behind them with a coil of wet line on one shoulder. The remark had been filed where she kept corrections, beside drink and footing and which direction trouble would arrive from.

Jaina let her gaze go back to the water. "You have become very free with your opinions."

"You've become very expensive to transport."

Another near-laugh. Closer this time.

The wind shifted. Jaina felt the line of wrongness tighten again before any sail moved. Dagny raised one hand from the helm without taking her eyes off the dark ahead.

"Now, mage."

Jaina set the mug down by her boot and stepped clear of the rail.

She did it properly after that.

Not reaching blind. Not forcing. She gathered cold from the spray blowing across deck and from the skin of the sea itself, drew it through the ache in her hands, and set it where the sail needed spine for a heartbeat longer than canvas could hold alone. Frost feathered over the line she chose and vanished as the strain changed. The *Wolfheart* climbed the next swell without yawing off. Dagny hauled them through the tack. Crew shouted, answered, hauled. The moment passed. Jaina released before the answering pressure could find her fully.

North did not like being denied. It pressed anyway.

The hours became that exchange repeated with small variations cruel enough to stay interesting.

Bad angle. Answering gust. Dagny's order. Jaina's correction. Rope burn through wet gloves. Salt drying on her mouth and being replaced by more salt. Lantern light swinging gold over faces that kept narrowing into the same stripped concentration. A sailor on pump duty changing out with another so quickly Jaina only noticed because the cough was different. Cyndia appearing once from the hatch with a blanket around one of the younger rangers' shoulders, then vanishing below again after making certain the captain needed nothing she could steal or threaten out of the ship.

Near dawn the sea worsened.

Not higher. Meaner.

The swells lost their broad patience and came at the hull with cross-angles in them, left over from weather somewhere beyond sight. Spray iced where it hit the forward rail. One crewman took a spill at the foredeck and fetched up against a cleat hard enough to split his lip. Dagny never wasted a curse on him. She only pointed with two fingers, and another sailor was there with a rag before the blood finished mixing with saltwater.

Jaina's shoulders had begun to burn in a steady band. Every spell lodged there after use instead of leaving cleanly. She knew the feeling. It meant she was borrowing from reserve now, not from the easy upper store of trained reaction.

Daylight returned as diluted pearl through cloud. It improved nothing except visibility.

With the deck fully seen, the labor looked larger. Hands reddened raw despite gloves. Knots retied one-handed because the other hand was occupied hanging onto life. Canvas patched in three places old enough to have become permanent geography. Buckets set under two separate drips below the aft hatch. A life at sea measured not in romance or distance but in what broke, what was mended, and how long the mending held.

Jaina found herself respecting Dagny against inclination.

The captain wasted no movement and no attention. She knew which complaint belonged to weather, which to rigging, which to fear in a green hand's face. She carried the *Wolfheart* the way a good field commander carried a retreating line, never indulging the fantasy that things were fine, never surrendering to the evidence that they weren't.

Around midmorning Dagny ordered reduced sail and came off the helm long enough to cross to where Jaina stood with one palm against the mainmast.

"We've got another day of this if the sea doesn't take pity," she said.

"Has it ever?"

"No. That's why sailors stop asking." Dagny's eyes narrowed. "Can you buy me more speed without drawing whatever's out there straight down on us?"

Jaina considered the northward pressure, the repeated lashback every time she extended too far, the ache already building behind her eyes. "Some."

"Not a mage's some."

"A real some, then."

"Better."

Dagny tipped her chin toward the stern. "Do it from there. Keeps you out from under my line hands."

Jaina moved aft. The deck pitched under a cross-sea that tried to slide her toward the scupper, and she caught the rail with more urgency than grace. Sylvanas, two paces away and pretending not to track her footing, failed to look unconcerned by a margin Jaina found oddly cheering.

From the stern the wake stretched behind them in a white mangled seam that vanished into the grey. No land. No birds. No sign that the world held anything beyond this one bad ship and the thing ahead objecting to its approach.

Jaina planted her staff butt in the deck seam beside her boot, laid both hands on the worn wood, and began to work.

The trick was not force. Force only made the answer larger.

She found the ship's motion first: the lift under the bow, the drag along the hull, the split-second lag as canvas took instruction from rope and mast and hand. Then she slid her magic into the same pattern, not making a second engine exactly, more taking the sea's refusal and shaving the worst of its edge where it struck. Frost laced the stern rail, crawled up the staff head, melted in runnels over her gloves. Breath burned in and out. Each correction had to end before the resistance noticed it fully. Enter, brace, release. Enter, brace, release.

For a while it worked.

The *Wolfheart* lengthened her stride through the water. Not a miracle. A grudging improvement. Enough for the sailors handling the after lines to exchange one sharp glance that counted as gratitude in people too busy to speak it. Dagny changed course by degrees to spend the gain immediately.

Jaina kept going.

Cloud lowered. Time lost edges. Noon might have passed or failed to arrive at all. The cold seeped through layers and settled in the joints. Once, when she blinked hard to clear her sight, the deck kept tilting after her eyes had reopened, as though the ship had entered her skull and taken command of the fluid there.

Sylvanas appeared at her left with a strip of salted fish and a heel of bread wrapped in cloth.

"Eat."

Jaina stared at the bread.

The old taste surged up so fast it almost folded her over. Mold where there was none. Sickness in the flour. The memory had lived at a tolerable distance all morning because the sea had occupied every other sense. One look at the crumb and it shoved itself forward again, rancid and intimate.

"No."

Sylvanas's gaze dropped from her face to the untouched food and back. "You need it."

"I said no."

The answer came too sharp. A sailor two paces away glanced over and thought better of lingering in it.

Sylvanas did not move. Wind flattened her hood string against her throat, released it, flattened it again. "Then broth when they've got it hot."

Jaina wanted to apologize and had no use for apologies that solved nothing. She said, more carefully, "Later."

Sylvanas held the bread where it was for one breath longer, reading the refusal without asking it to explain itself, then handed the whole cloth bundle to one of the rangers instead. "Later," she said.

That mercy nearly hurt worse.

The next answering gust struck during a tack and came down with enough violence to drive the bow off course despite Dagny's correction. Jaina pushed too hard in response.

The wind found her at once.

It did not hit skin. It hit the line of spell she had extended beyond prudence and rammed cold up it like iron through a sheath. Her teeth clacked together. White flared across her sight. The deck lurched out from under her boots, or she lurched away from it, and for one blank instant there was only the certainty of falling with her hands still locked around the staff.

The fall stopped at the shoulder.

Sylvanas had hold of the back of her coat.

Not gentle. Efficient. A drag backward into the lee of the stern ladder where a body could collapse without being immediately washed under three sailors' boots. Jaina's knees hit planks hard enough to bruise. Her staff clattered beside her, the sound thin and humiliating in the storm of rope and shouted orders above it.

She sucked air that would not go deep enough.

"Again," Dagny roared from somewhere forward. "Bring her around, damn you, again."

Jaina tried to rise on the first order in her own head.

Sylvanas shoved her back down with one hand on the stern ladder rung before she got halfway. "No."

"I can still—"

"No."

Spray blew under the ladder and struck them both. Jaina pushed wet hair out of her eyes with an unsteady hand. The deck around them swung and settled and swung again. She hated that her body could not decide which angle counted as upright.

"We lose time," she said.

"If you pitch over the rail, we lose more."

"I wasn't pitching over the rail."

"You were a thought from it."

Jaina looked up at her.

Sylvanas crouched because standing would have made the conversation visible to everyone on deck. Water ran from the point of her jaw in intermittent drops. Her expression had gone spare with concentration, the way it did before an arrow left the string or an order left her mouth. Nothing soft there either. Only decision.

"Dagny can carry the ship," she said. "Your part is to remain someone who can stand when she needs you again."

Jaina laughed once, ugly with breathlessness. "Since when do you care for captains' margins?"

"Since one agreed to take us north."

That would've been enough on its own. Transaction. Necessity. Fit for both of them. But Sylvanas looked at the hand Jaina had braced on the deck and said, quieter, "And since you have mistaken damage for usefulness every day I've known you."

The words landed with the force of something simpler than accusation.

Jaina might have answered any number of ways if the ship had been still. She might have told Sylvanas exactly where she could put field observations on a mage's working thresholds. She might have said Antonidas's name and regretted it. She might, worst of all, have said thank you.

Instead the deck rose under her knees, making speech irrelevant for a moment, and in that pause truth arrived in a smaller form than she'd expected.

She was tired enough to obey.

The realization sat there between them, indecently bare.

Sylvanas reached to the hook by the stern companion and took down a heavier cloak, one of the ship's oilskins gone stiff with old salt. She wrapped it around Jaina's shoulders with the brisk economy of covering freight and stood before the gesture could gather more meaning than warmth required.

"Count fifty breaths," she said. "Then decide whether your magic has improved."

Jaina did not trust herself to answer.

So she counted.

At seventeen her pulse stopped trying to leave through her throat. At thirty the reeling in her inner ear eased enough for the planks to become one surface again instead of several competing proposals. At forty-two she realized Sylvanas had not returned to her previous post but had taken station three paces off, where she could watch the stern approach and Jaina at the same time without appearing to do either.

At fifty Dagny's voice hit the air again. "Mage."

Jaina rose.

This time she went with the ship's need instead of her own appetite to prove endurance.

Less reach. Cleaner timing. When Dagny called, Jaina answered. Between calls she conserved what remained. The work lost any flavor of triumph. Good. Triumph had no business here. They were not conquering distance. They were purchasing it by the hour with rope burns, soaked wool, and the body's increasingly sullen compliance.

By the second night the sea had entered everything.

Blankets smelled of brine. Hair dried stiff with it. The skin at the corners of Jaina's mouth cracked when she spoke. Below deck, where she finally allowed herself to sit for part of one watch, the hull groaned around the ribs like some exhausted animal forced to keep breathing by the weight of men walking its spine. One lantern swung over stacked crates, spare line, a barrel of lamp oil, and the narrow shelf where the rangers had wedged themselves to sleep in turns without rolling to the floor.

Cyndia passed her a tin cup of broth gone greasy on top.

Jaina drank it because liquid was easier to negotiate than bread. Salt. Onion cooked to surrender. A scrap of fish too soft to chew much. The warmth spread through her chest and stopped there, unwilling to promise more.

"Still upright," Cyndia observed.

"By rumor alone."

"Good enough." Cyndia hooked one thumb toward the ladder overhead. "Your ranger is glaring holes in the hatch every time someone takes too long coming below."

Jaina held the cup in both hands. "My ranger?"

Cyndia's mouth twitched. "If you like, I can call her someone else's. It won't improve the weather."

Jaina set the cup down before she spilled it. "You seem cheerful for a woman on a ship."

"I enjoy surviving in places that ought to kill other people."

"Comforting."

"You'll live."

Spoken with Farstrider authority, in the tone of a quartermaster informing death that it would wait its turn. Jaina closed her eyes for a moment. The hull's sway pulled at the bones behind them.

When she climbed to deck again, night had thinned but not broken. A dimness ahead suggested cloud reflecting off something paler than cloud. The cold had sharpened beyond sea-cold into a cleaner knife. Spray striking the rail no longer only froze. It crusted.

Northrend.

Not revelation. Recognition at a distance. The pressure she'd been following for days no longer sat solely in magic. It had entered air and water and light themselves, flattening all three into a harder register.

She stood beside the mainmast with the oilskin still around her shoulders and watched the eastern dark become a slow metallic blue. The crew spoke less now, not from peace but from depletion. Each order on deck had been reduced to the fewest words capable of moving the right body. Dagny's voice sounded flayed and perfectly functional.

Sylvanas came up to her with no waterskin this time, only her own presence and a glance north.

"You were right," Jaina said before she could decide against it.

Sylvanas did not ask about which right. "Usually."

"About the ship. About stopping." Jaina rubbed cracked salt from one thumb joint. "I keep thinking if I can still stand, I can still spend. As if standing were the measure."

"It is a poor one."

The answer might have irritated her yesterday. Now it only sounded exact.

Jaina looked over the rail at water thickening in color from black to gunmetal. "Antonidas always preferred proof purchased dearly. So did I."

Sylvanas's gaze shifted to her face and stayed there a breath, no longer. "And?"

And I have made a habit of confusing cost with truth.

She did not say it. The sentence was too neat, too ready for a page in some future history she had no intention of writing.

What came out instead was, "The body keeps the old lessons longer than the mind does."

"Then teach it another one."

Simple as a command. Difficult as anything she had done this year.

Jaina nodded once.

That was all.

The sky opened by degrees. Under the new light shapes emerged ahead that had been hiding inside the horizon: not land yet but a density in the world, a pallor where cloud and sea no longer met cleanly. Then came the first broken pan of ice, small as a tabletop, rolling past the hull with a dull knock. Another followed. Then three together, edges blue-white and rotten.

Dagny swore with genuine feeling this time. "Forward watch!"

A sailor went to the bow with pole and gaff. Speed bled away. The *Wolfheart* changed from hard passage to careful entry, every sound on board suddenly more deliberate because careless wood on hidden ice would kill them more efficiently than any haunting wind had managed.

Jaina reached for the pressure ahead and found it unchanged in direction, changed in scale. Broader now. Near enough that individual eddies of wrongness could be distinguished within the whole, like separate bells ringing under the same cloth.

Land shaped itself at last out of the pallor.

Black rock first, then snow caught in the seams of it, then a low shoulder of coast running east and west beneath cliffs furred with old ice. No welcoming harbor line, no neat approach channel. Northrend did not rise from the sea. It waited in it, ugly and unornamented, indifferent to whether anyone found it beautiful.

"Daggercap Bay," Dagny said, and spat over the side.

The name did nothing to soften the place.

They edged in on soundings and shouted corrections. One sailor with a pole at the bow. Another heaving lead and calling depth. Dagny reading current and rock color and the way the water wrinkled over what it concealed. Jaina kept her magic to herself unless the hull needed one nudge off a drift line. Even that rang louder here.

The bay opened around a hooked point of stone, and the first sign of lateness was not the dock.

It was the wreck hauled above the tide line.

Half a longboat, charred down one side to naked ribs, lay canted among black rocks above the tide mark. Burn pattern old enough to have soaked weather. Not last night. Not recent. Ahead of them by time as much as by distance.

Jaina straightened slowly.

The second sign stood farther in: new pilings driven along the inner cove, rough and hurried, their tops axe-bright where the cuts had not yet greyed. Men had expanded this landing in haste and then left enough traces for the cold to keep. Crates under hide covers near the shore path. A collapsed cookfire ring. Trampled snow dirtied by many boots and hardened again in layers.

No people. That made it worse.

Where there had been labor there should have been stragglers, guards, someone too tired or too injured to move with the main force. Instead the bay held only gull-less silence, the lap of dark water under ice, and those remains of use after use had gone.

"How long?" Cyndia asked from behind Jaina.

Dagny gave the shoreline one hard sweep of her eyes. "Long enough to cut more dock, land stores, and move on. More than days."

Weeks, Jaina thought.

Not because anyone needed to say it. Because the wood said it. The burnt hull said it. The emptied camp edge and the crust over old boot marks said it.

Arthas had not just passed through. He had been here long enough to organize absence.

The northward pull in her head no longer resembled a line. It resembled a road opening under snow, already used, already dangerous, already beyond any hope of catching at the shore.

The *Wolfheart* touched dock with a muted bump.

Lines went over. Crew jumped to make fast. No one cheered landfall. No one aboard had mistaken this for arrival in the comforting sense.

Jaina bent to lift her staff and had to pause halfway because her hands were shaking again.

Not from the sea this time.

Sylvanas came alongside, gaze on the shore, on the burnt boat, on the crude cache mound, on the tracks leading inland where the wind had failed to erase them completely.

"They're ahead," Jaina said.

"Yes."

No softening. No false promise that the gap could be wished smaller.

Jaina drew one long breath of air so cold it seemed to scour the lungs on the way in. Tar and salt had gone out of it. Ice, char, old camp grease, spruce somewhere inland beneath the harder mineral smell of the coast. Another continent. Another stage of the pursuit. Too late for comfort. Not yet too late for purpose.

She stepped onto the dock.
