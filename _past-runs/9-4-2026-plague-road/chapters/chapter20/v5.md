# Chapter 20: The Voyage

Seastone Harbor stayed hidden until the last turn of the switchback.

Wet stone, slipping hooves, Cyndia ahead with one hand out to judge the path. Then the coast opened below them: six berths, a salt-paled net yard, the bronze dish of the last signal cairn at the harbor's eastern lip, houses crouched under the cliff, and beyond them the North Sea, flat and hard under a darker sky.

The cold changed first. Road cold carried damp and old inland smoke. This came clean off the water and found the seams in Jaina's gloves at once. Tar, kelp, old nets. At the outer berth rode the *Wolfheart*: dark hull, patched canvas, rigging mended more often than replaced. Big enough to try for another continent. Small enough to make the attempt offensive.

By level ground her thighs had begun to shake. Harbor mud clung to the horseshoes with a wet suck. Cyndia was already counting doors, boats, men, the distance from stable to dock. Sylvanas only raised two fingers. The rangers broke without a word.

Jaina dismounted and almost missed the ground. She steadied herself on the mare's neck. The sending-stone sat cold under her coat. She left it there.

"Captain?" one of the harbor hands called. "Riders."

A woman came down the gangplank hitching a weather-stiff coat over one shoulder, broad through the chest, braid gone iron-grey at the temples. Her boots hit the dock with long-practiced certainty.

"North run?" she asked.

"Yes," Sylvanas said, before Jaina could.

The woman glanced at the staff. "You're the mage."

"If the crossing needs one."

"Everything north needs one." She spat into the mud. "*Wolfheart's* mine. Dagny Ironhull. Northrend costs triple this late, and that's if the sea's merely bad."

She named a number that bit deeper into the purse strap on Jaina's shoulder.

"Done," Sylvanas said.

Dagny's brows tightened. "Half now. Half on landfall, if landfall happens. Afternoon tide. Horses stay here. I won't have them breaking their legs on my deck when the weather turns."

Jaina turned to the mares. Steam barely rose from their flanks in the harbor cold. The chase had carried them this far on hoof. From here it would depend on wet planks and Dagny's tide math.

The mare nudged her sleeve, looking for the apple she did not have.

"I'm aware," Jaina murmured, laying a hand on the white stripe above her nose.

Dagny snorted. "Tell the horse if you like. Stable fee still stands."

That pulled a dry sound from Sylvanas.

Saddles came off under fast hands. Cyndia cut the bedrolls down to what heavy weather would tolerate. A ranger rewrapped arrow bundles in oilcloth. Jaina sorted her reagent pouches for salt and quick reach. At the harbor's eastern lip, the last Farstrider signal cairn caught a dull light while a runner brought a brass relay cylinder still warm from his hand. Route, vessel, destination, departure hour. Cyndia sent him uphill again before the tide changed.

The sending-stone knocked once against Jaina's ribs when she rose. The temptation had burned out somewhere between Stratholme and the coast. Even so, her fingers brushed it through the wool. Smooth surface. Dead chill.

Sylvanas came up beside her with a waterskin. "You're squinting at a rock."

"An expensive rock."

"Leave it in your coat, then. We're boarding."

By the time Jaina lowered the skin, Sylvanas was halfway to the gangplank.

The horses went last, led uphill two by two toward the cliff-path livery. Her mare vanished around the first bend. Then she stepped onto the *Wolfheart*.

The deck moved at once. Wet planks flexed unlike road or stone; the whole vessel answered the sea in small muscular shifts her knees resented on principle. Sailcloth smelled of old salt and drying mildew. Tar line. Wet rope. A bucket tapping wood in an irregular rhythm.

Crew moved around them without wasted motion. Cyndia chose a place with the gangplank and the hatch in view. Jaina set her staff by the rail and tightened the leather wrap around its head.

"Cast off," Dagny called.

Line splash. Pole thudding against dock timber. The quay began to slide away.

Slowly. The ship drew off by inches. The houses under the cliff narrowed together; a barefoot boy on the dock shaded his brow to watch the fools sailing north in weather like this. The cairn held longest, bronze catching one last smear of light above the eastern lip. Then breakwater stakes. Then cliff line.

Jaina took out the sending-stone, turned the dull amber weight once in her palm, and shoved it back into her coat.

Sheltered chop gave way to a longer roll. Rigging strain replaced the last shore sounds. Gulls vanished. Spray salted the rail. With the harbor behind them, the ache behind her teeth sharpened and the pull stopped smearing sideways through road and ward-stone. It drew straight over the bow into the dark.

"Well?" Dagny called from the helm.

"North."

Dagny barked a laugh. "Useful."

The first tack went clean. The second did too.

On the third, the wind failed in a way weather should not fail. Canvas snapped wrong. The *Wolfheart* lost her easy slice and shuddered broadside to the swell hard enough that the loose bucket tore free and skidded across the deck. A sailor trapped it with a boot.

"Again," Dagny said.

They came about. Canvas filled. For six breaths the schooner held her line. On the seventh the bow stopped climbing and slammed back into the swell. The boom jerked hard enough to wrench the vessel sideways. Tooth-ache. Pressure behind the eyes. The same wrongness the ward-stones had given her, only bigger now, with no wall or roof to blunt it.

Dagny turned her head. "That's no sea wind."

No.

Jaina braced a hand on the rail and reached with the part of her mind she had spent weeks trying not to indulge.

Cold struck first. Then distance. The pull bunched ahead of the bow and shoved back each time the schooner tried to nose north. Old bread flooded her mouth—rancid, immediate, the taste that had haunted her since Stratholme.

She sent out a narrow probe, frost-thin, the way she might test a ward seam with two fingers.

The headwind hit at once.

It sheared sideways and down. Sailcloth cracked. The boom lurched. Spray blew over the bow hard enough to sting. Dagny was already shouting for reef points and line hands, but she spared Jaina one glare.

"That's you."

"Yes."

"Can you stop doing it?"

"Not if we mean to find what's causing it."

"Splendid."

The crew needed hands. Jaina caught the line thrown to her and hauled with the sailor beside her. Rope bit through wet gloves. Each time the prow nosed north, the pressure stiffened and the canvas snapped back against them.

By full dark they had found the only way forward: Jaina working with the ship instead of against it. When Dagny called, she laid frost under the windward canvas to stiffen it for a heartbeat while the line hands hauled. When a gust struck broad where it should have hit quartering, she shaved just enough from its edge to spare the mast the worst of it. Anything larger brought the headwind down harder.

Reef at Dagny's shout. Release at the next. Brace knees with the roll. Duck wet line. Learn where the planks grew slickest beneath the scuppers. Learn which sailor cursed before trouble and which after. Learn the pump-handle rhythm below when spray found a seam.

The harbor was gone within an hour. By midnight the coast was gone too. Outside the lantern glow there was only black water, white rips where crests tore, and spray blowing flat off the tops. Sylvanas appeared in intervals: one gloved hand on a shroud, posted where the footing turned worst, watching the deck with the same patience she used for ambush ground.

Somewhere in the longest watch, Dagny shoved a mug into Jaina's hand. Weak steam. Boiled leaves. Iron kettle.

"Drink."

Jaina obeyed. Bitter. Too hot. Wonderful.

Dagny kept her attention on the sail. "You push when I call for it. No heroics between calls."

"I hadn't planned any."

"You've got the face for them."

Jaina took another swallow. "You don't know me."

"I know mages. Close enough." Dagny jerked her chin north. "Whatever's ahead notices when you knock. From here on you knock only when I tell you the hull can bear the answer. Understood?"

Jaina bit the inside of her cheek. "Understood."

"Good. Lee rail. Half a glass. You look ready to disgrace yourself."

Nothing came up over the lee rail. The nausea stayed lower, dense and ugly under the ribs.

"You keep choosing environments that hate you."

Sylvanas had come to wait beside the stanchion. Salt shone on the leather at her shoulders. Her bow was wrapped high to keep the string dry. She held another waterskin.

Jaina drank and handed it back. "I would have accepted a warmer one."

"Then we should have boarded a better ship."

"On whose purse?"

"The captain's, ideally."

What escaped Jaina came close enough to laughter to sting.

The hull climbed another swell.

"If you lock your knees," Sylvanas said, "the ship will throw you."

"Thank you. I had mistaken this for stable ground."

"You mistake many things for things that will obey you."

Her attention never left sail, helm, and deck traffic. She had used the same tone for the waterskin and the warning about locked knees.

Dagny raised a hand from the helm. "Now, mage."

Jaina set the mug by her boot and stepped clear of the rail. This time she waited for Dagny's signal. She drew cold from spray and sea, ran it through the ache in her hands, and set it where the sail needed spine for one heartbeat longer than canvas could hold alone. Frost feathered over the line she chose and vanished as the strain changed. The *Wolfheart* climbed the next swell cleanly. Dagny hauled them through the tack. Jaina released before the answering pressure hit full force.

The headwind shoved back anyway.

Bad angle. Dagny's order. Jaina's correction. Rope burn through wet gloves. Salt drying on her mouth and being replaced by more. Lantern light swinging gold over faces stripped down to the same hard focus. Cyndia came up once with a blanket around one of the younger rangers' shoulders, then vanished below again.

Near dawn the sea worsened.

The swells lost their broad patience and came crosswise. Spray iced along the forward rail. One crewman went down on the foredeck and fetched up against a cleat hard enough to split his lip. Dagny only pointed; another sailor was there with a rag before the blood finished thinning into saltwater.

A burn settled across Jaina's shoulders. Each spell lodged there instead of leaving cleanly. Daylight returned as diluted pearl through cloud and improved nothing except visibility. With the deck fully seen, the labor looked harsher: hands reddened raw despite gloves, knots retied one-handed because the other hand was busy keeping a body upright, canvas patched often enough to count as original.

Around midmorning Dagny crossed to where Jaina stood braced on the mainmast.

"Another day of this, if the sea stays cruel."

"Has it ever not been?"

"No. That's why sailors stop asking." Dagny squinted north. "Can you buy me more speed without drawing whatever's out there straight down on us?"

Jaina weighed the pressure ahead, the lashback each time she reached too far, the ache behind her eyes. "Some."

"A real some, then. From the stern. Keeps you clear of my line hands."

A cross-sea tried to slide Jaina toward the scupper as she moved aft. She caught the rail with more urgency than grace. Two paces away, Sylvanas went very still, one hand tightening on the shroud.

From the stern the wake stretched behind them in a white mangled seam and vanished into grey. No land. No birds. Only the stern lantern and the next wall of water lifting toward them.

Jaina planted her staff beside her boot and set both hands on the worn wood.

Brute force would only bring the wind back harder.

She found the ship's motion first: lift under the bow, drag along the hull, the brief lag as canvas took instruction from rope and mast and hand. Then she slid her magic into the same motion, shaving the worst edge off the opposing drag where it struck. Frost laced the stern rail, crawled up the staff head, melted down her gloves. In, brace, release. In, brace, release.

For a while it worked.

The *Wolfheart* lengthened her stride by degrees. No miracle. A grudging gain. Enough for the sailors at the after lines to trade one sharp glance before Dagny spent the advantage at once.

Cloud sank until noon looked like late dusk. Jaina stopped trusting the sky to tell her the hour. Once, after a hard blink, the deck kept tilting after her eyes opened again, the fluid inside her skull sloshing on its own.

Sylvanas appeared at her left with a strip of salted fish and a heel of bread wrapped in cloth.

"Eat."

Jaina stared at the bread.

The old taste surged up so fast it nearly folded her. Mold where there was none. Sickness in the flour. One look at the crumb shoved it forward again, rancid and intimate.

"No."

Sylvanas's gaze dropped to the food and rose again. "You need it."

"I said no."

Too sharp. A sailor two paces off gave them one glance and moved away.

Sylvanas did not. Wind snapped the hood string against her throat. "Then broth when they've got it hot."

Jaina wanted to apologize and had no use for apologies that changed nothing. "Later," she said.

One heartbeat. Then Sylvanas handed the bundle to a ranger. "Later."

The next answering gust hit during a tack. It drove the bow off course despite Dagny's correction, and Jaina pushed too hard in reply.

The recoil struck at once.

Cold rammed back along the line of spell like iron through a sheath. Her teeth clacked. White flared across her sight. Deck and balance vanished together, and for one blank instant there was only the certainty of falling with both hands locked around the staff.

The fall stopped at the shoulder.

Sylvanas had the back of her coat. She dragged Jaina into the lee of the stern ladder, where a body could go down without being trampled. Knees hit planks hard enough to promise bruises. The staff clattered beside her with a thin humiliating sound.

"Again!" Dagny roared from somewhere forward. "Bring her around, damn you, again!"

Jaina tried to rise.

Sylvanas shoved her back down with one hand on the ladder rung. "No."

"I can still—"

"No."

Spray shot under the ladder and struck them both. Jaina pushed wet hair out of her face with an unsteady hand.

"We lose time," she said.

"If you go over the rail, we lose more."

"I wasn't going over."

"You were one bad roll from it."

Jaina looked up.

Sylvanas had crouched close to keep the exchange under the deck noise. Water ran from the point of her jaw in irregular drops. Her expression had gone spare.

"Dagny can carry the ship," she said. "Your work is to stay on your feet until she needs you again."

Jaina let out one ugly half-laugh. "Since when do you care what a captain can spare?"

"Since one agreed to take us north."

That should have been enough. Then Sylvanas's attention flicked to the hand Jaina had braced on the deck.

"And since you keep trying to pay for everything in blood."

Jaina had no answer for that. The deck rose under her knees and made speech irrelevant.

Sylvanas took down a heavier oilskin from a hook by the stern companion and wrapped it around Jaina's shoulders with the brisk economy of covering freight.

"Count fifty," she said. "Then decide whether your magic has improved."

Jaina did not trust herself to answer, so she counted.

At seventeen her pulse stopped trying to leave through her throat. At thirty the reeling in her inner ear eased enough for the planks to become one surface again. At forty-two Sylvanas had not gone back to her old post. She had taken station three paces off, where she could watch the stern approach and Jaina both.

At fifty Dagny's voice cut through the deck noise again.

"Mage."

Jaina rose.

This time she went with the ship's need instead of her own appetite to prove endurance. Less reach. Cleaner timing. When Dagny called, Jaina answered. Between calls she conserved what remained. The gain was small and ugly, but it held.

Below deck, when she finally allowed herself part of one watch, the hull worked at every seam under the swell. Lantern light swung over stacked crates, spare line, a barrel of lamp oil, and the narrow shelf where the rangers had wedged themselves to sleep without rolling to the floor.

Cyndia passed her a tin cup of broth with grease cooling on top.

Jaina drank because liquid was easier than bread, because the bread-taste was already there. Salt. Onion boiled to surrender. A scrap of fish too soft to argue with. The warmth spread through her chest and stopped there.

"Still upright," Cyndia said.

"By rumor alone."

"Good enough." Cyndia jerked a thumb toward the ladder. "Your ranger is glaring holes in the hatch every time someone takes too long coming below."

"My ranger?"

Cyndia's mouth twitched. "If you like, I can call her someone else's."

Jaina set the cup down before she spilled it. "You sound cheerful for a woman on a ship."

"I enjoy surviving in places that ought to kill other people."

"Comforting."

"You'll live."

Cyndia said it dry as inventory and took the cup before the deck could tip it into Jaina's lap.

When she was alone, Jaina leaned once against the lee cloth and shut her eyes. Tar seam. Lamp smoke. Hull-knees creaking under the lift. The ugly thought arrived there in the dark built for sleeping and not sleep at all: Kel'Thuzad had broken with Dalaran because he had been certain. Arthas had broken with everything because he had been certain. She had left on certainty too. Sylvanas's hand on the ladder rung. The flat no over deck noise. The thought stayed because her body had obeyed before her pride had caught up.

When Jaina climbed back to the deck, night had thinned without breaking. A pallor ahead suggested cloud lit from below. The cold had sharpened beyond sea-cold into a cleaner knife. Spray no longer only froze. It crusted.

Northrend.

The pressure she had been following no longer lived only in magic. It was in the air now, in the metal taste of the dawn.

She stood beside the mainmast with the oilskin still on her shoulders. The crew spoke less, not from peace but depletion. Each order had been pared to the fewest words that would move the right body. Dagny's voice had gone raw enough to scrape, but every command still landed clean.

Sylvanas came up beside her, one glove trailing once over a frost-rimed shroud.

"You were right," Jaina said before she could stop herself.

"Usually."

"About the ship. About stopping. I keep thinking that if I can still stand, I can still spend."

"Poor measure."

Jaina rubbed cracked salt from her thumb joint and let the answer stand.

"Antonidas always preferred proof purchased dearly," she said after a while. "So did I."

Sylvanas turned her head. "And?"

The obvious answer sat there, raw as the skin at Jaina's mouth. What came out instead was, "I can know better and still keep pushing."

"Then teach it another one."

Jaina tipped her chin once.

Under the growing light, shapes emerged ahead: first a pallor in the world, then the first broken pan of ice rolling past the hull with a dull knock. Another followed. Then three together, blue-white at the edges.

Dagny swore. "Forward watch!"

A sailor went to the bow with pole and gaff. Speed bled away. The *Wolfheart* changed from hard passage to careful entry, every sound aboard more deliberate now. One careless strike on hidden ice would stove the hull faster than the headwind had.

Jaina reached for the pressure ahead and found it broader now. Near enough that the pull split: one drag along the shore path, another deeper inland, a third pooled cold around the bay mouth.

Land shaped itself out of the pallor. Black rock first. Then snow in the seams. Then a low shoulder of coast under cliffs furred with old ice. No welcoming harbor line. No neat approach channel. Northrend stood ahead in the sea.

"Daggercap Bay," Dagny said, and spat over the side.

They edged in on soundings and shouted corrections. A sailor with a pole at the bow. Another heaving lead and calling depth. Dagny reading current, rock color, the crease of water over what it concealed. Jaina kept her magic to herself unless the hull needed one nudge off a drift line. Even that rang louder here.

Beyond a hooked point of stone, the bay opened: a narrow inner cove under sharp walls, one side built out with newer pale timber atop older dwarven trade docks blackened by weather. At the far end, three hulls stood black in the ice, ribs charred bare above frozen water. Closer in, a burned longboat lay canted among black rocks above the water mark. The burn had taken weather.

Then the second proof. New pilings driven along the inner cove, rough and hurried, their tops still pale where the cuts had not greyed. Crates under hide covers near the shore path. A collapsed cookfire ring. Snow and frozen mud packed by many boots across the dock heads and hardened again in layers.

No people.

Where there had been labor there should have been stragglers, guards, someone too tired or too injured to keep up. Instead the bay held gull-less silence, dark water under ice, and the remains of use after the users had gone.

"How long?" Cyndia asked behind her.

Dagny swept the shoreline once. "Long enough to cut more dock, land stores, and move on. More than days."

The dock cuts were still pale, but the boot ruts at the camp edge had frozen, thawed, and frozen again under a skin of drift.

Weeks.

Jaina reached for the pull inland. It had widened, not faded. He had not stopped here. He had only passed through.

The *Wolfheart* touched dock with a muted bump. Lines went over. Crew jumped to make fast. No one aboard mistook landfall for comfort.

Jaina bent for her staff and had to pause halfway; her hands had begun shaking again.

Sylvanas came alongside, attention moving over shore, burned boat, crude cache, inland tracks the wind had failed to erase.

"They're ahead," Jaina said.

"Yes."

She drew in air so cold it scoured the lungs on the way down. Tar and salt were gone from it. Ice. Char. Old camp grease. Spruce somewhere inland under the harder mineral bite of the coast. The inland pull caught at her before the deck-roll had left her knees.

She stepped onto the dock.
