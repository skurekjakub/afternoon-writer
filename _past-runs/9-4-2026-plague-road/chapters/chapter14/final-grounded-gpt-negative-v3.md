# Chapter 14: The Revelation

The south road dropped out of Caer Darrow country and into the working land of Eastweald, which made the whole thing worse. Cut wheat stood in pale stubble. Orchard ladders leaned in the last apple rows. A wagon groaned north under sacks of meal and a tied-down chest. Smoke from supper fires lay low under the evening damp, and every farm they passed still looked arranged around the old assumptions: table, lamp, bread, sleep.

Chalk clung in the cracks of Jaina's fingertips. Lake water had not taken it. Canteen water had not taken it either. Her mare's reins were tack-slick in the same hand. Cyndia held the left flank. Sylvanas rode forward-right by half a horse, close enough that Jaina could hear leather tighten when a question was coming.

She kept the useful stack ready in her head. Grain carried the outside of the plague. The body learned the rest. Death completed it. Kel'Thuzad expected to return from it. The chain ran north to the Lich King and farther still. Under that sat the part she did not want to touch: the slate board in the sanctum, the cup gone cold on the desk, chalk dust on Kel'Thuzad's fingers, and Sylvanas in the doorway knowing in one glance that Jaina had been taught in a room built over butcher's work.

She could give the first stack aloud.

The second would hurt.

"Kel'Thuzad wasn't working for Lordaeron alone," she said.

"Go on."

"He named the Lich King in Northrend. He named the Burning Legion above that. This plague is the opening of something larger."

"A conquest."

"Yes."

"For whose army?"

"The dead first." Jaina watched the ruts instead of Sylvanas. "After that, whatever they're clearing the road for."

A farm dog ran along a hedge barking at the horses, lost courage, and fell back. Sylvanas let it bark itself empty.

"The grain," Sylvanas said.

"Only the shell." A cart coming the other way jolted past them, axle screaming, flour dust feathered white across one rope-dark sack. "It survives the road, the granary, the bakehouse oven. People eat from it once, twice, every day because why wouldn't they. The first layer settles in the gut. After that the body does the work for them. It starts learning a pattern it should never know. By the time the sickness looks like sickness, death is only the latch."

"What stands up when the latch opens?"

"Motion. Hunger. Direction. Enough for a corpse to get to its feet when the priest thinks the prayers are done."

"And the wards?"

"They stop force coming over a wall. They don't stop the loaf already cooling on a kitchen board." She swallowed. "A city can ward a gate. It can't ward every kitchen in it."

Sylvanas's gloved hand closed once on the reins.

"Hearthglen."

"Yes."

"Stratholme."

"Yes."

A farmer at a trough raised his head as they passed, eyes moving from Sylvanas's bow to their speed to the road behind them, measuring how close the trouble had come to his own fence posts.

"He built it in layers," Jaina said. "That's why the first reads kept catching the loud part and missing the quiet part. The grain was the loud part. The deeper work stayed hidden until it had time."

"You sound like he had you at a lectern."

"He had a room arranged for instruction."

"I saw the school below it."

Dust came off a merchant cart ahead of them and shredded in the crosswind. Jaina let that settle over the silence before she answered.

"I came in through Jandice," she said. "Oak tables. Ledgers. Shelves made to look like a scholar's pride. She wanted me seated before he started."

"I came in through the workrooms."

"I gathered that."

Sylvanas did not look at her. "Brass drawers. Iron tables. Villagers strapped down and still breathing under leather. Crates of hands. Parts sorted and labeled. He was teaching you over that floor."

Jaina's fingers drew the reins tighter without meaning to. Her mare tossed her head and settled again.

"Jandice chose my route."

"Yes."

"She wanted me impressed before I was useful."

"Was she right?"

The seam between two wheel tracks blurred. Jaina fixed on the dark line where one rut had held ditch water and the other had not.

"Useful enough to keep listening."

"Good," Sylvanas said. "That's the shape that matters."

Jaina faced forward. Wind pressed the orchard smell flat and carried it past.

"I did not go there to be impressed."

"No." Sylvanas's answer came dry and immediate. "You went there to understand."

"Yes."

"And he built the room for exactly that sort of visitor."

There was no defense in it she could use without sounding small.

"How it worked mattered," Jaina said.

"Then give me the part that matters next."

Jaina drew a slow breath. Cyndia checked the empty right flank again, quick and automatic, then the road ahead.

"He expected death to be temporary."

Sylvanas turned a fraction. "Speak plainly."

"He built a higher version for himself. Most dead rise crude. Herd-pulled. Enough for motion and command. He believed he'd solved the part that keeps memory and mind through death."

"Believed."

"He took an arrow through the throat and still sounded inconvenienced."

"You should have told me that first."

"Yes."

Leather creaked. Hooves struck through a shallow patch of mud and climbed out.

"But you told me."

"Yes."

They rode on. Late sun caught a far pane of farmhouse glass and flashed copper across it. Behind them the island lay out of sight, but the room stayed with her.

Warmth broke from Cyndia's side. The amber core of the relay crystal in her hand had lit through the dirt on her knuckles.

"Relay," she said.

She edged her horse closer and held the stone across. Sylvanas took it between two fingers. The glow stained her glove. Lor'themar's voice came through thin with distance and the long chain of stations between.

"Ranger-General."

"Speak."

"The Convocation has issued formal recall. Immediate return to Silvermoon for review and direct report. I am required to tell you the order stands above your present discretion."

Flat country gave Jaina nowhere to step aside from the words. A ditch. A hedge. Another field. Nothing else.

"There is more fear behind it than yesterday," Lor'themar went on. "They have heard enough of Hearthglen to be frightened and enough of the rest to think distance still solves it."

No one answered. A woman walking north with a basket on one hip shifted her child farther from the road as they passed.

"Sylvanas," Lor'themar said, dropping rank and title together. "If you have a reason I can spend, give me one."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the south road. "Tell them Hearthglen held for an hour and the dead kept getting back inside the walls. Tell them the plague answers through the body and no ward line ends at the skin. Tell them I am not riding north while cart traffic is still feeding south."

"That buys noise, not votes."

"Then buy noise."

The crystal hummed once under her fingers.

"Understood," Lor'themar said.

The light went out. Sylvanas handed the stone back. Cyndia closed her fist around it and put her mare on at once.

No one suggested slowing.

Farm smoke pressed flat under the damp. Traffic thinned. A priest on a mule. Two men hauling a broken wheel. A boy driving three goats with a switch and looking over his shoulder twice before he let them pass.

The sending stone at Jaina's belt warmed next. Violet light leaked through mithril filigree and across the heel of her hand. Antonidas never called twice in close sequence unless the space between had been full of people arguing.

She drew it free. "Archmage."

"Jaina. I have your earlier report. I need the fuller version now."

Need. Good.

She pictured him in the Violet Citadel at the Council table, one old man upright in his robes and five slower ones around him.

"Kel'Thuzad confirmed the plague is part of something larger," she said. "The Lich King is real. Northrend sits at the head of it. There is another power above him still, called the Burning Legion."

The pause after that was long enough to hear hoofbeats, harness bells from a distant wagon, wind in the hedge, all the little country sounds going on unchanged.

"And how does it work?"

"The grain carries the outside of it. Repeated meals drive it into the body until the body learns how to answer. Death completes that lesson. What rises in most cases is low and crude, but it answers. Enough for motion. Enough for command. Enough for a city to kill its own defenders while the walls are still standing."

"Can it be stopped?"

"Not broadly. Not with what I have in hand." Red sun burned in ditch water when the road dipped. "There may be places to cut it. Granary floors. Baker's bins. Holding yards where the flour still sits in bulk. Anywhere the shell can be broken before the body learns the rest."

"Yet," Antonidas said, and beneath the weariness the old teacher in him was already braced for that answer from her.

"Hearthglen is already in crisis. Arthas is moving. I am moving toward him."

"Come home."

The mare stepped around a buried stone and the world tipped the same ugly way the sanctum had tipped it.

"I can't."

"Child."

"If I ride north now, I bring you a theorem and leave the south to burn with it. If I keep riding, there may still be time to use part of it before Stratholme starts proving me right."

Wind hissed through the hawthorn hedge. Cyndia's mare tossed foam from the bit. Sylvanas gave no sign of listening, but she was taking every word.

"You are not obliged to solve this alone," Antonidas said.

Kel'Thuzad's room came up so hard her mare hit the next rut badly: one desk, one cup, Kel'Thuzad looking at her the way Antonidas used to when she reached the next line before he had to say it.

"No," Jaina said. "I know that."

The stone stayed hot in her palm.

"Then live through the next answer," Antonidas said.

That roughened something in her chest, but his voice held.

"Send what you can when you can. I will move what I am able."

"That is never the comforting version with you."

"No," he said. "It isn't."

Her chest loosened a fraction. There was the old dry impatience again, battered but present.

"Antonidas."

He waited.

She could not give him the room. Could not give him Kel'Thuzad's approval, or the one heartbeat in which the design had looked clean before judgment caught up.

"He wasn't mad," she said instead.

Silence on the line.

"No," Antonidas said. "I know."

The stone went dead in her hand.

She slid it back into its pouch. The leather felt clammy.

"You told him most of it," Sylvanas said.

"Most."

"Not the room."

"No."

"Very well."

Dusk thickened between the hedges, and hoofbeats broke hard from a side lane.

Cyndia's mare cut left across the road, bow up, broadhead level before the rider even cleared the turn. Sylvanas checked hard and turned in the saddle. Jaina caught her own reins short as a Farstrider burst out caked to the knees in mud with smoke worked into his cloak.

"Farstrider relay! South track!"

"Speak," Sylvanas said.

"Hearthglen held, then nearly failed again after dark. Uther is on the field. Arthas did not remain. He took the main body south-east at speed. Stratholme is his line now."

"How bad?"

"Bad enough the wounded are being burned before their kin are done saying the words over them."

Northbound wagons flashed through Jaina's mind again. Bedding. Trough water. Farmers feeding horses under a sky that still looked survivable.

"Numbers."

The rider hauled himself back from what he had seen. "No count worth trusting. Too much churn. Too many civilians in the withdrawal. Priests say bodies answer again after any lull longer than a few minutes. Captain Falric's line calls it a held city. The men coming off the wall call it a furnace with gates."

"How long ahead is the prince?" Sylvanas asked.

"Hours. Less if he pushed through the last relay without halting."

Of course he had.

Stratholme opened in Jaina's head as routes and feed lines: Market Row bakehouses, granary courtyards, warehouse lanes by the north service gate, carts threading flour into every district.

"Go back," Sylvanas told the rider. "Tell the next north station we are on the south road without stop. Keep the relays hot."

He wheeled his horse so hard it skidded in the mud before it found purchase.

Cyndia lowered her bow only when the noise had gone.

"Hours," Jaina said.

Sylvanas turned fully toward her for the first time since the road began. "If you have the ugly narrow possibility, now is when it becomes either a plan or dead weight."

Jaina dragged the Stratholme map from her satchel. Sweat had curled one corner. She flattened it against her thigh and laid the city she remembered over the black lines on the page.

"The feed points. Granaries first, if the flour is still in bulk. Market Row bakehouses after that. The warehouse lanes by the north service gate. Holding yards. Anywhere the shell still sits in quantity and hasn't gone to table yet."

"Break how."

"Cold, if I can lay it over the stores fast enough. Fire if I can't, and if the choice comes down to grain or living people. It won't cure what has already settled. It could keep part of the city from taking the next dose. It could blunt the turn if we beat the army in."

"Could."

"Yes."

"And if we're late."

Smoke lay south over the fields, thin and dark above the next rise.

"Then we learn that soon enough to do something with the answer."

Sylvanas held her gaze one beat longer.

"Good," she said. "That's a road answer."

Approval struck under Jaina's ribs and stayed there.

The ditches blackened first, then the stubble. Horses breathed harder. Cyndia checked the empty right again and said nothing.

"How far to the next relay?" Jaina asked.

"One hour at this pace," Cyndia said.

"Half," Sylvanas said.

No one argued.

They drove the horses harder. Eastweald closed down around them. Orchard rows turned to black combs. Windows kindled in farmhouses behind them, one here, three there. Horse lather flecked Jaina's boot. One bell rang close, slow and domestic. Farther south another answered sharp with alarm.

Stratholme lay open in her head: Market Row ovens, granary doors, warehouse lanes, service yards, every place the city fed itself, every place the plague still had to pass. Sylvanas kept the half-horse at her right shoulder. The map snapped once against Jaina's knee in the wind.

Smoke lay south over the fields, thin and dark above the city, and they drove toward it before the supper bells behind them had finished crossing the hedges.
