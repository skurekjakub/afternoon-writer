# Chapter 14: The Revelation

The King's Road south led them out of the Scholomance basin and back into the rolling farmlands of the Eastweald — country that ought to have been entirely ordinary.

Late sun struck the harvested wheat fields. Rows of apple and plum trees climbed the terraced hills in dark green bands. Draft horses groaned, hauling wooden wagons north packed with canvas sacks of winter root vegetables and iron-banded household trunks. A mastiff barked beyond a hawthorn hedge. The Eastweald kept working. They had come out of a necromancy school built over butcher's rooms and back into mud, split-rail fences, and people still thinking supper would happen if they got home before dark.

Alchemical chalk still coated the ridges of Jaina's fingertips. She had scrubbed at it in the shallows of Lordamere Lake, then from her leather canteen while they rode, and the pale white line kept coming back.

Cyndia held the left flank as she always did. Once, while a timber cart rattled past, the elven ranger's head flicked right to check the treeline before coming forward again. Habit. Older than the last few hours and sharper for them.

Sylvanas rode ahead and to the right by half a horse, close enough to hear every word.

Jaina kept one stack of facts ready for the road: Andorhal grain as the necrotic carrier, the human body absorbing the payload through repeated ingestion, physical death triggering the reanimation rune, Kel'Thuzad anticipating his own resurrection through a phylactery, the psychic tether reaching north across the sea to the Lich King and farther still. Under that sat the memory of the room. The slate chalkboard. The cold cup of tea on the mahogany side table. Kel'Thuzad's voice warming when he realised she understood his runic formulas. Sylvanas in the doorway, seeing the horrific scale of the slaughterhouse at once.

She could say the first stack aloud.

Speaking the second stack would hurt.

Jaina shifted in the saddle. The leather creaked. Speak from the first stack, then. Keep the rest boxed until the miles and the questions stopped landing together.

"Kel'Thuzad's Cult of the Damned isn't acting alone," she said.

Sylvanas did not turn. "Go on."

"He named the Lich King, Ner'zhul, imprisoned in Northrend. He named the demon lords of the Burning Legion commanding him. This isn't a localised curse. King Terenas's kingdom is being fed necromantic sleeper cells, pushed toward a demonic war before Lordaeron even knows the vanguard has landed."

Hooves beat their four-count into the packed dirt.

"A conquest," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

"For whose army?"

"The undead first. After that, the demons who hold their leashes."

Sylvanas went still in the way she did before drawing her bowstring. Her courser kept the half-step. So did she.

Jaina kept going without letting the pause stretch. "The grain is only the delivery mechanism. That much we knew. He filled in the arcane mechanics."

"Then fill it in."

She shaved off the necrotic diagrams that sounded too much like the chalkboard in the crypts. "The plagued grain survives transport, silo storage, and oven baking. Peasants eat it and keep eating it. The necromantic compound bypasses the stomach lining, seeping into the bloodstream and nervous system. The host's body starts rewriting itself to accept shadow magic. When the host dies, the corpse doesn't decay. Death triggers the payload."

"Releases what."

"Motion. Hunger. A hive-mind directive." Jaina kept her voice even. "Enough necrotic energy to forcefully resurrect the corpse and hand control of the motor functions to Northrend."

Cyndia's mare stamped through a flooded wagon rut. The harness rings stayed quiet; Thalassian leatherwork was built for stealth. Her own gear came through more clearly: a canvas pack strap at her shoulder, the Kirin Tor sending stone knocking at her hip, the oak staff butt tapping her stirrup when the road jarred.

"And the city wards?" Sylvanas asked.

"Arcane barriers deflect kinetic force and external fel corruption. They do nothing against a necrotic plague digested at the dinner table and carried past the gates inside human hosts. A city can ward its outer walls. It cannot ward every loaf of bread in its pantries."

Sylvanas's left hand tightened once on the leather reins.

"Hearthglen," she said.

"Yes."

"Stratholme."

"Yes."

The Darrowshire crossroads opened ahead, marked by a weathered shrine to the Holy Light and a stone trough where two barley farmers watered their oxen. One looked up as the riders passed. He watched their speed, their grim faces, and the recurve bow on Sylvanas's back, measuring how near the war was to his own fields.

Jaina went on. "He built the spell in layers. That's why the Kirin Tor divinations failed. The raw grain screams with magic because the outer shell is entirely visible to basic detection spells. But once baked and digested, the deeper runic signature masks itself inside the host's natural life force until the moment of death."

"You sound as if he had you at a desk for an afternoon lecture in Dalaran."

Jaina kept her eyes on the wagon ruts ahead. "He built a private study specifically to explain his work."

"I saw the slaughterhouse built under it."

Road dust lifted behind a merchant's cart ahead and broke apart in the crosswind. Jaina fixed on its wobbling rear axle because if she turned now she would read Sylvanas's half-step as judgment instead of measure.

"I came into Scholomance through the illusionist, Jandice Barov," she said. "Ledger books. Shipping manifests. Archive space. She wanted an audience."

"I came in through the livestock pens."

"I gathered that."

"Did you. There were brass drawers of severed human hands. A surgical theatre of infected peasants still breathing on operating tables. Another room with abomination parts sorted into butcher crates. He was teaching you over that floor."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the reins. "Jandice chose my route."

"Yes."

"She wanted me impressed by the logistical brilliance before I was useful to them."

"And was she right?"

Jaina stared at the muddy seam between two wheel tracks until it blurred. She could have lied. Instead, she found herself back in the study, alchemical chalk under her thumb, Kel'Thuzad smiling because he had finally found a mage who didn't need the simplified version of necromancy.

"Useful enough to keep listening," she said.

Nothing moved except the horses.

Then Sylvanas said, "Good. That is at least the true shape of it."

Jaina faced forward.

"I did not go to Scholomance to be impressed."

"No. You went there to understand the plague's mechanics."

"Yes."

"And he built that study for exactly that kind of Kirin Tor visitor."

There was no defence in that she could use without sounding pathetic. The cart ahead turned onto a hedge-cut lane. The road opened into wheat stubble and orchard shadow, the horizon feeling too broad and too exposed after stone corridors and magically sealed doors.

"The arcane formula matters," she said. "Even now."

"Then tell me the part of the formula that matters next."

Jaina drew a slow breath. "He engineered a phylactery ritual. He expects his death to be temporary."

This time Sylvanas turned a fraction. "Speak plainly."

"The infected peasants rise as mindless ghouls, driven by a hive-hunger. They have just enough motor function to hold a blade and follow a telepathic command. Kel'Thuzad designed a complex arcane anchor for himself to preserve his memories and intellect through the resurrection process."

"Believed."

"He was very calm for a man being shot through the throat."

Cyndia's head shifted left toward a stand of ash trees, then right again out of old Ranger reflex.

Sylvanas's bow-hand opened and closed on the reins. "You should have led with the fact that the necromancer can resurrect himself."

"Yes."

Sylvanas faced forward again. "But you did tell me."

They rode on between the barley fields. Sunset had started turning the sky the colour of beaten copper. Window glass on a distant farmhouse flashed once, catching the dying light.

She had chosen to omit the phylactery hours ago in the sanctum, and again on the island, and again when the road began. Each time keeping it back got harder. She stayed with the tactical facts; there was no time for Kirin Tor confessions on the ride south.

A sharp hum of magic struck from Cyndia's side.

"Silvermoon relay," the ranger said.

The communication crystal in her hand glowed a harsh, burning amber. She drew her mare closer and held it across. Ranger Lord Lor'themar Theron's voice came through the enchanted facet, clipped thin by the miles between them.

"Ranger-General."

Sylvanas reached for the stone. Her leather-gloved fingers brushed Jaina's knuckles in passing, quick as a spark, then were gone.

"Speak."

"The Convocation of Silvermoon has issued a formal recall. Immediate return to the capital for review and a direct report to the Sunstrider throne. Grand Magister Rommath requires me to tell you the order supersedes your field discretion."

Jaina wished for the dense pines of Silverpine, the mountains of Alterac, anything but flat country and open air.

Lor'themar went on, the political exhaustion rough in his voice. "The Magister's Lords heard that the Silver Hand knights are dying at Hearthglen. That panicked them. But they also heard the plague is a human ailment spreading through human grain. The Convocation believes the Runestones of Quel'Thalas and the Eversong border will shield us. They think distance will save the elves."

Sylvanas said nothing.

No one answered.

"Sylvanas," he said, dropping the military formality. "If you have a tactical justification I can leverage with the council, give me one."

She kept her eyes on the south road. "Tell Rommath that Hearthglen's garrison lasted less than an hour before their own militiamen resurrected inside the keep. Tell him the plague bonds to the marrow and no elven runestone detects a human carrying it. Tell the Convocation I am not abandoning Lordaeron while refugees are still blocking the northbound roads."

"That will buy me a screaming match with Lord Saltheril, not council votes."

"Then buy the screaming match."

The crystal hummed once under her grip.

"Understood," Lor'themar said.

The amber light went out.

Sylvanas handed the stone back to Cyndia and drove her spurs into her horse. The courser lengthened its stride at once.

No one suggested slowing.

They rode on.

Oak-wood smoke from farmstead chimneys flattened under the evening damp. Wagons thinned away until the three of them had the road mostly to themselves, passing only stragglers now: a Light-sworn priest on a mule with soot staining his white hem, two blacksmiths carrying a shattered wagon wheel, a woman walking north with a basket of root vegetables on her hip and a child's hand in her own.

The Kirin Tor sending stone at Jaina's belt flared next.

The arcane pulse was unmistakable before she touched it. Violet light leaked around the mithril filigree. Her stomach tightened with recognition. Archmage Antonidas never cast sending spells twice in quick succession unless the Violet Citadel was in an uproar.

She pulled the stone free. "Archmage."

"Jaina." His voice came thin with the sheer distance to Dalaran. "I have your preliminary scrying report. I need the uncensored version now."

Need. Good.

Jaina steadied her mare with her knees and spoke into the stone while the horses kept running. "Kel'Thuzad confirmed the plagued grain is the vanguard of a larger invasion. The orc shaman Ner'zhul is the Lich King, commanding from Northrend. The demon lords of the Burning Legion command him. They are using this plague to dismantle Lordaeron's armies from the inside out."

Antonidas did not answer at once.

The pause lasted just long enough for her to picture him pacing the Violet Citadel, feeling the entire political weight of the Six pressing down on him, realising Dalaran's magical defences were useless against bread.

"And what is the arcane mechanism?" he asked.

"The grain is laced with a dormant necromantic compound. Eating it binds the necrotic energy to the host's nervous system. Physical death completes the circuit. What rises from the peasantry are mindless ghouls, but they possess a hive-mind coordination. Enough to hold a sword. Enough to recognise Alliance armour and slaughter the city guard from the inside."

"Can the compound be neutralised?"

A red-gold line of sun burned out in the irrigation ditch. "Broadly? Not with the evocation magic I have in the field."

Antonidas made no sound.

"There may be localised interventions," she said. "The royal granaries. The guild bakehouses. Somewhere the raw grain can be destroyed before the bakers knead it into the food supply. I don't know enough about the rune structure yet to promise more than property damage."

"Yet," he repeated.

The word came out worn, and beneath it her old teacher was already there, braced for the reckless Kirin Tor prodigy to offer a suicidal plan.

"Hearthglen's outer defences have already collapsed," she said. "Prince Arthas is marching his forces. I am moving to intercept him."

"Come back to Dalaran."

The mare stepped around a loose paving stone and for one strange second she still felt the world tilt. Jaina kept facing forward because if she shut her eyes she would see Antonidas at an oak table in the restricted archives, his purple sleeves inked at the cuff, pushing a dangerous conjuration text across to her just for the sheer pleasure of watching her decipher it.

"I can't."

"Child."

"If I teleport to Dalaran now," she said, "I bring the Council of Six a necrotic theory and leave the Tirisfal Glades to burn with it. If I keep riding, I might be able to flash-freeze the grain stores before Stratholme becomes another casualty list."

Wind hissed through the orchard hedges. Cyndia's mare tossed foam from the iron bit and settled. Sylvanas gave no sign of listening, but she was memorising every syllable.

"You are not obliged to fight the Burning Legion's vanguard alone," Antonidas said.

Kel'Thuzad's study flashed up in her mind so hard her mare hit the next rut badly. One mahogany desk. One cup of tea. One necromancer's absolute pleasure in having his genius fully understood by a peer.

"No," Jaina said more quietly. "I know that."

The sending stone stayed warm against her palm. At last Antonidas said, "Then survive the attempt."

His voice roughened at the edges, just once.

"I will do my best."

"That has never been a comforting promise coming from you."

Her chest loosened a fraction. Antonidas sounded dry and impatient again.

"No," she said. "It isn't."

"Send your scrying logs when you have the mana to spare. I will mobilise the Violet Guard as best I can."

"Antonidas."

He waited.

She could not tell him about the study. Could not describe Kel'Thuzad's voice turned warm with academic approval. Could not give him the one horrifying heartbeat in which the necromantic plague's design had shown its sheer, elegant brilliance before her moral judgment caught up.

"He wasn't insane," she said instead.

Antonidas answered with a longer pause than before.

"No," Antonidas said at last, very softly. "I know."

The violet light died in her hand.

Jaina slid the stone back into its leather pouch. The runed metal was clammy. Her shaking hand was worse.

"You told the Archmage almost all of it," Sylvanas said.

Jaina turned then, because she could not ignore the elven general's accusation. Sylvanas still faced forward, her profile clean against the copper sky, her jaw set in the hard military line that meant absolute control rather than anger.

"Almost."

"The study again."

"The study again."

"Very well."

They rode another quarter hour in the gathering dusk before the military reality found them.

This interruption came as an Alliance courier cutting out of a side lane at a reckless angle, his draft horse blowing hard, his blue Lordaeron cloak tied wrong for speed. Cyndia moved first. Her mare slid left across the King's Road, an arrow already nocked to the string. Sylvanas's courser checked and turned half-sideways to block the path. Jaina's own mare bunched under her as she caught the reins short, ready to cast.

"Farstrider relay!" the rider shouted, seeing the elven armour. "South track!"

Cyndia kept the broadhead arrow aimed at his chest until he was within ten paces and panting hard enough to lose any pretended disguise. Mud striped his iron greaves to the knee. Woodsmoke and char had gotten into his tabard; Jaina caught the scent of old ash and hot pitch even across the road.

"Report," Sylvanas ordered.

"Hearthglen's inner keep held, but the outer defensive perimeter failed after dusk. Uther the Lightbringer arrived with the Silver Hand cavalry. Prince Arthas left the defence to Uther. The Prince took the First Legion infantry south-east down the King's Road. He's marching on Stratholme."

Jaina hated the hollow, haunted look on the courier's face.

"How bad is the infection rate?" Sylvanas asked.

"Bad enough that the Priests of the Light are burning the wounded alive before their families can administer the Last Rites, just to stop them from turning."

All at once she saw the dead in every mile behind them. The northbound refugee wagons. The sleeping child atop the bedding. The barley farmers at the trough. Ordinary Lordaeron work went on while poisoned shipments moved through the kingdom's veins.

"Casualty numbers," Jaina demanded.

The rider blinked, dragging himself back from whatever slaughter he had just ridden through. "No count worth trusting. It's a meat grinder. Too many uninfected civilians mixed into the withdrawal. The priests say the corpses keep resurrecting after any lull longer than a few minutes. Captain Falric's infantry calls it a held city. The footmen coming off the outer wall call it a furnace with gates."

Sylvanas leaned forward over her saddle horn. "How far ahead is the Crown Prince?"

"Hours. Maybe less if he pushed the First Legion through the last relay station without a supply halt."

Of course he had. Arthas wouldn't stop for supplies.

Jaina saw the tactical map whole at once: Hearthglen reduced to relay smoke and secondhand horror, Arthas already out beyond it, driving his men to exhaustion, and Stratholme still living its evening routine while contaminated flour sat in merchant bins, on pantry shelves, in kneaded dough, in cooling loaves.

"Ride back to your post," Sylvanas told the courier. "Tell the next northbound command station we are on the south road without stopping. Tell them to keep the relay horses saddled."

He snapped a rigid salute and wheeled his horse around so sharply the animal skidded in the mud before finding purchase.

Cyndia lowered her bow only when the clatter of his armour faded around the bend.

"Hours," Jaina said.

Sylvanas turned to her then, fully, for the first time since the road began. "If you have a desperate, property-destroying Kirin Tor intervention, now is when it becomes either a tactical plan or dead weight."

Jaina pushed damp blonde hair back from her temple. She set her thoughts against the architectural map of Stratholme she carried from old Dalaran geography lessons and recent military reports: the central market, the baker's district, the warehouse lanes, the cobblestone service roads that fed the capital its grain.

"The feed points," she said. "The royal granaries first, if the winter stores aren't fully dispersed to the markets already. The guild bakehouses after that. The main holding yards by the service gates. We target anywhere the raw grain still sits in enough bulk quantity to destroy before it disappears into human stomachs."

"Destroy how."

"Arcane frost, if I can pull enough ambient moisture to flash-freeze the silos at once. Raw evocation fire if I can't, and if the choice becomes burning the grain or butchering the people." The words came faster now because the abstract horror had finally taken a shape she could fight. "It won't cure the citizens who have already eaten it. But it will cut off the supply chain. It could blunt the sheer numbers of the undead army if we beat Arthas to the gates."

"Could," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

"And if we're late, and the city has already eaten the bread."

Jaina faced south. Plumes of black smoke smudged the horizon there, too thick and oily to be evening haze.

"Then we learn the limits of my magic in time to do something with the ashes."

Sylvanas stayed turned toward her for another second. Jaina waited for the ranger's cynical cut. Instead, Sylvanas said, "Good. That sounds like a war plan."

Something in Jaina's chest went unsteady for one beat and righted itself.

The irrigation ditches went dark first, then the wheat fields. The horses breathed harder now, white lather beginning to show at their nostrils and flanks. Cyndia checked the empty eastern treeline again before fixing her eyes south.

No one mentioned the exhaustion.

Jaina gathered the leather reins and let her mare lengthen into a full gallop. Her spine ached from hours of rigid stillness in the Scholomance crypts. Ahead and to the right, Sylvanas rode with the same brutal, focused economy she had brought to the elven border, to the Millhaven skirmish, to every war room Jaina had misread before understanding what it cost to be corrected by a military commander in perpetual motion.

Kel'Thuzad had worked alone in a sealed study, smugly pleased with his own intellect.

"How far to the next relay station?" she asked over the wind.

"One hour at this pace," Cyndia shouted back.

"Half," Sylvanas commanded.

No one argued.

They drove the horses harder.

The Eastweald darkened around them. Orchard shadows thickened into bands of near-black. Lanterns kindled in the farmhouses, one here, three there, square yellow lives lit blindly against the coming slaughter. Somewhere off the road, a monastery bell rang for evening vespers. Somewhere farther south, another iron bell answered in frantic, ringing alarm.

Jaina rode in the middle, the grid map of Stratholme opening behind her eyes. Grain yards. Bakeries. Market lanes. Timber doors still unlocked because no citizen yet believed their own pantries had been weaponised.

Sylvanas still kept the half-step, close enough to listen to the mage's breathing, and far enough to watch the shadows.

Three horses ran south into the deepening dusk, smoke ahead of them, ordinary Lordaeron behind.
