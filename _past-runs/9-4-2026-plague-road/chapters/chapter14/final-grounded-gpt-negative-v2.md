# Chapter 14: The Revelation

The King's Road south ran them out of the Scholomance basin and back into Eastweald country, which had the indecency to look ordinary.

Late sun lay over cut wheat. Apple and plum orchards climbed the low rises in dark bands, the lower branches already in shade. Wagons groaned north under sacks of turnips, seed grain, and household trunks banded in iron. A mastiff barked behind a hawthorn hedge. Somewhere beyond the fields, a bell rang for the evening meal.

Eastweald kept doing what farm country does. Fences. Mud. Supper smoke. People still hurrying home before dark. They had climbed out of butcher rooms under Caer Darrow and come back to a road where a broken axle still mattered more than the end of kingdoms.

Alchemical chalk still held in the whorls of Jaina's fingers. She had scrubbed at it with lake water on the island and again from her canteen while they rode. The white kept finding its way back.

Cyndia held the left as she always did. When a timber cart rattled past, her eyes cut to the empty right-side treeline before coming forward again. The habit had outlived the woman who used to fill that ground.

Sylvanas kept half a horse ahead and to the right. Close enough to hear her. Far enough that the spacing did not feel accidental.

Jaina kept one stack of truths ready for the road. Andorhal grain carried only the outer shell. Repeated eating taught the body the rest. Death released the answer. Kel'Thuzad had built a finer version for himself and spoken of return as a matter of preparation, not faith. Above all of it sat a will in Northrend and something worse above that.

Underneath sat the room. Oak under her palms. Chalk on her thumb. Kel'Thuzad's voice warming when she kept pace with him. Sylvanas in the doorway with blood on her sleeve and the whole place arriving in the wrong order.

The first stack would travel. The second would not.

"Kel'Thuzad isn't the whole chain," she said.

Sylvanas did not turn. "Go on."

"He named the hand above him. Ner'zhul. The Lich King, bound in Northrend. And above him, the Burning Legion."

Hooves beat a four-count into the packed road.

"A conquest," Sylvanas said.

"A preparation for one."

"Against whose line."

"All of Lordaeron first. After that, whatever still stands."

Sylvanas's shoulders settled into a harder stillness. A larger shape than she had wanted. She let it sit there.

"Start with the part we can still use," she said.

Jaina nodded once. "The grain carries the shell and nothing more. That's why it survives wagon beds, silo damp, even the oven. People eat it once, then again. By then the deeper work is already in the blood and nerves. The body has been taught what to answer to."

"By what."

"Exposure. Repetition. Time." She kept her voice level. "The dead don't rise because the loaf itself is stronger than it looked. They rise because the body finished the lesson after the bread was gone."

Sylvanas rode another few lengths in silence. "What comes back."

"Crude answer first. Motion. Hunger. Enough will to move with the others who died from the same stores. Enough to be pointed."

"Pointed by whom."

"Whoever is holding the upper chain strongly enough."

Cyndia's mare splashed through a flooded rut. The ranger's tack stayed quiet, good Thalassian leather built for long patrols and worse weather than this. Jaina heard her own gear more clearly: the sending stone knocking against her hip, the butt of her staff tapping leather every time the road jarred.

"And the wards," Sylvanas said.

"Still useless for this."

"Why."

"Because a city can ward its walls." Jaina looked past Sylvanas at a farm lane running west between hedges, clean wagon ruts cut deep in the mud. "It cannot ward its supper. Not once the thing has gone through milling, baking, and a man's own belly."

The words sat between them. Ahead, the Darrowshire crossroads opened wide enough to show a roadside shrine to the Light, a stone trough, two farmers watering oxen. One of them looked up as they passed. He read their speed, the bow on Sylvanas's back, the face Jaina hadn't managed to smooth yet, and kept one hand on the yoke, trying to hold his small part of the world in place.

"The Kirin Tor missed it because the loud part was meant to be found," Jaina said. "The raw grain reads strong. Too strong. It keeps the eye up on the surface. The work underneath it vanishes into living tissue and waits there until death strips the cover off."

"You sound," Sylvanas said, "like he sat you down and walked you through it."

Jaina kept her eyes on the road. "He did."

That got a glance. Brief. Sideways. Gone again.

"He built a study for it," Jaina said before Sylvanas could ask the next thing. "Jandice Barov brought me in through ledgers, manifests, and archive rooms. She wanted me looking at the design before I looked at the stock."

"I came in through the stock."

Jaina's grip tightened on the reins. "I gathered that."

"Did you." Sylvanas's voice stayed flat. "There were brass drawers full of hands. Tables with villagers still breathing while they were opened. Crates of stitched parts waiting to become something worse. He was teaching over that floor."

Eastweald rolled by on either side of them, green-black orchard rows and low stone boundary walls, country too worked to hide anything and hiding everything.

"Jandice chose my route," Jaina said.

"Yes."

"She wanted me seated before she wanted me useful."

"And was she right."

The question landed clean. Jaina stared at the road long enough for a grain cart ahead to turn off into an orchard lane and leave them open country again.

"Long enough to keep listening," she said.

Nothing moved except horses and harness.

Then Sylvanas said, "Good."

Jaina looked over.

"Better the true shape of it."

Shame rose and settled again. No room for it on this road. She forced her mouth back to the work.

"He didn't mean the village dead as his finished answer," she said. "They were cheap. Broad. Good enough for war. He built something finer for himself."

Sylvanas's bow hand flexed once on the reins.

"Say that plain."

"He worked a return for his own mind. Memory. Judgment. Intention. The rest of them are blunt instruments. He doesn't expect to stay dead."

"Believes," Sylvanas said.

Jaina heard Kel'Thuzad's voice again. Calm. Certain. Theorems and personhood spoken over old stone.

"He was calm for a man with an arrow through the throat," she said.

Cyndia's head turned left toward a stand of ash trees, then back to the road. Old drill. Empty right. Forward again.

"You should have led with that," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

They rode on through barley stubble and orchard shadow. Sunset had started working copper into the clouds, beating flat against the road where wagon wheels had polished the damp dirt hard.

A pulse of magic flared from Cyndia's side.

"Silvermoon relay," the ranger said.

The crystal in her hand burned amber. She brought her mare in close and held it across Sylvanas's path. Lor'themar Theron's voice came through thinned by distance and restraint both.

"Ranger-General."

Sylvanas took the crystal. Her gloved knuckles brushed Jaina's hand in passing. Quick. Instrumental. Gone.

"Speak," she said.

"The Convocation has issued a formal recall," Lor'themar said. No padding. No ceremony beyond the first word. "Immediate return to Silvermoon for review and direct report to the Sunstrider throne. Rommath required me to say the order supersedes your field discretion."

Sylvanas said nothing.

Lor'themar went on, the exhaustion rough in his voice now. "The Magister's Lords heard the Silver Hand was losing men at Hearthglen. That frightened them. They also heard the plague rides human grain and human dead. They think the Runestones of Quel'Thalas and the Eversong line make it someone else's disaster."

Jaina listened to Sylvanas breathing and wished, absurdly, for the deep pines of Silverpine or the stone turns through Alterac. Anything but this open country where nothing interrupted bad news except more of the same road.

"If you have a reason I can spend," Lor'themar said, "give it to me now."

Sylvanas kept her eyes on the south road. "Tell Rommath Hearthglen nearly failed again after dark. Tell him the dead keep standing back up inside the walls after any lull long enough for the priests to breathe. Tell him the runestones can catch a spell at the border. They do nothing to bread already inside a man."

Silence on the crystal.

"That buys me noise," Lor'themar said at last. "Not votes. Saltheril is already shouting."

"Then buy noise."

The amber light went dead in Sylvanas's hand.

She gave the crystal back to Cyndia and dug her heels lightly into the courser. The horse lengthened at once. No one suggested slowing.

The Kirin Tor stone answered next.

Violet seeped between the mithril lines before Jaina even touched it. Antonidas did not send twice in quick succession unless Dalaran was already on its feet. She pulled the stone free.

"Archmage."

"Jaina." His voice came thin with distance and kept no courtesy beyond her name. "I have the first report. I need the uncensored one."

Need. Good.

Jaina steadied her mare with her knees and spoke into the stone while the road kept bucking under them.

"Kel'Thuzad confirmed the plague is not the whole war. Ner'zhul is the will above it, holding from Northrend. He calls himself the Lich King. Above him is the Burning Legion."

Antonidas did not answer at once.

"The grain carries only the outer shell," Jaina said before the silence could turn into interruption. "Repeated eating teaches the body the rest. Death releases the answer. What rises is crude and directed enough to turn a town against itself."

"And Kel'Thuzad."

"He built a vessel for himself." The words scraped on the way out. "Something meant to keep his mind intact. He does not expect death to end him."

The stone stayed hot in her palm.

"What can you stop," Antonidas asked.

There. Better question. The road question.

"Raw stores," Jaina said. "Granaries before milling. Guild bakehouses before distribution. Holding yards before the sacks are broken up and sent through the city. If I reach Stratholme in time, I can freeze stores or burn them. I cannot promise more than ruined food and a little time."

"Can the contamination itself be stripped out."

"Not from the living in any way I would trust enough to tell you it can."

Wind hissed through the hedges. Cyndia's mare threw foam from the bit and settled again. Sylvanas kept her place without once glancing over, which meant she was hearing every word.

"Come back to Dalaran," Antonidas said.

Jaina stared fforward unblinking because if she shut her eyes she would see him at the oak table in the restricted archives, violet cuffs inked dark, sliding some barely permitted text across to her just to see if she could crack it before supper.

"I can't."

"Child."

"If I come back now, I bring the Council of Six a clean explanation and leave Stratholme to find out the rest with its mouth full."

The wind took the last words and flattened them out over the road.

"You are not obliged to ride into that city alone," Antonidas said.

Kel'Thuzad's room flashed up in her head so sharply her mare stepped badly and nearly lost the rut. Oak desk. Cold cup. A dead man's academic pleasure.

"No," Jaina said, quieter. "I know."

The stone stayed warm in her hand. At last Antonidas said, "Then survive the attempt."

His voice roughened once and no more.

"I will do my best."

"That has never been a comforting promise from you."

Her chest loosened a fraction. There he was again. Dry. Impatient. The part of him the Violet Citadel never got.

"No," she said. "It hasn't."

"Send what logs you can. I will move the Violet Guard and whatever the Council will let me move past itself."

"Antonidas."

He waited.

Jaina watched the road unroll between horse ears and wagon ruts and the first evening lanterns kindling in far farm windows.

"He wasn't mad," she said.

A longer pause that time.

"No," Antonidas said at last, his voice low. "I know."

The violet light died in her palm.

Jaina slid the stone back to her belt. The metal felt clammy. Her hand was worse.

"You gave the Archmage everything except that," Sylvanas said.

Jaina turned then because ignoring that one would have been cowardice.

"Yes."

"The study again."

"The study again."

Sylvanas looked ahead once more. "Understood."

They rode another quarter hour in the thickening dusk before the road threw them a courier.

He came out of a side lane at a killing pace, draft horse blowing hard, blue Lordaeron cloak tied wrong for speed. Cyndia moved first. Her mare slid left across the road and an arrow was already on the string. Sylvanas turned her courser half-sideways to block the lane. Jaina shortened her reins and gathered the spell in her hand.

"Farstrider relay!" the man shouted when he saw elven armor. "South track!"

Cyndia kept the broadhead fixed on his chest until he was close enough to lose any claim to disguise. Mud striped his iron greaves to the knee. Smoke and hot pitch had gotten into his tabard. Jaina could smell both.

"Report," Sylvanas said.

"Hearthglen's inner keep holds," the courier said, sucking air between the words. "The outer line didn't after dusk. Uther the Lightbringer arrived with Silver Hand cavalry. The prince left him there and took the First Legion south-east on the King's Road. He's riding Stratholme."

Jaina hated the look on the man's face more than the words. Men looked like that after they had outrun a place instead of leaving it.

"How bad," Sylvanas asked, "is the turn."

"Bad enough the priests are burning wounded before families can finish the rites." His mouth worked once and failed. "Captain Falric calls it a held city. The men coming off the wall call it a furnace with gates."

All at once Jaina saw the northbound wagons they'd passed. Bedding piled high. Children asleep on top of it. Farmers at the Darrowshire trough. Orchard ladders propped against trees. The country kept working while the south line came apart one loaf at a time.

"How far ahead is Arthas," Sylvanas said.

"Hours. Less if he didn't stop at the last relay."

Of course he hadn't.

"Go back," Sylvanas said. "Tell the next north station we are on the south road without stopping. Tell them to keep the relays hot."

The courier wheeled his horse around so hard it skidded in the mud before finding grip.

Cyndia lowered her bow only when the noise faded.

"Hours," Jaina said.

Sylvanas turned fully toward her for the first time since the road began. "If you have the ugly narrow answer, now is when it becomes either a plan or dead weight."

Jaina pushed damp hair back from her temple. Stratholme opened behind her eyes as food routes: the central market, the baker's quarter, the warehouse lanes, the roads feeding them all.

"The grain yards first," she said. "If the sacks are still whole, we hit them before they're broken up. Then the guild bakehouses. Then any holding yard along the warehouse lanes where the raw stock is still sitting in bulk."

"Break it how."

"Frost if I can get enough of the air working with me. Fire if I can't." Once she started, the map took over. "If the choice comes down to grain or people, the grain burns before the next bread goes out. It won't save what has already been eaten. It may stop the next feeding."

"And if we're late."

Jaina looked south. Smoke smudged the horizon there, too thick to mistake for evening haze.

"Then we learn that quickly enough to choose the smaller fire."

Sylvanas held her gaze another beat.

"Good," she said. "That's a road answer."

Approval hit Jaina crookedly enough to hurt before it steadied. No repair in it. No absolution. Only use.

The ditches went dark first, then the fields. The horses breathed harder. White lather showed at the mouth and chest. Cyndia checked the empty right again and said nothing.

No one spoke of exhaustion.

Jaina gathered the reins and let her mare lengthen into a full gallop. Her spine ached from hours locked too straight, first in Scholomance and now on this road. Ahead and right, Sylvanas rode with the same brutal economy she brought to every problem that could still be solved by motion.

Kel'Thuzad had worked alone in a closed room and loved the sound of his own certainty.

"How far to the next relay?" Jaina called over the wind.

"One hour at this pace," Cyndia shouted back.

"Half," Sylvanas said.

No one argued.

They drove the horses harder.

Eastweald darkened around them. Orchard rows thickened into black bands. Farm windows kindled one here, three there, yellow squares lit blind against what was coming. Somewhere off the road a monastery bell rang for vespers. Farther south another bell answered in alarm.

Jaina rode in the middle with Stratholme open behind her eyes. Grain yards. Bakeries. Warehouse lanes. Timber doors still unlocked because no one inside them yet believed bread had turned on the city.

Sylvanas kept the half-step. Close enough to hear Jaina breathe. Far enough to watch the shadows.

Three horses ran south into the deepening dusk, smoke ahead of them, ordinary Lordaeron behind.
