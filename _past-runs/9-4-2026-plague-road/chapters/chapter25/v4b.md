# Chapter 25: The Stinger

## I

The answer through the wall had been three light taps. Above that, the blade beat through the white palace stone every fourteen seconds.

Jaina lay on the narrow cot with her palm braced against the flagstones at the head of it and counted the gaps Frostmourne kicked down from the evidence vault above. Cold bit through skin, through tendon, into the wrist where the iron ring sat. Winter stopped at the skin. This came through masonry.

Fourteen.

The cell smelled of damp stone, old torch pitch, and the thin sourness of water left too long in a bucket. None of it displaced the wrongness in the palace foundations. The service dark under Lordaeron's bright halls carried the same cold in its torch brackets, cart axles, and sweating mortar.

Fourteen again.

She rolled onto her side and pressed two fingers into the mortar seam between stones. The pulse moved through the palace like hostile rhythm, no longer broad and dumb. It had narrowed since the verdict. It was reaching.

Above her, someone crossed the vault corridor. The step came through the ceiling first, then the man inside it: boot leather gone damp at the sole, knee stiff with the cold, supper somewhere upstairs, shift almost done. Frostmourne brushed the edge of that passing mind, weighed it, and let it go.

Not this one.

Jaina swallowed against the copper at the back of her throat.

Twelve.

That was new.

The pace had shortened within minutes, not hours. Worse than that, each pulse came back carrying traces. She could not read names through two floors of stone and an evidence room built for ordinary crimes. Above her, the guard's step dragged for half a pace, then squared again. The next pulse left old ash on her tongue and a hard upright strain that still wanted orders. Devotion lasted.

She sat up too fast. Chain scraped the cot frame. Her fingers had gone numb; she dug them into her thigh until the sting brought them back.

Through the wall on her left, Sylvanas was awake.

Jaina had known for twenty minutes. Elven sleep had one rhythm. Just Sylvanas's chain settling once on the far side, then a long stillness that kept time with slow measured breaths.

Jaina leaned off the cot until her shoulder touched the wall and kept her voice low.

"It's sorting."

A pause. Then Sylvanas, roughened by prison, too little sleep, and stone air: "Sorting what."

"Minds. It touches, tests, lets go."

Silence for a few breaths. Jaina could hear none of the cell beyond the wall, only the faint chain-shift that said Sylvanas had changed posture without wasting motion.

"Which kind does it keep."

Jaina shut her eyes. She gave the read. Sylvanas cut straight to what a rider or guard could use.

"Devotion," Jaina said. "The kind that wants someone else to tell it what to do."

The answer cost a small drag of iron from the other side.

"How long."

"The gap was sixteen last watch. It's twelve now." She stopped before she turned it into numbers, projections, and all the other things that belonged to a Kirin Tor worktable instead of a prison wall. Plain speech carried best through stone. So did danger. "Days. Fewer than we had."

"Before Lor'themar."

"Before the verdict."

A cart wheel thudded somewhere in the service dark above. Voices rose, blurred, and died in the stone. Lor'themar was up there somewhere with road dust still on him, trying to force sense into rooms that moved paper faster than doors. None of it would move fast enough.

The blade thudded overhead again.

Eleven.

Jaina set her forehead against the wall. The irons on her wrists had warmed hours ago. Now even that thin heat had gone. Frostmourne's cold was working deeper into the palace stones, turning the cell into one more surface that answered it.

"Sylvanas."

"Here."

Jaina flattened her hand on the masonry between them. Grit pressed into her palm. What she wanted to say had been waiting since the throne room. Since before that, if she was honest. Since Northrend. Since the moment Frostmourne came down for her and Sylvanas moved without hesitation into the gap.

She had known the answer there. The seam in the blade's field. The three schools that would bite together if she forced them hard enough. She had known every part of it.

She had still failed with it in her hands.

She could still see the chalk under her own nails from the sanctum. Kel'Thuzad's hand had been careful on the page, margins neat enough to make the horror look orderly.

And he had done it alone.

Arthas had gone the same way by a different road. Certainty. Momentum. No one left beside him with enough standing to say stop. Uther gone. She had gone. He kept using the next step to justify the one before it.

Kel'Thuzad. Arthas.

Two men sure they could do it alone.

Jaina leaned harder into the wall until the mortar bit.

"I was wrong," she said.

From the other side: "Only now?"

The corner of her mouth twitched despite the cold. Sylvanas's humor had survived plague roads, necromancers, Northrend, and a death sentence. Of course it had survived a palace cell.

"About what being right could do," Jaina said. Her wrist ring grated when she shifted. "I left Dalaran because no one believed the readings. I crossed your border alone because I thought proof would force the world to behave."

Sylvanas listened. No interruption. No mercy either.

Jaina tucked one knee up on the cot and kept her hand on the wall so the cell stayed real.

She touched the masonry between them with each name. "Millhaven. Andorhal. Scholomance. Northrend. Every time I thought seeing it first meant I owned what came after."

Through the wall, Sylvanas said, "You were carrying one answer."

"And one answer would have gotten me killed." Jaina drew a breath and kept going before shame could close her throat. "I saw the pattern at Millhaven. Alone, I would still have taken the wrong road after. I could read the grain network. I could not move through it without your people. At Scholomance I looked at Kel'Thuzad's notes and saw too much of myself in them, and even then I still thought the difference was that I was right and he wasn't."

"He wasn't."

"He was right about pieces," Jaina said. Her wrist ring clicked once against stone. "So was Arthas. That was the ruin of both of them. They had no one at the shoulder to catch the bad turn early. No one close enough to say no and make it stick."

The chain in Sylvanas's cell shifted once.

"Arthas," Sylvanas said.

"Yes." Jaina tapped two fingers against the wall. Once. "By Northrend there was no one left in reach of him. The throne room only worked because you were there. The answer was mine. The seconds were yours. If you hadn't bought them, all my knowing would have died in my head."

Silence stretched while Sylvanas listened. Jaina could picture none of her, only the set of attention in the wall between them.

"That's closer," Sylvanas said.

Jaina laughed once, softly, with no humor in it. "You want cleaner wording while we're chained under the evidence vault."

"I want the sentence that costs you."

The blade pulsed above them.

Ten.

Jaina closed her eyes. The pulse traveled down through the vault, through corridor stone, through the cell wall under her cheek. She said it into the masonry between them.

"I was never going to be enough alone at the border, in Lordaeron, or in the throne room," she said. "We stopped him because we were right together, with both of us doing the work."

From the far side of the wall came a quiet sound. More like the breath a fighter let out when a blow landed where it was meant to.

"Good," Sylvanas said. "Now tell me what to do with that."

So Jaina did.

She tapped the next interval against the wall. "It skims a mind and leaves a trace. Grief burns off fast. Devotion stays. That's the one it keeps worrying."

Jaina tapped once against the wall. Sylvanas answered before the echo died.

"How far?" Sylvanas asked.

"Past the vault corridor," Jaina said. "Not the whole palace. Not yet."

"If a guard leaves its reach?"

"Depends how deep it got."

"Not linear, then."

"No. Faster each time."

"If someone lies about it."

"I don't know. If someone speaks true—"

The pulse hit two seconds early.

She went still on the cot.

Cold tightened overhead, no longer broad through the stone but focused. The cot slat under her thigh gave one dry tick. Fine hairs lifted along her forearm. The pulse had leaned toward the place her words had landed.

Jaina kept her own voice flat.

"It hears what's true."

"Good," Sylvanas said at once. "Then we know one more place to cut it."

The pulse eased. Barely.

Jaina let the breath out through her nose and pressed her palm harder into the wall until feeling came back into her fingers.

Beyond the stone, Sylvanas's chain gave another small scrape, then went quiet. The pause came back leaner after that, as if she had cut the report down to its hard parts.

Jaina kept her palm on the mortar seam. On the far side, Sylvanas's chain answered once. Between those two small sounds, the blade still had somewhere to go.

Above them, Frostmourne kept counting down.

## II

Sylvanas was still turning Jaina's report through her head when the second cold entered the cell.

The blade's cold she knew now. It came from above and soaked downward through the vault floor and the palace foundations, steady as seep-water. This was different. It gathered under the door and stood there.

Torchlight from the corridor shrank along the threshold. The little fan of gold beneath the bars thinned to a blade-width smear.

Sylvanas rose at once.

The chain at her ankle checked her two paces from the door. She had measured that useless freedom in the first hour: cot, bucket, wall, two steps, iron. No bow. No knife. No room even for a proper rush. Nothing left to her but balance, judgment, and the fact that she was still alive.

Through the wall on her right, Jaina's breathing caught once.

So Jaina felt it too.

The torch outside guttered lower. Pitch hissed in the bracket. Then the light went out.

Darkness pressed into the cell. Palace dark still had traces in it: a seam under the door, white stone holding a memory of light, the shape of bars where a torch usually struck them. This came in thick. The air itself grew heavier, enough that drawing breath took work.

Something stood outside the door.

Sylvanas could not hear steps. Could not hear cloth. She smelled dungeon stone, old pitch, stale rushes from some corridor sweep long since gone dry, and under all of it a preserved taint that had never belonged to anything meant to remain alive.

The lock did not turn.

The door did not move.

The visitor came through anyway.

One moment the cell held only Sylvanas and the dark. The next it held another body, and the space between them shortened under its size. Weight settled into the flagstones. Air displaced. The thing had presence enough to make the cot, bucket, and barred door feel like toys arranged around it.

Its wing joints had tucked themselves tight just to fit; even so, the tips nearly brushed bar and wall. Horns or ridges cut into the dark above. Nothing that big should have entered a room this small without splintering it.

Its eyes were green-gold and old.

Watching.

Amused, perhaps, though not kindly.

"Ranger-General," it said.

The voice came from everywhere at once: air, stone, ankle iron, the bars at the door taking the sound and giving it back. Low. Courteous. Patient in the way large predators were patient.

"You have been troublesome from the first."

Sylvanas kept her hands loose at her sides. Empty hands, but she would not let them look helpless. Weight on the flagstones. A real chest taking air. Wing joints cramped to fit a human cell. Too large for illusion, too solid for any trick she knew. She would name it when the name bought her something.

"Your prince did well enough," the visitor said. "Granaries emptied. Bakers fed whole districts. The dead learned their work. The blade came home. All that before he lost his nerve and his life in the same room."

"Arthas is dead," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

No heat in the answer.

It watched without blinking, patient enough to make the iron on her ankle feel lighter and more useless by the breath.

"The sword will take another hand. The ovens he fed are spent, but the roads he opened remain open. You still never found all the cells. One man was useful. He was never the whole design."

Above them, Frostmourne thudded once through the ceiling. The creature tipped its head at the sound.

Sylvanas kept that too.

"You made it," she said.

The pause that followed was careful.

"I was there when it was forged."

Sylvanas held still. The chain pulled cold at her ankle. She did not look down.

"You could have stepped in before the throne room," she said.

"I could have."

"You didn't."

The folded wings shifted, a controlled rustle that brushed the dark without stirring air.

"Arthas had done what was needed. After that, the question was yield."

"Yield."

"How many ovens took the flour. How many cellars stacked it. How many frightened men took orders faster once the dead were in the streets." The voice stayed calm. "Quite a lot, as it happens."

Through the wall Jaina moved, one soft scrape of chain. Listening blind.

Sylvanas kept her own face still. The creature's gaze touched her hands, her throat, then the ankle chain, weighing what in the room could still be used against her.

"What do you want," she said.

It considered her long enough to make the answer deliberate.

"I wanted you to understand the size of what you touched." Its head angled toward the vault above, toward the same stone that kept carrying Frostmourne's pulse into both their cells. "You killed the wielder. The power behind him is still here."

Neither of them spoke.

"There are granaries your hunt never reached," it went on. "Cells your networks never named." Frostmourne thudded overhead again. The creature tipped its head as if listening down the stone with them. "It knows what devotion tastes like now," it said. "Your prince taught it that much."

Jaina had said the same thing through the wall, and hearing it here, from the mouth of a thing that spoke of Frostmourne as craft, chilled Sylvanas more deeply than the dark had.

"You came down here to boast," Sylvanas said.

One corner of its mouth moved.

"No. Boasting is for victories that need witnesses." It kept its attention on her. "I came because you had earned a glimpse of the hand behind the work."

Its head inclined a fraction.

"You followed the pattern well. Better than most. I stood beside you more than once and wondered whether this would be the day you finally turned the right way."

Cold moved once through Sylvanas's spine and nowhere on her face.

More than once.

The empty wagon on the Millhaven road. The trader east of Andorhal. The half-seen shape by the Barov trees. The aide at Lor'themar's tent. The Stratholme merchant with the ledger under his arm.

No. Hold the line. Let it say more first.

"You let us get close," she said.

"Close?" the visitor echoed. "I let you remain alive while you were still useful to the work already in motion. There is a difference."

Through the wall Jaina's breathing had steadied again. Good. Let her hear the tone if not the words. Tone alone would tell her what kind of thing had come into the room.

"The anti-blade spell surprised me," the creature said after a beat. "So did your choice to stand inside its reach while she built it. I will remember both."

Not next time, Sylvanas thought. She kept that to herself.

The visitor turned toward the door it had ignored on the way in. The motion had no waste in it. No human shift of shoulder and hip. It rotated whole, a single hinged weapon fitting itself around too small a room.

At the bars and timber that meant nothing to it, the creature paused.

When it spoke again, it used another voice.

Older.

"Kalar'shorel'anor."

The syllables came clean and old. Something in the back of her jaw locked.

Not Common. Not any living form she had heard spoken in camp or court.

Ancient Kaldorei.

The words struck her harder than any threat it had made.

May the stars keep your road.

It passed through the door as if through fog. No sound. No strain. No scrape against bar or hinge. The dark held for a few heartbeats after it was gone, then thinned. Torchlight returned with a wet pop in the corridor bracket and laid itself back across the threshold.

Sylvanas did not move.

The cell smelled of spent pitch, damp bars, and that old dry taint fading by degrees. Above, the blade pulsed again. Nine seconds now, if Jaina was right.

But what hit Sylvanas in the chest was Kalar'shorel'anor.

She knew that farewell.

From the road.

The old grain trader near Millhaven, empty wagon, sharp local knowledge, a blessing strange enough to snag in her ear as they parted.

May the stars keep your road.

The trader east of Andorhal weeks later, different face, same useful answers, same farewell.

The figure near the Barov trees, half seen at distance, voice carrying over evening air just before it vanished into treeline.

The aide in Lor'themar's camp, competent and forgettable, closing a briefing at the tent flap with the same old phrasing.

The merchant in Stratholme, ledger under one arm, every answer precise, market soot on his cuff, the same blessing at the edge of the stall.

Five faces.

One farewell.

In the returned torchlight the five farewells hit one after another: wagon creak at Millhaven, mud off the Andorhal road, evening air by the Barov trees, canvas snapping at Lor'themar's tent, market soot in Stratholme. Same blessing each time. Same hand underneath.

How many had been real?

No answer to that yet. Sylvanas found the better question at once.

What had it failed to hide?

The faces would change. The voice would change. Those were tools. But the farewell had survived five masks and a prison visit. Habit, then. Something old enough not to hear itself anymore.

Through the wall, Jaina waited in the dark, hearing only breath, chain, and the resumed pulse above them.

Sylvanas sat on the cot at last. Chain clicked against the frame.

She would have to give this to Jaina when the corridor quieted. Not the whole thing at once. Too much, too raw, too easy to lose in the wall-code they used. Just the hook of it. The phrase. Five faces. Same farewell.

Chain clicked again against the cot as she leaned forward.

She touched two fingers to the cot frame. First the phrase. Then five taps for the faces. Then silence while she held the rest in her teeth until a better crack opened.

Above her, Frostmourne thudded through the palace stone.

It was still hunting.

So was she.
