# Chapter 25: The Stinger

## I

The blade's pulse came through the ceiling at intervals she had been counting since the torches guttered.

Jaina pressed her palm flat to the flagstone above the cot. Cold bit inward through the heel of her hand, through the thin skin between her knuckles, into the small bones of the wrist where the iron sat. The chill was not winter. Winter had a floor. This pulled heat out of living tissue and kept pulling, and the stone that carried it vibrated at a frequency no palace mason had ever set into mortar.

Fourteen seconds.

She counted breaths, because her pulse was unreliable now. The widened channel Frostmourne had torn through her in the throne room still hummed behind her ribs, a bread-warm ache that flared whenever the blade's cycle peaked above her. She had learned to separate the two rhythms — her own damage and the sword's appetite — the way a sailor learns to parse two currents running against each other under the same hull.

Fourteen seconds between pulses. Last watch it had been sixteen.

The cycle was tightening.

She rolled onto her side and laid two fingers against the mortar between the flagstones overhead, reading the cold the way she had once read ley junctions in Dalaran's lower archives: not for content, not for language, but for tempo and direction. The blade was not broadcasting blind. Its hunger had narrowed in the hours since the verdict — or since Lor'themar had left, she could not tell which, because the palace had stopped marking time for prisoners who were not supposed to require it.

Above her, a guard's mind brushed the edge of the blade's radius.

It hit her like a fingertip sliding across a taut wire. The guard was thinking about his shift ending, about the cold, about whether the corridor assignment was worth the hazard pay. Ordinary thoughts. The blade caught them and held for half a second — testing the weight, tasting what lay beneath — then let go. Not this one. Not enough crack to work into.

Jaina's stomach turned. She swallowed bread-taste and copper and kept counting.

Twelve seconds.

Acceleration was real. Not merely radiating now; it was sorting. Each pulse came back to her bearing trace impressions of the minds it had touched — she could not read names or faces, only the texture of what the sword found in them. Grief tasted like ash. Ambition ran hot and thin, metallic at the edges. Devotion was the worst. Devotion came through with the sword's own cadence riding it, and Jaina could not always tell where the blade's hunger ended and the guard's faith began.

She pulled her hand from the stone and sat up. Chain dragged across the cot frame at her wrist. Her fingers were numb to the second knuckle, and she pressed them into her thigh until the sting brought them back.

Through the wall on her left, Sylvanas was awake.

She knew because the breathing on the other side of the masonry had shifted twenty minutes ago from the long rhythm of elven rest to the shorter cadence that came with attention. No tapping. No words. Just the presence on the other side of two feet of stone, steady and listening.

Jaina leaned her shoulder into the wall and spoke. The masonry blunted her consonants into something dense and half-swallowed, and she kept her voice low enough that the guard at the end of the corridor would hear only murmur.

"It's sorting."

A pause. Then, through the stone, Sylvanas's voice — stripped of its usual polish, worn to the same rasp that prison and sleeplessness gave everything. "Sorting what."

"Minds. It's not reaching blind anymore. The pulses come back shaped. Different texture depending on what it finds."

Silence for four breaths. Then: "Which texture holds longest."

Jaina closed her eyes. Sylvanas always cut straight to the operational question — since the first day the mage had pushed past trespasser and into the longer, uglier work they'd done together. Jaina read the mechanism. Sylvanas asked what the mechanism would do next.

"Devotion," Jaina said. "The kind that doesn't know it's hollow."

Another silence. Longer this time. Through the mortar she could hear Sylvanas shift on the cot, a chain-link touching stone.

"How long."

"The gap was sixteen seconds last watch. It's twelve now. If the curve holds—" She stopped herself from giving the number. The number would sound clinical, and clinical was wrong for what lived two floors above them. "Days. Fewer than before."

"Before Lor'themar."

"Before the verdict."

Jaina heard what the pause meant. Lor'themar was above them now, presumably working whatever leverage road dust and Farstrider rank could buy in a human court that had just condemned the only people who understood the weapon upstairs. Vereesa was with the mages. Outside corroboration was gathering. None of it would move fast enough.

The blade pulsed again. Eleven seconds.

She put her forehead against the cold stone and let the wall hold the weight of her skull for a moment. The iron at her wrists had warmed to body temperature hours ago, which meant her body temperature had dropped to meet the iron, which meant the blade's cold was settling into her through the building's bones the same way it was settling into every other piece of living tissue within its expanding radius.

"Sylvanas."

"Here."

The word came through the stone with the specific quality of a person who would remain in the conversation as long as the conversation needed to exist, even if neither of them could see the other's face. It was not comfort. Comfort had no application in a cell. It was operational presence — the same thing it had been on the grain road, at Andorhal, through the Barov lands, in the freezing dark beneath Frostmourne's cavern ceiling while Muradin bled and the blade sang.

Jaina sat with what she wanted to say. It had been building since the throne room, or since before the throne room — since Northrend, since the moment Arthas had raised Frostmourne against them and the only thing between the blade and the mage had been Sylvanas's body, moving without hesitation into the space where the sword could reach her.

She had known the theory. The anti-blade sequence, the seam in the field, the three-school combination. She had known all of it. And all of it would have died in the knowing if Sylvanas had not bought her the seconds.

Kel'Thuzad had known things too. More than Jaina, probably, about the deeper structures. He had known the plague's architecture, the necromantic lattice, the Lich King's reach. He had understood the mechanism from the inside. He had been Antonidas's colleague before he was anything else, and his research notes — she could still feel the shape of the handwriting in her memory, the margin habits that mirrored her own — had been meticulous, brilliant, and utterly alone.

Kel'Thuzad had walked that conviction into exile, into the plague's creation, into a death that proved nothing except that intellectual certainty without friction could cross every line a person drew for themselves and keep going.

Arthas had carried the same disease. Different symptoms, same pathology. Certainty without counterweight. Conviction without anyone beside him willing to say wait, rethink, here's what you're not seeing. Uther had tried. Jaina had left years before. And Arthas had walked the same spiral Kel'Thuzad walked — alone, certain, the certainty escalating unchecked until Stratholme burned and Northrend consumed him and the sword ate whatever was left.

The mage and the prince and the necromancer. Three minds that never doubted the quality of their own seeing. Two of them died alone.

The difference was the wall she was leaning against and the woman on the other side of it.

"I was wrong about something," Jaina said.

Through the stone: "Only one thing?"

Despite everything — the cold, the iron, the pulse above them, the dark — the corner of Jaina's mouth moved. Sylvanas's humor survived prison the way it survived everything else: by arriving flat and refusing to be softened by context.

"The lie," Jaina said. "The one I carried. I thought being right was enough. I left Dalaran because no one believed the leyline readings. I came north alone because I was the one who could see the pattern, and if the proof was strong enough, people would have to listen." Her palm found the stone again. "That was never what stopped Arthas."

Sylvanas did not answer immediately. The silence held shape, the way silences between them had learned to hold shape — full of listening, empty of interruption, a deliberate space that meant speak the rest of it.

"Millhaven," Jaina said. "We both saw the convergence. Different methods, same answer. I would have seen it alone, eventually. But alone, I would have taken the wrong road after. Andorhal — the grain network, the distribution hub. I needed your contact chain and you needed my readings and neither of us admitted we were building the same picture until the picture was already built." She took a breath. "Scholomance. I looked at Kel'Thuzad's notes and saw my own handwriting. Same notation system. Same margin habits. That should have been the warning. It wasn't. I just kept thinking the difference was that I was right and he was wrong."

Through the wall: "You were right."

"I was right alone. That's the part that matters. Kel'Thuzad was right alone too, about the mechanism. Brilliant, meticulous, and nobody said wait. Nobody said show me the part you're not seeing. He walked his convictions off a cliff and never looked down because there was no one beside him to grab his arm."

The chain on Sylvanas's side of the wall shifted. A long silence — long enough that Jaina could hear the guard at the corridor's end scratch his jaw and mutter something to himself about the cold.

"Arthas," Sylvanas said. The one word carried the full weight, and anything else would have diluted it.

"Same road. Different entry point. He had Uther. He had me, years ago. But Uther gave up at Stratholme when the framework broke, and I'd already gone. By the time Arthas reached Northrend, his certainty had no friction left. Nobody beside him who could say you are wrong about this, and here is why."

Jaina tapped two fingers against the stone. The gesture was deliberate — a marking beat, the same rhythm they'd used through a hundred miles of investigation to punctuate a conclusion.

"The throne room only worked because you were there. The sequence was mine. The seconds were yours. The answer never belonged to only one of us."

Sylvanas's voice came through the stone, sharper now, honed to the edge that meant she was already turning the conclusion into something she could use.

"That's not a confession, Proudmoore. That's a tactical summary."

"It's both."

"Then say the part that costs you."

Jaina closed her eyes. The blade pulsed overhead. Ten seconds.

"I was never going to be enough alone. At the border, at Andorhal, in the throne room — none of it. Everything that worked — every piece of it that actually stopped him — worked because we were right together. Not because I was right and you were useful."

The words crossed the wall harder than the verdict had. The truth settled into her chest, heavy, finally set down after being carried too long and too far. The old lie — being right is enough, intelligence is authority, the proof will speak for itself — cracked along the seam it had been developing since Millhaven, since Kel'Thuzad's notes, since Stratholme. It cracked and she let it.

From the other side of the wall came a sound she had never heard Sylvanas make before. Quiet. Closer to the exhale that follows a blow that lands clean, the breath after a thing that has been true for a long time is finally spoken aloud in a room where it matters.

"Now," Sylvanas said. "Tell me how to use that."

She leaned her back into the cold wall and started packaging intelligence.

The blade's accelerating cycle. The sorting behavior. The texture profiles — grief, ambition, devotion, the specific quality of want that gave the sword purchase. The timeline compression. All of it translated into the wall-language of taps and short sentences they had built over months of fieldwork, compressed now into a medium that required them to say everything in half the words. She laid out the intelligence the way she would lay out a field report: what she knew, what she inferred, what required corroboration she could not provide from a cell.

Through the wall, Sylvanas asked questions that sharpened the report. How far did the sorting reach. Whether the blade's interest in a mind persisted after the guard moved past its radius, or reset with each pass. Whether the intervals were linear or accelerating on a curve.

"Curve," Jaina said. "The gap between twelve and eleven was shorter than the gap between sixteen and fourteen."

Silence. Then: "How does it respond when you speak about it."

Jaina opened her mouth to answer and felt the pulse arrive two seconds early.

She stopped. Held still. The blade's cold pressed through the flagstone above with a focus it had not shown before — a tightening of the ambient pressure into something aimed, something that had heard the analysis and found it interesting. Not because the blade understood language. Because truth about its nature, spoken aloud, carried a specific resonance the sword could taste the way a predator tastes blood-scent on a current of air.

"It's listening," Jaina said.

"Good," Sylvanas said. "Now we know one more thing it does."

## II

The second cold came without warning.

Sylvanas had been turning the report over — cycle compression, sorting behavior, devotion as the vulnerability most exploited, the blade's spike when Jaina spoke accurately about its function — when the temperature in her cell dropped in a way that had nothing to do with Frostmourne.

The blade's cold was a constant: deep, architectural, radiating through the palace's foundations the way damp radiates through limestone. She had mapped its rhythm across her body over the hours since the verdict. Shoulders first, then the small of the back where the cot met the wall, then the soles of the feet through the flagstones. A consistent pressure from above, steady and directionless the way heat from a forge was steady and directionless. Background.

This cold entered through the cell door.

It came from the corridor, and it carried weight. A presence — something that displaced the existing atmosphere the way a body displaces water. Torchlight at the base of her cell door dimmed. Steadily, deliberately, as though the flame was losing a negotiation with whatever occupied the corridor on the other side.

Sylvanas stood.

Chain pulled taut at her ankle two paces from the door. She had measured the cell within the first hour of confinement: the chain allowed her the cot, the bucket, and two steps in any direction. No weapon. No bow. No ranger's knife, confiscated with the rest at sentencing. She stood inside the chain's radius and faced the door with her hands at her sides, empty.

Through the wall on her right, she heard Jaina's breathing change. One sharp intake — the mage had felt it too. Whatever stood in the corridor hit the magical spectrum hard enough to bleed through two feet of masonry and reach a mage's awareness on the other side.

Torchlight at the door's base guttered and went out.

Dark followed that was not the dark of an unlit corridor. Corridors held ambient light — other torches, the pale luminescence of quartz veins in the palace stone, faint phosphor of lichened mortar. This dark had edges. It pressed against the cell the way deep water presses against a hull, and the air inside the stone room gained a thickness that made each breath arrive with slightly more effort than the last.

Something was standing at her door.

She could not see it. Could not hear footsteps. Could not smell anything except the mineral cold of the dungeon stone and a new note beneath it — old, organic, the scent of something preserved past its natural duration. Embalming without the sweetness. Life sustained by a method that did not require circulation.

Her cell door's lock did not turn. Hinges did not move.

Visitor came through the door the way cold comes through glass — without opening, without breaking, without the surface acknowledging the transit. One moment she was alone in the dark. The next, the cell held a second presence, and the air between them compressed.

She could see it now. Or rather, she could see where the dark organized itself into a shape taller than any elf or human she had encountered in two centuries of ranging. Ceiling of the cell was low — eight feet of mortared stone — and the shape filled it. Horns or ridges crested the skull, scraping or nearly scraping the masonry. Wings folded against a torso that should not have fit through the door frame, even if the door had opened. Proportions wrong in the specific way that meant they had never been designed for a humanoid scale, and the body wearing them made no effort to adjust for the space.

Eyes. Green-gold, without pupils, set in a face she could not fully resolve because the surrounding dark wrapped the features tight. The eyes held intelligence — not the blade's mindless seeking, but something old and patient and entertained.

It looked at her the way a scholar looks at a footnote that turned out to be relevant after all.

"Ranger-General." Voice came from everywhere in the cell — from the stone, from the air, from the resonance of the masonry itself. Low, unhurried, with the particular courtesy of someone who has waited for this conversation and sees no need to rush now that it's arrived. "You have been difficult to schedule."

Sylvanas said nothing. Her hands stayed at her sides. Her weight settled into the balls of her feet — reflex, useless without a weapon but impossible to override, the body preparing for what the mind knew it could not fight.

Her mind was already working. The entry method ruled out corporeal transit — phasing or dimensional fold, which narrowed the possibilities to three, two of which were extinct. The voice ruled out projection or illusion: this was a physical body occupying physical space, displacing physical air, standing on flagstones that carried its weight in the micro-vibrations she could read through the soles of her boots.

What was left, she was not willing to name yet.

"Your prince made excellent progress," the visitor said. No malice in the tone. No pleasure. The same flat disinterest a quartermaster gives when reviewing supply figures that came in above projection. "The plague dispersed. The cult embedded. The Scourge sufficiently autonomous. The blade recovered. All of this before his... inconvenient resolution in the throne room."

"Arthas is dead," Sylvanas said.

"Dead," the visitor agreed. "Which is a setback. Not a decisive one." The green-gold eyes moved across her face with the unhurried attention of something that had seen this species before, many times, on many worlds, and found the variations interesting the way a lepidopterist finds variations interesting. "The blade will find another hand. It always does. The architecture he built — the plague network, the cult cells, the Scourge's chain of command — none of that required him specifically. He was the catalyst. The catalyst has been consumed. The reaction continues."

The voice paused. Somewhere above them, the blade pulsed. It arrived at the same instant the visitor tilted its head, and she understood from the tilt that the visitor knew the pulse too — the way a craftsman knows the vibration of a tool he built, familiar and proprietary.

"You built it," she said.

Green-gold eyes returned to her face. Silence lasted precisely long enough to communicate that the visitor was deciding how much of this conversation to permit.

"I was present," the visitor said, "at its forging."

The sentence sat in the cell. It did not elaborate. It did not need to. Present at its forging placed this creature at the Frozen Throne, placed it in the councils of the Lich King, placed it at the inception point of the weapon that had consumed Arthas and murdered Muradin and nearly killed Jaina and was currently eating the palace from the inside out. And the visitor spoke of it with the bored precision of someone describing a project that had delivered on specification.

"The pair of you came closer than projected," the visitor continued. "The anti-blade work. The three-school combination. Unorthodox. My models did not account for a ranger-general willing to stand inside the convergence radius while the mage assembled the sequence." A pause. "That variable will be corrected in future iterations."

Sylvanas held still. Every ranger instinct in her body was screaming data: the posture shifts, the micro-pauses, the careful distribution of intelligence designed to demonstrate how much the visitor knew while revealing how little could be leveraged against it. This was not combat. This was a briefing. The visitor was telling her what had happened and what would happen next, and the telling was its own kind of weapon — the knowledge that they had disrupted a plan, not ended one.

"You could have intervened before the throne room," she said.

"I could have."

"You didn't."

"Arthas was always a variable with a limited shelf life. The question was never whether he would burn out. It was how much infrastructure he would leave behind when he did." The visitor shifted its wings. The movement displaced air in the small cell and carried with it the old-organic scent, stronger now, layered with something mineral and sulfuric that Sylvanas's Ranger training could not place because the training had not been designed for this. "He left quite a lot. The blade alone is worth the investment."

Somewhere through the wall, she heard Jaina shift. The mage was listening. Could not hear the words — masonry swallowed consonants and compressed vowels — but could feel the presence, the wrongness, the specific displacement of ambient magic that a visitor of this magnitude created just by standing in a room.

"What do you want," Sylvanas said.

The visitor regarded her for a long moment. The green-gold eyes did not blink — had not blinked once since the conversation began.

"I want you to understand the scope," it said. "You stopped a man. The architecture is not a man. It does not eat. It does not sleep. It does not make promises to old mentors that it cannot keep. The plague is seeded in six granaries your investigations never reached. The cult operates in cells your ranger network has not mapped. The blade—" A fractional tilt of the horned skull toward the ceiling. "—the blade knows where to look, now. Your prince taught it that. It knows the shape of devotion. It knows the taste of conviction. It will find someone."

Sylvanas's jaw did not tighten. She held the muscles deliberately loose, because the visitor was watching for exactly the kind of micro-response that would give it a reading of her internal state, and two centuries of command had taught her that the face you show the enemy is the face the enemy plans against.

"You came to monologue," she said.

The green-gold eyes narrowed. Not anger. Amusement, the slow-cooked kind that had aged past humor into something structural.

"I came to be seen," the visitor said. "You have been looking at my work for months. The grain route. The distribution network. The cult's embedding protocol. All my design. I thought it courteous to let you see the designer." The voice dropped lower, and the walls resonated with it. "Consider it professional respect. Ranger to ranger. You tracked the pattern beautifully. You simply did not understand what was at the other end of it."

Visitor turned toward the door. The movement was not human — the body rotated as a single unit, no hip-torso separation, no weight transfer, wings folding tighter against the spine with the mechanical precision of something that wore a body the way a soldier wears armor: functional, maintained, not personal.

At the door that it had never opened, the visitor paused.

"Kalar'shorel'anor," it said.

Words came out in a voice that did not belong to the one it had been using. Older. Musical in a way that the Common tongue could not reproduce. Vowels carried the specific tonal quality of a language designed to be spoken under trees that no longer existed, by a people who had not yet broken into factions, before the Sundering had scattered them across a shattered continent and turned one tongue into three.

Ancient Kaldorei. Pre-Sundering.

May the stars keep your road.

It went through the door the same way it had come — without opening, without sound, without the stone acknowledging that anything had passed through it. Dark compressed, held for three seconds, then released. Torch at the door's base relit with a small, guttering pop, and the cell was empty.

Sylvanas stood motionless in the returning light.

Chain at her ankle clinked against the flagstone as her weight settled. The cell smelled of extinguished torch-pitch and the fading mineral trace of whatever the visitor had displaced. The blade's pulse arrived overhead — nine seconds now, the compression still accelerating — and she let it wash through her without counting because the thing in her chest was not the blade.

*Kalar'shorel'anor.*

She knew the phrase.

She knew it because she had heard it five times in the last four months, and each time it had arrived in a different mouth, spoken by a different face, carrying the same inflection that she had noted and re-noted and never assembled into a single line of evidence because the faces had been wrong. Too ordinary. Too civilian. Too perfectly integrated into the world they moved through to read as anything except a coincidence of phrase.

The grain trader at Millhaven. The first time — early, before the investigation had found its shape. She and Jaina had been working the supply routes, tracking the plague's distribution network backward from the infected villages. The trader had been helpful. Good local knowledge. Knew which roads ran where, which wagons carried what, which settlements had shifted their purchasing patterns in the weeks before the sickness arrived. A solid contact. Sylvanas had marked him as useful and moved on.

He had said the farewell as they parted. *May the stars keep your road.* She'd noted it. An archaic phrasing. Not Thalassian, not quite — something beneath it, the root-language that Thalassian and Darnassian had both grown out of, pre-Sundering, before the Highborne schism split the tongue. A curiosity. Nothing more. People carried old phrases the way they carried old coins — through habit, through inheritance, without examining the metal.

Same trader, three weeks later, on the eastern road. Different context, same face, same knowledge, same farewell. She had logged the repetition and noted that the man's territory seemed to expand in precise correlation with the investigation's movement. Another item for the file. Low priority. The plague's trail was louder.

Retreating figure outside Andorhal. Not a face — a silhouette, walking away from the village perimeter at a pace that read as deliberate rather than urgent. She had been on the ridgeline with the ranging party, and the figure had turned, just once, at the treeline. Too far for features. Close enough for the voice to carry across the evening air, and what it carried was the phrase. *Kalar'shorel'anor.* Shouted. Not to anyone visible. Delivered to the distance the way a temple phrase is delivered — outward, without expectation of reply.

She had sent two scouts down the ridge. They found footprints that ended at the road and boot leather that did not match any listed trader.

Military aide in Lor'themar's camp. Weeks later, the investigation deep in the Barov territories. A Farstrider logistics officer she had not worked with before — new transfer, good credentials, attached to the resupply effort. Professional. Competent. Forgettable in the specific way that competent logistics officers are forgettable, because their work is to not be noticed. He had briefed her on route security and road conditions, and when the briefing ended, he had stood at the tent flap and spoken the farewell as though it were routine.

She had turned her head. The aide's face was already in shadow, half-out of the tent, and the phrase hung in the canvas-filtered air between them for exactly as long as it took her to decide it was coincidence for the fourth time.

Merchant at Stratholme. The last one. Days before the confrontation with Arthas, the city still intact, the investigation closing on the cult's operational structure. She had been working the market district, mapping supply chains that fed the cult's embedded cells. The merchant sold dried herbs and alchemical components — legitimate stock, legitimate licenses, legitimate history going back three years according to the guild records she had pulled. He had answered her questions and offered no resistance and wished her well at the stall's edge with the phrase that by then had become a splinter she could not locate but could feel whenever she turned her mind to it.

*May the stars keep your road.*

Five faces. Five voices. Five bodies that had occupied space and displaced air and left footprints and answered questions with knowledge that was always exactly sufficient and never excessive.

One farewell.

In the returning torchlight of her cell, standing in the chain's radius with the visitor's old-organic scent still fading from the air, Sylvanas let the five memories collapse.

Not a coincidence of phrase. An operative's signature. The same creature that had stood in her cell and spoken of forging the blade and designing the plague network and watching their investigation with professional respect — that creature had walked beside them for months. Different body each time. Different voice. Different occupation, different territory, different context. But the same farewell, carried like a maker's mark pressed into each disguise, because the being wearing the faces was old enough that certain habits had calcified past the point of operational discipline.

It had watched them work. It had fed them intelligence — real intelligence, good intelligence, the kind that moved an investigation forward at exactly the pace the investigation needed to move to serve the architecture the visitor had designed. Every contact, every helpful stranger, every local asset with the right knowledge at the right time — how many of them had been genuine, and how many had been the same green-gold eyes wearing a different skull?

That question was the splinter working its way to the surface.

*Kalar'shorel'anor.*

She could hunt it now. Not the faces — the faces would change. Not the voice, not the body, not the occupation or the knowledge base. All of that was camouflage, and the camouflage was better than anything her Ranger training had been designed to penetrate.

But the farewell was a seam.

Ten thousand years of operational habit, compressed into a phrase that the visitor could not stop using because the phrase was older than the disguises, older than the infiltration protocol, older than the architecture it had built on this world. The words belonged to a time before the creature had learned to wear other bodies, and it carried them the way any creature carries the things it learned before it learned to lie.

A transferable filter. Something she could teach a network to listen for. Five confirmed instances, one verified source, a linguistic marker that did not require seeing the face or knowing the body or understanding the scope of what they were fighting. Just the words. Just the stars-keep-road phrase spoken at departure, tracked and cross-referenced and mapped against every other departure in every other investigation that the Farstrider network had ever logged.

Sylvanas sat on the cot. The chain pooled at her ankle. Through the wall, Jaina was still there — she could hear the mage's breathing, slightly faster now, the rhythm of someone who had felt what just happened even if the words had not carried through stone.

She did not tap the wall. Not yet. What she had was too new and too large for the compression of their prison code, and the guard at the corridor's end had not yet resumed his patrol, which meant the guard had felt the visitor too and was standing somewhere in the dark deciding whether what he'd experienced was real.

Sylvanas sat with the five faces and the one farewell and the hunt that would outlast this cell, this sentence, this chapter of the war they had thought was over.

The blade pulsed above her.

She did not count the interval.
