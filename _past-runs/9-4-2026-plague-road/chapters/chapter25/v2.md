# Chapter 25: The Stinger

## I

The blade beat through the ceiling every fourteen seconds.

Jaina lay on the cot with her palm against the flagstone above her head and counted the gaps. Cold bit through skin, through tendon, into the wrist where the iron ring sat. Winter stopped at the skin. This kept going.

Fourteen.

Her own pulse was useless. Frostmourne had opened her in the throne room and left a wrong ache under her ribs that still flared when the sword stirred overhead. She had learned to tell that pain from the blade's rhythm. One was hers. One belonged to the thing in the vault.

Fourteen again.

She rolled onto her side and pressed two fingers into the mortar. The pulse moved through the palace stone with a hungry regularity, no longer broad and dumb. It had narrowed since the verdict. It was reaching.

Above her, a guard crossed whatever corridor ran past the vault.

The contact came down the stone and into her hand: one brush of a mortal mind against the blade's field. Shift almost done. Cold in the joints. Supper waiting somewhere upstairs. Small thoughts, tired thoughts. Frostmourne caught on them for half a heartbeat, weighed them, and let go.

Not this one.

Jaina swallowed against the copper at the back of her throat.

Twelve.

That was new.

The pace had shortened within minutes, not hours. Worse than that, each pulse came back carrying traces. She could not read names. She could feel the shape of what the blade found. Grief came through dry and gray. Ambition burned thin and sharp. Devotion lingered longest. Devotion came back wearing the sword's cadence so cleanly that for a beat she could not tell where the person's faith ended and the blade began.

She sat up. Chain scraped the cot frame. Her fingers had gone numb; she dug them into her thigh until the sting brought them back.

Through the wall on her left, Sylvanas was awake.

Jaina had known for twenty minutes. Elven sleep had one rhythm. Sylvanas listening in the dark had another. No tapping yet. No whisper through the stone. Just the weight of another mind holding itself ready on the far side of two feet of masonry.

Jaina leaned into the wall and kept her voice low.

"It's sorting."

A pause. Then Sylvanas, roughened by prison and too little sleep: "Sorting what."

"Minds. It touches, tests, lets go."

Silence for a few breaths.

"Which kind does it keep."

Jaina shut her eyes. That was how it had always worked between them. She brought the mechanism. Sylvanas asked what it meant in the field.

"Devotion," Jaina said. "The kind that wants someone else to tell it what to do."

Stone carried a small shift of chain from the other cell.

"How long."

"The gap was sixteen last watch. It's twelve now." She stopped before she turned it into numbers and projections. Plain speech carried best through stone. So did the danger upstairs. "Days. Fewer than we had."

"Before Lor'themar."

"Before the verdict."

That pause had weight too. Lor'themar was somewhere above them, road dust still on his boots, trying to force sense into a court that had condemned the only two people in the city who understood the weapon in the vault. Vereesa was with the mages. Evidence was moving. None of it would move fast enough.

The blade thudded overhead again.

Eleven.

Jaina set her forehead against the stone. The irons on her wrists had warmed hours ago. Now even that warmth had gone thin. The sword's cold was working deeper into the palace stone.

"Sylvanas."

"Here."

Jaina let her hand flatten on the masonry between them.

What she wanted to say had been waiting since the throne room. Since before that, if she was honest. Since Northrend. Since the moment Frostmourne came down for her and Sylvanas moved without hesitation into the gap.

She had known the answer there. The seam in the blade's field. The three schools that would bite together if she forced them hard enough. She had known every part of it.

She had still failed with it in her hands.

Kel'Thuzad had known things too. More than she did about the deeper workings. He had known the plague from the inside. He had known where it would go if no one stopped him. He had written it all down in that careful hand of his, margins neat, argument exact, horror tucked inside scholarship so cleanly it almost passed for virtue.

And he had done it alone.

Arthas had gone the same way by a different road. Certainty. Momentum. No one left beside him with enough standing to say stop. Uther gone. Jaina gone. They kept using the next step to justify the one before it.

Kel'Thuzad. Arthas.

Two men sure they could do it alone.

Jaina leaned harder into the wall.

"I was wrong," she said.

From the other side: "Only now?"

The corner of her mouth twitched despite the cold. Sylvanas's humor had survived plague roads, necromancers, Northrend, and a death sentence. Of course it had survived a cell.

"About the part that mattered," Jaina said. "I thought if I saw clearly enough, that was the work done. I left Dalaran because no one believed the readings. I crossed your border alone because I thought proof would force the world to behave."

Sylvanas listened. No interruption. No mercy either.

"Millhaven," Jaina went on. "Andorhal. Scholomance. Northrend. I kept telling myself I was the one who knew what came next. Even after that stopped being true."

Through the wall, Sylvanas said, "You were carrying one answer."

"And one answer would have gotten me killed." Jaina drew a breath and kept going before shame could close her throat. "I saw the pattern at Millhaven. Alone, I would still have taken the wrong road after. I could read the grain network. I could not move through it without your people. At Scholomance I looked at Kel'Thuzad's notes and saw too much of myself in them, and even then I still thought the difference was that I was right and he wasn't."

"He wasn't."

"He was right about pieces," Jaina said. "So was Arthas. That was the ruin of both of them. They had conviction and no friction. No one at their shoulder who could say no, that's the wrong piece, start again."

The chain in Sylvanas's cell shifted once.

"Arthas," Sylvanas said.

"Yes." Jaina tapped two fingers against the stone. Once. "By Northrend there was no one left in reach of him. The throne room only worked because you were there. The sequence was mine. The seconds were yours. If you hadn't bought them, all my knowing would have died in my head."

Silence stretched while Sylvanas listened.

"That's closer," Sylvanas said.

Jaina laughed once, softly, with no humor in it. "You want cleaner wording while we're chained in a cellar."

"I want the sentence that costs you."

The blade pulsed above them.

Ten.

Jaina closed her eyes and said it into the stone between them.

"I was never going to be enough alone at the border, in Lordaeron, or in the throne room," she said. "We stopped him because we were right together, with both of us doing the work."

From the far side of the wall came a quiet sound. More like the breath a fighter lets out when a blow lands where it was meant to.

"Good," Sylvanas said. "Now tell me what to do with that."

So Jaina did.

She gave her the blade in pieces they could use. The shrinking interval. The way it sampled each mind that crossed its reach. The flavors it held onto. The way devotion lasted longest. The way grief burned out faster. The way speaking true things about it made the pulse sharpen.

They slipped back into the language they had built on the road: short lines, clear handoffs, no wasted ornament.

"How far?" Sylvanas asked.

"Past the vault corridor. Not the whole palace. Yet."

"If a guard leaves its reach?"

"Depends how deep it got."

"Linear?"

"No. Faster each time."

"If someone lies about it."

"I don't know."

"If someone speaks true."

Jaina opened her mouth to answer.

The pulse hit two seconds early.

She went still.

Cold tightened overhead, no longer broad through the stone but focused, attentive. The pressure narrowed on her voice, on the shape of what she had just said, on the fact of being known and named.

Jaina kept her own voice flat when she spoke.

"It hears accuracy."

"Good," Sylvanas said at once. "Then we know one more place to cut it."

The pulse eased. Barely.

Jaina let out the breath through her nose.

Beyond the wall, Sylvanas's chain gave another small scrape. Thinking already. Turning the blade over from every side it offered her.

Jaina kept her palm on the stone.

The prison had taken sight, touch, movement, any honest route between them. They could still pass the blade between them.

Above them, Frostmourne kept counting down.

## II

Sylvanas was still turning Jaina's report through her head when the second cold entered the cell.

The blade's cold she knew now. It came from above and soaked downward through stone, steady as seep-water. This was different. It slipped under the door and stood there.

Torchlight at the base of the cell dimmed.

Sylvanas rose at once.

The chain at her ankle stopped her two paces from the door. She had measured that in the first hour: cot, bucket, wall, two steps of useless freedom in any direction. No bow. No knife. Nothing left to her but balance, judgment, and the fact that she was still alive.

Through the wall on her right, Jaina's breathing caught once.

So the mage felt it too.

The torch guttered out.

Darkness pressed into the cell. Palace dark still had traces in it: a seam of light under a door, the pale bleed of old stone, the memory of a corridor beyond. This came in thick. The air itself grew heavier, enough that drawing breath took work.

Something stood outside the door.

Sylvanas could not hear steps. Could not hear cloth. She smelled dungeon stone, stale torch pitch, and under both a dry preserved scent that had never belonged to anything meant to remain alive.

The lock did not turn.

The door did not move.

The visitor came through anyway.

One moment the cell held only Sylvanas and the dark. The next it held another body, and the space between them shortened under its size.

It was tall enough to make the cell feel smaller. Horns or ridges brushed the shadow near the ceiling. Wings folded close along a frame built for wider places than this. The proportions were wrong in ways no armor could explain. They belonged to something that treated doors like they did not apply to it.

Its eyes were green-gold and old.

Watching.

Amused, perhaps, though not kindly.

"Ranger-General," it said.

The voice came from everywhere at once: air, stone, the iron ring at her ankle answering the sound. Low. Courteous. Patient in the way large predators are patient.

"You have been troublesome from the first."

Sylvanas kept her hands loose at her sides. Empty hands, but she would not let them look helpless.

Her mind moved while she stood. Physical presence, not illusion. Weight on the flagstones. Air displaced. Voice with body behind it. Transit through stone without impact. That narrowed the field sharply, and what remained she refused to name until naming was useful.

"Your prince did well enough," the visitor said. "The plague spread. The cult rooted itself. The dead learned their work. The blade came home. All that before he lost his nerve and his life in the same room."

"Arthas is dead," Sylvanas said.

"Yes." No heat in the answer.

It studied her face with scholarly patience.

"The sword will take another hand. The roads he opened remain open. You still never found all the cells. One man was useful. He was never the whole design."

Above them, Frostmourne beat once through the ceiling. The creature tipped its head at the sound the way a smith might acknowledge a familiar hammer strike.

Sylvanas kept that too.

"You made it," she said.

The pause that followed was careful.

"I was there when it was forged."

Sylvanas held still.

"You could have stepped in before the throne room," Sylvanas said.

"I could have."

"You didn't."

The folded wings shifted, a controlled rustle in the dark.

"Arthas had done what was needed. After that, the question was yield."

"Yield."

"How much plague could spread, how much fear it could buy, how many people death would force into line before he broke." The voice stayed calm. "Quite a lot, as it happens."

Through the wall Jaina moved, one soft scrape of chain. Listening blind.

Sylvanas kept her own face still. The creature was reading her for weakness, for the shape of alarm, for where to press if it chose to press.

"What do you want," she said.

It considered her long enough to make the answer deliberate.

"I wanted you to understand the size of what you touched." Its head angled slightly toward the vault above. "You killed the wielder. The power behind him is still here."

Neither of them spoke.

"There are granaries your hunt never reached," it went on. "Cells your networks never named. The blade already knows more than it did this morning. Your prince taught it. It knows what devotion tastes like now. It will use that."

Jaina had said the same thing through the wall, and hearing it here—from the mouth of a thing that spoke of Frostmourne as its craft—chilled her more deeply.

"You came down here to boast," Sylvanas said.

It showed recognition.

"No. Boasting is for victories that need witnesses." It kept its attention on her. "I came because you had earned a look at the hand behind the work."

Its head inclined a fraction.

"You followed the pattern well. Better than most. I stood beside you more than once and wondered whether this would be the day you finally turned the right way."

Cold moved once through Sylvanas's spine and nowhere on her face.

More than once.

The grain road. Andorhal. Barov country. Stratholme. All those useful strangers, all that tidy help arriving where it could do the most work and cost the least suspicion—

No. Hold the line. Let it say more first.

"You let us get close," she said.

"Close?" the visitor echoed. "I let you remain alive while you were still useful to the shape of events. There is a difference."

Through the wall Jaina's breathing had steadied again. Good. Let her hear tone if not words. Tone was enough to know how dangerous a thing was.

"The anti-blade spell surprised me," the creature said after a beat. "So did your choice to stand inside its reach while she built it. I will remember both."

Not next time, Sylvanas thought. She kept that to herself.

The visitor turned toward the door it had ignored on the way in. The motion had no waste in it. No human shift of shoulder and hip. It rotated whole, a single hinged weapon.

At the wall of iron bars and wood that meant nothing to it, the creature paused.

When it spoke again, it used another voice.

Older.

Musical in a way Common could not carry.

"Kalar'shorel'anor."

Ancient Kaldorei.

The words struck her harder than any threat it had made.

May the stars keep your road.

It passed through the door as if through fog. No sound. No strain. The dark held for a few heartbeats after it was gone, then thinned. Torchlight returned with a wet pop at the base of the door.

Sylvanas did not move.

The cell smelled of spent pitch and that old, dry taint fading by degrees. Above, the blade pulsed again. Nine seconds now, if Jaina was right.

But what hit Sylvanas in the chest was Kalar'shorel'anor.

She knew that farewell.

From the road.

The old grain trader near Millhaven, empty wagon, sharp local knowledge, a blessing strange enough to snag in her ear as they parted.

May the stars keep your road.

The trader east of Andorhal weeks later, different face, same useful answers, same farewell.

The figure near the Barov lands, half-seen at distance, voice carrying over evening air just before it vanished into treeline.

The aide in Lor'themar's camp, competent and forgettable, closing a briefing at the tent flap with the same old phrasing.

The merchant in Stratholme, ledger under one arm, every answer precise, every guild record in order, the same blessing at the edge of the stall.

Five faces.

One farewell.

In the torchlight of the cell, the memories folded together so fast it almost hurt. A tell. An old habit worn smooth enough to survive disguise.

The creature had walked beside them for months.

Fed them a name here, a road there, a warning when it served. Cleared the next stretch of road before they reached it. Let them see exactly enough to keep them moving where it wanted them moving. Every helpful stranger, every contact with just the right piece, every road pointed half a degree in the useful direction—

How many had been real?

No answer to that yet. Sylvanas thought of the better question at once.

What had it failed to hide?

The faces would change. The body would change. The voice would change. Those were tools. But the farewell had survived five masks and a prison visit. That meant the habit behind it belonged to something older than discipline. Older than this campaign. Older, perhaps, than the world as it stood now.

Through the wall, Jaina waited in the dark, hearing only breath and silence and the resumed pulse above them.

Sylvanas sat on the cot at last. Chain pooled around her ankle.

She would have to give this to Jaina when the corridor quieted. Not the whole thing at once; too much, too raw, too easy to lose in the wall-code they used. But the core of it. The phrase. The pattern. The fact that one being had worn five mortal shapes and left the same ancient footprint in speech every time.

Farstriders, quartermasters, runners, and archive mages could listen for the phrase.

Not tonight. Maybe not tomorrow. But outward, once there was any crack at all. Farstriders, quartermasters, runners, mages with archives, anyone who could hold a phrase and compare it against another one spoken miles away by a stranger who should not know it.

Five confirmed uses. One source.

Above her, Frostmourne thudded through the palace stone.

It was still hunting.

So was she.
