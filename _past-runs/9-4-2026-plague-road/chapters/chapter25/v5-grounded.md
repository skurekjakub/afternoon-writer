"Sylvanas."

"Here."

Jaina pressed her bare palm flat against the freezing masonry separating their cells. What she needed to say had been clawing at her throat since the throne room. Since Northrend, really. She’d figured out the flaw in the blade's magic. She knew exactly which three schools of arcane theory she could force together to break it. But none of that mattered—she would have died with the answer locked in her head if Sylvanas hadn't moved when she did.

Kel'Thuzad had gone down his path alone. Arthas had done the same, driven by a terrible momentum with no one left at his side holding enough authority to tell him to stop.

Jaina leaned into the wall until the rough mortar dug into her skin.

"I was wrong," she said.

"Just figuring that out now?" Sylvanas's voice was dry, muffled by the stone.

The corner of Jaina's mouth twitched despite the biting chill. "I was wrong about what simply 'being right' could accomplish. I abandoned Dalaran because the council wouldn't look at my readings. I crossed your borders alone because I thought undeniable proof would somehow force the rest of the world to fall in line."

Sylvanas let the silence hang.

Jaina pulled her knees to her chest on the hard cot. "Millhaven. Andorhal. Scholomance. Northrend. Every time I uncovered a new horror, I thought being the first to see it gave me the power to control what happened next."

"You only had one piece of the puzzle."

"And carrying it alone nearly got me killed," Jaina said, pushing the words out before pride could stop her. "I saw the plague's pattern at Millhaven, but without your scouts, I would have walked straight into an ambush. At Scholomance, I read Kel’Thuzad’s notes and saw too much of myself in his margins. I still thought the only difference between us was that I was right and he was mad."

"He was mad."

"He was right about the pieces. Just like Arthas was," Jaina argued. "That’s what destroyed them both. They marched down their paths alone. They had no one standing at their shoulder to catch the fatal mistake early. No one they respected enough to say no, and make it stick."

In the next cell, Sylvanas's chains rattled heavily. "Arthas."

"Yes." Jaina tapped two fingers rhythmically against the stone. "By the time he reached Northrend, he was completely out of reach. Our strike in the throne room only worked because you were there. I had the magical countermeasure, but the seconds it took to cast were yours. If you hadn't bought them for me, all my brilliant knowledge would have died with me."

The silence stretched longer this time.

"Getting warmer," Sylvanas finally said.

Jaina let out a sharp, breathless laugh. "You're seriously critiquing my phrasing while we're chained up beneath the evidence vault?"

"I want the sentence that actually costs your pride to say."

Above them, the blade’s oppressive aura pulsed through the ceiling.

Ten seconds.

Jaina closed her eyes, resting her forehead against the damp stone. "I was never going to be enough on my own. Not at the border, not in Lordaeron, and not in that throne room. We only stopped him because we did it together."

A quiet, steady exhale came through the wall.

"Good," Sylvanas said softly. "Now, tell me how we use that to survive."

So Jaina did. She shifted to the analytical, the practical. "The blade's magic skims a victim's mind and leaves a residue. It burns right through surface emotions like grief, but it anchors to deeper things. Devotion. That’s what it keeps feeding on."

"What's its range?"

"It bleeds past the vault corridor. Not the whole palace yet, but it's spreading."

"What happens if a guard walks out of its radius?"

"Depends on how deep the magic dug its hooks in."

"So its growth isn't linear."

"No. It gets exponentially faster each time it feeds."

"And if someone lies in its presence?"

"I don't know," Jaina admitted. "But if someone speaks the absolute truth—"

A wave of suffocating cold slammed down through the ceiling, hitting two seconds early.

Jaina froze on her cot. The ambient chill of the dungeon sharpened into a focused, icy pressure pressing directly against her skull. The blade hadn't just pulsed randomly; it had leaned violently toward the exact spot where her confession had landed.

Jaina forced her voice to stay perfectly level. "It listens for the truth."

"Good," Sylvanas said, a predator's edge returning to her tone. "Then we know exactly where to cut it."

The crushing pressure slowly receded.

On the other side of the wall, Sylvanas shifted, her chains scraping against the floor before settling into absolute stillness. Jaina kept her hand pressed flat against the mortar. Between them, an unspoken rhythm caught hold.

Above their heads, Frostmourne kept counting down.

Sylvanas was still turning Jaina’s theory over in her mind when a different kind of cold bled into the cell. 

Frostmourne’s chill was a constant, heavy pressure weeping down from the ceiling. This new cold was deliberate. It slithered under the gap in the heavy oak door and simply stood there. 

Out in the corridor, the torchlight thinned to a sickly sliver.

Sylvanas stood immediately. 

Her ankle chain pulled taut exactly two paces from the door. She quickly cataloged her reality: a cot, a bucket, damp stone, two steps of slack, and heavy iron. She had no bow. No dagger. No room to build momentum for a strike. She had only her balance, her judgment, and the stubborn fact that she was still breathing.

Through the right-hand wall, Jaina’s breath hitched. A sharp, sudden inhale. 

So Jaina felt it, too.

In the hallway, the torch sputtered and died. Darkness shoved its way through the bars, so thick and heavy that drawing breath suddenly took effort. Sylvanas smelled damp dungeon stone, old pitch, and rotting rushes—but beneath it all lay the preserved, suffocating stench of something that had no business being alive. 

The lock didn't turn. The heavy timber door didn't creak. 

The visitor simply stepped through it. 

One second, the cell held only Sylvanas and the dark. The next, a massive body crowded the tiny space, displacing the air. Great, leathery wing joints tucked in tight against the stone walls. In the pitch black, the faint outline of sweeping horns scraped near the ceiling. Its eyes burned a sickly, ancient green-gold. 

"Ranger-General," it said. 

The voice didn't just come from its throat; it reverberated through the stone floor and the iron cuff around her ankle. It was a low, courteous rumble. It had the infinite patience of a predator that had already trapped its meal.

"You have been troublesome from the very beginning."

Sylvanas kept her hands loose at her sides, refusing to give it the satisfaction of seeing her brace for an attack. 

"Your prince performed well enough," the visitor continued, amused. "He emptied the granaries. He ensured the bakers fed entire districts. The dead woke and learned their new trades. The blade finally came home. He accomplished all of that before he lost his nerve, and his life, in the very same room."

"Arthas is dead," Sylvanas stated flatly.

"He is." It watched her without blinking. "And the sword will simply find another hand. The ovens he loaded are spent, but the supply lines he severed remain broken. Your little hunt never found all of our sleeper cells. One prince was a useful tool. He was never the entire grand design."

Above them, Frostmourne’s aura thudded like a heartbeat through the ceiling. The massive creature tilted its head, listening to the rhythm.

"You made it," Sylvanas said. 

The silence that followed was deliberate and careful. 

"I was present when it was forged."

Sylvanas didn't move a muscle. "You could have stepped in. Before the throne room. You could have stopped us."

"I could have."

"But you didn't."

The creature shifted, its leathery wings rustling against the stone. "Arthas had already done what was required. After that, the only question was how much his death would buy us."

"How much?"

"How many ovens took the tainted flour. How many cellars stacked it out of sight. How many terrified men fell in line once the dead began walking the streets." The rumbling voice remained perfectly even. "Quite a lot, as it happens."

Through the wall, Jaina shifted. The faint, metallic scrape of her chain against the floor was the only sound. She was listening blind.

Sylvanas kept her expression entirely blank. "What do you want?"

"I wanted you to understand the sheer scale of what you’ve touched." The creature angled its horned head toward the vault above them. "You killed the man wielding the weapon. But the hand guiding him is still right here."

Neither of them spoke for a long moment. 

"There are granaries your rangers never found," it murmured. "Cult cells your spies never named." Frostmourne pulsed overhead again. "The blade knows what true devotion tastes like now. Your fallen prince taught it that much, at least."

Jaina had deduced the exact same thing just minutes ago through the wall. But hearing it here, from a monster that spoke of the cursed sword as a simple piece of craftsmanship, chilled Sylvanas to the marrow. 

"You came down here just to gloat."

One corner of the creature's mouth twitched upward. "No. Gloating is for petty victories that require an audience. I came because you earned a glimpse behind the curtain." It bowed its head a fraction of an inch. "You tracked the plague's pattern remarkably well. I stood beside you more than once, wondering if today would be the day you finally looked in the right direction."

Ice trailed down Sylvanas's spine, but she locked her face into a mask of stone. 

*More than once.*

Her mind raced backward. The empty wagon she’d inspected on the Millhaven road. The jumpy trader east of Andorhal. The shadow slipping through the Barov estate trees. The quiet aide hovering near Lor'themar's command tent. The Stratholme merchant clutching a ledger.

"You let us get that close."

"Close?" the visitor mocked softly. "I let you continue breathing because you were still useful in driving the panic forward. There is a vast difference."

Through the masonry, Jaina's breathing had leveled out. She was recording every word. Good.

"That warding spell the mage cast to check the blade surprised me," the creature admitted. "As did your choice to stand inside the blade's reach to buy her the time to weave it. I will remember both of those things."

The visitor turned back toward the solid timber door. The movement was entirely alien—there was no human shifting of weight or pivot of the hips. It rotated as a single, fluid mass, like a weapon reorienting itself in a space too small to hold it. 

Before phasing back through the bars and wood, it paused. 

When it spoke again, the voice shifted. The rumbling bass vanished, replaced by something much older and far more resonant. 

"*Kalar'shorel'anor.*"

The syllables were clipped, clean, and perfectly accented. Sylvanas's jaw locked so hard her teeth ached. 

It wasn't Common. It was Ancient Kaldorei. 

*May the stars keep your road.*

The creature stepped through the door as if walking through morning fog. The crushing darkness lingered in the cell for a few rapid heartbeats, then evaporated. Out in the corridor, the torch flared back to life with a wet hiss, throwing harsh light across the stone threshold. 

Sylvanas stood perfectly still. 

Above, the blade pulsed again. Nine seconds between beats now, if Jaina’s math held up. 

But it wasn't the sword that occupied Sylvanas's mind. It was the parting phrase. *Kalar'shorel'anor*. It hit her square in the chest. 

She knew that specific farewell. 

The wagon driver at Millhaven. The nervous trader in Andorhal. The shadow in the Barov trees. Lor'themar's quiet aide. The merchant in Stratholme. 

Five different faces. 

One identical farewell. 

In the flickering torchlight, the memories slammed into place: the creak of the wagon wheels, the sucking mud of the eastern road, the cool evening air under the estate trees, the snap of canvas at the command tent, the choking soot of the market. It was the exact same blessing every time. The exact same inflection. 

How many of those encounters had been the creature? All of them? 

She didn't have the answer yet. But the better question was already forming. 

What had the immortal creature failed to hide? 

It could change its face. It could change its voice. Those were just tools. But that farewell had survived across five different masks, and now, a prison gloat. It was a habit. A tic. Something so old and deeply ingrained that the creature didn't even hear itself saying it anymore. An immortal's blind spot.

Through the wall, Jaina waited in the dark, hearing only the scrape of Sylvanas's chain and the steady, hungry pulse of the sword above them.

Sylvanas finally sat down on the hard cot. The iron links clattered loudly against the wooden frame. 

She needed to pass this to Jaina as soon as the corridor guards settled. The phrase. The five faces. The identical farewell. 

She rested two fingers against the cot's frame, planning the rhythm. First, she would tap out the syllables of the phrase. Then, five sharp raps for the faces. 

Above her, Frostmourne thudded through the palace stone. 

It was still hunting. 

But now, so was she.
