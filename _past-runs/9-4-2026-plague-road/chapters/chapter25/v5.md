# Chapter 25: The Stinger

## I

The answer through the wall had been three light taps. Above that, Frostmourne beat through the white palace stone every fourteen seconds.

Jaina lay on the narrow cot, palm braced against the flagstones, and counted the gaps the evidence vault above kicked down into her cell. Cold bit through skin and tendon into the wrist beneath the iron ring. Winter stopped at the skin. This came through masonry.

Fourteen.

The cell smelled of damp stone, old torch pitch, and water gone sour in its bucket. None of it displaced the wrongness in the foundations. The service dark under Lordaeron's bright halls carried the same cold in its torch brackets, cart axles, and sweating mortar.

Fourteen again.

She pressed two fingers into the mortar seam. The pulse had narrowed since the verdict. It was reaching.

Above her, someone crossed the vault corridor. Boot leather damp at the sole, knee stiff with cold, shift nearly done. Frostmourne brushed the edge of that passing mind, weighed it, and let it go.

Not this one.

Jaina swallowed against copper on her tongue.

Twelve.

That was new.

The pace had shortened within minutes. Worse, each pulse came back carrying traces. The guard's step dragged for half a pace, then squared again. The next pulse left ash on her tongue and the hard upright strain that still wanted orders. Devotion lasted.

She sat up too fast. Chain scraped the cot frame.

Through the wall on her left, Sylvanas was awake. Elven sleep had one rhythm. Sylvanas's chain had settled once twenty minutes ago, then gone still around slow measured breaths.

Jaina leaned until her shoulder touched the wall and kept her voice low.

"It's sorting."

Sylvanas answered at once, roughened by prison and too little sleep. "Sorting what."

"Minds. It touches, tests, lets go."

Silence. A faint chain-shift through the wall.

"Which kind does it keep."

"Devotion. The kind that wants someone else to tell it what to do."

"How long."

"Sixteen last watch. It's twelve now." She bit back projections. "Days. Fewer than we had."

"Before Lor'themar."

"Before the verdict."

A cart wheel thudded somewhere in the service dark above. Lor'themar was up there somewhere with road dust still on him, trying to force sense into rooms that moved paper faster than doors.

The blade thudded overhead again.

Eleven.

Jaina set her forehead against the wall.

"Sylvanas."

"Here."

She flattened her hand on the masonry between them. What she wanted to say had been waiting since the throne room. Since Northrend, if she was honest. She had known the seam in the blade's field. She had known which three schools would bite together if she forced them hard enough.

And she would still have died with that answer in her head if Sylvanas had not moved.

Kel'Thuzad had gone alone. Arthas too, by a different road: certainty, momentum, no one left beside him with enough standing to say stop.

Jaina leaned harder into the wall until the mortar bit.

"I was wrong," she said.

"Only now?"

The corner of her mouth twitched despite the cold.

"About what being right could do. I left Dalaran because no one believed the readings. I crossed your border alone because I thought proof would force the world to behave."

Sylvanas let the silence hold.

Jaina tucked one knee up on the cot. "Millhaven. Andorhal. Scholomance. Northrend. Every time I thought seeing it first meant I owned what came after."

"You were carrying one answer."

"And one answer would have gotten me killed." Jaina kept going before shame could close her throat. "I saw the pattern at Millhaven. Alone, I would still have taken the wrong road after. I could read the grain network. I could not move through it without your people. At Scholomance I saw too much of myself in Kel'Thuzad's notes and still thought the difference was that I was right and he wasn't."

"He wasn't."

"He was right about pieces. So was Arthas. That was the ruin of both of them. They had no one at the shoulder to catch the bad turn early. No one close enough to say no and make it stick."

The chain in Sylvanas's cell shifted once.

"Arthas."

"Yes." Jaina tapped two fingers against the wall. "By Northrend there was no one left in reach of him. The throne room only worked because you were there. The answer was mine. The seconds were yours. If you hadn't bought them, all my knowing would have died in my head."

Silence stretched.

"That's closer."

Jaina laughed once. "You want cleaner wording while we're chained under the evidence vault."

"I want the sentence that costs you."

The blade pulsed above them.

Ten.

Jaina closed her eyes and said it into the masonry between them.

"I was never going to be enough alone at the border, in Lordaeron, or in the throne room. We stopped him because we were right together."

From the far side of the wall came a quiet exhale.

"Good," Sylvanas said. "Now tell me what to do with that."

So Jaina did.

"It skims a mind and leaves a trace. Grief burns off fast. Devotion stays. That's the one it keeps worrying."

"How far?"

"Past the vault corridor. Not the whole palace. Not yet."

"If a guard leaves its reach?"

"Depends how deep it got."

"Not linear, then."

"No. Faster each time."

"If someone lies about it."

"I don't know. If someone speaks true—"

The pulse hit two seconds early.

She went still on the cot.

Cold tightened overhead, no longer broad through the stone but focused. The pulse had leaned toward the place her words had landed.

Jaina kept her voice flat.

"It hears what's true."

"Good," Sylvanas said at once. "Then we know one more place to cut it."

The pressure eased.

Beyond the wall Sylvanas's chain scraped once, then went quiet. Jaina kept her palm on the seam. On the far side, Sylvanas answered once. Between those two small sounds, the blade still had somewhere to go.

Above them, Frostmourne kept counting down.

## II

Sylvanas was still turning Jaina's report through her head when a second cold entered the cell.

The blade's cold seeped down from above. This gathered under the door and stood there.

Torchlight thinned beneath the bars.

Sylvanas rose at once.

The chain checked her two paces from the door. Cot, bucket, wall, two steps, iron. No bow. No knife. No room for a rush. Balance, judgment, and the fact that she was still alive.

Through the wall on her right, Jaina's breathing caught once.

So Jaina had it too.

The torch outside guttered and died.

Darkness pressed into the cell until drawing breath took work. Something stood outside the door. Sylvanas smelled dungeon stone, old pitch, stale rushes, and under all of it a preserved taint that had never belonged to anything meant to remain alive.

The lock did not turn.

The door did not move.

The visitor came through anyway.

One moment the cell held only Sylvanas and the dark. The next it held another body, crowding the room by sheer size. Air displaced. Wing joints tucked tight. Horns or ridges cut the dark above. Its eyes were green-gold and old.

"Ranger-General," it said.

The voice came from air, stone, ankle iron, and door bars alike. Low. Courteous. Patient in the way large predators were patient.

"You have been troublesome from the first."

Sylvanas kept her hands loose at her sides.

"Your prince did well enough," the visitor said. "Granaries emptied. Bakers fed whole districts. The dead learned their work. The blade came home. All that before he lost his nerve and his life in the same room."

"Arthas is dead," Sylvanas said.

"Yes."

It watched without blinking.

"The sword will take another hand. The ovens he fed are spent, but the roads he opened remain open. You still never found all the cells. One man was useful. He was never the whole design."

Above them, Frostmourne thudded once through the ceiling. The creature tipped its head at the sound.

"You made it," she said.

The pause that followed was careful.

"I was there when it was forged."

Sylvanas held still.

"You could have stepped in before the throne room."

"I could have."

"You didn't."

The folded wings shifted.

"Arthas had done what was needed. After that, the question was how much it bought."

"How much."

"How many ovens took the flour. How many cellars stacked it. How many frightened men took orders faster once the dead were in the streets." The voice stayed calm. "Quite a lot, as it happens."

Through the wall Jaina moved, one soft scrape of chain. Listening blind.

Sylvanas kept her face still.

"What do you want."

"I wanted you to understand the size of what you touched." Its head angled toward the vault above. "You killed the wielder. The power behind him is still here."

Neither of them spoke.

"There are granaries your hunt never reached," it went on. "Cells your networks never named." Frostmourne thudded overhead again. "It knows what devotion tastes like now. Your prince taught it that much."

Jaina had said the same thing through the wall. Hearing it here, from a thing that spoke of Frostmourne as craft, chilled Sylvanas more deeply than the dark had.

"You came down here to boast."

One corner of its mouth moved.

"No. Boasting is for victories that need witnesses. I came because you had earned a glimpse of the hand behind the work."

Its head inclined a fraction.

"You followed the pattern well. I stood beside you more than once and wondered whether this would be the day you finally turned the right way."

Cold touched Sylvanas's spine and nowhere on her face.

More than once.

The empty wagon on the Millhaven road. The trader east of Andorhal. The half-seen shape by the Barov trees. The aide at Lor'themar's tent. The Stratholme merchant with the ledger under his arm.

"You let us get close."

"Close?" the visitor echoed. "I let you remain alive while you were still useful to the work already in motion. There is a difference."

Through the wall Jaina's breathing had steadied again. Good.

"The spell that checked the blade surprised me," the creature said. "So did your choice to stand inside its reach while she built it. I will remember both."

The visitor turned toward the door it had ignored on the way in. The motion had no waste in it. No human shift of shoulder and hip. It rotated whole, a single hinged weapon fitting itself around too small a room.

At the bars and timber that meant nothing to it, the creature paused.

When it spoke again, it used another voice.

Older.

"Kalar'shorel'anor."

The syllables came clean and old. Something in the back of her jaw locked.

Not Common.

Ancient Kaldorei.

May the stars keep your road.

It passed through the door as if through fog. The dark held for a few heartbeats after it was gone, then thinned. Torchlight returned with a wet pop in the corridor bracket and laid itself back across the threshold.

Sylvanas did not move.

Above, the blade pulsed again. Nine seconds now, if Jaina was right.

But what hit Sylvanas in the chest was Kalar'shorel'anor.

She knew that farewell.

Millhaven. Andorhal. The Barov trees. Lor'themar's tent. Stratholme.

Five faces.

One farewell.

In the returned torchlight the moments hit one after another: wagon creak at Millhaven, road mud east of Andorhal, evening air by the Barov trees, canvas snapping at Lor'themar's tent, market soot in Stratholme. Same blessing each time. Same hand underneath.

How many had been real?

No answer yet. The better question came first.

What had it failed to hide?

The faces would change. The voice would change. Those were tools. The farewell had survived five masks and a prison visit. Habit, then. Something old enough not to hear itself anymore.

Through the wall, Jaina waited in the dark, hearing only breath, chain, and the resumed pulse above them.

Sylvanas sat on the cot at last. Chain clicked against the frame.

She would have to give Jaina the hook when the corridor quieted. The phrase. Five faces. Same farewell.

She touched two fingers to the cot frame. First the phrase. Then five taps for the faces.

Above her, Frostmourne thudded through the palace stone.

It was still hunting.

So was she.
