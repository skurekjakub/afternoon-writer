# Chapter 7: Andorhal

Andorhal looked worse from inside the gate than it had from the rise.

Distance had made the city into roofs, smoke, and pressure. Up close it resolved into paperwork. Wagons crawled through two inspection lanes under the low arch. Teamsters stood in line with their reins looped loose in one hand and folded permits in the other. A clerk in brown wool sat at a table beside the gatehouse, pressing wax seals with a speed that suggested long practice and no imagination. Grain dust lay over the flagstones in a thin gold film. Hoofbeats struck the stone and came back flat from the walls. Behind it all ran the busy human noise of a place still certain it was feeding the kingdom.

That certainty was the danger.

Sylvanas let her horse drift half a step ahead of Jaina's as they entered the slow turn toward the table. Cyndia held the left rear, closer than she had on the open road. Lyriel kept the right, eyes moving over the wagon queue, the gatehouse slit windows, the idle spears in the hands of men who had not yet understood what they were guarding. None of the guards looked sick. None looked frightened. They looked bored, which was worse. A city already dying should have shown strain. This one had found a way to make corruption part of the day's work.

"Purpose?" the clerk asked without looking up.

Jaina answered before the pause had room to grow. "Quartermaster review and ward inspection. Dalaran correspondence pending local verification."

The man looked up then, taking in the travel dirt, the horses, the elf at her shoulder. His eyes stopped on Sylvanas, returned to the paper, and became more careful.

"Orders?"

Jaina produced the copied authorization Corin had arranged to have waiting on the road if they reached the city intact. She had improved the lie on the ride in, trimming it down until it sounded exactly like the kind of thing a busy office would prefer not to question. The clerk took the page, squinted, found the right seal, and lost interest in the rest. Jaina's voice helped. Calm. Precise. Not the overexplained version she had used on the border. She had learned what human institutions liked hearing from one another and hated to hear from outsiders. A tone could be a key as surely as iron.

The guard nearest the table glanced at Sylvanas's bow. "Elven quartermasters now?"

"Only on the days your roads get interesting," Sylvanas said.

The man opened his mouth, thought better of it, and looked to Jaina for the safer argument. Jaina spared him one cool blink.

"If your city records grain movement by district instead of by source shipment, I'd rather know that before I waste the afternoon asking the wrong office."

That did it. The clerk shoved the paper back across the table and pointed with the butt of his seal press. "Inspection office first building past the square. Mage quarter beyond that. Don't stable in the main market if you're staying overnight. They'll rob you clean."

"What a relief," Sylvanas said. "I was worried Andorhal lacked civic character."

They moved on before the man could decide whether she had insulted him.

Inside, the city pressed closer than the view from the ridge had promised. The street beyond the gate sloped into a market broad enough for three wagon lines and still crowded at the edges with sellers of bread, onions, lamp oil, tack, cured meat, and the thousand other things a working city bought without thinking. House fronts gave way to storehouses almost at once. Men rolled sacks off carts onto shoulder pads and carried them inward at a trot learned from repetition. A woman with flour on both forearms shouted figures through a counting window. Somewhere nearby a baker cursed a late load. Somewhere else a child laughed. No one here behaved like a population living above its own grave.

Sylvanas tracked the shape instead of the life. Where the traffic thickened. Where it pinched. Which alleys would take a body out of sight quickest. Which roofs had line on the square. Which workers watched loads, and which watched people watching loads. The walls were low because Andorhal had never expected war at the gate. The real defenses were routine, speed, and the fact that half the kingdom needed what moved through these streets.

Jaina turned her head once, slightly, taking in the market and the wagon flow with her own different attention. The pressure of the place showed in the set of her mouth. Grain dust had already caught on the dark blue of her cuffs.

"Worse?" Sylvanas asked.

"Broader," Jaina said. "The whole district is carrying it."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the answer I have before I get into the right rooms."

Fair enough.

The inspection office proved to be a narrow stone building wedged between a cooper's yard and an exchange hall whose front shutters stood open to the street. Ledgers moved through the place the way arrows moved through a Farstrider drill: fast, in order, and with little room for interruption. Three clerks looked up when the four of them entered. The one at the back looked as though he had forgotten to sleep for a month.

He was younger than Sylvanas expected. Twenty, perhaps. Human-young enough that the robe still looked borrowed on him. Ink stained two fingers on his left hand. A stack of copied notices leaned in uneven towers around his desk, each marked with district names and dates. He had the look of a man who had been shouting into a storm with proper paperwork and had gotten exactly as far as that deserved.

Jaina gave her name.

The young mage made a sound that belonged to a man finding religion too late to prepare for it. He stood so quickly he struck his knee on the desk.

"Apprentice Corin, lady— Archmage— I mean, not yet Archmage, obviously, but—"

"Corin," Jaina said. "Breathe first. Collapse later."

He obeyed, barely. His eyes flicked to Sylvanas, then to the two rangers at the door, and some of the panic gave way to a fiercer thing. Relief, perhaps, though it arrived in him looking almost identical to fear.

"Antonidas said if the pattern held you'd come," he said. "I thought that was comfort language. I have everything. Or nearly everything. Not everything. Some of it went missing, but I copied most of what went missing before it missed itself."

Sylvanas liked him at once for that sentence alone.

"Show me the part that matters first," she said.

Corin swallowed and cleared a place on the desk by moving two towers of notices onto the floor with no sign that he understood what that said about his life. Then he opened a folder tied with blue cord.

"Distribution irregularities," he said, and now that he was on his ground his voice changed. Still fast, but steadier, built from the work instead of from nerves. "Route marks from Andorhal to the east road, repeated over six weeks. Recorded shortages that aren't shortages, because the city books balance after adjustment, but the adjustments always arrive from above and always after I ask for the underlying counts. Warehouse ward flares in the granary district on nights when no licensed spellwork is logged. I sent three reports to Dalaran and one to the local magistracy. The magistracy sent me a form asking whether rats might explain the grain discrepancies. Dalaran sent me a note thanking me for my diligence."

He said the last line without self-pity. That improved him further.

Jaina bent over the copied route marks. Corin moved with her, laying out sheets in the order his mind preferred. Sylvanas stayed standing. From there she could watch the door, the window, and the way the other clerks kept pretending not to listen.

"You have names?" she asked.

"No reliable ones. Rotations, yes. Faces, some. A few handlers who sign for loads they never touch. Not enough to arrest anybody with."

"Have you seen the mechanism?"

"No." The answer cost him. "Residue. Disturbed wards. Missing stock that reappears in the books with the same total and a different route history. Enough to know something's wrong. Not enough to make anyone useful believe it's wrong in the right direction."

Jaina looked up from the papers. "These ward flares. Show me the pattern."

Corin had already separated those pages. Of course he had. He placed them on top, his hand shaking only once. "They don't match guild work. Too shallow for formal repair, too regular for accident. I thought somebody was using the granary district as a transfer point for unauthorized enchantment. Then the sickness reports started to stack under the grain discrepancies and I stopped believing in separate problems."

"Good," Sylvanas said.

He blinked at her.

"You've wasted your time on the right enemy."

That landed harder than praise would have. His shoulders drew up, then set differently. Less like a clerk braced to apologize, more like a man remembering he had not been mad after all.

Corin got them through the next doors quickly. He knew which porter to bribe with rank language, which senior functionary would rather not meet Jaina's eyes, which store of copied ledgers had been "reorganized" and therefore needed to be accessed through the room behind it. The institution turned tangible around him: keys, seals, drawers, shelf codes, signatures, stamped notices. Dalaran's higher machinery might have swallowed his work whole. On the ground, the boy himself was excellent.

By the time they left the exchange office the sun had moved across the square and the market noise had shifted toward the harsher rhythm of midday loading. Corin had given them route books, flare records, two names worth watching later, and one better thing than names: a place in the city where Jaina could work without clerks breathing through the door.

"There's a storeroom above the old assay chamber," he said as he led them back into the street. "Nobody uses it because the roof leaks and because the leak only happens when it rains from the east, which everyone treats as a personal insult from the weather rather than a maintenance issue. The residue samples are there. I moved them after the second set went missing from the official archive."

"Sensibly criminal of you," Sylvanas said.

His mouth twitched. There it was: the first proof of a sense of humor surviving inside the paperwork.

They found Havel in the granary district itself, because that was where a man with his face would live even if his office stood somewhere cleaner.

Captain Aldric Havel wore militia leather powdered white with the same grain dust coating every beam and lintel in the district. He had a nose that had been broken at least once and a manner that suggested he expected every conversation to begin with somebody else asking why he had not solved a problem above his pay. When Corin brought them in through the side yard of a counting house, Havel did not salute. He looked Sylvanas up and down, looked at Jaina's cuffs, looked at Cyndia and Lyriel taking the corners without orders, and decided to skip straight to usefulness.

"You the ones asking why my workers keep dying in clusters no physician can agree on?" he asked.

"Among other things," Sylvanas said.

"Good. I was afraid you were tax people."

He took them up to a narrow office overlooking three loading yards and the alley between them. From the window the district showed its bones. Grain moving in and out. Lift crews on rope hoists. Book men at tables. Sweepers. Cartwrights. Porters. Enough legitimate labor to hide a war inside if the war understood patience.

Havel knew the district by habit, which made him valuable immediately. He named streets without checking. Named foremen. Named which crews drank too much, which ones stole, which ones fought, and which ones did none of those things and therefore worried him more. He had no case fit for a court. He had pattern. A tall laborer with a limp who never touched the stock but always knew when inspectors changed route. Two brothers on the west yard who worked sober, clean, and never once took wages to the taverns with the others. Night crews that grew more regular after a new supplier line opened three months ago. Sickness clustering around the same men who spent least time near the public wagons and most time near repack tables under covered awnings.

"You've been watching them like suspects," Sylvanas said.

"I have suspects," Havel said. "What I don't have is proof that survives daylight."

He said it without bitterness. He had already spent that.

"Three months?" Jaina asked.

"Near enough. New route pressures before that, but three months since I started seeing men in ordinary aprons stand like drilled soldiers whenever anybody shouted wrong in the yard."

That matched too neatly with the road tightening north of here. Confirmation, not revelation. Good. The chapter needed proof that the line held. Not a fresh theory every ten miles.

Havel gave them lists. Shift changes. Yard assignments. Which storehouses had quiet hours that made no commercial sense. Which ledgers never slept where they were supposed to sleep. Sylvanas listened with one eye on the yard below, where men moved sacks in steady relays across planks. Most looked at the load. Some looked at each other. Eight, she thought after the first ten minutes, then revised to at least eight. Too many bodies carrying themselves like laborers who expected knives.

Cyndia appeared in the doorway without sound. "Tall one with the limp just checked the alley mouth instead of the incoming team. Third time in ten minutes."

Havel's jaw set. "That's Teren. Hired on as dock transfer six months ago. Never drank, never courted, never once took a day to complain."

"How inconsiderate of him," Sylvanas said.

Lyriel came in behind Cyndia. "North yard pair trading marks with the counting window. Left hand to sleeve, right hand low. Not local street talk."

Havel turned toward the window too fast to disguise how much he needed to see it with his own eyes. When he did, whatever last part of him had feared he might be inventing the whole thing left his face.

Good. Two ignored witnesses, one built of ledgers and one of dust, now standing inside the same answer.

The decision to split the work arrived without ceremony because the city had already written it for them.

Corin had the mage-side rooms. Havel had the yards. Jaina could read the contamination where it lived in residue, ward flare, sample cloth, and treated grain. Sylvanas could read the men who guarded those things and the routes that joined them. Keeping both halves together any longer would not be caution. It would be drag.

They stepped out into the alley behind Havel's office. Noise from the loading yard rolled over the wall in steady bursts: shouted counts, rope creak, a wagon bed slamming once against its brace. Corin held two brass keys and three sheets of copied flare records. Havel had already started sketching shift times on the back of a spoiled manifest. Cyndia and Lyriel waited with the horses in the strip of shade beside the wall, watching opposite directions.

Jaina looked at the keys in Corin's hand, then at the district beyond Sylvanas's shoulder. She didn't ask permission. Good again.

"The residue room first," she said. "Then the repack floor if he can get me through without announcing it to the entire quarter."

Corin nodded too quickly. "I can."

Sylvanas took the spoiled manifest from Havel, scanned the shift blocks, and handed it back. "You take the north and west lanes with me. We watch the reliefs, the hands, who leaves sober, who leaves in pairs, and who checks exits before they check loads."

Jaina's gaze touched hers briefly. No softness in it. No need for any. Only the clean exchange of one lead taking one half and the other taking the other.

"If you find the floor treatment before I come back," Sylvanas said, "I want the useful part first."

"You usually do."

"And if your useful part turns out to be 'the whole building is wrong'?"

"Then I'll do better than that."

That was enough.

They split.

The granary district gave up its people more readily than its rooms.

Sylvanas spent the next hours moving through the seams of it with Havel at one shoulder and the rangers stretched where sightlines demanded. Cyndia drifted along the outer lane, left hand near the bow grip, attention cutting across rooflines and alley mouths. Lyriel took the tighter right, quicker, more visible when visibility was the point. Havel played local authority for them and did it well. He knew when to nod at a foreman, when to stop and ask about missing tally chalk, when to complain loudly enough that workers lowered their eyes and returned to their motions.

From inside that ordinary patrol the wrong men began to stand out.

One never wiped sweat from his face because he never glistened, not even in the heat trapped between the storehouses. Another kept his gloves on while handling rough sackcloth that made every other porter strip down to skin. Two more left the break alley separately and reached the far corner together with the timing of men who had practiced never to meet in public. A clerk in a plain vest signed three separate loads without once glancing at the line of wagons attached to them. Teren of the limp checked exits at each shift turn. Another man near the west wall counted heads each time the yard bell struck, though headcount was no part of his station.

Eight, yes. And organized.

Sylvanas marked them by behavior instead of by face. Faces shifted too easily in a city. Habits took longer. Hidden route marks appeared where honest books went crooked: chalk scuffs on post bases, a nick on a loading hook, twine tied twice around a latch instead of once. Havel showed her an outbuilding used for damaged sacks. Inside, behind a false stack of spoiled barley, she found oilcloth bundles holding ledger scraps, preserved powders, and two small vials wrapped in felt. Not enough to explain the whole mechanism. Enough to prove the district kept its own mouth clean and armed.

"They've got caches under civilian cover," Havel said quietly.

"No," Sylvanas said. "They are civilian cover."

That chilled him more than the vials had.

By dusk she had the bones of the cell. At least eight working assets. Two layers. Yard hands and clerks below, handlers above. Supplies moving off-ledger through spoilage rooms and repack tables. Quiet discipline in the men who abstained from taverns after shift because drunk laborers talked and these ones could not afford to. One thing still missing: the top voice. Whoever sat above the local pattern had not shown themselves where a militia captain could find them.

The room Corin had found for Jaina lay above an assay chamber that smelled of old stone, damp plaster, and the sharper edge of disturbed reagents. Rain had not come from the east today, so the leak had spared the floor. Corin had laid out the residue samples on a trestle table under three shuttered lamps. Jaina stood at the center of them with her sleeves rolled and grain dust up both arms. Blue light moved and died between her fingers in brief, controlled passes. She looked colder than she had at noon, which usually meant she was close to an answer.

Sylvanas stopped in the doorway and took in the scene before she spoke. Corin's copied notes sat open under stone weights. A square of treated cloth lay pinned by knives at the corners. One tray held sifted grain from three different yards. Another held scrapings from repack boards and the lip of a mixing basin that should have carried nothing but flour. Jaina had built the city down to objects. Good work. Off her sightline, and good work.

Corin saw her first and straightened from where he had been holding one lamp high to catch residue sheen on a board sample.

"Don't say anything for one breath," Jaina said.

Sylvanas counted one in silence.

Then Jaina stepped back from the table.

"They're treating the grain here," she said. "Not at the farms, not on the road. Here, after milling, during repack."

"How?"

"A preserving shell laid into the outer dust of the grain. Necromantic, precise, and built to survive handling that should ruin a simpler work. Moisture carries it deeper. Milling spreads it. Baking doesn't kill it because the shell isn't the whole design. It only needs to survive long enough to enter the body. After that the body does the expensive part for them."

Corin looked like a man standing near thunder and trying not to grin.

Jaina reached for the pinned cloth and held it up. Grain dust shimmered in the lamplight.

"This came from the repack floor beams," she said. "The strongest signature sits in the residue no one bothers to sweep properly at height. They dust the loads after weighing. Light enough to hide in ordinary handling loss, regular enough to make every sack that passes through here part of the same system."

"And the dead?" Sylvanas asked.

"Still the body's work after. Still repeated exposure. Still the host finishing what the grain begins. That part hasn't changed." Her mouth tightened, thinking faster than her words. "But the sophistication is worse than I thought. This isn't contamination improvised by zealots. It's built like serious research. Layered. Controlled. Somebody understood exactly how much of the shell to leave detectable and how much to bury in the process."

"Useful."

Jaina's eyes flicked up. Tired, annoyed, and bright with the one thing she got when the puzzle finally stopped dodging. Not triumph. Clarity.

"You wanted the useful part first."

"This qualifies."

Corin made an involuntary sound of agreement and then looked embarrassed by his own existence.

"What about the body trigger?" Sylvanas asked.

"Not here." Jaina shook her head. "This is carrier-side. Cleanly enough that I'd trust it in the field. The host-side lock still needs something we haven't reached. But Andorhal gives us the packaging method, and it gives it to us hard. Whoever runs this place isn't smearing rot into grain and praying. They're dosing a kingdom."

There it was. An answer she had earned out of sight, handed back in language that did not ask Sylvanas to admire it, only to act on it.

So she acted.

"At least eight embedded in the district," Sylvanas said. "Two-tier structure. Clerks and labor below, handlers above. Off-ledger caches in spoilage rooms. Shift discipline tight enough that Havel clocked it months ago without being able to name why. They check exits before stock. They don't drink after work. They count each other."

Jaina set the cloth down slowly.

"One machine," she said.

"One machine," Sylvanas agreed.

They laid it together on the trestle table after that, her yard map over Jaina's residue sheets, Corin's copied flare records crossing Havel's shift blocks, route marks pinned under knives at the corners. The city changed shape under their hands. Not adjacent leads. One system. Mage-side treatment riding human-side discipline. Grain dust on Jaina's cuffs, loading-yard chalk on Sylvanas's glove, both halves of the same city's mouth.

Corin hovered on the edge of the table, adding dates when asked and silence when not. Havel arrived midway through the assembly and went still when he saw Jaina's residue board aligned with his yard sketch. He had come prepared to report more suspicion. He found structure instead.

"Those rooms on the west repack line," he said, tapping one marked square. "If that's where the treatment's happening, the night crew changes there at second bell. Short overlap. Enough confusion to take someone."

"Take?" Corin echoed.

"A live one," Sylvanas said. "If we keep chasing residue and route marks, we learn what this layer already knows. If we pull a living operative out tonight, we may get the next tier before the district realizes it's been opened."

Jaina's finger rested on the flare pattern sheet. "I still want the alchemical trail preserved. If they scrub the boards before dawn, we lose some of the proof."

"And if we wait for perfect proof, the men who can erase themselves walk home and sleep."

"I didn't say wait until dawn."

"No," Sylvanas said. "You said preserve the trail first."

The air in the room tightened. Not because either of them was wrong. Because each had reached the same machine from a different door and hated leaving any piece of it untouched.

Havel looked between them with the expression of a man who had just realized the city's fate might depend on whether two frightening women preferred one kind of right answer over another.

Corin, to his credit, kept breathing.

Jaina planted both hands on the table and thought once, hard enough that Sylvanas could see the stillness arrive.

"Fine," she said. "We do both. Capture first, but I mark the room beforehand. If they torch or scour anything after, I'll know what changed. And if the prisoner talks nonsense, the residue work lets me test what part of the nonsense is built to mislead."

Better.

"Cyndia and Lyriel hold the outer line," Sylvanas said immediately. "Havel gives us the yard timing and local exits. Corin keeps the city-side access quiet and closes the mage doors behind us. Jaina reads whatever the prisoner gives us against the mechanism. I take the body."

"You make everything sound charming," Jaina said.

"It's a gift."

That got the faintest shift at the corner of her mouth. Gone at once. Enough.

They built the capture plan over candlelight and chalk. Fallback routes through the spoilage yard, through a cooper's lane, over a drainage wall if the district alarmed. Which door Havel could open without a key. Which clerk would notice a missing handler first. Where Corin's authority stopped being useful and started being suspicious. Cyndia and Lyriel came in wet-eyed from the grain dust and took their instructions without comment. Cyndia absorbed the outer routes in one look. Lyriel asked only where the second body should fall if the first body did not cooperate.

Useful women. All of them.

By the time the plan held, the city outside had quieted into its night shape. Not silence. Wagon noise gone, tavern noise up, a different economy taking over the streets. Through the wall came the scratch of Corin recopying names into a hand steadier than the one he had started the day with. Havel had gone to place the right men where their absence would not show. Cyndia and Lyriel were walking the perimeter turns outside. Jaina had taken the next room to rework her sample notes into something she could use at speed if the night went wrong.

Sylvanas remained at the trestle table with the chalk map and the cooling candle stubs.

One shared job, she thought, and disliked the phrase for sounding too soft. Still true.

The city would not have opened for her alone. She could have read the yards, the exits, the discipline, the men who held themselves wrong for labor. She could not have reached the repack shell hidden in dust on beams and in the lip of a basin no clerk had thought worth cleaning. Jaina had done that cleanly, out of sight, and brought back an answer fit to move on. No haze left in it. No need to stand over her shoulder and make it smaller.

From the next room came the dry rasp of a page turned, then the brief pause that meant Jaina was weighing one set of notes against another. No uncertainty in the sound. Work. Only work.

Good.

Sylvanas shifted the chalk marker between her fingers and looked down at the capture route one last time. North yard, west repack, spill room, alley, wall. Simple enough to fail honestly if it failed at all.

She did not bother naming what had changed. Naming invited ceremony. Ceremony invited lies.

The truth was smaller and harder than that.

Take Jaina out of this city tonight and half the answer vanished with her. Leave her where she was and the machine held. That was all. No sentiment in it. No softness. Only the shape of the work, and the fact that the shape now required two people who trusted each other's unseen hands.

Outside, boots turned on the stones below the window. Cyndia on one pass, Lyriel on the next, the perimeter keeping its own time. In the room beyond, Jaina's quill scratched, stopped, scratched again.

Sleep could wait. The capture could not.

Sylvanas rose, took the map, and went to wake the night.
