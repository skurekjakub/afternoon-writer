# Chapter 18: The Wall

The stone had gone warm under Jaina's palms hours ago. It held the heat still, even now, though the light had begun to thin and the smoke had changed color twice since Arthas went below.

Stratholme ought to have been louder than this.

Twenty-five thousand people, market bells, cart wheels, chapel bells, dogs, arguments in alleys, the stupid thousand-piece hum a city made simply by being alive. A city this large should have pushed sound upward in a constant body. Instead the noise came in cuts. A roof beam giving way. A captain's voice carrying three streets and stopping. Hooves on stone. One scream, thin and sudden, then nothing where whole neighborhoods ought to have been.

Below her, the fires worked district by district.

No wild sheet of fire. Separate burns. One line taking a row of houses. Another tightening around a square. Another chewing through a warehouse block while teams moved ahead of it, behind it, around it. The city had been cut into tasks and was being finished as tasks.

Her fingers tightened on the parapet.

Soot had gathered in the cracks of the stone. It blackened the lines of her skin. Flour still sat there too, pale in the webbing of her hand, stubborn as memory. She could feel both when she shifted her grip: grit and powder, warehouse and wall, the useless proof of how little she had actually changed.

Smoke blew across the inner wall and brought the whole city with it. Burning timber. Wet stone heated too fast. Bread. Gods. Bread still. Burned now, charred and bitter, but under it the same wrong sweetness that had ridden out of Andorhal in sacks and carts and ordinary kitchens, patient enough to wait for ovens and hands and hunger to do half the work for it.

It coated the back of her throat. More than smell. The thing itself. The residue left after too many bodies had answered at once.

She had never felt anything like it.

Villages had given her clusters. Houses. Streets, sometimes. Millhaven had been a knot. Andorhal had been a throat. Even Hearthglen, from a distance and through secondhand reports, had still been a defended point on a map. Stratholme was scale she had only theorized. The contamination had passed so far through stock, ovens, blood, and death that it had changed the air above the city. The answer no longer lived in stores and sacks. It lingered in the burned-out shape of what came after.

She closed her eyes for one breath. Two.

The pattern stayed.

No neat theorem. Nothing neat remained in it. A drag in the air. A spent pull, as though some vast hand had clenched and unclenched beneath the streets and left every stone remembering pressure. The grain signature, loud once, had burned down to ash. The deeper thing had shown itself only at the moment it no longer mattered for Stratholme.

Her mind kept working anyway.

It was the old failure in her, the old faith that if she followed the thought hard enough it would eventually become useful, eventually become enough. She hated it. She could no more stop it than she could stop hearing the occasional crack of a collapsing roof.

At her left, Sylvanas had not moved for long enough that the ranger's stillness had become another piece of the wall.

Jaina did not look at her. She could read the shape without turning. One hand resting light on the parapet. Weight balanced on both feet. Bow unstrung now, because there was no target worth an arrow from here and because anyone still alive below was beyond saving by either woman. Smoke in the leather. Smoke in their hair. Smoke in the folds of Jaina's sleeves every time the wind shifted.

The silence between them held.

Below, men crossed Market Row in ordered files.

The sight of them pulled her eye south again, then inward, then backward years in one sick, efficient motion.

Arthas's hands.

She had not seen them well at first from this height. Only pale. Bare. Then the angle changed and the braziers at the gate took them full, skin clean to the wrists. Too clean. He had found water. He had made time. He had come out of a murdered city carrying no blood on him at all.

The image would not let go.

It slid under every other memory and found the older ones that had always sat there waiting for a shape to fit them. His hand on a map board, flattening argument into route. His hand straightening a circlet in polished metal once, years ago, more annoyed by how it sat than by the people waiting on the answer it represented. His hand turning, palm up, when he wanted agreement to fall into it as though assent were simply the natural next motion of the room.

She had seen the speed in him before.

The speed had even been beautiful once, because she had been young enough to mistake decisiveness for strength and because he had known how to make certainty look generous. He listened quickly. He smiled quickly. He decided quickly. For a few months she had thought his speed might be what her own mind lacked.

Then Alterac.

Hardly a true crisis. Farmland under dispute, a lord with papers, families with no papers, a season that would not wait for legal correctness to become justice. Arthas had heard the case, found the clean line, and stepped onto it before the mud had finished drying on the claim. Correct enough to defend. Fast enough to ruin thirty families. She had watched him do it and felt something in herself go cold long before she found words for why.

Now a whole city burned below the same habit.

She had been right then. Right at Strahnbrad. Right at the gate yesterday. Right in every direction that had mattered.

Useless every time.

Heat touched her face from a district below the wall. Not enough to warm. Enough to remind the skin it was there.

Her body caught the residue again, that wrong aftertaste beneath smoke, and the thought turned without asking permission.

The countermeasure had not failed.

It had struck where the thing entered the world and proved itself there, in warehouses and holding yards and ruined stock collapsing into stinking spoil beneath her hands. Yet the city had still answered. Too wide. Too deep. Too many kitchens. Too many bodies already carrying the lesson. The grain was real. The grain was deadly. The grain was also only the part of the mechanism that ordinary eyes could follow.

Somewhere below, a horse screamed.

It stopped fast.

She opened her eyes and stared down at the smoke until the city blurred at the edges.

Kel'Thuzad had stripped the moral line out of the work in the name of understanding. Arthas had stripped the human line out of command in the name of duty. The names differed. The justifications differed. The movement beneath them did not. Both had found the clean answer and followed it past the point where another person could still slow them.

And she—

Jaina's nails bit stone.

She had stood in rooms too long because the answer was there. She had pushed one thought farther because she could not bear to leave it half-built. She had told herself, again and again, that understanding was action in an earlier form, that the distance between the two could be crossed later, after one more proof, one more layer, one more finished line on the page.

The city below offered the argument against that faith in separate fires.

She did not belong in the same moral company as either of them. The thought itself was obscene. Kel'Thuzad had built a plague. Arthas had culled a city. Yet the fear remained because it lived lower than morality, in habit, in the shape her mind made when it found a line to follow. The engine. That was the part that frightened her. Not likeness. Capacity.

Bread-taste wrongness still sat in her throat.

She swallowed against it and got nothing for the effort.

At some point the bells had stopped. She could not remember when. Their absence had become part of the silence so completely that imagining them still felt impossible.

The wind turned again. Smoke rolled over the parapet. Sparks lifted and died.

Sylvanas moved.

Only that. A shift of leather. A boot coming a half-step closer on the stone. Then the weight of a hand on Jaina's left shoulder, firm through smoke-stiff cloth and travel grime.

No gentling in it. No pity either.

Direction.

"South," Sylvanas said.

Her voice had gone rough with smoke. It suited the word.

"We go south."

Jaina stood one beat longer.

Not from refusal. Her body had simply forgotten, for a little while, that movement remained possible.

The hand stayed where it was.

That was what made the turning happen. Not comfort. Not argument. Weight. A line from shoulder to spine to boots to stone. Something outside herself taking hold of the fact that bodies could still be made to choose one direction over another.

She pushed off the parapet.

Heat lived in the stone even as she took her hands from it. It clung to her palms for the first two steps and was gone by the third.

The stair waited where it had waited all day, cut into the inner wall, worn shallow at the center by generations of watch-boots and now streaked with soot.

Sylvanas took it first.

Jaina found that merciful at once. A pale braid to follow. Boots on stone. One hand skimming the wall when the stair narrowed at the turn. Jaina watched those things and kept descending.

Below them, Cyndia was already in motion.

The elf had found the strip of yard at the base of the stair where the horses could stand clear of the worst crush and had turned it into a working pocket of order by sheer refusal to do anything else. Three mounts. Water skins uncapped. Tack rechecked. One strap tightened under a gray mare's belly even as Cyndia looked up, counted them, and moved on to the next necessary thing without asking whether either woman was ready to speak.

The practical mercy of it almost undid Jaina more than the wall had.

She hit the last step harder than she meant to. Her knees felt the jolt clear through the saddle soreness she had been ignoring since dawn. Cyndia held out a skin. Jaina took it. The water tasted of leather and iron and dust. She drank anyway.

Sylvanas was already at her own horse.

In the old days—no, not old. Two weeks. Ten days. Time had become nonsense since Scholomance—there would have been a fractional hitch here. A check. A look. Some tiny beat in which one of them waited to see whether the other still meant the same thing she had meant a moment before.

No hitch came.

Sylvanas checked the girth once, put a boot in the stirrup, and mounted.

Cyndia swung up as well, left-handed and neat despite the long day.

Jaina reached for her own pommel and found herself doing the same before she had finished noticing it. Boot, stirrup, saddle, gather reins. Her body took the center-left weight it knew from the run into Stratholme. Cyndia drifted forward on the other side. Sylvanas settled at point.

The line remade itself.

No one said anything about it.

That, more than the silence on the wall, told Jaina what had changed.

They rode out through the northern lanes and onto the south road under a sky still stained by Stratholme's smoke. The city fell behind them slowly, because a place that large could not leave the eye all at once. First the inner wall. Then the higher roofs. Then only the dark smear above the horizon and the separate plumes where fire still found work.

Road took over.

Hooves on packed earth. Leather creak. The pull in Jaina's thighs each time her mare adjusted to the ruts. Smoke in her clothes turning colder now that it was no longer backed by the heat of the city itself. Her hands stung when the wind found the flour-raw skin along her knuckles.

South.

For the body, south was simple. Out from the wall. Down the road. Away from the fire.

For her mind, nothing had become simpler at all.

She kept seeing the city now as the proof it had given her too late. The wrongness on the air had changed with distance; the bread-tainted drag weakened as the miles opened between her and the walls. Yet the pattern behind it held sharp. If the grain alone had been the whole engine, the city should have read like saturation and then fade. Instead it had answered to a note struck elsewhere, each body carrying enough of the prepared work to respond when distance called it.

Grain carried it in.

Bodies learned it.

Death completed it.

A farther hand woke it.

She took one hand from the reins long enough to drag her notebook from the saddlebag. The mare twitched an ear back at the motion and kept on. Jaina braced the book against her thigh and made herself write despite the road's jarring.

outer shell in grain

host carries deeper pattern

city scale = answer, not source

relay?

The last word sat there and offended her with its thinness.

She scratched it out once so hard the nib tore the page. Wrote again beneath it.

broadcaster

That was worse in a different direction. Too clean. Too clever. Too far from the smell of bread and blood and smoke.

She closed the book without solving the word.

Ahead, Sylvanas lifted one hand from the reins and touched two fingers to the pouch at her belt.

"Stone," she said.

Jaina stared at her. Understanding caught up a beat later.

Of course. Antonidas.

The sending-stone had ridden in her saddlebag all day, an accusation she had not yet had room to answer. She drew it out now. Even after hours against her notes and spare wrappings, the carved surface held a little warmth, as if it had been waiting in a hand instead of in leather.

She thumbed the activation groove.

Blue light rose soft between her fingers, wavered once with the motion of the horse, then steadied.

Antonidas came through in fragments first. Breath. Static. A voice already half-shaped around fear and discipline.

"Jaina."

She had meant to answer cleanly. Instead what came out first was, "I'm here."

Too fast. Too young. She hated herself for it at once.

The line hissed, then cleared enough for his next breath to arrive.

"Word reached us from Stratholme by three mouths and none of them matched. Are you hurt?"

The question should have been easy.

She looked down at her hand on the reins. Flour in the skin seams. Soot at the nail beds. A small burn along the thumb where warehouse magic had flashed back through rotten stock. All of it suddenly ridiculous beside the city on the horizon behind them.

"Alive," she said. Her throat rasped on it. "Tired. Nothing I can't sit through."

Silence. Not absence. Him thinking in it.

Around her, the road kept moving. Cyndia had fallen a little wider to give her space without breaking the formation. Sylvanas did not turn in the saddle, but one ear stayed angled back.

"I can put an extraction team on the road before nightfall," Antonidas said. The formal register had returned, which meant his fear had gone colder, not smaller. "Southshore first. Dalaran from there. If you name the road, I'll clear it."

The offer landed with all the weight of home.

Warm rooms. Lamps. Ink that did not shake on the page. Shelves. Her own bed in the apprentice quarters, too narrow and too short, yet still a bed that belonged to a life in which cities did not end this way. Antonidas across a worktable, asking the next precise question, making the horror fit into language that could be carried.

She could not bear it.

Not because she wanted none of it. Because wanting it all at once was so violent it nearly folded her in half over the saddle.

Smoke still marked the north behind them. Ahead, the road bent through thinning trees toward land that knew nothing yet of what had happened on the wall.

"Not yet," she said.

The words surprised even her with how flat they were.

The stone stayed warm in her palm. Antonidas made a sound too quiet to be a word.

"Jaina."

It held warning this time. Plea too. And the old authority, though weakened by distance and by the simple fact that she was no longer in any room where his rank could physically alter what came next.

She looked past Sylvanas's shoulder to the road.

"The city proved it," she said. "I need—"

No. Not that. Need was too clean for it.

"The grain isn't enough to explain Stratholme."

She heard herself slipping toward lecture and forced the line back down before it could lose the body entirely.

"It carried the plague in. We knew that. But the city answered all at once, and it answered after the stock was already moving out of our hands. There is a hand farther up the chain. It reaches across distance and wakes what the grain has already taught the body to hold."

The horse stumbled in a rut. She caught herself with knees and one hard pull on the reins.

Antonidas said nothing for three breaths.

When he spoke again, the softness in his voice was worse than command would have been.

"Come home with the answer you have."

She shut her eyes once.

For one hideous instant she nearly said yes.

Then the wall came back under her palms. Arthas below it with clean hands. Market Row in ordered fires. The residue dragging at the back of her throat. The city-scale answer arriving exactly one chapter too late.

"I can't," she said.

Not won't. Could not. The distinction mattered to her if to no one else.

"Jaina—"

"I'm alive," she said, harsher now because the first answer had not been enough and because she needed a harder thing than grief to hold the line in place. "I'm moving. That's what I can give you tonight."

Another silence.

Then, quieter, "Moving where?"

South, the road said beneath her.

North, said everything else.

She looked again at the smoke behind them, flattened now by distance into a bruise against the sky.

"For now?" she said. "South."

Sylvanas's head turned a fraction at that. Not toward her. Toward the word.

Antonidas let the answer stand, which was the closest thing to mercy he could manage.

"Keep the stone on you."

"I will."

"Write while you can. Even scraps."

That almost made her laugh. The sound rose, found nothing in itself worth becoming, and died.

"I have been."

The line held a moment longer. He was still there, wanting ten more questions and understanding before he asked even the first that none of them would help. When the light went out, it did so gently. The warmth stayed in the stone a second longer than it should have.

She tucked it away.

Nobody spoke.

The road widened, then narrowed again where carts had chewed its edges into mud. Crows lifted from a hedgerow at their passing. The wind changed by increments. Smoke thinned. Under it came a colder trace from the north-east, faint yet unmistakable once it reached her: salt somewhere far ahead, water beyond the last rise of land.

Open coast.

Her notebook was still in her lap. She opened it again and this time did not chase the word. She wrote only what the body knew.

grain carries

body keeps

distance wakes

Kel'Thuzad dead

system still answers

The mare's stride jostled the letters crooked. She let them stand.

A rider came out of the dusk behind them at a run.

Cyndia heard her first. Jaina knew that by the way the elf's shoulders changed before any sound reached human ears. Then came the bird-call — short, sharp, Farstrider clear — and the answering call from farther ahead where some advance scout had hidden in the last line of trees.

Friendly.

Urgent anyway.

The relay horse hit the road behind them hard enough that mud sprayed from its chest and flanks. Foam flecked the bit. The rider herself looked scarcely steadier than the animal beneath her, braid half out, one glove missing, dispatch tube already in hand before she pulled alongside.

"Ranger-General."

Sylvanas reined in. The rest of them did the same.

The rider passed the tube across. Sylvanas broke the seal with her thumb and read in the last usable stripe of light.

Jaina watched the ranger's face because the habit had become automatic: information first arrived there in its effects.

The effect this time was small and bad.

Her mouth thinned. Nothing more. Enough.

"Say it," Cyndia said from the far side.

Sylvanas did not look up from the page.

"Arthas has taken ships on the northern coast." Her voice stayed level. "Bound for Northrend."

The word went through Jaina straight and cold.

Not surprise. The shape had already been there in the road, in the model, in the unfinished logic of the plague. Yet hearing the place named turned pressure into geography all at once.

Northrend.

Ice. Open sea. The far source of too many things.

Sylvanas read the next line.

"Uther rides west for Capital City."

Another.

"The force going with Arthas is selected. Men who stayed with him at Stratholme."

No one answered.

The relay rider shifted in the saddle, not fidgeting, simply trying not to fall out of it. Her horse stood with its head down and sides heaving.

Jaina looked from the dispatch tube to the darkening road ahead, then down to her notebook, still open on her thigh.

grain carries

body keeps

distance wakes

Kel'Thuzad dead

system still answers

North.

She did not write the word. There was no room for it on the page now that it had moved into the world.

Sylvanas rolled the dispatch once and handed it across to Cyndia. "Camp another mile south. Rotate the relay horse out. She eats before she reports again."

"Yes, Ranger-General."

Practicality took the rider from them. Cyndia moving. The relay scout blinking once as if she had not expected to survive the message into ordinary treatment. Horse led aside. Waterskin. Hands on tack. The necessary small salvations that kept larger decisions from happening in a void.

South remained under their horses.

North took everything else.

They rode on as the light finished going.

Evening put the road into the body by force. The drop in temperature where sweat began to cool under wool. The ache settling deeper into the knees with each mile. The saddle leather growing harder, not softer, as exhaustion stripped the padding of ordinary thought away. Somewhere behind them Stratholme still burned. They could no longer see individual plumes, only the stain it made against the horizon.

Jaina kept waiting for someone to speak the obvious thing.

No one did.

Sylvanas rode at point as she had since the wall. Back straight. Hands quiet on the reins. Once, only once, she turned her head toward the north-east where the wind carried that colder trace of sea.

The motion reached Jaina as surely as touch.

The sending-stone, tucked back into her saddlebag, had not fully lost its warmth.

Home on one side of leather. The notebook on the other. Between them, road and horse sweat and the rhythm of travel that would have meant safety in any saner chapter.

South for now.

North where the prince had gone.

North where the unanswered note in the plague pointed now with a narrow, terrible certainty.

North where the next thing waited, whether it was source or trap or both.

Hooves beat the road into a steady pulse under them. Evening gathered in the ditches. Ahead, somewhere out in the dark, a campfire would need to be built, horses rubbed down, watches set, food forced down by people too tired to want it.

Jaina rode toward all of that and could not stop hearing the other direction.

No one said the word.

By the time full dark took the road, it no longer needed saying.
