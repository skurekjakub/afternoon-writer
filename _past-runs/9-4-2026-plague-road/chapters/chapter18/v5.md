# Chapter 18: The Wall

The stone had gone warm under Jaina's palms hours ago. It still held the heat, though the light had thinned and the smoke had changed color twice since Arthas went below.

Stratholme ought to have been louder.

A city this size should have thrown up market bells, cart wheels, Alonsus Chapel bells, dogs, alley arguments, the stupid thousand-piece hum of being alive. Instead the noise came in cuts. A roof beam giving way. A captain's voice carrying three streets and stopping. Hooves on stone. One scream, thin and sudden, then nothing where whole neighborhoods ought to have been.

Below her, fire ran district by district. One line taking a row of houses. Another tightening around a square. Another chewing through a warehouse block while teams moved ahead of it, behind it, around it. The city had been cut into sections and finished one at a time.

Jaina tightened her grip on the parapet.

Soot had gathered in the cracks of the stone. It blackened the lines of her skin. Flour still sat pale in the webbing of her hand, stubborn as memory. Grit and powder. Warehouse and wall.

Smoke blew across the inner wall and brought the city with it. Burning timber. Wet stone heated too fast. Bread. Gods, bread still. Burned now, charred and bitter, but under it the same wrong sweetness from Andorhal grain, sacks, carts, ordinary kitchens, lying dormant until ovens and hands and hunger did half the work.

It coated the back of her throat. Not just smell. Residue, left after too many bodies had answered at once.

Villages had given her clusters. Houses. Streets, sometimes. Millhaven had been a knot. Andorhal had been a throat. Even Hearthglen, heard through other mouths, had still been a defended point on a map. Above Stratholme the air itself had changed. Here the grain had stopped being enough.

She closed her eyes for two breaths.

The pattern stayed.

Only a drag in the air remained. The grain signature, loud once, had burned down to ash. Jaina could read the deeper shape only after Stratholme was beyond saving.

That was the old failure in her, the faith that if she followed the thought hard enough it would become useful. She hated it.

At her left, Sylvanas had not moved for so long that the ranger's stillness had become another part of the wall. Jaina did not look at her. She could read the shape without turning: one hand on the parapet, weight balanced on both feet, bow unstrung because there was no target worth an arrow from here.

Below, men crossed Market Row in ordered files.

She followed them south again, then inward, then backward years in one sick motion.

Arthas's hands.

From this height they had first read as pale. Bare. Then the angle changed and the braziers at the gate caught them full, skin clean to the wrists. Too clean. He had found water. He had made time. He had come out of a murdered city carrying no blood on him at all.

She kept seeing it, and below that older memories: his hand flattening an argument into route on a map board, his hand turning palm-up when he wanted assent to fall into it as though agreement were simply the next motion of the room.

The speed had even been beautiful once. She had been young enough to mistake decisiveness for strength.

Then Alterac.

Farmland under dispute. A lord with papers, families with none, a season that would not wait. Arthas had found the clean line and stepped onto it before the mud finished drying on the claim. Correct enough to defend. Fast enough to ruin thirty families.

Now a whole city burned below the same habit.

She had been right then. Right at Strahnbrad. Right at the gate yesterday.

Useless every time.

The wrong bread-taste caught her again, and she turned back to the thought before she could stop herself.

The countermeasure had landed where the thing entered the world. It had worked there, in warehouses and holding yards and ruined stock collapsing into stinking spoil beneath her hands. Yet bodies across the city had still answered. Too wide. Too deep. Too many kitchens. Too many bodies already carrying the lesson. The grain was real. The grain was deadly. It was only the layer ordinary eyes could follow.

Somewhere below, a horse screamed.

It stopped fast.

She opened her eyes and stared into the smoke until the city blurred at the edges.

Kel'Thuzad had stripped the moral line out of the work in the name of understanding. Arthas had stripped the human line out of command in the name of duty. Different names. Different justifications. Both had found the clean answer and kept going after the people beside them tried to slow them.

And she—

Jaina dug her nails into the stone.

She had stood in rooms too long because the answer was there. She had pushed one thought farther because she could not bear to leave it half-built. She had told herself understanding was only action in an earlier form. Below her, the separate fires made that faith impossible.

She did not belong in the same moral company as either of them. The thought itself was obscene. Kel'Thuzad had built a plague. Arthas had culled a city. Yet the fear remained because it lived lower than morality, in habit, in the shape her mind took when it found a line to follow. The fear was simpler: she could keep chasing the answer after the bodies in front of her should have stopped her.

Bread-taste wrongness sat in her throat. Swallowing did nothing.

At some point the Alonsus Chapel bells had stopped. She could not remember when. Their absence had become part of the silence so completely that imagining them still felt impossible.

The wind turned again. Smoke rolled over the parapet. Sparks lifted and died.

Sylvanas moved.

A shift of leather. A boot half a step closer on the stone. Then a hand on Jaina's left shoulder, firm through smoke-stiff cloth and travel grime.

"South," Sylvanas said, voice roughened by smoke.

"We go south."

Jaina stood one beat longer. Sylvanas kept her hand there.

She pushed off the parapet.

Heat lived in the stone even after she took her hands from it. It clung to her palms for two steps and was gone by the third.

The stair cut down the inner wall, worn shallow by generations of watch-boots and now streaked with soot.

Sylvanas took it first.

Jaina found that merciful at once. A pale braid to follow. Boots on stone. One hand skimming the wall when the stair narrowed at the turn. She kept descending.

Below them, Cyndia had already turned a strip of yard into a Farstrider pocket of order. Three mounts. Water skins uncapped. Tack rechecked. One strap tightened under a gray mare's belly even as she looked up, counted them, and moved on without asking either woman to speak.

Jaina hit the last step harder than she meant to. The jolt climbed into her knees through the saddle soreness she had been ignoring since dawn. Cyndia held out a skin. Jaina took it. Leather. Iron. Dust. She drank anyway.

Sylvanas was already at her horse.

Once there would have been a hitch here. A check. No hitch came.

Sylvanas checked the girth, put a boot in the stirrup, and mounted. Cyndia swung up too, left-handed and neat despite the long day. Jaina reached for her own pommel and found herself doing the same. Boot, stirrup, saddle, gather reins. Center-left. Cyndia drifting forward on the other side. Sylvanas at point.

No one said anything about it.

They rode out through the northern lanes and onto the south road under a sky still stained by Stratholme's smoke. They left the city behind slowly. First the inner wall. Then the higher roofs. Then only the dark smear above the horizon and the separate plumes where fire still found work.

Then came hooves on packed earth. Leather creak. The pull in Jaina's thighs each time her mare adjusted to the ruts. Smoke in her clothes turning colder now that it was no longer backed by the city's heat. Wind needling the flour-raw skin over her knuckles.

South.

For the body, south was simple. Out from the wall. Down the road. Away from the fire.

The bread-tainted drag weakened as the miles opened between her and the walls. The deeper pattern stayed sharp. If grain alone had been the whole design, the city should have read as saturation and fade. Instead each body had learned enough of the work to answer when something distant called it.

Grain carried it in.

Bodies learned it.

Death completed it.

Something farther away was still setting it off.

She took one hand from the reins long enough to drag her notebook from the saddlebag. Jaina braced the book against her thigh and wrote through the road's jarring.

outer shell in grain

body keeps deeper pattern

city scale = answer, not source

relay?

She scratched it out so hard the nib tore the paper. Then shut the book without finding the right one.

Ahead, Sylvanas lifted one hand from the reins and touched two fingers to the pouch at her belt.

"Stone," she said.

Jaina stared at her. Then understood.

Of course. Antonidas.

The sending-stone had ridden in her saddlebag all day, an accusation she had not yet had room to answer. She drew it out now. Even after hours against her notes and spare wrappings, the carved surface held a little warmth.

She thumbed the activation groove.

Blue light rose between her fingers, wavered once with the horse's motion, then steadied.

Antonidas came through in fragments first. Breath. Static. A voice already half-shaped around fear and discipline.

"Jaina."

She had meant to answer cleanly. Instead what came out first was, "I'm here."

Too fast. Too young. She hated herself for it at once.

The line hissed, then cleared.

"Word reached us from Stratholme by three mouths and none of them matched. Are you hurt?"

She looked down at her hand on the reins. Flour in the seams. Soot at the nail beds. A small burn along the thumb where warehouse magic had flashed back through rotten stock. Ridiculous beside the city on the horizon behind them.

"Alive," she said. Her throat rasped. "Tired. Nothing I can't sit through."

Around her, they kept moving. Cyndia had widened a little to give her space without breaking the line. Sylvanas did not turn in the saddle, but one ear stayed angled back.

"I can send riders before nightfall," Antonidas said. "Southshore first. Dalaran from there. If you name the road, I'll clear it."

Home hit her in a single blow. Warm rooms. Lamps. Ink that did not shake on the page. Antonidas across a worktable in Dalaran, making the horror small enough to carry.

She could not bear it. Wanting it all at once nearly folded her over the saddle.

"Not yet," she said.

The stone stayed warm in her palm.

"Jaina."

Warning this time. Plea too. And authority, though distance had thinned it.

She looked past Sylvanas's shoulder to the road.

"The grain isn't enough to explain Stratholme."

The horse stumbled in a rut. She caught herself with knees and one hard pull on the reins.

"It carried the plague in. We knew that. But the city answered all at once, after the stock was already moving out of our hands. The grain carried it in, but it isn't what is setting them off now. The bodies are answering a call beyond the stock."

Antonidas said nothing for three breaths.

When he spoke again, the softness in his voice was worse than command.

"Come home with the answer you have."

She shut her eyes once.

She nearly said yes.

Then the wall came back under her palms. Arthas below it with clean hands. Market Row in ordered fires. Residue dragging at the back of her throat. She had understood too late.

"I can't," she said.

"Jaina—"

"I'm alive," she said, harsher now because grief would break the line if she let it. "I'm moving. That's what I can give you tonight."

Silence.

Then, quieter, "Moving where?"

She looked again at the smoke behind them, flattened now by distance into a bruise against the sky.

"For now? South."

Sylvanas's head turned a fraction at that. Not toward her. Toward the word.

Antonidas let the answer stand.

"Keep the stone on you."

"I will."

"Write while you can. Even scraps."

Her thumb found the torn notebook page through the saddlebag flap.

"I have been."

The line held a moment longer. He was still there, wanting ten more questions and knowing none would help. When the light went out, it did so gently. The warmth stayed in the stone a second longer than it should have.

She tucked it away.

Nobody spoke.

The road widened, then narrowed where carts had chewed its edges into mud. Crows lifted from a hedgerow at their passing. Smoke thinned. Under it came a colder trace from the north-east: salt somewhere far ahead, water beyond the last rise of land.

Open coast.

Her notebook was still in her lap. She opened it again and this time did not chase the word. She wrote only what the body knew.

grain carries

body keeps

distance wakes

Kel'Thuzad dead

pattern still answers

The mare's stride knocked the letters crooked. She let them stand.

A rider came out of the dusk behind them at a run.

Cyndia heard her first; the elf's shoulders changed before any sound reached human ears. Then came the bird-call — short, sharp, Farstrider clear — and the answer from farther ahead where some scout had hidden in the last line of trees.

Friendly.

Urgent anyway.

The relay horse hit the road behind them hard enough that mud sprayed from its chest and flanks. Foam flecked the bit. The rider looked scarcely steadier than the animal beneath her, braid half out, one glove missing, dispatch tube already in hand before she pulled alongside.

"Ranger-General."

Sylvanas reined in. The rest of them did the same.

The rider passed the tube across, damp leather knocking once against Sylvanas's bracer. Sylvanas broke the seal with her thumb and read in the last usable stripe of light.

Jaina watched the ranger's face. Her mouth thinned. Nothing more.

"Say it," Cyndia said.

Sylvanas did not look up from the page.

"Arthas has taken ships on the northern coast. Bound for Northrend."

The word went through Jaina straight and cold.

She had already begun to read the road as north. Hearing Northrend named fixed the next road on the map.

Northrend.

Ice. Open sea. The far source of the plague.

Sylvanas read on.

"Uther rides west for Capital City."

Another line.

"He took men who stayed with him at Stratholme."

No one answered.

The relay rider shifted in the saddle, fighting to stay upright. Her horse stood with its head down and sides heaving.

Jaina looked from the dispatch tube to the darkening road ahead, then down to her notebook, still open on her thigh.

grain carries

body keeps

distance wakes

Kel'Thuzad dead

pattern still answers

North.

She did not write the word. There was no room for it now that it had moved into the world.

Sylvanas rolled the dispatch once and handed it across to Cyndia.

"Camp another mile south. Rotate the relay horse out. She eats before she reports again."

"Yes, Ranger-General."

Cyndia turned to the camp work, and the rider went with her. The scout blinked once as if she had not expected ordinary treatment. Horse led aside. Waterskin. Hands on tack.

South remained under their horses.

North rode in the dispatch tube, her notebook, and the salt wind ahead.

They went on as the light finished going.

By full dark, the road was only bodily fact. Sweat cooling under wool. The ache settling deeper into the knees with each mile. Somewhere behind them Stratholme still burned, though now it was only a stain against the horizon.

Jaina kept waiting for someone to speak the obvious thing.

No one did.

Sylvanas rode at point as she had since the wall. Back straight. Hands quiet on the reins. Once, only once, she turned her head toward the north-east where the wind carried that colder trace of sea.

It touched Jaina as surely as a hand.

The sending-stone, tucked back into her saddlebag, had not fully lost its warmth.

Home on one side of leather. The notebook on the other. Between them, road and horse sweat.

South for now.

North where the prince had gone. North toward the coast. North toward whatever was still calling the dead from farther out.

No one said the word.

The road disappeared into full dark.
