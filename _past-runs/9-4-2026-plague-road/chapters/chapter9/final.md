# Chapter 9: The Trap

Eastweald had been kept.

North of Andorhal the roads stayed clear, the ditches cut back, the dead orchards trimmed for sightlines. Sylvanas rode at the head of the formation and let her mare choose the firmer ground where wheel-ruts had dried hard. Pale fungus crusted the ditch stones. Up close it smelled like a cellar sealed too long.

Cyndia ranged left along a broken wall that vanished into a black-crowned orchard. Lyriel held the right. Jaina rode between them with her hood down, bare fingers on her staff.

At the next fork Cyndia lifted two fingers without looking back. Wagon scoring on the left branch. Recent. Lyriel glanced toward a chapel ruin half-swallowed by nettles.

"No birds."

"None since dawn," Cyndia said.

Jaina looked at the mud by the rut. "Grain weight. Two days, perhaps three."

Sylvanas sent them left with a turn of the wrist.

The road bent round another spread of dead trees. Beyond them sat a farmhouse with one side caved in, two outbuildings, and a yard too orderly for weather. The churn was old. The order was fresh.

Jaina went still before she lifted her head.

"Stop."

Cyndia checked left. Lyriel turned to take the rear. Sylvanas studied the house: one more ruin until the nearness showed the tidy emptiness of a place people had expected to return to.

"What kind?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina drew a short breath. "Fresh. Deliberate."

Nothing moved. Broken roofline. One hanging gate. Dead apple trees beyond the well.

"How fresh?"

"Hours, perhaps. Less if the cold had not slowed it."

Lyriel spat into the weeds. "Convenient."

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

Jaina's voice went flatter. "This isn't spillover. They worked here in force. I can feel the shape of it from the yard."

Sylvanas let her gaze travel once round the property. Orchard north. Low wall east. Wagon tracks in the yard. A half-collapsed lean-to on the west side where a patient man could hide trouble.

"We take it," she said. "Cyndia left. Lyriel right and rear. No heroics around the buildings. Jaina with me."

"If this is what I think it is—"

"Then you get your look. Fast."

They dismounted in the yard. The horses hated the place at once. Lyriel caught two sets of reins before the panic could spread. Cyndia led them back toward the road and tied them under the lee of the wall.

Sylvanas crossed the yard with her bow in hand. The well bucket had split down one seam. Amber grit lay in the cracks of the stone rim. Reagent.

Inside, the house smelled of wet plaster, old straw, lamp smoke, and magic worked too often in too small a room.

The kitchen floor had been scored in circles and cross-cuts deep enough to hold powder. Jaina crouched at the threshold and put two fingers near the nearest line without touching it.

"Binding work," she said. "Part of a larger shape. This section was built to survive handling."

Shelves stripped. Crockery broken on purpose. A side table blackened by heat. Three cloudy bottles, one intact, one shattered, one wax-stoppered.

"Door," Sylvanas said.

Jaina stopped, looked down, and swore softly. "You were right."

"Usually."

The threshold held another faint line under the soot. Jaina knelt, peered, and made a face.

"Alarm line. Built to bite anyone reading from outside."

"Can you kill it?"

"Already did."

"Good. Read."

Jaina went to one knee in the center of the room, staff across her thighs.

"This is better than Andorhal," she said. "Smaller. Cleaner. They left enough to read the work under the carrier lines."

Sylvanas took a position where she could see the yard, the back room, and half the kitchen window.

Jaina closed her eyes.

The room changed without flash. Heat shimmered low over the flagstones while the rest stayed cold. Frost crept up the broken bottle and stopped.

Sylvanas counted breaths.

At thirty, Cyndia clicked once from outside the left window: clear. At forty-two, Lyriel answered from the yard wall: clear.

At fifty, Jaina said, "The body isn't only carrying it."

"I know."

"No. The body is part of the lock. They aren't only poisoning people. They're teaching the flesh to answer, then waiting for death to finish it."

"Can you use that?" Sylvanas asked.

"Part of it. Give me another—"

She cut off as the first crossbow snapped from the orchard.

The bolt punched chalk and plaster across the room. Two more followed, lower. One took the doorframe where Sylvanas had been. She was already moving.

"Out."

Cyndia answered with an arrow from the yard. Lyriel shouted a hard ugly word to the horses.

Jaina was still half inside the working. Sylvanas reached her as a man came through the shattered side door with a short sword and buckler. Living. Hooded. Fast enough to be trained. Not fast enough.

She put an arrow through his throat at six feet, dropped the bow, caught Jaina by the front of the coat, and hauled her clear as the man behind him tried to force the same gap.

"Move."

Jaina blinked once and came back hard. "I have part of it."

The second man came through low. Sylvanas kicked the chair into his knees and drew her knife in the same motion. He saw the knife, lunged anyway, and died for it. Behind him, movement filled the yard. The orchard had grown men.

Jaina's staff flashed blue-white. Frost sealed the broken kitchen window in a jagged bloom just as a bolt tried to come through it.

"Rear room," Sylvanas said.

"There isn't one."

"Then make one."

Lyriel was at the back threshold, bow half raised, one side of her face powdered with plaster. "Three left, two on the well, one roof."

"Roof first."

Cyndia's arrow answered from outside. A body struck shingles, then ground.

The line held for three breaths more. Then a bolt took Sylvanas in the side under the ribs, through leather and across flesh, and drove the air out of her.

White. Then a bright hard seam under the ribs. She checked the blood once and ignored it.

Jaina checked it too.

"Sylvanas—"

"Later."

That one word cost more breath than she liked.

Jaina's face tightened. "I can give you what keeps it alive through storage and baking. The final step is missing."

"Carry the useful half."

"I am."

Another bolt hit the frozen window and bounced away. Lyriel loosed through the door and someone screamed outside.

Sylvanas snatched up the intact bottle from the table, caught lamp oil through whatever else had been in it, and hurled it into the back corner where the floor already sagged. Jaina understood at once. Frost bit the glass. It split with a crack that filled the room with fire, smoke, and burning oil.

They went through the smoke instead of the door. Cyndia met them in the yard with the horses already loose and Lyriel covering the orchard edge.

"North wall," Sylvanas said.

"Low enough to jump," Lyriel said.

"Then jump it."

Jaina caught Sylvanas's elbow before she put weight on the wrong side. "You'll tear it wider."

"I know."

Her hand was cold and steady. Sylvanas let her have one heartbeat to shove folded cloth under the cut and wrap the bandage once round her waist.

"That won't hold if you keep twisting," Jaina said.

"Then I won't."

They cleared the north wall, caught the horses beyond it, and rode out through orchard rows where dead leaves had turned to black pulp underhoof.

No one spoke for half a mile.

When the trees thinned, Sylvanas passed the salvaged scraps back to Jaina without turning. "Tell me we got something worth bleeding for."

Jaina looked once. "Yes. The part that keeps it alive through storage and baking. Proof the body matters before death. The final step is still missing."

"Still no waking turn."

"No. But enough to point higher."

Jaina unfolded one scrap fully. "There was a mark under the reagent shelf. Estate road, not Andorhal town use. Northeast approach. Private stores. Few hands cycling through."

Cyndia held out a hand. Jaina passed the scrap over.

"Walled compound," she said. "Working road, but no village road. Someone wants traffic in and witnesses out."

Lyriel snorted. "Sounds charming."

By the time the rise came into view, the shape was clear: a compound above the road.

It sat three hours farther on, on a low rise with scrub oak at its shoulders. Two outbuildings. Main house. Cart shed.

Sylvanas reined in below the rise and studied it through a gap in the trees.

The perimeter was better than the farmhouse. No obvious watch. Tracks kept to one lane. Yard clean. Trained ground.

Jaina drew up beside her and closed her eyes for half a breath.

Sylvanas did not wait for the report. She had already found the seam: a shallow dip along the north side where the rise broke the line from the outbuildings.

Her side hurt. Every delay now came priced in blood.

"There," she said. "North side. We move through the dip."

Jaina opened her eyes. "Wait."

Flat. Certain. Sylvanas disliked it.

"I need one sweep."

"You have half of one."

"Half won't do on this ground."

The seam still looked right.

Jaina touched her sleeve. "Sylvanas."

She moved.

"Cyndia left," she said. "Lyriel right. Jaina with me. Quick and low."

Jaina made a noise of frustration that should have pleased her. It did not.

They moved on foot through the scrub oak. The ground dipped exactly where Sylvanas had read it: good cover, easy angle into the yard wall.

Halfway down, Jaina stopped so abruptly Sylvanas nearly hit her.

"No."

The word was for the ground.

"What's wrong?"

Jaina's attention fixed on empty air. "The corridor isn't empty."

Lyriel was already stepping into it from the right.

There was no flash.

Cold light bloomed across the dip. The air cracked.

Lyriel vanished.

No fire. No scream. White light, ozone over sweet rot, bare ground where the right flank had been.

Jaina's barrier went up a heartbeat later and caught the second pulse before it reached Sylvanas's chest. Blue-white light crawled over unseen lines in the air.

"Back," Jaina snapped.

This time Sylvanas obeyed before the sentence ended.

Cyndia had already dropped to one knee left of the dip, bow up, eyes locked on the empty place to the right with a ferocity that changed nothing.

There was nothing to recover. No body. No blood. Only scorched grass and after-light burning itself out.

Sylvanas shoved Jaina back behind the oak line as another ward fired where Jaina's knee had been.

Then the whole slope woke.

Crossbowmen rose from slit trenches in the field. One on the shed roof. Two behind the low stone wall. Living again. Always living when it cost them most.

Sylvanas fired on instinct. The man on the shed roof pitched backward without loosing. Cyndia took the left trench. Jaina held the barrier a fraction too low, corrected, and caught the next volley on the second try.

No time for the missing. The field asked for the living answer first.

Sylvanas used the oak trunks as cover, moved three paces, loosed, moved again. The wound in her side bit every turn. She kept her face blank and her breathing shallow.

Jaina spoke without looking at her. "Three layers. The outer one burns on spacing. The second waits for panic retreat. Don't go uphill blind."

Sylvanas adjusted at once. "Cyndia, on my arrow. Jaina, hold the left choke. Two steps only when I move."

"Understood."

They broke contact by inches instead of speed. Jaina peeled the second layer open just long enough to let them clear it. Sylvanas covered the exit. Cyndia answered every order, but once the right flank pulled her eyes and had to be dragged back.

By the time they reached the tree line beyond the trap ground, the fight had turned too expensive uphill. No pursuit followed.

Sylvanas put one hand against a trunk and left it there until the world widened again.

Jaina came close, checked the fresh blood on the second bandage, and kept silent.

"Move," Sylvanas said.

They moved.

After that every choke point had teeth.

At the first narrow lane through the scrub, Jaina swept once and nodded. "Clean."

Only then did Sylvanas take them through.

They regrouped at dusk on a ridge above fields gone silver with frost. One road bent deeper into Barov country. The other curved south-west toward Hearthglen.

Cyndia unsaddled with the hard economy of a woman making sure the horses still existed because looking anywhere else cost too much. Jaina spread the scraps and notes across a flat stone. Sylvanas sat because standing pulled at the wound.

The absence in the formation had weight.

"What do we have?" Sylvanas asked.

Jaina laid one scrap over another. "The farmhouse and the compound use the same family of binding. The compound's edge work is cleaner. More than one site. Someone upstream is making the method match."

"Barov?" Sylvanas asked.

"Barov roads. Barov access. Storehouses, probably. Nothing stronger than that yet."

"Good. Keep it there."

Cyndia tapped one route mark with a stripped fingernail. "Estate approaches. Three of them. Main road, north service lane, and a half-hidden cut through the orchard east of the wall."

"And the other line?" Sylvanas asked.

Cyndia kept her eyes on the southern road. "I saw someone peel out of the trap site."

Jaina looked up sharply. "You saw someone get out?"

"Maybe. Tall. Hood up. Too far for a face. He moved before the crossbows did."

"Anything else?" Sylvanas asked.

Cyndia's gaze drifted right, then forward. "When he broke the tree line he called to the men by the lower ditch. I only caught the end."

Sylvanas waited.

Cyndia said, "'May the stars keep your road.'"

The phrase struck where it always did: nowhere useful and too deep.

The old seller on the road south of Strahnbrad. Forgettable face. Memorable farewell.

Jaina heard the shift without fully knowing why. "You've heard that before."

"Yes."

"Same man?"

"I don't know."

Hooves came up the ridge road from the north at a pace too deliberate for enemies and too tired for any courier.

Cyndia was on her feet before the second stride, bow on the approach.

Sylvanas knew the rider before the horse cleared the rise.

Lor'themar reined in below them on a lathered bay, one broken buckle hanging loose at his shoulder. He took in the camp in one sweep: the missing place, the blood on Sylvanas's side, Cyndia standing where two rangers should have stood.

"You look terrible."

"You've improved too."

"Convocation?" Sylvanas asked.

"Among other delights."

Lor'themar reached inside his coat and produced two packets, one sealed in Silvermoon wax and the other in the narrow plain hand of a Dalaran copyist. He handed the second to Jaina. Sylvanas took the first without waiting.

"That one is supposed to sound official," he said. "The official line is grave concern over the widening scope of your authority, your absence from the northern defenses, and the number of reports arriving without proof a comfortable room can read."

Sylvanas broke the seal with her thumb. "And the real line?"

Lor'themar looked at the blood on her bandage. "I spent three days keeping them from recalling you by sheer precedent and wounded dignity. I bought you this much road by arguing that if they cut your line now, all they gain is ignorance with better posture."

Jaina opened her own packet. Her face barely changed.

"Antonidas?" Sylvanas asked.

"The Council wants the proof laid out clean before they'll commit more people," Jaina said.

Lor'themar laughed once. "Remarkable. We are being buried by two institutions in exactly the same language."

He looked at the papers spread on the stone.

"Tell me what you have before someone older and more decorative than I am decides we should stop having it."

They told him.

Barov marks kept surfacing in Eastweald—storehouses, estate roads, scraps too consistent to ignore and still too thin to spend as accusation. The farmhouse had given them one ugly fact: the body did part of the work before death, but the last turn was still missing. The compound had been built for scouts. Hearthglen chatter kept rising on the south-west road, and with it Arthas. The old farewell had surfaced again.

Lor'themar listened the way he always had, weighing each fragment before the next.

When they finished, he said, "You don't have one road anymore."

"No," Sylvanas said.

"And you don't have four women either," he said, still looking at the papers.

Jaina folded the Dalaran note once with great care. "If the Barov roads are real and still moving, I can get through doors you can't."

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

Jaina met her eyes over the map. "And if both your institutions keep turning inward, Arthas becomes a lever whether we like him or not."

"Also yes."

Lor'themar rubbed a hand over his mouth. "Then stop pretending there's a version of this where you stay one team and chase both before the paper walls close."

Sylvanas caught Cyndia's eyes on the south-west road and did not look right.

"You take the Barov thread," she said to Jaina.

Jaina held the silence for a beat.

"Alone," Lor'themar said, because someone had to.

"Because she's the right one," Sylvanas said. "Not because she's the spare body."

Jaina's chin lifted a fraction.

"The estate marks, the road scraps, what you pulled from the farmhouse," Sylvanas said. "You carry all of it. You do not push Barov beyond what the papers earn. If a door opens because they still think you're only a Kirin Tor problem, use it."

"I know how to walk into hostile houses politely," Jaina said.

"That was never the concern."

Lor'themar almost smiled. "Touching."

Sylvanas ignored him and looked to Cyndia. "You ride with me."

Cyndia nodded once.

"Hearthglen first," Sylvanas said. "Then Arthas if he's still within reach. We need rank, soldiers, and a man who moves before committees finish introducing themselves."

Jaina's eyes flicked to the bandage at Sylvanas's side. "You need that wound looked at before you try to bully a prince."

"He's human. It won't take much."

Lor'themar pinned the Convocation packet to the map with two fingers. "You understand what this means if either of you fails?"

"Yes," Sylvanas said.

"I understand what it means if we do nothing," Jaina said.

The light bled out of the fields. Jaina wrapped the farmhouse notes in oilcloth and slid them into her satchel with the Barov routes and Cyndia's estate sketches.

"When do you leave?" Lor'themar asked.

"Now," Sylvanas said.

"Of course."

They saddled in the dark. Jaina checked Sylvanas's bandage again and retied it tighter when she found blood under the edge.

"That will hurt," she said.

"Yes."

"Good."

Dry enough to be hers. Sylvanas let it stand.

Then Jaina stepped back, mounted, and turned her horse toward the Eastweald road without looking for permission.

Sylvanas mounted more slowly than she would have liked. Cyndia was already in place on the left.

Jaina touched two fingers to the edge of her hood in a gesture too small for a salute and too deliberate for accident.

Sylvanas answered with the same economy. "Get through the door first."

"Send word before the paper walls close."

"You too."

Lor'themar took Jaina's rein long enough to turn her cleanly onto the eastern road. He did not touch Sylvanas's.

The roads divided below the ridge.

Jaina took the Barov line alone with the notes, the estate scraps, and the incomplete answer wrapped against the cold.

Sylvanas turned south-west with Cyndia, the wound under her ribs, and the new price of every bad decision in the space where the right flank used to be.

No one looked back.
