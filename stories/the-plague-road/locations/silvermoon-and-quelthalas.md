# Silvermoon & Quel'Thalas — Location Reference

## Silvermoon City

**Role in story:** Chapter 1, Scene 1 (Sylvanas receives the assignment); mentioned throughout as Sylvanas's anchor point and political authority.

### Architecture & Atmosphere
The high elven capital is a city of arching golden spires, crimson banners, and arcane geometry. Buildings flow with organic curves — less constructed than grown, as if the city crystallized from concentrated magic. White stone and gilded metal dominate; sunlight catches polished surfaces at every angle. Magical lanterns burn without flame. Fountains move water in patterns that defy gravity. The air hums faintly with ambient arcane energy, a constant background frequency that visiting humans describe as "singing" and that native elves no longer consciously notice.

The city existed for millennia before the Third War. Nothing about it looks temporary. Every archway, every carved lintel, every arrangement of flowering vines on a balcony reflects centuries of incremental refinement by people who expect to live long enough to see the results.

### Key Districts

**Sunfury Spire** — The royal palace and administrative heart, located in the northern reaches. A grand structure of golden spires and red-and-white pennants. Contains council chambers, the Inner Sanctum where the Convocation meets, the regent's apartments, scrying chambers, and portal rooms. The Spire is where political power concentrates — Sylvanas despises having to attend sessions here.

**Court of the Sun** — The ceremonial courtyard below Sunfury Spire. The city's largest fountain occupies its center — water shaped into elaborate arcane helixes by enchantment. Used for celebrations, speeches, arrivals of emissaries. The Convocation address in Chapter 1 would happen here or in the Spire's council chamber.

**The Bazaar** — Commercial and social hub. Market stalls, merchants, banks, the main inn. Vibrant color — silks and spices and polished metalwork. The most "alive" part of the city in terms of foot traffic and ambient noise. Smells of incense, hot metal from the forge district, cooked food.

**Walk of Elders** — The sweeping avenue from the southern entrance to the city center. Lined with statues of ancestor-heroes, gardens, scholarly alcoves. Farstrider initiates pass through here for rites. The avenue's design forces arrivals into a perspective trick: the Spire appears to grow from the horizon as you walk, so that by the time you reach the Court of the Sun, it dominates the sky.

**Murder Row** — The clandestine district parallel to the Bazaar. Dimly lit, narrow, where the criminal underworld and secret police operate. Sylvanas would know this district exists and what it's for, but has no professional interest in it — Farstrider business is conducted in the field, not the shadows.

### Sensory Anchors for Prose
- Sound: The ambient hum of ward-stones and arcane geometry. Water in the fountain districts. Wind chimes tuned to harmonic frequencies. The absence of mechanical noise — no hammering, no wheels on cobblestones. It's eerily quiet by human city standards.
- Smell: Bloodthistle and peacebloom in the gardens. The metallic tang of concentrated mana near the Spire. Clean stone — the city self-maintains through enchantment.
- Light: Golden. Everything catches and refracts sunlight through crystal and gilded surfaces. At dawn and dusk the whole city turns amber.
- Touch: Stone that's warm to the touch (enchantment maintains temperature). Air that has a faint static charge near ward-nodes.

## Eversong Woods

**Role in story:** Backdrop to Chapter 1's opening. The landscape Sylvanas rides through en route to the Thalassian Pass. Mentioned as the homeland she's leaving behind.

### Geography & Character
Golden-leafed forests stretching south from Silvermoon toward the border. The trees are old — ancient hardwoods with wide canopies that filter sunlight into perpetual amber twilight at ground level. Undergrowth is managed by centuries of elven stewardship: it's a forest that appears natural but is actually curated, every clearing and game trail maintained by Farstrider patrols.

The Dead Scar — the swath of land later destroyed by Arthas's Scourge — does NOT exist at this point in the timeline. Eversong is whole, unbroken, the golden heart of Quel'Thalas.

Rivers and streams wind through the forest, clear, cold, flowing south toward the Elrendar River. The soil is dark, rich, maintained by both natural fertility and subtle enchantment.

### Key Landmarks

**Windrunner Spire** — The ancestral home of the Windrunner family, located in southwestern Eversong/Ghostlands border. A tall, graceful spire surrounded by gardens and smaller outbuildings. Before the Scourge invasion, it was a living family estate. Sylvanas grew up here. Alleria left from here for the Dark Portal. Vereesa still visits. The Spire is loaded with personal significance — but Sylvanas is not the type to dwell on it, so it appears in narration only as a geographic reference point, something she passes without stopping.

**Windrunner Village** — The settlement in the shadow of the Spire. Once a peaceful high elf hamlet. Destroyed during the burning of Quel'Thalas by the Old Horde and their Amani allies in the Second War. Rebuilt since, but carrying scars. Most of the Windrunner family perished here in earlier conflicts — Lirath, Sylvanas's younger brother, died here. The village is a place of both home and grief.

**Farstrider Retreats** — Outposts scattered through the forest where rangers rest, resupply, and coordinate patrols. Functional, minimal: sleeping platforms in the canopy, weapon caches, ward-stones linked to Silvermoon's detection grid. Sylvanas knows every one of these by heart.

### Sensory Anchors
- Golden light through amber canopy. Dappled shadow on the forest floor.
- Birdsong — specific to Eversong, distinct from Lordaeron species. Melodic, repetitive, almost hypnotic.
- The smell of old leaves, running water, the faint ozone of ward-magic.
- Silence. Elven forests are quiet. The sound of a bow being drawn carries.
- Farstrider travel should read as horse travel in established early-warcraft terms: hoofbeats under the canopy, tack whisper, foam on hard-ridden field horses, and the controlled stillness of mounts trained for patrol work rather than parade. See [Thalassian Horses](stories/the-plague-road/locations/thalassian-horses.md).

## Thalassian Pass

**Role in story:** Chapter 1, Scene 2 (Sylvanas meets Jaina at the ward-stones).

### Geography
The primary gateway into Quel'Thalas from the south. A natural bottleneck in the terrain where the landscape transitions from Eversong's golden forests to the more temperate (and less enchanted) woodlands of northern Lordaeron. The Pass is fortified: ward-stones at intervals, Farstrider patrols, watch platforms in the trees. It has served as a defensive chokepoint for millennia — against Amani trolls, against the Horde, and now against whatever is happening in the borderlands.

### Character
The Pass is where Quel'Thalas ends and the human world begins. The transition is palpable: the ambient magical hum fades, the trees lose their golden coloring, the temperature drops slightly (Silvermoon's climate enchantment extends to the Pass but not beyond). For Sylvanas, crossing the Pass is always a transition from certainty to compromise.

### Ward-Stones
Ancient monoliths placed at regular intervals along the Pass and the border. Each is a node in Silvermoon's detection grid — they register magical disturbances, unauthorized crossings, and large-scale energy signatures. Jaina is found kneeling at one of these in Chapter 1, running diagnostic magic on the ward network, mapping the entropic signature of whatever is happening in the borderlands.

The ward-stones are carved with Thalassian runes and hum at a frequency below conscious hearing. They're warm to the touch (ambient enchantment). Elves can sense them from fifty paces; humans have to be right on top of them.

### Sensory Anchors
- The gradual fading of the Eversong hum as you move south.
- Wind through the pass — a bottleneck effect that creates a constant breeze.
- The ward-stones' bass vibration, felt in the teeth more than heard.
- The visual transition: golden leaves giving way to green-brown-grey of human forests.
- Border traffic from the Quel'Thalas side should read as mounted horse traffic: shod hooves on stone, hard-breathed patrol horses under elven riders, reins and saddle leather close to the ward-stones, and animals trained to tolerate the hum of the defense grid. See [Thalassian Horses](stories/the-plague-road/locations/thalassian-horses.md).

## The Convocation of Silvermoon

The governing body of Quel'Thalas. Seven seats around a crescent table in Sunfury Spire's Inner Sanctum — the room described above (see Sunfury Spire), with its mosaic floor and ambient enchantment. The Convocation is self-governing: no monarch, no external authority, no higher court of appeal. Decisions are made by simple majority of the voting members present.

The seven seats represent the major pillars of Thalassian society: arcane scholarship, military defense, commerce, cultural preservation, civil governance, the Farstriders (Ranger-General's seat), and the Grand Magister's chair (first among equals, sets the agenda, breaks ties). The Ranger-General's seat has been Sylvanas Windrunner's for centuries. In her absence, Lor'themar Theron speaks as her deputy — advisory capacity only, no vote.

### How It Works

Sessions are called by the Grand Magister or by petition of any three members. Debate follows a formal protocol older than most human kingdoms: the petitioner speaks, each seat responds in order of seniority, the floor opens for rebuttal, the vote is called. The protocol is designed for deliberation, not speed. A routine session runs four hours. A contested one can stretch to twelve.

Votes are recorded in the Thalassian Archives. Abstentions count as present-but-not-voting — they don't block quorum but they don't support either side. A member who abstains is making a political statement: "I see the argument but I won't commit." It is the Convocation's equivalent of a held breath.

Runner messages from the field — Sylvanas's intelligence dispatches — are read aloud by the presiding clerk before debate begins. This means Lor'themar's political fight starts the moment the clerk finishes reading: the intelligence lands, and then he has to defend it against six other interpretations of what it means.

### The Fault Line

The Convocation's central divide during the plague crisis is not hawks vs. doves. It's **certainty vs. doubt.**

The certainty faction holds that Quel'Thalas's defenses are sufficient: the ward network covers every approach, elven immunity to the plague is a biological fact (not a belief — it IS a fact), and the Farstriders patrol the borders. Therefore the plague is a human problem requiring human solutions, and Sylvanas's deployment south of the border is an overreach of her mandate. This faction has evidence on its side — the wards DO work, elves ARE immune — and the evidence makes them dangerous, because they are drawing the correct conclusion from incomplete premises.

The doubt faction (effectively Lor'themar alone, with occasional sympathy from Magistrix Dawnstar) holds that defensive sufficiency is not the same as strategic wisdom — that a plague consuming the kingdom's largest trading partner and military ally is a threat to Quel'Thalas regardless of whether it crosses the border. This argument requires the Convocation to care about something beyond the wards, and the wards are the thing the Convocation was built to care about.

### Named Members

#### Grand Magister Vandellor

**Seat:** First chair. Agenda-setter, tiebreaker, senior authority.

**Appearance:** Old even by elven standards — his face carries the particular stillness of an elf past his third millennium, where the features don't age so much as settle into permanence. White-blond hair worn long and unbound (the scholar's tradition — practical hairstyles are for people who go outside). Hands that move with the deliberate precision of someone who has spent centuries handling volatile enchantments. His eyes are the blue-green of all high elves, but paler — washed out by decades of close work with arcane light.

**Voice and method:** Vandellor speaks like a man delivering a lecture to students who should already know the material. His arguments are meticulous, his evidence curated, his conclusions presented as inevitabilities rather than opinions. He does not argue — he explains. The distinction matters: you can debate an argument, but an explanation simply IS, and the person who disagrees with it is the one who doesn't understand.

**Position on the plague:** The wards are his life's work. Not metaphorically — he designed the current generation of ward-stones, refined the detection grid, calibrated the resonance frequencies that make the network self-sustaining. When Lor'themar argues that the plague is a threat, Vandellor hears: "Your wards are insufficient." His defense of isolation is, at its root, a defense of his legacy. He is not wrong about the wards — they will hold. He is wrong about what "holding" means when the world beyond them is on fire.

**Danger level:** High. He is the most intelligent person in the room, his evidence is correct, and his conclusions are catastrophically narrow. He wins votes by being right about the small question (will the plague breach our borders?) while ignoring the large question (does it matter if our allies are dead?).

#### Magistrix Anariel Dawnstar

**Seat:** Civil governance. The Convocation's center of gravity.

**Appearance:** Mid-range elven maturity — appears early thirties in human terms, but the eyes are older, the kind of eyes that have read ten thousand petitions and remember every one. Auburn hair cut shorter than tradition demands (a minor scandal when she first took the seat, now simply her look). She dresses practically for a magistrix — court robes, yes, but the kind you can walk quickly in, with pockets. She takes notes during sessions — not with magic, with a pen. The pen is a statement: she trusts her hand more than her enchantments for the things that matter.

**Voice and method:** Dawnstar asks questions. Where Vandellor explains and Sunsorrow declaims, Dawnstar interrogates — not aggressively, but with the patient specificity of someone who wants the complete picture before she commits. Her questions are the most dangerous thing in the Convocation because they force precision: "How many villages? Which grain routes? What is the transmission mechanism? What is the Ranger-General's proposed timeline?" She makes every argument show its work.

**Position on the plague:** Genuinely undecided for most of the story. She reads Sylvanas's field reports with visible concern — the contamination numbers are troubling, the organized distribution network is alarming — but she demands thresholds before she'll vote for mobilization. "Concern is not policy. What is the specific trigger for expeditionary authorization?" This makes her Lor'themar's best hope and greatest frustration: she can be convinced, but only by evidence that meets a standard she keeps raising as the political cost of mobilization becomes clearer.

**The abstention pattern:** When Dawnstar abstains, she is signaling to Lor'themar: "I'm not against you, but you haven't given me enough." Each abstention is an invitation to bring better evidence. Lor'themar reads this correctly and spends half his political energy finding the specific piece of intelligence that might convert her. Her eventual vote (post-Stratholme, when the scout company is authorized) is the hinge of the entire political subplot.

**Danger level:** Moderate. She is the person who COULD have made a difference earlier, and the story's cruelest irony is that her insistence on proper thresholds — a virtue in normal governance — is the specific thing that delays action until "too late" becomes a fact.

#### Lord Thaelian Sunsorrow

**Seat:** Cultural preservation. The Convocation's emotional anchor.

**Appearance:** The most visually impressive member of the Convocation — tall even for an elf, silver-white hair in an elaborate traditional style that takes an attendant twenty minutes to arrange each morning, ceremonial robes that cost more than a Farstrider's annual salary. He looks like a painting of what an elf should be. This is intentional. Sunsorrow's entire political identity is aesthetic — he represents the ideal of Thalassian civilization, and he represents it by embodying it.

**Voice and method:** Sunsorrow speaks in precedent. Every argument is historical: "In the Troll Wars, the Convocation determined..." "When the Amani threatened the southern pass in the fourteenth century..." "The traditions of this body require..." He does not engage with new evidence because new evidence is, by definition, without precedent, and governance without precedent is improvisation, and improvisation is what lesser civilizations do. His speeches are beautiful, moving, and completely disconnected from the current crisis.

**Position on the plague:** Human problems are human problems. Quel'Thalas's role is to maintain its borders, preserve its culture, and endure — as it has endured everything, for seven thousand years. The plague is tragic. The affected humans have his sympathy. Sympathy does not require military deployment. He votes against mobilization every time, not from malice but from a genuine, bone-deep conviction that Quel'Thalas's survival depends on not getting entangled in the affairs of kingdoms that rise and fall like weather.

**Danger level:** Low-to-moderate. He cannot be persuaded by facts because his argument is not factual — it's cultural. He'll vote against mobilization even after Stratholme because the principle hasn't changed. His danger is as a reliable opposition vote: Lor'themar can never win Sunsorrow, which means he needs EVERY other persuadable vote.

#### The Unnamed Four

The remaining four seats (arcane scholarship, commerce, military affairs beyond the Farstriders, and a rotating advisory position) are not detailed here. They can be developed in future dispatches if specific political dynamics require them. For now:
- Two generally vote with Vandellor (intellectual authority carries weight in a magocracy)
- One generally votes with Sunsorrow (cultural solidarity)
- One is genuinely independent and represents the story's wildcard vote if Lor'themar needs a surprise swing

### Prose-Writing Notes

**The Convocation is not a villain.** It is a governing body doing what governing bodies do: protecting certainty, demanding process, moving at the speed of consensus in a crisis that moves at the speed of plague. The reader should be frustrated by the Convocation the way Lor'themar is frustrated — not because they're evil but because they're RIGHT about the small things and WRONG about the large thing, and the gap between those two scales is where people die.

**Named members only appear in Lor'themar's messages and Vereesa's report.** The prose writer never visits Silvermoon directly. Vandellor, Dawnstar, and Sunsorrow exist as names in dispatches, as arguments summarized in Lor'themar's compressed political briefings, as the faces Vereesa describes after watching them vote. They are felt through their effects, not their presence.

**Dawnstar's abstentions are the political subplot's heartbeat.** Each abstention is a door that almost opened. Each one makes Lor'themar recalibrate. Track them: they are the pulse of the political fight.

**Banned words:** No "architecture" (use "framework" or "ward network" or "defense system"). No "geometry." No "register" for prose/vocabulary. No "data" (use "intelligence," "evidence," "numbers," "reports"). No "structural/structurally."
