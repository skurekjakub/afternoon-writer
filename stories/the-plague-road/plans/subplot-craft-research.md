# Subplot Craft Research — The Plague Road

Reference document for subplot decisions. Compiled from K.M. Weiland, Savannah Gilbo, and general craft knowledge.

---

## Core Principles

### "If it looks like a subplot, cut it." — K.M. Weiland

The most important takeaway from the research: **good subplots are inextricable from the main plot.** They don't run alongside the story — they're woven into its structure. If you can pull a subplot out and the main plot doesn't change, it's not a subplot — it's a distraction.

### The 3 Central Plotlines (Not Subplots)

Every well-formed story has three aspects that constitute the main plot:

1. **External Conflict** — the physical/action plot (the plague investigation, tracking the Scourge, stopping Arthas)
2. **Main Relationship** — the primary emotional throughline (Sylvanas × Jaina partnership arc)
3. **Internal Conflict** — the protagonist's character arc (Sylvanas's Lie/Truth: isolationism vs. engagement)

These three are the MAIN PLOT, not subplots. Everything else is subordinate.

### Subplot Relationships to Theme (Gilbo)

Every subplot must have one of three relationships to the main theme:

1. **Contradict the theme** — create dramatic irony by showing the theme failing in a parallel context
2. **Echo the theme** — reinforce the theme through a different lens or different characters
3. **Complicate the main plot** — add conflict, raise stakes, create obstacles

If a subplot does none of these — if it just runs alongside the main plot — it splits the story and ruins cohesion.

---

## Types of Subplot (Weiland)

### 1. Minor Character Relationships
Supporting cast conflicts that illuminate the protagonist's journey. Each relationship character must have their own goals that intersect with the protagonist's.

### 2. Minor POV Characters
Elevating supporting characters to "mini-protagonists" with their own external, internal, and relationship goals. **Note:** The Plague Road is single-POV (Sylvanas limited third), so this type doesn't apply directly — but secondary characters can still carry subplot weight through what Sylvanas observes.

### 3. Lower Levels of Antagonists
Smaller antagonistic forces the protagonist faces on the way to the "Big Boss." Not detours — stepping stones. Must ultimately feed into the main confrontation.

### 4. Minor Antagonist Goals
Sidelong antagonists with their own agendas at cross-purposes to the protagonist. Don't have to be villains — can be frustrated allies who make things harder.

---

## How Many Subplots?

- **Most novels: 2–3 subplots** (Gilbo)
- **Fantasy: potentially more**, but each must serve a purpose
- **Thriller/investigation: usually 1–2** — the procedural momentum is the engine; too many subplots dilute tension
- **Single-POV stories: fewer is better** — every subplot must be observable through the protagonist's lens

### The Climax Test (Weiland)
A subplot must be either:
1. Concluded in the Climax
2. Important to creating the Climax
3. Directly impacted by the Climax

If not, cut it.

---

## Subplot Timing

- Subplots typically enter at the start of Act 2 (after the main plot is established)
- Resolve in reverse order of importance (least important first)
- In investigation/thriller: subplots often emerge from the investigation itself — they're discoveries, not parallel tracks

---

## Application to The Plague Road

### What's Already Built Into the Main Plot

The Plague Road's "main plot" already contains the three central plotlines:

| Plotline | Content |
|---|---|
| **External conflict** | Plague investigation → Scourge tracking → Arthas assassination |
| **Main relationship** | Sylvanas × Jaina partnership (6-phase arc) |
| **Internal conflict** | Sylvanas: isolationism Lie → engagement Truth |

The partnership arc is substantial enough to carry the emotional weight. It doesn't need help from a romantic subplot or a "B-couple."

### The Question: Is There Room for Subplots?

**Yes, but sparingly.** This is a tight, focused thriller with dual POV (Sylvanas odd chapters, Jaina even chapters). The procedural momentum is the engine. Subplots should emerge organically from the investigation and the characters, not from a separate storyline.

### Recommended Subplot Approach: 2–3 Integrated Subplots

Each subplot should be a *thread within the investigation*, not a parallel track. They should be visible through both POVs (Sylvanas and Jaina), connected to the theme (isolationism vs. engagement / what it costs to care / intelligence without trust), and feed into the Climax.

---

## Proposed Subplot Threads

### 1. The Lor'themar Thread (Political / Thematic Echo)

**Type:** Minor character relationship + minor antagonist goals
**Theme relationship:** ECHOES the theme (isolation vs. engagement — but from the institutional angle)

Lor'themar is Sylvanas's second-in-command and the person she trusts most. He's sent back to Silvermoon in Ch 1 to manage the Convocation. His subplot plays out through messages, runners, and brief appearances:

- **Early chapters:** Lor'themar sends updates on Convocation politics. The Convocation is debating border budgets, not plague threats. Sylvanas reads between the lines — her government is choosing not to look.
- **Middle chapters:** As the plague's scope becomes clear, Lor'themar becomes Sylvanas's advocate in Silvermoon — trying to convince the Convocation to mobilize. They resist. The institutional version of Sylvanas's personal Lie: "not our problem."
- **Late chapters:** Lor'themar's efforts either succeed (too late) or fail (forcing Sylvanas to act alone). Either way, his subplot proves the theme: isolation kills.
- **Climax connection:** Whether reinforcements arrive or don't directly impacts the finale logistics.

**Why it works:** It's visible through Sylvanas's POV (messages, runners, brief meetings). It echoes the theme. It raises stakes (will Silvermoon help or won't it?). It feeds into the Climax.

### 2. The Mal'Ganis Thread (Antagonist / Thematic Contradiction)

**Type:** Lower level of antagonist + dramatic irony
**Theme relationship:** CONTRADICTS the theme (false engagement — pretending to help while engineering destruction)

Mal'Ganis is already designed as a hidden antagonist embedded in the investigation. His subplot is structural:

- **Disguised appearances:** He appears as helpful NPCs — a knowledgeable Farstrider, a cooperative village elder, a sympathetic Kirin Tor contact. Each appearance provides genuine useful information that advances the investigation.
- **The twist:** Every piece of information he provides is carefully curated to steer the investigation along a path Mal'Ganis has chosen. He's not obstructing — he's *directing*.
- **Two tells:** The dark stone ward ring and the ancient Kaldorei phrase ("Kalar'shorel'anor") appear across different disguises but won't be connected until late in the story.
- **Thematic knife:** Mal'Ganis is the dark mirror of Sylvanas's arc. She's learning that engagement (with Jaina, with the crisis) is necessary. He's demonstrating that engagement can be weaponized. The person who seems most helpful is the one doing the most damage.

**Why it works:** It's integrated into the investigation procedural. Every appearance is a scene Sylvanas interacts with. The reveals feed directly into the Climax. The thematic contradiction deepens the theme.

### 3. The Arthas Thread (Tragic Counterpoint / Thematic Mirror)

**Type:** Off-screen antagonist progression + thematic echo
**Theme relationship:** ECHOES the theme darkly (what happens when engagement becomes obsession)

Arthas is mostly off-screen in the first half, visible through reports, aftermath, and eventually direct encounters:

- **Early chapters:** Arthas is a name in reports — the Prince of Lordaeron who's investigating the plague from the human side. His methods are aggressive but understandable.
- **Middle chapters:** Reports of Arthas's escalation. He's not just investigating — he's *pursuing*. The Culling of Stratholme is the watershed moment.
- **Late chapters / Northrend:** Direct encounters. Arthas has crossed a line Sylvanas hasn't. Same crisis, opposite arc — he engaged with the plague and it consumed him.
- **Thematic knife:** Arthas is the cautionary version of Sylvanas's arc. She's learning to care about the crisis beyond Quel'Thalas's borders. He cared so much it destroyed him. Same theme (engagement), opposite outcome. The difference: Sylvanas has Jaina as an anchor. Arthas pushed everyone away.

**Why it works:** It's visible through Sylvanas's POV (reports, aftermath, encounters). It directly creates the Climax. It deepens the theme by showing the cost of engagement without partnership.

## What NOT to Add

- **A separate romance subplot** — The partnership arc already carries the emotional weight. Adding a romantic B-couple would dilute it.
- **Silvermoon court intrigue as its own plotline** — It should only exist as context for the Lor'themar thread. We don't cut away from Sylvanas to see Convocation sessions.
- **Jaina's personal backstory as a subplot** — Her background (Antonidas, Kirin Tor, family) should emerge through conversation and observation, not as a parallel track.
- **A "monster of the week" subplot** — Each undead encounter should escalate the main investigation, not be a self-contained mini-adventure.
- **Any subplot that requires leaving Sylvanas's POV** — Single-POV means every subplot must be observable through her eyes.

---

## Integration Pattern

The three proposed subplots share a design:

1. **All are observable through Sylvanas's POV** — messages, encounters, aftermath
2. **All connect to the theme** — echo (Lor'themar, Arthas) or contradict (Mal'Ganis) "isolation vs. engagement"
3. **All feed into the Climax** — reinforcements (Lor'themar), the trap (Mal'Ganis), the final fight (Arthas)
4. **None require parallel tracks** — they're threads within the investigation, not detours

This gives us a tight 3-subplot structure for a 24-chapter single-POV thriller:
- **Political thread** (Lor'themar / Silvermoon) — "will anyone help?"
- **Hidden antagonist thread** (Mal'Ganis) — "who's really pulling the strings?"
- **Tragic mirror thread** (Arthas) — "what does it cost to care too much?"
