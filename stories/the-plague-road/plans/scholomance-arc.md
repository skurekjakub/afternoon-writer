# The Scholomance Arc — Chapters 9–14

**Partnership phase transition:** TESTS OF FAITH → SAVING EACH OTHER (the crack between them IS the test)

---

## Arc Overview

The mid-book pivot. What was a joint investigation splits into two parallel threads as grief, arrogance, and a dreadlord's manipulation drive Sylvanas and Jaina apart for the first time. Sylvanas's thread leads to Prince Arthas — lighting the fuse of his canonical descent into madness. Jaina's thread leads to Kel'Thuzad inside Scholomance — facing her dark mirror alone. Both threads converge at the necromancer's sanctum, where the first real crack in the partnership opens.

### Pacing guardrails

This is the book's densest middle run. The danger is not lack of events. The danger is blur.

- **Ch 10** must stay the **entry + choice** chapter. It gets Jaina inside, shows the curated surface, and ends on her decision to go deeper. Do not let it absorb the real ideological duel.
- **Ch 11** is the **hard external interrupt**. It keeps the arc from becoming six chapters of inward scholarship and partnership fallout.
- **Ch 12** is the **duel + moment** chapter. It should feel categorically different from Ch 10: not another guided descent, but the actual collision with Kel'Thuzad's mind.
- **Ch 13** is the **damage event**. Sylvanas arrives, misreads the room, and the crack opens. Keep it about the read and the wound, not generic cleanup.
- **Ch 14** is the **redirect**. It carries the changed spacing, the unsaid damage, and the handoff to Hearthglen. Keep road motion and new outside pressure visible so it does not become a second pure fallout chapter.

### Thematic Engine

**The Three-Way Mirror at maximum power.** Jaina alone with Kel'Thuzad is the dark mirror operating without interference. Everything that separates her from him — intelligence, conviction, institutional rejection, frustration with failure — is something they SHARE. The only thing that separates them is the partnership. And in this moment, she's without it.

**Sylvanas triggers the WC3 storyline.** Driven by grief over Lyriel, Sylvanas goes to the most powerful person available — Prince Arthas — and convinces him the plague is real. She does the smart thing. She seeks institutional power. And what she sets in motion is the entire Arthas tragedy: Hearthglen, Stratholme, Northrend, Frostmourne. The woman who will eventually assassinate Arthas is the woman who put him on the path.

**Mal'Ganis is steering.** The breadcrumb that leads Sylvanas to Arthas comes from one of Mal'Ganis's disguises. He WANTS this. The Legion needs Arthas charging into the plague investigation — it leads him to Stratholme, to Northrend, to Frostmourne. Mal'Ganis is playing chess on both sides.

### The Crack

Sylvanas arrives at Scholomance and finds Jaina *listening* to Kel'Thuzad. Not agreeing — listening. Following the argument. Intellectually engaged with the enemy. From a three-hundred-year-old Ranger-General's perspective, that's not a moral failing; it's a tactical vulnerability. A partner who can be drawn into an intellectual conversation with a necromancer can be delayed, distracted, made to hesitate at the wrong moment.

From Jaina's side: the humiliation of being seen in that moment. And the worse humiliation of knowing Sylvanas's read is partially correct.

This is the first REAL damage to the partnership — not contrived drama, but genuine character conflict arising from who these people actually are.

## Chapter 9: The Trap
**POV:** Sylvanas
**Setting:** The Eastweald — Cult territory north of Andorhal, approaching Barov family lands
**Key characters:** Sylvanas, Jaina, Cyndia, Lyriel, Cult acolytes
**Stakes:** Both mistakes happen. Lyriel dies. The partnership is tested from both sides.
**Partnership phase:** TESTS OF FAITH — both mistakes, then the split

### The Mistakes

**Jaina's mistake (first).** At a Cult outpost, Jaina detects a fading magical signature — the residue of a mass-animation ritual. She insists on fifteen minutes of in-situ analysis. Sylvanas says they don't have fifteen minutes. Jaina insists — the mechanism is right here, she can map the full ritual matrix, develop a countermeasure. The Cult ambush hits at minute eight. Living operatives with weapons. Sylvanas fights rear-guard while Jaina scrambles to break off her analysis. Sylvanas takes a wound covering her. The bandage Jaina wraps later is an accusation neither voices.

**Sylvanas's mistake (second, same chapter).** They find a weak point in the Cult's perimeter defenses — a gap in the patrol route, a stretch of dead ground. Sylvanas reads it as an approach vector. She pushes through without waiting for Jaina's arcane sweep, still bleeding under the bandage, operating with the compressed judgment of a wounded commander who's lost fifteen minutes she wanted back.

The gap is bait. A kill zone rigged with necromantic traps. The kind Jaina would have detected in three seconds. Lyriel triggers the first ward. She dies.

### The Aftermath

Cyndia checks the right flank twice.

Sylvanas doesn't apologize. She doesn't need to — the empty space where Lyriel stood says everything. She starts waiting for Jaina's arcane sweep before advancing. The look that becomes their shorthand for "check this" is born from this death.

Jaina starts asking "how long?" before committing to sustained analysis. She starts checking sight lines. The question is new. It acknowledges time as a resource someone else pays for.

### The Split

As they regroup, two investigative threads emerge:
- The **grain trail** — contaminated shipments trace back through the supply chain to **Barov family holdings** in the Eastweald. A noble house with extensive agricultural estates. The name appears in too many shipping manifests.
- Cyndia reports an encounter near the road: a man — retreating figure, she only caught the back of him — spoke to her briefly. Something about the prince being at Hearthglen with a column from Capital City. Then, as he turned away: *"May the stars keep your road."* A phrase in Common that Cyndia found odd but forgettable. Sylvanas hears the report and something pricks at the edge of memory — a refugee who said the same words south of Millhaven. She files it and moves.

Sylvanas decides: she'll find Arthas. If a prince of Lordaeron with an army can be aimed at the Cult's infrastructure, that's worth more than another week of two-woman reconnaissance. Grief sharpens the logic — Lyriel is dead because they're two scouts and one mage against a continental network. They need institutional power.

Jaina takes the Barov trail. She has Kirin Tor credentials, academic pretense, and the arrogance of someone who's never met an intellectual problem she couldn't crack. She'll trace the grain to its source.

They split. For the first time since Chapter 3, they operate alone.

### Subplot intersection

**Lor'themar runner:** The Convocation sent inspectors to verify Sylvanas's reports — the Cult sites were clean (Mal'Ganis's sanitization). Lor'themar's evidence is evaporating. His handwriting is tighter than Ch 1, the sentences clipped. The Convocation is grinding him down.

**Antonidas (sending stone):** The Kirin Tor is conducting its own "review" of Jaina's leyline data — academic code for "we're building a case to recall you." Two institutions, same response: delegitimize the field operative.

## Chapter 10: The Estate
**POV:** Jaina
**Setting:** Barov family estate → Caer Darrow → Scholomance
**Key characters:** Jaina, Barov family member(s), Cult acolytes (disguised as servants/scholars)
**Stakes:** Jaina infiltrates the Cult's academy through the front door. Alone.
**Partnership phase:** TESTS OF FAITH → operating without the partnership for the first time

### The Barov Estate

The Barov family: old money, minor nobility, extensive grain holdings across the Eastweald. Their name is clean — no criminal connections, no political scandals. Just a family whose estates happen to be at the center of the contaminated grain network.

Jaina arrives at the estate under Kirin Tor authority. She doesn't pretend subtlety — that's not who she is. She uses her usual directness and the political weight of Antonidas's name. She demands an audience. The Barovs' initial reaction: confusion, alarm, a polished attempt to deflect. *We don't know what you're talking about. Our grain operations are perfectly normal. You're welcome to inspect our records.*

But Jaina is persistent and politically formidable. She pushes. She mentions "scholarly activity" at Caer Darrow. She references the "research community" that seems to have established itself at the old family fortress. Her tone shifts from investigator to *interested colleague*. She's a Kirin Tor mage — the greatest magical institution on the continent. She's heard fascinating things about the work being done there. Could she possibly see it?

The Barovs are caught between refusing (which confirms something worth hiding) and allowing access (which risks exposure). They choose controlled access — a guided tour, carefully curated, showing only what they want her to see.

### Inside Scholomance

Jaina enters Scholomance through the front door. Not a raid, not a siege — a guided visit. The Barovs walk her through what they present as a "scholarly academy." It is, in a sense — Kel'Thuzad originally modeled it on the Kirin Tor's structure. Libraries, lecture halls, research chambers. The architecture is the Barovs' ancestral home, repurposed.

But Jaina is an arcane prodigy. She can feel the necromantic energy in the walls like heat from an oven. The wards are sophisticated — layered, interlocking, designed by someone who understood magical theory at the highest level. She recognizes the craftsmanship. It's Kirin Tor methodology, applied to necromancy. The intellectual elegance is unmistakable.

She asks the right questions with the wrong (from the Barovs' perspective) underlying motivation. She reads the ward structures while they watch her face. Her guides show her the presentable rooms; she maps the building's arcane signature, which tells her about the rooms they're NOT showing.

At some point, the pretense thins. She's seen enough. She knows what this place is. The question is what she does next.

### The Decision

Jaina could leave. Report to Antonidas. Come back with a force. The smart thing — the thing Sylvanas would tell her to do.

She doesn't. Because Kel'Thuzad is HERE. The architect. The man who designed the plague that's killing a continent. The former Council of Six member whose brilliance built an engine of genocide. And Jaina — arrogant, used to being the smartest person in the room — wants to FACE him. Not just stop him. Understand him. Prove that his logic is wrong. Prove that her mind is the equal of his.

She goes deeper. Alone.

## Chapter 11: The Fuse
**POV:** Sylvanas
**Setting:** The road south → Arthas's camp near Hearthglen
**Key characters:** Sylvanas, Cyndia, Arthas Menethil, Captain Falric, (Uther nearby)
**Stakes:** Sylvanas lights the fuse of Arthas's canonical descent. She doesn't know what she's starting.
**Partnership phase:** Operating solo. The partnership's absence is felt as tactical deficit.

### Without Jaina

Sylvanas operating alone for the first time since Ch 3. The absence is tactical, not emotional — she registers it as a gap in her operational capability. No arcane sweep. No ward detection. No magical analysis. She moves the way she moved before Jaina: faster, more cautious at chokepoints, relying on Cyndia as sole scout instead of a two-ranger pair. The formation is wrong. She compensates.

### Finding Arthas

The Mal'Ganis breadcrumb was accurate — Arthas is in the field near Hearthglen with a column from Capital City. He's been investigating Blackrock orc raids (Mal'Ganis's manufactured distraction — the orc raids were designed to keep Arthas chasing the wrong threat). But reports from Hearthglen are concerning him: something wrong with the harvests, illness spreading, people dying in ways that don't match any plague the healers recognize.

Sylvanas approaches the camp. A Ranger-General of Silvermoon requesting audience with a prince of Lordaeron — diplomatic protocols, chain of command, the formalities she'd normally delegate to someone who cares about them. Falric handles the introduction. Arthas receives her.

### The Meeting

The meeting is tense. Sylvanas and Arthas have encountered each other once before (Ch 6, Strahnbrad) — a brief, inconclusive interaction where he was focused on orcs and she was focused on the plague. Now she's in his camp, uninvited, with intelligence he doesn't have.

She presents the evidence. Not all of it — the things that matter. The grain network. The contamination mechanism. The Cult's infrastructure. The scope. She leaves out Kel'Thuzad (Jaina's problem), leaves out the Lich King (they haven't confirmed that yet), focuses on what a military commander needs: WHERE the threat is, HOW it works, HOW BIG it is.

Arthas is skeptical at first. Then his skepticism curdles into something worse: certainty. He BELIEVES her. Not because her evidence is overwhelming (it is), but because it explains things he's been seeing for weeks — the orc raids that don't quite make tactical sense, the reports from Hearthglen, the illness spreading through villages his army has passed. The pieces click. And once they click for Arthas, he acts. Immediately, decisively, without the measured analysis that Jaina would apply.

He grasps the conclusions without the analytical foundation. Speed of certainty, absence of doubt.

### The Fuse

Arthas decides to investigate Hearthglen personally. He's already mobilizing. Uther will follow. The military machinery of Lordaeron is about to collide with the plague at speed, and the collision will produce Stratholme.

Sylvanas watches him give the orders. She reads his face: conviction, anger, the particular kind of righteousness that doesn't leave room for second thoughts. She recognizes the type — she's commanded for three centuries, she knows what happens when a powerful person stops listening.

She's done the smart thing. She's aimed institutional power at the threat. The prince of Lordaeron will handle the Cult's military infrastructure while she and Jaina continue the investigation.

She doesn't know what she's started.

### Redirect

Word reaches Sylvanas (via Cyndia's Farstrider relay) about Jaina's movements — she went to the Barov estate, she went to Caer Darrow. Sylvanas reads between the lines: Jaina found something, Jaina went inside, Jaina is operating without backup in enemy territory. The tactical assessment is immediate: her partner is exposed.

She moves.

## Chapter 12: The Necromancer
**POV:** Jaina
**Setting:** Deep inside Scholomance — Kel'Thuzad's inner sanctum
**Key characters:** Jaina, Kel'Thuzad
**Stakes:** Jaina faces her dark mirror alone. The academic duel. The moment of weakness.
**Partnership phase:** The partnership's absence at its most dangerous

### Kel'Thuzad

He's waiting. Not fleeing, not fighting — waiting. With the calm of a man who has already won and knows it.

Kel'Thuzad: former member of the Kirin Tor's Council of Six. Antonidas's colleague. A genius who was expelled for pursuing necromantic research the rest of the Council considered abhorrent. He traveled to Northrend, pledged his soul to the Lich King, returned with power and purpose, built the Cult of the Damned from nothing, and engineered a plague that will destroy a kingdom.

He recognizes Jaina. Not personally — by reputation. Antonidas's protégée. The prodigy. The one everyone in Dalaran whispered about. He's genuinely interested.

### The Academic Duel

This is not a confrontation — it's a conversation. Kel'Thuzad doesn't threaten Jaina. He *teaches.* He explains the Scourge with the intellectual passion of a researcher presenting his life's work. The Lich King. The Burning Legion. The plague's mechanism. The scope of what's coming. He uses the register of a Kirin Tor lecture — formal, precise, eager to be understood. Because that's who he was, before everything else: a scholar. A teacher. A man who found something extraordinary and wanted someone smart enough to appreciate it.

Jaina engages. Not because she agrees — because her mind does what it always does: it follows logic. It probes. It tests. She argues with him the way she would argue with a thesis defense — attacking the foundations, probing the assumptions, looking for the flaw. And Kel'Thuzad WELCOMES the argument. He's been surrounded by Cult acolytes who can barely grasp the basics. Here, finally, is a mind worthy of the conversation.

The duel escalates. Jaina pushes harder: the ethics, the suffering, the genocide. Kel'Thuzad answers with terrifying coherence. Death is transformation. The Scourge doesn't destroy — it perfects. The living are fragile, brief, inefficient. The undead are eternal, obedient, unified. He's not wrong about the facts — he's wrong about what the facts MEAN. But the logic, the chain of reasoning from premise to conclusion, is airtight IF you accept the premises.

And for one terrible moment — Jaina follows the argument past the point where she should stop. Not agrees. *Follows.* Sees the logic. Understands how someone brilliant arrives at this conclusion. Understands how she COULD arrive at this conclusion, given different premises, different losses, a different century of frustration with institutional failure.

The three-way mirror at maximum power: Kel'Thuzad is Jaina without partnership. Brilliance without connection. Conviction without trust. Being right, alone, all the way to monstrousness.

### The Moment

Jaina catches herself. But not before the moment happens — the heartbeat where the logic was beautiful, where the architecture of the argument was more compelling than the horror of its conclusions. The scholar in her, the part that loves elegant systems, the part that can't help following a chain of reasoning to its end — that part listened too long.

She knows it happened. And she can't unknow it.

## Chapter 13: The Arrival
**POV:** Sylvanas
**Setting:** Caer Darrow → inside Scholomance → Kel'Thuzad's inner sanctum
**Key characters:** Sylvanas, Cyndia, Cult defenders, Jaina, Kel'Thuzad
**Stakes:** Sylvanas arrives. She reads the room. The crack opens.
**Partnership phase:** The test that damages the partnership

### Approach

Sylvanas reaches Caer Darrow. The Barov estate is quiet — the family either fled or were never truly in control. Cyndia scouts the fortress's exterior while Sylvanas reads the signs: Jaina entered through the front door and didn't come out. The building's magical signature, even to Sylvanas's non-mage senses, is wrong — a low hum in the walls, a vibration at the edge of perception.

She goes in the Farstrider way. Not through the front door.

### Through Scholomance

Sylvanas moves through Scholomance alone (Cyndia holds the exit — the last thread to the outside). She reads the building as terrain: sight lines, chokepoints, fall-back positions, kill zones. The necromantic infrastructure is secondary to her — she can't analyze it, but she can read what it DOES: dead things that used to be people, standing in corridors like furniture. She avoids them. She moves.

The deeper she goes, the more wrong it gets. The architecture shifts from "converted fortress" to something that was designed by a mind that doesn't think about human comfort. The hallways are too long. The rooms have no windows. The doors face the wrong direction for any defensive logic she's ever encountered. This is a place built by someone who doesn't care about the living.

### The Room

She finds them.

Jaina standing. Kel'Thuzad sitting. No weapons drawn. No combat in progress. Just two people talking. The necromancer's posture: relaxed, engaged, the body language of a professor in office hours. Jaina's posture: leaning forward. Listening. *Engaged.*

Sylvanas reads the room in a heartbeat.

What she sees: her partner — the woman she's trusted with her flanks for weeks, the woman whose arcane sweep she learned to wait for, the woman whose analysis saved her life in Andorhal — standing in the sanctum of the enemy, intellectually engaged in conversation. Not under duress. Not feigning compliance. Listening with the focus that Sylvanas has learned to recognize as Jaina's deepest mode of attention.

What she DOESN'T see: the internal fight. The moment that already passed. The horror that Jaina already caught and is already processing. Sylvanas arrives after the worst of it — but the posture, the engagement, the fact that Jaina hasn't attacked or fled — tells a story. And Sylvanas is a three-hundred-year-old intelligence operative who has never, in three centuries, been wrong about reading a room.

### The Crack

Jaina turns. Sees Sylvanas. The expression on Sylvanas's face — not anger, not betrayal. Something worse: recalculation. The fundamental math of partnership being re-run. *Can I trust this person?*

Kel'Thuzad observes this with academic interest. He says something that makes it worse — acknowledges Jaina's intellectual engagement, compliments the quality of her questions. He's not lying. He means it. And meaning it makes it devastating.

They kill him. Together. The partnership operating as a unit, seamless, the behavioral integration from Ch 9 fully activated — Jaina's barriers, Sylvanas's arrows, the seams invisible. It takes seconds.

Kel'Thuzad dies with scholarly calm, as if proving a theorem. His last words are an observation about the plague's distribution, not a plea for life.

He is dead. The crack is open.

## Chapter 14: The Revelation
**POV:** Jaina
**Setting:** Scholomance → the road out → the weight of what happened
**Key characters:** Jaina, Sylvanas, Cyndia (waiting at the exit)
**Stakes:** What Kel'Thuzad revealed. What the crack costs. Where the investigation goes next.
**Partnership phase:** TESTS OF FAITH → SAVING EACH OTHER (damaged but functional)

### The Intel

Kel'Thuzad told Jaina everything before Sylvanas arrived. Not under duress — because he enjoyed teaching, and because he believed it wouldn't matter:
- **The Lich King.** An entity imprisoned in Northrend. Created the plague. Reaching out telepathically to recruit mortals. The Cult of the Damned is his instrument, not the other way around.
- **The Burning Legion.** The cosmic force behind the Lich King. The plague is one operation in a campaign that spans worlds. Lordaeron is a stepping stone.
- **The scope.** The grain is in the food supply. Cities, towns, villages — everyone who ate bread from Andorhal's granaries in the last months is a walking corpse. The question isn't whether they'll turn — it's when.

Jaina delivers this intel to Sylvanas. Clinically, precisely, in the register of an intelligence briefing. She does not mention the moment. Sylvanas does not ask.

### The Crack's Weight

The damage is not dramatic. There's no confrontation, no argument, no accusation. The damage is operational: Sylvanas's timing shifts. The fraction-of-a-second trust delay that didn't exist before — the extra beat before she commits to Jaina's assessment, the faint hesitation before she turns her back. Micro-calibrations. The kind of thing only someone who has been operating in perfect sync would notice.

Jaina notices.

She can't explain what happened in that room because the truth is worse than any explanation: she wasn't being manipulated. She wasn't under a spell. She was following a chain of logic because that's what her mind does, and for one heartbeat the logic led somewhere monstrous, and she followed it there. Her Lie — "being right is enough" — almost killed her. Not physically. Something worse.

### What Sylvanas Doesn't Say

Sylvanas learned something in that room. Not about Kel'Thuzad — she didn't need to understand his arguments to understand the threat. She learned something about Jaina: that Jaina's greatest strength (the mind that follows every argument to its end) is also a vulnerability. That the woman she trusts with arcane sweeps and ward analysis can be engaged — not turned, not corrupted, but ENGAGED — by the right intellectual opponent.

She files this. She doesn't speak it. She adjusts.

### The Road

They leave Scholomance. Cyndia falls in. The formation is the same — Cyndia left, Jaina center, Sylvanas ranging. Except Cyndia checks the right flank twice (the ghost of Lyriel), and Sylvanas's operational spacing is a half-step wider than it was before they split (the ghost of what she saw in that room).

Word arrives via Farstrider runner: Hearthglen has been overwhelmed. The contaminated grain hit the city's supply before anyone knew what to look for. The dead are rising. Prince Arthas is already moving toward Stratholme.

The fuse Sylvanas lit is burning.

## Arc Impact on Subsequent Chapters

### What shifts:

**Old Ch 13 "The Prince"** → Partially absorbed into new Ch 11 "The Fuse" (Sylvanas meets Arthas). What remains: Arthas arriving at Hearthglen, the three-way friction with Uther, the partnership reveal to outsiders. This becomes new **Ch 15 "The Prince"** but needs revision — Arthas now has intelligence from Sylvanas (Ch 11), not from Kel'Thuzad's death. He's been investigating since she told him. The collision at Hearthglen is the consequence of the fuse she lit.

**Old Ch 14 "The Split"** → The "choice to be partners" moment shifts. In the old version, they choose each other over institutional loyalty. In the new version, they choose each other DESPITE the crack — which is stronger. This becomes new **Ch 16** but the emotional context is different: the split/choice happens with the crack still open.

**Stratholme chapters** → Move from Ch 15-16 to Ch 17-18. The crack adds weight: Sylvanas watching Stratholme knowing SHE sent Arthas here. Jaina watching it knowing her moment with Kel'Thuzad parallels what's happening to Arthas (conviction without partnership → monstrousness).

**Total chapter count:** 24 → 26. All subsequent chapter numbers shift +2.

### Partnership arc adjustment:

Phase 3 (Tests of Faith) now covers Ch 9-14 instead of Ch 9-10. The "test" is much bigger — not just tactical mistakes, but the first real separation and the crack that results. Phase 4 (Saving Each Other) starts in the new Ch 15 with the partnership damaged but functional, and Stratholme as the event that welds it back together through shared witness to atrocity.

### The Mal'Ganis thread:

Disguise sightings so far:
1. South of Millhaven (Ch 3-4 area) — refugee with "May the stars keep your road"
2. **Ch 9** — retreating figure near the road, tells Cyndia about the prince at Hearthglen. Same farewell.

The pattern: Mal'Ganis is steering events. He led Sylvanas to Arthas because the Legion NEEDS Arthas to charge into the plague investigation. Every disguise sighting is a manipulation that looks like happenstance.
