# The Tribunal Mechanism

**Purpose:** How the political and legal aftermath of Ch 23's throne room works. The prosecution's narrative. The evidence that was competence reframed as conspiracy. The mechanism that turns the investigation's thoroughness into the case for its condemnation.

**Relevant chapters:** Ch 24 (The Tribunal), Ch 25 (stinger/setup for Book 2)
**Dependencies:** Ch 23's throne room outcome (Terenas dead, Arthas dead, Frostmourne's bearer-bond ruptured while its deeper tether to Ner'zhul remains intact), the dismissed warnings from Ch 22, Sylvanas's defiance of the Convocation (Ch 19-20)

---

## Part 1: The Legal Framework

### Jurisdiction

The throne room is Lordaeron sovereign territory. The crime is regicide — the murder of King Terenas Menethil II. Under Lordaeron martial law (invoked automatically upon the death of a reigning monarch), jurisdiction falls to a tribunal of senior magistrates appointed by the surviving court. The tribunal operates under wartime authority: no jury, no appeal, accelerated proceedings. The framework exists for exactly this situation — the assassination of a king — and it is thorough, ancient, and merciless.

The complication: the accused are foreign nationals. Sylvanas is a citizen of Quel'Thalas, holding the rank of Ranger-General — a head-of-military position in a sovereign allied state. Jaina is a citizen of Kul Tiras and a member (currently suspended) of the Kirin Tor. Under normal diplomatic conditions, both would be subject to extradition proceedings or diplomatic negotiation before any trial. But martial law suspends normal diplomatic conditions. The tribunal has authority to try anyone present in Lordaeron territory at the time of the regicide, regardless of nationality.

The Convocation's disavowal (Ch 19-20) becomes relevant here: Silvermoon formally severed its connection to Sylvanas when she defied the recall. The disavowal means Quel'Thalas will not claim her as a diplomatic asset. The Ranger-General of Silvermoon, operating in defiance of her own government's orders, has no sovereign protection. She is, in the tribunal's eyes, a rogue military officer operating on foreign soil without authorization.

Jaina's position is equally exposed. The Kirin Tor's last formal lane in Ch 22 established that Dalaran was aware of the "Northrend intelligence" but treated it as unverified even while Antonidas physically moved a tiny advisory cell to Capital City. Jaina's presence in Lordaeron — operating without Kirin Tor sanction, having severed her formal connection when she followed the investigation past Dalaran's approved scope — means the Kirin Tor cannot claim her without admitting they knew about the threat and failed to act. Antonidas believes her. Antonidas can stand in the hall, object, and ask for time. He still cannot publicly defend her in a sworn official capacity without implicating the Kirin Tor in the intelligence failure.

### The Charges

1. **Regicide** — the murder of King Terenas Menethil II. The prosecution's theory: S&J entered the throne room with the intent to assassinate the king, using the "Northrend threat" as a pretext to gain access.
2. **Attempted regicide** — the attack on Prince Arthas Menethil. The prosecution frames the three-school casting as an unprovoked magical assault on the crown prince.
3. **Conspiracy** — months of coordinated activity (the investigation itself) reframed as preparation for the assassination. Every chapter of the investigation becomes evidence of planning.
4. **Espionage** — a foreign military officer (Sylvanas) operating on Lordaeron soil without authorization, gathering intelligence on the kingdom's defenses, military positions, and political vulnerabilities.
5. **Possession of forbidden weapons** — Frostmourne. The prosecution argues that S&J brought the blade to Lordaeron (they didn't — Arthas did) or that their knowledge of its properties proves involvement with its creation.
6. **Necromantic practice** — guilt by association with the Scholomance investigation (Ch 9-14). S&J spent weeks studying necromantic techniques, interviewing Cult operatives, and handling cursed materials. The prosecution reframes this as training, not investigation.

## Part 2: The Prosecution's Narrative

The prosecution's case is elegant because it is built entirely from true facts arranged to support a false conclusion. Every piece of evidence is real. Every connection is documented. The only thing that is wrong is the direction of causation.

### The Theory

Two foreign agents — an elven military officer operating outside her chain of command and a young mage operating outside her academic institution — spent months moving through Lordaeron territory, gathering intelligence on the plague, the Cult of the Damned, and the kingdom's defenses. They recruited Prince Arthas into their operation (Ch 3), directed his investigation toward Northrend (Ch 11), and followed him there — not to stop him, but to ensure the blade's retrieval. When Arthas returned to Capital City with Frostmourne, the agents returned separately, having sent warnings ahead (Ch 22) — not to prevent the prince's return, but to establish an alibi of concern. They entered Capital City without announcing themselves (still Ch 22), infiltrated the palace (Ch 23), and were present in the throne room when the king was murdered.

The theory requires the tribunal to believe that S&J orchestrated Arthas's corruption as a weapon against Lordaeron. It is absurd. It is also more plausible than the truth, because the truth requires believing that a single cursed sword can turn a prince into a death knight, that the Lich King is a real entity directing events from a frozen throne, and that two women with no political standing uncovered a continental conspiracy that every intelligence service in the Alliance missed.

The truth sounds like madness. The prosecution sounds like statecraft.

### The Evidence, Weaponized

**Every warning becomes an alibi:**
- The carrier pigeons (Ch 22 Beat 7): "They sent warnings they knew would be ignored — not to save the king, but to document their own apparent concern."
- The sending-stone messages to Antonidas: "They informed the Archmage — a co-conspirator or a useful fool — to create a trail of apparent diligence."
- The Farstrider relay (Ch 22): "They maintained a military intelligence network on Lordaeron soil — the operational footprint of a covert operation, not an investigation."

**Every correct decision becomes premeditation:**
- The Scholomance investigation (Ch 9-14): "They spent weeks studying necromancy — the same discipline used to create Frostmourne. Training, not investigation."
- The pursuit to Northrend (Ch 19-20): "They followed Arthas to the blade's location — not to prevent retrieval, but to witness it. To confirm their weapon was in place."
- The return crossing (Ch 22): "They raced back to Lordaeron with suspicious urgency — the urgency of agents whose timeline required them in Capital City before the operation's final phase."

**Every act of defiance becomes evidence of allegiance elsewhere:**
- Sylvanas's defiance of the Convocation recall (Ch 19): "The Ranger-General refused a direct order from her sovereign government. She chose the operation over her oath. Who issues orders that supersede a sovereign's?"
- Jaina's departure from Kirin Tor scope: "The mage operated beyond her institution's authorization for months. No student of the Kirin Tor acts with this independence unless directed by a higher authority."

**The partnership itself becomes evidence:**
- "An elven Ranger-General and a human Kirin Tor mage do not form an operational partnership by accident. The cross-faction coordination, the shared intelligence, the complementary skill set — this is a designed team, not a chance pairing."

## Part 3: The Defense Failures

### Antonidas

The Archmage knows the truth. He believed Jaina's reports from the beginning, spent the merged return chapter trying to mobilize the Kirin Tor through every lane left to him, and then physically brought himself and two Kirin Tor magi to Capital City when the paper lanes failed. He filed formal concerns, invoked alliance protocols, requested emergency sessions, and reached the palace margins in person. All of it still failed against the wall of Lordaeron's celebration and the legal machinery that follows it.

At the tribunal, Antonidas could testify that Jaina's intelligence was accurate, that the Kirin Tor received and credited it, that the formal responses to Lordaeron were genuine attempts to prevent the catastrophe, and that he himself was in the city before the murder trying to force access. This testimony would demolish the prosecution's "manufactured alibi" theory.

But Antonidas cannot testify in any sanctioned way without exposing the Kirin Tor. His testimony would reveal: the Kirin Tor knew about the Northrend threat, moved people physically, and still failed to act with sufficient force to prevent the regicide. The Archmage's defense of Jaina is also the Kirin Tor's confession of impotence. The political cost — the revelation that Dalaran's most powerful mages knew a king was in danger, came to the city, and still could not stop it — is one the Kirin Tor council will not pay.

Antonidas is silenced not by distance but by loyalty. He can stand in the gallery. He can ask for continuance. He can request expert examination of Frostmourne. He cannot save his student without condemning his institution.

### Uther

The Lightbringer is the prosecution's most dangerous vulnerability. Uther was at Stratholme (Ch 17). He saw Arthas's descent firsthand. He broke with the prince publicly, was stripped of command, and traveled to Capital City to warn the king — a warning the palace's celebration logic refused to hear before the throne room ever opened.

Uther's testimony would establish: Arthas was already compromised before the throne room, the prince's behavior escalated progressively from Stratholme through Northrend, and S&J's warnings were consistent with what Uther himself observed. The paladin's credibility is enormous — decades of service, unimpeachable honor, no connection to foreign powers.

The prosecution's counter: Uther was dismissed from command for insubordination. He is a disgraced officer with a personal grudge against the prince who superseded him. His testimony is the bitterness of a mentor who lost control of his student, not the analysis of a credible witness.

The dismissal is thin. It would not survive scrutiny if the tribunal were genuinely seeking truth. But the tribunal is not seeking truth — it is seeking resolution. The kingdom needs someone to blame for its dead king, and two foreign agents are a cleaner answer than "the prince was corrupted by an ancient evil and there was nothing anyone could do."

### Lor'themar Theron

Sylvanas's second-in-command. The Farstrider who would take command if the Ranger-General were removed. Lor'themar knows Sylvanas's character, her operational record, her motivation. He could testify to her integrity, her chain of reasoning, the genuine nature of the investigation.

Lor'themar arrives too late. The Convocation's disavowal delayed any Silvermoon response. By the time Lor'themar receives word (through the same Farstrider relay that Cyndia maintained) and reaches Capital City, the tribunal is already in session. His testimony, if admitted, would be powerful — but the tribunal is operating under martial law's accelerated timeline, and late witnesses require a motion to reopen that the prosecution will oppose.

### Vereesa Windrunner

Sylvanas's sister delivered the Convocation's disavowal in Ch 20. She is a potential character witness — a family member who can speak to Sylvanas's nature and motivation. But Vereesa delivered the disavowal. Her testimony would require her to explain why she carried a message she disagreed with, why Silvermoon's response was political rather than substantive, and why the Convocation chose to sever ties with its Ranger-General rather than investigate her claims. The testimony opens wounds within the Windrunner family and within Silvermoon's political establishment that neither entity wants opened.

## Part 4: Frostmourne as Evidence

The blade is the prosecution's centerpiece and its greatest risk.

### The Prosecution's Use

Frostmourne is found in the throne room after the confrontation. Jaina's three-school casting does more than rattle it: it ruptures Arthas's clean bearer-channel while leaving the blade's deeper tether to Ner'zhul intact. The result is not a harmless relic or a severed weapon. It is an unheld blade still carrying the Lich King's pressure, still consuming, radiating, and seeking. The blade remains actively hostile and any qualified mage can detect that hostility without understanding it.

The prosecution presents the blade as evidence of S&J's involvement with dark magic:
- "The accused demonstrated intimate knowledge of the blade's properties — knowledge that could only be obtained through study or collaboration with its creators."
- "The accused performed a complex magical operation on the blade during the throne room confrontation — an operation that requires preparation, practice, and expertise in necromantic binding. This is not the improvised response of innocent bystanders."
- "The blade's presence in the throne room — brought by Prince Arthas, whom the accused recruited into their operation in Ch 3 — is consistent with the prosecution's theory that the prince was a tool, not a perpetrator."

### The Risk

Frostmourne is evidence that cuts both ways. Any serious examination of the blade would reveal: it is an artifact of the Lich King's creation, it predates S&J's involvement by millennia, its consuming field is actively hostile to living beings (making "collaboration" with its wielder implausible), and its binding patterns are consistent with Jaina's testimony about the Scourge's magical methodology.

But serious examination requires experts willing to study a necromantic weapon. The Kirin Tor could provide such experts — Antonidas even brought two magi to the city — but he cannot commit them to a sanctioned examination without exposing the Kirin Tor's prior knowledge, and the tribunal can frame any unsanctioned handling as evidence tampering. Independent mages could be consulted — but the tribunal's accelerated timeline does not accommodate lengthy magical analysis.

The prosecution needs the blade to remain mysterious. The defense needs the blade to be understood. The tribunal's timeline favors the prosecution.

## Part 5: The Verdict and Its Function

### The Likely Outcome

The tribunal finds S&J guilty. Not because the evidence is conclusive — it isn't — but because the political need for a clean answer outweighs the judicial need for truth. A kingdom with a dead king needs someone to punish. Two foreign agents who were present in the throne room, who have no sovereign protection, who can be convicted without implicating any Lordaeron institution — they are the cleanest possible resolution.

The verdict is: guilty of conspiracy and regicide. The sentence is: death, commuted to indefinite imprisonment pending appeal. The commutation is political — executing a Ranger-General and a Kirin Tor mage creates diplomatic complications that the fragile post-regicide government wants to avoid. Imprisonment allows the tribunal to claim justice while the actual political situation develops.

### The Thematic Function

The tribunal is the investigation's cruelest irony. S&J spent twenty-two chapters being right — tracking the plague, identifying Kel'Thuzad, finding Frostmourne, understanding Arthas's corruption, racing home to stop the assassination. They were right about everything. They were right early enough to kill the death knight and break the prince's hold on the blade. And the very thoroughness that made them right — the warnings sent, the intelligence gathered, the channels exhausted — becomes the evidence that convicts them.

The investigation's competence is indistinguishable from conspiracy, because competent conspiracy looks exactly like what S&J did: gathering intelligence, recruiting assets, maintaining communication networks, operating outside official channels, arriving at the scene of the crime with foreknowledge and specialized capabilities.

Being right, alone, with evidence, in a world that does not want to hear — this is the story's engine. The tribunal is where the engine produces its final, most bitter output: the women who killed the death knight, broke his hold on the blade, and still could not make the room believe them are tried for the destruction they failed to fully prevent.

### Book 2 Implications

The tribunal verdict sets the conditions for Book 2:
- S&J are imprisoned, stripped of rank and standing
- Their allies (Antonidas, Uther, Lor'themar) are politically compromised
- Frostmourne is in Lordaeron custody — still tethered to Ner'zhul, still dangerous, poorly understood, and actively seeking
- The Scourge threat is not resolved — Arthas is dead, but the plague, the Cult, and the wider operation continue without him
- The partnership is tested by separation, by the weight of unjust conviction, by the question of whether being right was worth the cost
- The escape/exoneration arc requires S&J to prove the truth that the tribunal refused to hear — while imprisoned, while separated, while the world they saved moves on without them
