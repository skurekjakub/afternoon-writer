# The Partnership Arc — Sylvanas × Jaina

**Arc type:** Competence collision → grudging respect → tests of faith → mutual rescue → peak synchronization
**NOT romance.** This is a professional partnership forged under pressure. The intimacy is tactical, not emotional.
**POV rule:** The arc advances through the POV woman's revised assessment of the other woman. In Jaina POV chapters, the weight falls on Jaina realizing Sylvanas is not a blunt military obstructionist. In Sylvanas POV chapters, the weight falls on Sylvanas reclassifying Jaina from dangerous academic liability to load-bearing specialist.

---

## Phase 1: Threat Assessment / Contained Asset (~Ch 1–4)

### Sylvanas's View of Jaina
A diplomatic disaster in violet robes. Not a research partner — a trespasser caught inside the ward network, claiming leyline sensitivity as justification for a sovereign violation. Sylvanas doesn't know Jaina yet; she knows what Jaina REPRESENTS: Antonidas's protégée, monitored threat classification, the Kirin Tor's intelligence apparatus walking into Quel'Thalas uninvited. In Ch 1 she's a security incident. In Ch 2 she becomes a reluctant asset — the first person who provides a coherent explanation for the anomalies Sylvanas will discover at Millhaven. "Contained asset" is generous. "Prisoner with useful information" is more accurate.

**Key expression:** Sylvanas internally calls her "the mage" for the first several chapters. Never "Jaina." The name shift is a marker — when Sylvanas starts using her name in internal narration, the contempt has cracked.

**Specific contempt beats:**
- Ch01 (The Border): Sylvanas receives word of a captured human mage inside the ward boundary while wrapping up a troll patrol. Rides south furious. Recognizes Jaina Proudmoore — monitored threat classification — and the situation escalates from trespasser to sovereign incident.
- Ch02 (The Mage): During interrogation, Jaina speaks in simplified register — shorter words, fewer clauses, the downshift academics use with non-specialists. Sylvanas catches it and files it without comment. Later, when the wildlife attack hits on the road south, her read remains only half-wrong: the mage has poor field instincts, but she can improvise necessary magic fast enough that the formation has to make working space for her instead of merely hauling a prisoner.
- Ch03–04: After Millhaven and the sick-village follow-up, Sylvanas is still clocking academic recklessness — Jaina will kneel in wrong places and trust the room too much — but she is now also forced to admit that the mage turns magical chaos into usable threat intelligence faster than ground-read alone can.

### Jaina's View of Sylvanas
In even-numbered chapters (Jaina's POV), we see her assessment directly. She views Sylvanas as a military obstructionist — dangerous, precise, and fundamentally uninterested in the science of what Jaina has found. The custody arrangement is a power play: Sylvanas keeping Jaina under military control rather than treating her as a subject-matter expert. Jaina simplifies her register not from malice but from assumption — she's never met a soldier who tracked the methodology behind arcane contamination readings. The Ranger-General seems like a blunt instrument. A very sharp blunt instrument, but the wrong tool for this investigation.

**What Jaina actually thinks (shown directly in her chapters):**
- Sylvanas is a blunt instrument. Good at capturing people. Not useful for the kind of investigation Jaina needs to conduct.
- The custody arrangement is a power play — Sylvanas keeping Jaina under military control rather than treating her as a subject-matter expert.
- The fury is unnerving. Not shouting, not posturing — the Ranger-General's anger is precise and cold and doesn't leave room for the possibility that Jaina might be right.
- Sylvanas's tactical observations are obvious to anyone who's read a basic text on field operations. Jaina's read twelve.

**Specific contempt beats (direct POV):**
- Ch02: Jaina's frustration during interrogation is physical — she's been saying the same thing for eight hours and nobody will listen. The leylines are screaming. The ward-stones are compromised. Nobody cares because she's a trespasser.
- Ch02: She delivers conclusions in simplified register because she genuinely believes she's talking to someone who won't understand the full version. The register correction (Sylvanas revealing she read the confiscated map) lands like a slap. The moment she realizes she's been condescending to someone who was paying attention.
- Ch02: The road attack forces Jaina to watch Sylvanas run a live fight as geometry instead of panic — riders, lanes, kill order, working space for the mage when arrows stop being enough. It is the first time Jaina sees battlefield command rather than custodial force.
- Ch03: At Millhaven, the discomfort isn't that Sylvanas is wrong — it's that Sylvanas arrived at the same conclusion through an entirely different discipline, and that discipline has value Jaina never accounted for.

### The Cracks
- **Ch02 (The Mage), Beat 9:** Jaina's register shift to condescension during the ride south — Sylvanas reveals she read the confiscated map and understood the methodology. Jaina's register doesn't shift back, but the silence afterward has a different quality. First hairline crack.
- **Ch02 (The Mage), Beats 11-12:** Deer plus wolves. Jaina sees Sylvanas turn chaos into a workable combat shape; Sylvanas, without saying it, starts treating Jaina's magic as necessary field capability instead of theoretical baggage.
- **Ch03 (Millhaven), Beat 7:** The tracking pass. Sylvanas realizes Jaina's magical read can narrow the field problem the way spoor narrows it for a ranger; Jaina's usefulness becomes concrete instead of abstract.
- **Ch04 (The Sick Village), Beats 2 and 7:** Jaina watches Sylvanas win access with tone and timing rather than force, then lay logistics beside Jaina's mechanism until the two halves click. The investigative partnership starts here, not later.
- **Ch06 (The Pattern), Beats 4-6:** Arthas's dismissal teaches Jaina that Sylvanas reads command psychology and institutional self-protection faster than she reads people. The next shift is no longer about intelligence versus stupidity; it is about which kind of intelligence sees the human obstacle first.

## Phase 2: Grudging Respect (~Ch 5–8)

### The Grain Road
The investigation forces cooperation. They can't track the plague's distribution with just magic (Jaina) or just tactical analysis (Sylvanas). The contamination pattern is BOTH — arcane mechanism distributed through military-grade logistics.

**Key shift:** Sylvanas stops calling her "the mage" in internal narration. The first use of "Jaina" is unremarked — it happens mid-thought during a tactical assessment, as naturally as using any other proper noun. No fanfare. Just a name where a title used to be.

**Jaina's shift (visible in her POV chapters):** Jaina stops register-shifting. She starts delivering conclusions at full complexity and then waiting for Sylvanas's response — not to check comprehension, but because she genuinely wants the tactical angle. The pause that used to be pedagogical becomes collaborative. In Jaina's chapters, we see this from the inside: the moment she realizes she's waiting for the tactical read not because she should, but because the analysis feels incomplete without it.

**Specific respect beats:**
- **The sick village (~Ch 4):** Jaina's analysis says the grain has already landed, but the people are still alive and only beginning to fail. Sylvanas's instinct says quarantine: seal the village, restrict movement, keep the contamination from spreading. Jaina hesitates because the place still looks savable. Sylvanas forces the arithmetic into the open. It is not cruelty; it is field timing. Jaina hears it and, for the first time, does not flinch from the logic.
- **The north-road trader:** On the road out of Millhaven, an old grain seller is visibly shaken to find the village silent. Sylvanas works him like a scout report - calm, precise, no wasted force - and gets more from his surprise than from his words. He confirms that the settlements ahead are still trading and only lightly touched by sickness, which makes Millhaven read as an escalation point rather than the regional norm. Jaina watches the exchange and recognizes a skill she does not have: pulling intelligence out of a witness by pressure applied at exactly the right angle.
- **The Cult watcher:** Sylvanas spots a watcher who doesn't flinch. In the debrief, she explains to Jaina how she identified the operative — gait, posture, the stillness that comes from waiting rather than resting. "Every person has a reason for standing still. His reason was us." Jaina's response: she calculates the watcher's sightline radius and maps every position they've been in during the last two hours, reconstructing what the operative could and couldn't have observed. Sylvanas reads the map and adds tactical implications Jaina's geometry missed. They're building on each other's work for the first time.

### Andorhal
- **The infiltration:** They pose as supply inspectors. Sylvanas hates it — disguise, deception, bureaucratic pretense. But the mission requires it. Jaina handles the institutional navigation (she knows how Kirin Tor inspections work, adapts the cover). Sylvanas provides the threat assessment (which doors to check, which guards are real vs. Cult-planted, escape routes). They're dividing labor by aptitude without discussing it.
- **The Cult discovery:** When they find the Cult's grain-poisoning operation, both are horrified — but their horror maps differently. Jaina sees the mechanism (how the plague agent is introduced, the alchemical precision). Sylvanas sees the organizational structure (how many operatives, the command hierarchy, the logistics of running a covert operation inside a functioning city). Together they have the full city-level picture Andorhal can give them. Alone, each had half.
- **Kel'Thuzad named, not seen:** They never get a clean sight of the architect. The name surfaces in fragments after the capture operation and the city's kill-switch response. Jaina recognizes what it means institutionally - Antonidas's expelled failure now sitting somewhere north of Andorhal - while Sylvanas reads what it means tactically: a disciplined mind high enough in the chain to stay unseen and close enough to silence a compromised operative mid-sentence. Their reads arrive together. This is the moment grudging respect crystallizes into dependence.

## Phase 3: Tests of Faith (~Ch 9–14 — The Scholomance Arc)

### The Rule
Both make mistakes. Both mistakes stem from their core identity — the very thing that makes them excellent is what makes them wrong. The rescues that follow prove that the partner's skillset isn't supplementary — it's essential. Then the split: operating alone for the first time, each faces their dark mirror. The crack opens.

### Sylvanas's Mistake: The Soldier's Blunder

**When:** During the assault on Kel'Thuzad's sanctum (~Ch 9).

**What:** Sylvanas reads the tactical situation and makes a battlefield decision — push through a weak point in the Cult's defenses to reach Kel'Thuzad before he can escape. It's the right call in a conventional engagement. It's wrong here because the weak point is bait. Kel'Thuzad left it open deliberately, funneling them into a kill zone rigged with necromantic traps that Sylvanas can't see because they're arcane, not physical.

**The cost:** A Farstrider dies. Not from combat — from a trap that detonated when the ranger crossed a threshold. The kind of trap Jaina would have detected in three seconds if Sylvanas had waited for the arcane sweep instead of pressing the advantage.

**Sylvanas's response:** She doesn't apologize. She doesn't verbalize the failure. But the narrative voice — her internal monologue — acknowledges it in the way she catalogs what happened: *She'd read the kill zone as a gap in their perimeter. She'd been right about the perimeter. She'd been wrong about what was inside it. The distinction had cost Lyriel her life.*

**What this teaches her:** The military lens has blind spots that kill people. Jaina's arcane sweep isn't caution — it's a necessary component of threat assessment that Sylvanas's training doesn't include. She'd been unconsciously treating Jaina's methodical approach as academic slowness. It's not slowness. It's a different speed of thoroughness.

### Jaina's Mistake: The Scholar's Blunder

**When:** During the investigation phase, approaching a Cult outpost (~Ch 9, before or during the ambush).

**What:** Jaina detects a fading magical signature — the residue of a mass-animation ritual, hours old, still readable. She wants to analyze it in situ. Right now. This is the plague's mechanism at the point of deployment — if she can map the full ritual matrix, she can develop a countermeasure. She argues for fifteen minutes on-site. Sylvanas says they don't have fifteen minutes — the Cult knows they're in the area, the outpost is a forward position, and stopping to study it turns them into a stationary target.

**Jaina insists.** Not from recklessness but from the absolute conviction that understanding the mechanism is worth the risk. She's wrong — not about the value of the analysis, but about the time. The Cult ambush hits at minute eight. Not undead this time — living Cult operatives with weapons and the willingness to die for Kel'Thuzad's vision.

**The cost:** Sylvanas has to fight a rear-guard action against six Cult operatives while Jaina scrambles to break off her analysis and contribute to the defense. The first thirty seconds are the worst — Jaina is mentally half-in the ritual matrix, her concentration shattered, her combat magic slow to spin up. Sylvanas takes a wound covering her. Not life-threatening, but enough to bleed, enough to hurt, enough that the bandage Jaina wraps later is an accusation neither of them voices.

**Jaina's response:** She completes the analysis later, from memory, working backward from the fragments she captured. It takes her three hours instead of fifteen minutes, and the results are less complete. She doesn't apologize either — but she starts checking sight lines before she sets up a diagnostic circle. She starts asking Sylvanas "how long?" before she commits to a sustained analysis. The question is new. It acknowledges that time is a tactical resource she hadn't been accounting for.

**What this teaches her:** Comprehension is not the same as survival. The scientist's instinct — stand closer, look longer, gather more — has a cost that someone else pays. Sylvanas's urgency isn't anti-intellectual. It's the calculus of a person who has watched people die during the gap between "we should study this" and "we should run."

### The Aftermath of Both Mistakes
No confrontation. No argument. No dramatic scene where they hash it out. That would be the AI-writing version. Instead:

- Sylvanas starts waiting for Jaina's arcane sweep before advancing through unfamiliar terrain. She doesn't announce it. She just stops at the threshold and looks at Jaina. The look becomes their shorthand for "check this."
- Jaina starts asking "how long?" and respecting the answer. When Sylvanas says "two minutes," she works in two minutes. When she needs more, she says so and Sylvanas adjusts the security perimeter. The negotiation becomes their shorthand for "this matters but so does not dying."
- Neither acknowledges the shift verbally. The shift is in behavior, readable only through Sylvanas's narration noticing that Jaina is doing something she didn't used to do, and that she herself is doing something she didn't used to do, and neither of them has talked about it.

### The Split (~Ch 10–13)

**See `scholomance-arc.md` for full details.** Lyriel's death (Ch 9) triggers the first separation. Jaina follows the Barov grain trail to Scholomance. Sylvanas, grief-driven and steered by a Mal'Ganis breadcrumb, hunts toward Arthas.

**For the partnership, the split does three things:**

1. **Proves dependence by removal.** Both operate less effectively alone. Sylvanas's tactical approach without Jaina's arcane sweep is reckless (she knows it — Lyriel is proof). Jaina's analytical approach without Sylvanas's operational awareness walks her into Kel'Thuzad's parlor.

2. **Tests identity under pressure.** Jaina alone with her dark mirror (Kel'Thuzad) faces the full weight of the three-way mirror — same training, same conviction, same methodology. Without Sylvanas's friction, her intellectual arrogance has no counterweight. Sylvanas alone with Arthas lights the fuse of his descent — tactical decisiveness without Jaina's analytical caution. Both act in character. Both produce catastrophic consequences.

3. **Opens the crack (Ch 13).** Sylvanas arrives at Scholomance and finds Jaina *listening* to Kel'Thuzad — not agreeing, not collaborating, but intellectually engaged in a way that reads as dangerous from the doorway. The trust delay: a fraction-of-a-second hesitation that didn't exist before. Jaina notices the hesitation. Neither confronts it. The crack doesn't break the partnership — it damages the automatic trust. For the rest of the Scholomance arc, micro-calibrations appear: Sylvanas's timing shifts, the extra beat before committing to Jaina's assessment. Jaina feels the change without being able to name it.

### The Reformation (~Ch 14)

They choose to continue together DESPITE the crack. This is the first time the partnership is a conscious choice rather than operational necessity — and it's made heavier by the damage. Sylvanas is choosing to work with someone she now knows has a vulnerability. Jaina is choosing to work with someone who saw her at her worst.

## Phase 4: Saving Each Other (~Ch 15–18)

### The Pattern
By this phase, the saves aren't dramatic rescue scenes — they're operational. One covers the other's vulnerability as naturally as breathing. The partnership has become load-bearing: remove either person and the mission collapses. The crack from Ch 13 is present but functional. The head-start source attempt at Stratholme starts the weld by showing Sylvanas exactly what Jaina does with dangerous knowledge; the wall finishes it.

**Specific save beats:**
- **The Kel'Thuzad confrontation (Ch 13):** Jaina shields Sylvanas from a necromantic blast that would have killed her outright — the magic is fast, Frostmourne-adjacent, the kind of power Sylvanas's physical reflexes can't dodge. Jaina doesn't announce it. The shield is up before Sylvanas finishes her dodge. When it's over, Sylvanas says nothing. She nocks another arrow and keeps fighting. The save is folded into the combat, not dramatized.
- **The Stratholme head start (Ch 16):** Jaina turns Kel'Thuzad's plague math into field work at once — granaries, bakehouses, cart routes, whatever can still be reached before the city finishes feeding itself. Sylvanas buys her seconds, doors, and sight lines. The save runs both ways: Jaina uses the knowledge to try to save strangers, and Sylvanas sees what that knowledge is for before the wall ever asks her to trust it.
- **The Stratholme exit (Ch 18):** They watch the Culling from the walls. When it's over, Jaina can't move. Not frozen — shattered. She's standing, but the expression on her face is the expression of someone whose operating system just crashed. Sylvanas doesn't comfort her. She puts a hand on Jaina's shoulder — a physical anchor, the tactile equivalent of a compass bearing — and says, "South. We go south." She saves Jaina not from danger but from paralysis.
- **The ship voyage (Ch 20):** On the voyage to Northrend, Jaina pushes too hard with the wind magic, accelerating the ship. She's bleeding from the nose, hands shaking. Sylvanas says, "Stop." Jaina says she can maintain it. Sylvanas says, "You arrive dead, you're useless. Stop." The save is pragmatic, not tender — but the pragmatism IS the tenderness, in Sylvanas's language.
- **The Frostmourne cave (Ch 21):** The retreat. Sylvanas is covering Jaina. Jaina is shielding Sylvanas. They're pulling Muradin's body between them. Neither speaks. The coordination is physical — a language they've built over weeks of shared movement. One steps left, the other adjusts right. One fires, the other shields the arc. Arthas watches them retreat with that horrible smile. Sylvanas's internal note: *Two months ago I'd have retreated differently. Two months ago I wasn't retreating with her.*

### Stratholme as Emotional Bonding Point
The Culling is where the partnership shifts from professional to personal. Not through conversation — through shared witness. They stand on the wall above Stratholme and watch a city burn. They hear the screaming. Neither speaks because there's nothing to say. But afterward, a line has been crossed. They're no longer collaborating on a mission — they're bound by what they've seen. Comrades, in the way that word is only earned through things you can't describe to anyone who wasn't there.

**The shift in Sylvanas's narration:** After Stratholme, Sylvanas's internal references to Jaina change tone. Not warmer — more precise. She stops assessing Jaina and starts integrating her. Jaina's position, stamina, emotional state, magical reserves — these become as automatic in Sylvanas's tactical awareness as her own arrow count. She doesn't decide to do this. It just happens. She notices it retrospectively, in a quiet moment, and files it without commentary.

## Phase 5: Competence Recognition — Northrend (~Ch 19–22)

### What's Different
They've been in the field together for weeks. The friction is gone. Not smoothed over — burned away, like the impurities in forge-steel. What's left is harder and more precise.

**Observable symptoms of synchronization:**
- Communication in shorthand. A raised eyebrow from Sylvanas means "threat left." A hand-gesture from Jaina means "magical anomaly ahead, twenty meters." They developed this language without discussing it — field-adaptive communication, the kind that only emerges when two people have operated in shared danger long enough.
- They split tasks by aptitude without discussion. When they enter a new environment: Jaina reads the magical terrain, Sylvanas reads the physical terrain. They report to each other simultaneously, overlapping, building the composite picture in real time. An outsider watching would think they've worked together for years.
- Muradin sees it. The dwarf, who has commanded his own soldiers for decades, watches them operate and says something like: "You two drill together, or does this come natural?" Sylvanas: "We improvise." Muradin, to Jaina: "She always this humble?" Jaina: "She's never humble. She's being accurate." The exchange is the first time a third party names what they've become.

### The Frostmourne Fight — The Loss That Proves the Partnership
They lose. Arthas with Frostmourne is beyond them. But the FIGHT demonstrates peak synchronization:

- Sylvanas's arrows create openings; Jaina's spells exploit them.
- Jaina's shields buy Sylvanas repositioning time; Sylvanas's mobility draws Arthas's attention away from Jaina's casting.
- When the tide turns against them — Frostmourne's anti-magic field eating Jaina's spells, the cold draining Sylvanas's muscle response — they read the retreat simultaneously. No word spoken. Sylvanas fires a covering volley; Jaina raises an ice wall. They grab Muradin and go.
- The retreat is immaculate. Two people, one unconscious dwarf, against a death knight with a cursed runeblade, and they get out clean. Not because they overpowered him — because they understood their limits exactly and disengaged at the precise moment where retreat was still possible.

**Sylvanas's internal assessment post-fight:** She reviews the combat the way she reviews every engagement — what worked, what failed, what she'd change. The conclusion she reaches, and doesn't share: *Nothing. I wouldn't change the partner. I wouldn't change the tactics. We lost because the opponent was superior in capability, not because we failed in execution.* This is the highest compliment a military mind can offer: we did everything right and it wasn't enough. The failure is external, not internal.

**Jaina's mirror-assessment (direct — Ch 22 is Jaina's POV):** Jaina says, after the retreat, during the march back to the coast: "In the cave. When Frostmourne's field collapsed my third barrier. You were already repositioning before I dropped it. How did you know?" Sylvanas: "Your left hand. It drops half a second before the spell fails. You compensate with your right but the left telegraphs." Jaina stares at her. "You've been watching my casting that closely?" Sylvanas: "I've been watching everything that closely. It's what I do." From Jaina's POV, the sentence lands differently — not as a job description but as something she can't categorize. The Ranger-General watches everything. That "everything" now includes the micro-movements of Jaina's hands during spellcasting. From the inside, Jaina feels the weight of being seen that precisely by someone she's spent months learning to trust — and she doesn't know what to do with it.

## Phase 6: Peak Competence — The Throne Room (~Ch 23)

### Jaina's Proving Ground
The user's directive: killing Frostmourne-enhanced Arthas is Jaina's peak competence arc. She proves she can stand with Sylvanas's centuries of experience and not just participate but actively save the Ranger-General from death.

### The Setup
The race back from Northrend strips them to essentials. Exhausted. Under-resourced. Racing against a timeline they can't beat. When they arrive at Capital City, they have: Sylvanas's bow and remaining arrows, Jaina's staff and whatever magic she can channel, their combined intelligence about the enemy, and each other. That's it. No army. No backup. Two women against the thing the prince has become.

### The Infiltration — Combat Synchronization at Maximum
The palace is infiltrated with disguised undead. The service passages become a kill corridor. They fight through it together:

- **Sylvanas** handles the physical combat — arrows in corridors, blade work when range collapses, ranger-speed movement through confined spaces. She is in her element: close-quarters, target-rich, every angle a kill-lane.
- **Jaina** handles the magical layer — detecting undead through walls, collapsing wards on their ambushers, ice-locking doorways behind them to prevent being flanked. She's also shielding them both from the Frostmourne-adjacent aura that permeates the palace.
- **The key moment:** A trap. Undead behind and before. The corridor funnels them into a crossfire. Sylvanas goes low, Jaina goes high — literally, levitating three feet off the ground to cast over Sylvanas's arrows. They clear the corridor in twelve seconds. Neither trained for this specific maneuver. It emerged.

### The Throne Room — Jaina's Apex

**The sequence:**
1. They reach the doors. Arthas is already inside with Terenas. Frostmourne's resonance is overwhelming — the weapon screams in frequencies only Jaina can hear, and what she hears almost drops her. She pushes through. Because the alternative is not pushing through.
2. The doors. Terenas's voice: "My son—" Arthas's response. The blade. The kill. They're too late for the king.
3. They are NOT too late for Arthas.

**Sylvanas's arrow:** She fires the instant Frostmourne moves. The shot is perfect — throat, through the gap in the death knight's armor. The arrow that would kill any living person.

**Arthas doesn't die.** Frostmourne's power. The same force that deflected Jaina's spells in the cave, that shattered Sylvanas's arrows. Arthas turns toward them with the arrow in his throat, his blood black, his eyes glacial.

**The fight that follows:**
- Arthas is stronger than the cave. The blade has fed on Terenas's soul. He's faster, harder to hurt, and the cold radiating from Frostmourne is actively shutting down both of them — Sylvanas's elven physiology slowing, joints stiffening, breath coming harder. Jaina's magic being eaten at the source.
- Sylvanas fights with everything she has. Centuries of martial skill, the accumulated combat intelligence of a thousand engagements. She's the best warrior in Quel'Thalas and it's not enough. Arthas batters through her defenses — one strike, two, three. The third sends her flying.
- **JAINA'S MOMENT:** Sylvanas is down. Not dead — injured, winded, trying to rise but the cold is in her muscles, Frostmourne's aura freezing her from the inside. She has seconds before Arthas closes the distance.
- Jaina doesn't shield. She doesn't run. She ATTACKS. Not the careful, analytical magic she's used all story — the raw, adaptive, I-will-invent-something-in-the-next-two-seconds brilliance that Antonidas saw in her when she was seventeen. She finds the flaw in Frostmourne's field — not a weakness, a resonance point, the frequency where the cursed blade's power oscillates between absorption and emission. The gap is a fraction of a second. She times her spell to hit during that fraction.
- The spell isn't in any textbook. It's three different schools of magic braided together in a way that shouldn't work but does because Jaina Proudmoore is, at this moment, operating at the absolute ceiling of human arcane capability. Ice to slow the blade's movement. Arcane to penetrate the death-magic field. Fire — her weakest school, the one she never uses — to burn through the necromantic regeneration that's keeping Arthas alive despite the arrow in his throat.
- It works. Arthas staggers. The field drops. Frostmourne's scream changes pitch.
- Sylvanas rises. Arrow nocked. The second shot — this one through the eye, while Jaina's magic holds the field open.
- Arthas falls.

**Why this is Jaina's peak:** It's not raw power — it's adaptive intelligence under lethal pressure. The same quality that made her annoying (studying when she should be running, analyzing when she should be fighting) is what saves them both. She didn't overpower Frostmourne — she outthought it. In two seconds. While the woman she's spent months learning to trust was dying on the floor behind her.

**The final partnership beat (Jaina's POV — Ch 23 is hers):** After. Standing over the bodies. Breathing hard. From Jaina's interiority: she looks at Sylvanas and sees the woman who dismissed her as a dangerous academic eight weeks ago. The woman whose fury in the interrogation room almost made Jaina believe she'd made a mistake coming north. The woman who taught her that time is a resource someone else pays for.

That woman just rose from the floor with Frostmourne's cold in her muscles and put an arrow through a death knight's eye because Jaina held the field open for two seconds.

Jaina doesn't say anything. She doesn't need to. Sylvanas meets her eyes and what passes between them is the purest form of professional respect — the recognition that you have fought beside someone who is exactly as good as you are, in a different way, and that the combination of both is what won.

Then the doors open. The guards enter. And it doesn't matter anymore.

## Summary: The Arc in One Sentence Per Phase

1. **Contempt:** She's a mage who'll get herself killed / She's a soldier who can't think past her bow.
2. **Grudging respect:** She's competent in ways I didn't expect.
3. **Tests of faith:** We both made mistakes that cost people. We both learned.
4. **Mutual rescue:** I trust her to cover my blind spots because she's proven she can.
5. **Recognition:** We're not allies of convenience. We're the best team on this continent.
6. **Peak:** She just saved my life by being exactly what I spent the first week dismissing her as — and I am not the same person who dismissed her.
