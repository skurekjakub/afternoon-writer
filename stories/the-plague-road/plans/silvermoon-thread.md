## Arc I: Investigation (Chapters 1-8)

### Chapter 1 — The Baseline

**On-stage:** Lor'themar appears in person. Post-patrol in southeastern Eversong with Sylvanas.

**Political content:** Banter about the Convocation's upcoming budget review. Lor'themar rides north at the chapter's end — the annual dance of patrol projections, Amani corridor complement, the Convocation's ritual attempt to cut the southeastern patrol by a third. Routine. Easy. The last routine thing.

**Convocation state:** Business as usual. The plague is not yet on anyone's agenda. The Convocation is debating irrigation schedules and trade tariffs with Lordaeron.

**Function:** Establishes the baseline Lor'themar — sharp, wry, at ease. Establishes the Convocation as a mundane governing body, not yet an obstacle. The reader meets Silvermoon politics as background noise. It won't stay that way.

### Chapter 2 — No Political Content

Jaina's POV. The political thread is dormant. Jaina has no line into Silvermoon affairs.

### Chapter 3 — First Runner Message

**Off-stage:** First Farstrider runner reaches Sylvanas with Lor'themar's routine dispatch.

**Political content:** Budget allocations confirmed (they cut the Amani corridor by a fifth — better than a third, Lor'themar counts this as a win). Patrol schedule adjustments. A note about increased merchant traffic on the border roads. Subtext: the Convocation is focused on commerce and routine defense. No awareness of the plague intelligence Sylvanas is beginning to collect.

**Convocation state:** Unaware. The first contaminated villages haven't been reported through official channels yet.

**Function:** The runner-message mechanism is introduced. Farstrider relay — fast, reliable, carrying Lor'themar's voice across hundreds of miles. The reader learns how Silvermoon talks to its Ranger-General.

### Chapter 4 — Jaina's POV, No Runner

**Political content (indirect):** Jaina's investigation discoveries will eventually become the intelligence Lor'themar needs. But the Silvermoon thread doesn't pulse here — this is human-side work.

### Chapter 5 — First Escalation

**Off-stage:** Sylvanas sends her first formal field report north — the contaminated villages, the organized distribution network, the evidence of deliberate plague deployment.

**Political content (runner arriving):** Lor'themar's response is prompt. He has presented Sylvanas's report to the Convocation. Their response: concern noted, investigation authorized within existing mandate, no additional resources. The report has been "received and assessed." Lor'themar's message includes a subtext Sylvanas reads immediately: the Convocation heard the report and decided it was a Farstrider problem, not a national one.

**Convocation state:** Mild concern. Vandellor notes that the ward network shows no anomalies along the border. The contamination is south of Thalassian Pass — human territory. Dawnstar asks what the transmission mechanism is. Sunsorrow notes that Lordaeron has its own military and suggests the appropriate response is a diplomatic inquiry, not a military one.

**Function:** First taste of the political wall. The intelligence is good. The response is inadequate. Lor'themar begins the long fight.

### Chapter 6 — The Questions Begin

**Off-stage:** Runner exchange continuing.

**Political content:** Lor'themar's message is more compressed than Ch 3 — the political shorthand is tighter. He reports: Vandellor questioned the Ranger-General's authority to conduct operations south of the border. Lor'themar cited the standing border-defense mandate, which covers threats "approaching or affecting Thalassian security." The Convocation accepted this — barely. Dawnstar abstained from the vote to censure (first abstention — the pattern begins).

**Convocation state:** The questioning has begun. Not opposition yet — procedural challenge. "Does the Ranger-General have the authority to do what she's doing?" Lor'themar holds the line, but the line is now contested.

**Function:** The Convocation shifts from indifference to scrutiny. Lor'themar is now spending political capital to maintain Sylvanas's operational freedom.

### Chapter 7 — Intelligence Exchange

**Off-stage:** Multiple runner exchanges. The pace accelerates.

**Political content:** Sylvanas's reports now include the Scholomance lead and the evidence of a command authority behind the Cult of the Damned. Lor'themar's messages shift: he's no longer just defending Sylvanas's mandate, he's actively advocating for escalation. He asks for specific intelligence — contamination rates, village counts, evidence of military-grade organization — because these are the numbers that might move Dawnstar from abstention to support.

**Convocation state:** Split forming. Vandellor maintains the ward-based argument. Sunsorrow digs into cultural precedent. But the intelligence is getting harder to dismiss. Two of the unnamed members are showing discomfort with the "not our problem" position.

**Lor'themar's tone:** The humor is thinning. His messages still have the dry wit, but it's deployed less often and the observations are sharper. "The Convocation debated your Andorhal report for four hours. The vote was five to two against mobilization."

**Function:** The political fight is now active combat. Lor'themar is running an advocacy campaign while managing the Convocation's increasing hostility toward Sylvanas's operation.

### Chapter 8 — The Wall Hardens

**Off-stage:** Runner message — the longest yet.

**Political content:** The Convocation votes formally against expeditionary authorization. Five to two (Vandellor, Sunsorrow, and three unnamed against; two unnamed in favor; Dawnstar abstains). Lor'themar reports the vote count, the arguments, and — for the first time — a direct threat: the Convocation is "reviewing the scope of the Ranger-General's current deployment" and may issue a recall if the operation expands further.

**Convocation state:** Active opposition. The Convocation has moved from skepticism to institutional resistance. They are not malicious — they genuinely believe the wards are sufficient and Sylvanas is overstepping — but the effect is obstruction.

**Lor'themar's tone:** The half-smile is audible in the message's final line. A sardonic note — the kind of joke a man makes when the alternative is screaming. "I will continue to present your intelligence. They will continue to assess it. We are all performing our assigned functions admirably."

**Function:** End of Arc I for the political subplot. The wall is built. The Convocation has said no. Lor'themar has spent his opening moves. The fight shifts from "convince them" to "outlast them."

## Arc II: Scholomance (Chapters 9-14)

### Chapter 9 — Second Physical Appearance

**On-stage:** Lor'themar appears in person at the border. The second snapshot — gaunt, sleep-deprived, the circles under his eyes, the nose-bridge rubbing habit that didn't exist in Ch 1.

**Political content:** He delivers the Convocation's current stance (formal review of Sylvanas's authority, possible recall, deep skepticism about the partnership with a human mage). He collects Sylvanas's Scholomance intelligence — this is the ammunition he needs. The necromantic training facility, the command chain, the evidence that the plague is not natural but manufactured — this is the kind of evidence that might crack Dawnstar's threshold.

**Convocation state:** Actively hostile but not yet decisive. The recall is threatened but not enacted — Lor'themar has delayed it by framing Sylvanas's operation as "intelligence gathering within existing mandate" rather than "military deployment beyond borders."

**Lor'themar's physical tells:** The armor not quite maintained. The buckle not seated. The humor still present but arriving slower, with edge. Sylvanas notices. She does not comment. The silence is the loudest thing.

**Function:** The deterioration made visible. Lor'themar's body tells the story of the political war's cost. Also: the intelligence handoff — he takes back evidence that might move the Convocation.

### Chapter 10 — The Diplomatic Incident

**Off-stage:** Runner message.

**Political content:** The Convocation has raised the partnership with Jaina as a diplomatic concern. A human mage — a citizen of Dalaran, an apprentice of the Kirin Tor — working alongside the Ranger-General without formal diplomatic authorization. Vandellor frames this as a treaty violation. Sunsorrow frames it as a cultural insult. Lor'themar must now fight on two fronts: defending the operation's existence AND defending Jaina's presence.

**Convocation state:** The Jaina angle is the new weapon. It's politically elegant — attacking the partnership is easier than attacking the intelligence, because the intelligence is strong but the diplomatic precedent is genuinely unclear.

**Function:** The political fight escalates from "should we mobilize?" to "should we recall our Ranger-General for working with a human?" The ground shifts under Lor'themar.

### Chapter 11 — No Political Content

The investigation deepens. Scholomance intelligence is being processed. The political thread is quiet — Lor'themar is consolidating, not advancing.

### Chapter 12 — The Favor Account

**Off-stage:** Runner message — compressed, urgent.

**Political content:** Lor'themar reports that he has successfully delayed the recall vote by three weeks. The cost: he called in a favor from the commerce seat, promising Farstrider patrol adjustments that benefit southern trade routes. The favor is old — accumulated over decades — and now it's spent. His message includes a stripped-down political assessment: "I have perhaps four more such debts I can collect. Use them wisely."

**Convocation state:** The recall is delayed, not defeated. Lor'themar is buying time with political capital that does not regenerate.

**Lor'themar's tone:** Operational. The wit is gone from this message. Pure briefing: numbers, timelines, political costs. The man who joked about "performing our assigned functions admirably" is now counting ammunition.

**Function:** The economics of political combat made explicit. Lor'themar has a finite reserve of influence and he's spending it. The reader understands that time is the cost: every week Sylvanas operates in the field costs Lor'themar a debt he can never recollect.

### Chapter 13 — Scholomance Aftermath Intelligence

**Off-stage:** Runner exchange — rapid.

**Political content:** The Scholomance intelligence reaches Silvermoon in full. Lor'themar presents it: the necromantic training facility, the manufactured plague, the command chain, the evidence of organized military-grade operations. The Convocation's response is a split — the first genuine fracture. Vandellor holds his position (the wards are sufficient) but two unnamed members are visibly shaken. Dawnstar asks new questions — not procedural but substantive: "What is the projected spread rate if the distribution network is not disrupted?"

**Convocation state:** Cracking. Not broken, but the certainty faction's hold is weakening. The Scholomance evidence is harder to dismiss than village contamination reports.

**Function:** The turning point's first tremor. The intelligence that Sylvanas risked everything to gather is doing what intelligence is supposed to do: changing minds. Slowly. Too slowly.

### Chapter 14 — No Political Content

Chapter focused on investigation resolution and transition. The political thread holds its position.

## Arc III: Arthas (Chapters 15-18)

### Chapter 15 — Arthas Changes the Map

**Off-stage:** Runner message.

**Political content:** Arthas's military mobilization — a crown prince leading Lordaeron's forces against the Scourge — reaches Silvermoon through both Lor'themar's political channels and Farstrider border intelligence. Lor'themar leverages this immediately: if the human crown prince considers the threat serious enough to mobilize a military force, the Convocation cannot maintain that it's "not our problem."

**Convocation state:** Vandellor counters elegantly: "Human problems require human responses. The prince's mobilization confirms that Lordaeron is addressing its own crisis. Our intervention is neither requested nor required." The argument is infuriating because it's internally consistent.

**Function:** The Arthas deployment is a new political weapon for Lor'themar — and Vandellor immediately neutralizes it. The political chess continues.

### Chapter 16 — Stratholme Reaches Silvermoon

**Off-stage:** Runner message — urgent.

**Political content:** The Stratholme intelligence arrives. The purge. The prince's choice. The scale of the crisis now undeniable — a major human city destroyed by its own prince in response to the plague. Lor'themar presents this not as a military argument but as a humanitarian one: "Lordaeron is consuming itself. When it falls — not if — the Scourge will look for new borders to test."

**Convocation state:** The Stratholme news shakes even Sunsorrow. The cultural isolationist's argument depends on Lordaeron existing as a buffer state. If Lordaeron collapses, the buffer disappears. For the first time, Sunsorrow asks a question instead of making a speech.

**Lor'themar's tone:** Flat. Direct. No humor at all. The messages from this chapter forward read like field reports from a war correspondent — the political operator has become a war advocate.

**Function:** Stratholme is the earthquake. The Convocation's certainty is damaged. Not destroyed — Vandellor's ward argument still holds — but the ground it stands on is no longer stable.

### Chapter 17 — The Partial Victory

**Off-stage:** Runner message — the most important dispatch of the political subplot.

**Political content:** Lor'themar secures the scout company. Post-Stratholme, with the Convocation shaken and Dawnstar finally ready to vote, he proposes a compromise: not full expeditionary authorization (which Vandellor would block) but a forward scout company — twenty rangers authorized to move south and "verify reports." Dawnstar votes for. One unnamed member flips. The vote is four to three. The scout company is authorized.

**The cost:** This is Lor'themar's last major favor spent. He has traded his final political reserve for a concession that is militarily insignificant (twenty rangers won't stop the Scourge) but politically essential (the Convocation has authorized, for the first time, Thalassian military presence south of the border in response to the plague crisis). The scout company is the wedge. The precedent.

**Convocation state:** Fractured. The unanimity of the certainty faction is broken. Vandellor is furious — not at the scout company's size but at its existence, because he understands what Lor'themar understands: this is the precedent that leads to full mobilization.

**Function:** The hinge beat of the entire political subplot. Everything before this was Lor'themar losing. This is the first win — small, costly, and perfectly positioned to become the lever that moves everything.

### Chapter 18 — Consolidation

**Off-stage:** Runner message.

**Political content:** The scout company deploys. Lor'themar reports the deployment and the political aftermath: Vandellor has demanded a formal review of the authorization. Sunsorrow has filed a procedural objection. Dawnstar stands firm. The scout company is in the field, and removing it would require a new vote — which Lor'themar will make politically expensive.

**Convocation state:** The scout company exists. This is the fact that changes everything. A Thalassian military unit south of the border, authorized by the Convocation, reporting back intelligence that cannot be filtered through Vandellor's ward-based framework.

**Function:** The wedge holds. The political fight shifts from "should we send anyone?" to "should we send more?" — a fundamentally different question, and one where Lor'themar has the advantage because the scout company's reports will generate their own political pressure.

## Arc IV: Endgame (Chapters 19-25)

### Chapter 19 — The Hollowed Champion (Vereesa's Report)

**On-stage (indirect):** Lor'themar described through Vereesa's eyes. The third snapshot. See [`characters/lorthemar.md`](../characters/lorthemar.md) — "Ch 19: The Hollowed Champion."

**Political content:** Vereesa reports to Sylvanas: Lor'themar addressed the Convocation for an hour. No notes, no wit, no charm. Numbers, village names, death counts. He presented the full scope of the plague crisis — everything Sylvanas has sent, plus the scout company's own reports — and asked for full expeditionary authorization. The Convocation voted to recall Sylvanas instead.

**The recall vote:** The Convocation's logic is specific and terrible: Sylvanas has been operating beyond her mandate for weeks, the partnership with a human mage was never authorized, and the Ranger-General's personal investment in the crisis (they don't know about her relationship with Jaina, but they sense the operational passion has exceeded professional parameters) constitutes a judgment failure. The recall is not a punishment — it is a correction.

**Convocation state:** The certainty faction has reasserted control — not by winning the intelligence argument (which they're losing) but by shifting the argument to authority and mandate. They can't deny the plague is serious. They CAN deny that the Ranger-General has the right to fight it without authorization.

**Lor'themar's state:** Spent. The humor is gone. The political capital is gone. The favors are gone. He is fighting with nothing but facts and endurance, and the Convocation has decided that facts are not sufficient grounds for action without proper process.

**Function:** The nadir. The political subplot's darkest moment. The parallel with Antonidas is sharpest here — both advocates have been defeated by their systems, not because they were wrong but because the systems were designed to absorb being wrong and continue operating.

### Chapters 20-23 — The Slow Turn

**Off-stage:** Runner messages — the final push.

**Political content:** The intelligence accelerates. Arthas in Northrend. Frostmourne. The burned fleet. The Scourge's command chain exposed. Each dispatch from Sylvanas is another weight on the scale. The scout company's reports corroborate everything. Dawnstar is now a committed advocate for mobilization. Two unnamed members have shifted. Even Vandellor is wavering — not on the wards (he will never waver on the wards) but on the question of whether passive defense is sufficient when the enemy is actively consolidating.

**The slow reversal:** Lor'themar converts the scout company's presence into a lever: the scouts are in the field, they're sending reports, and their reports describe a military threat that will reach the border. The precedent he established in Ch 17 is now generating its own momentum. The question shifts from "should we send anyone?" to "the scouts we already sent are telling us we need to send more." This is political engineering at its most elegant — the small concession that creates the conditions for the large one.

**Convocation state:** Grinding toward mobilization. Not fast enough. Never fast enough. But the votes are shifting — one by one, session by session, as the intelligence becomes undeniable and the scout company's reports make isolation impossible to maintain.

**Function:** The political subplot resolves through accumulated pressure, not dramatic reversal. Lor'themar doesn't win a single decisive argument — he wins twenty small ones, and the accumulation becomes irresistible.

### Chapter 23 — Full Mobilization Authorized

**Off-stage:** Runner message — the authorization.

**Political content:** The Convocation authorizes full expeditionary mobilization. The vote is five to two (Sunsorrow against — always; one unnamed member against — loyalist). Vandellor votes for, marking the first time in the plague crisis that the Grand Magister has sided with Lor'themar. His stated reason: "The threat assessment has exceeded ward-based containment parameters. Expeditionary response is warranted." Translation: the wards are still good, but the world beyond them is bad enough that passive defense is insufficient.

**Lor'themar's state:** He won. The feeling is not triumph — it is the exhausted relief of a man who has been pushing a boulder uphill for twenty-three chapters and has finally reached the crest. He immediately begins organizing the expeditionary force. Speed matters now. Everything matters now.

**Function:** The too-late victory. The authorization comes in Ch 23 while the throne room is already the next live turn in Lordaeron. The expeditionary force still cannot arrive in time to change what happens — it can only arrive in time to respond to what happened. This is the political subplot's cruelest beat: Lor'themar won, and winning took too long.

### Chapter 24 — The Too-Late Arrival

**On-stage:** Lor'themar arrives at Capital City with the Thalassian expeditionary force — elven mages, Farstriders, a military presence that would have changed everything if it had arrived one day earlier.

**Political content:** The force includes mages who felt Frostmourne's power through the palace foundations. Their testimony is politically significant, but not because the tribunal hears it in time. The value is aftermath leverage: independent Thalassian corroboration that the palace is holding an active magical threat the human court does not understand. Lor'themar brings not just soldiers but a second evidentiary language.

**The Antonidas-Lor'themar convergence:** Both men hit the same end-state from opposite directions. Antonidas cannot appear without exposing the institution's earlier failure. Lor'themar appears one beat too late to matter to the verdict. One absent, one late, same system-trap.

**Function:** The political subplot's payoff. Twenty-four chapters of political combat produce a force that arrives too late to prevent the crisis but on time to shape its aftermath. Lor'themar's war wasn't wasted — it was delayed, and the delay will define Book 2.

### Chapter 25 — The Exit State

**Political content:** Lor'themar's forces are in Capital City. The expeditionary authorization gives Quel'Thalas leverage in the post-verdict struggle: custody pressure, vault scrutiny, and the question of whether Lordaeron is competent to hold what it just locked in its own evidence room. The Convocation's twenty-five-chapter obstruction has produced, finally, a Thalassian military presence in the human capital at the exact moment that presence can still shape aftermath even though it missed judgment.

**Book 2 setup:** Lor'themar is the political operator who must navigate the aftermath. His relationship with the Convocation is fractured — he won, but winning destroyed the political relationships that allowed him to operate. Vandellor voted for mobilization under duress and will remember the pressure. Dawnstar is an ally but the alliance cost her political capital too. Sunsorrow is alienated permanently. The Convocation authorized a force; it did not authorize the use Lor'themar may need to make of it.

**Function:** The thread is not resolved — it is transformed. The political subplot of Book 1 (should Silvermoon engage?) becomes the political subplot of Book 2 (Silvermoon is engaged — now what?). Lor'themar's war continues, with different weapons and higher stakes.

## Prose Writer Instructions

When writing any chapter, check this document for the political beat that corresponds to your chapter number. Every chapter that includes a runner message or a reference to Silvermoon should:

1. **Match Lor'themar's tone** to the progression table above
2. **Include specific Convocation details** — name at least one member when describing a vote (e.g., "Vandellor cited the wards again" or "Dawnstar abstained")
3. **Track the deterioration** — the reader should feel Lor'themar wearing down through his messages
4. **Cross-reference the Antonidas subplot** — when Lor'themar fails (Ch 19) and when Antonidas goes silent (Ch 24), the parallel should echo
5. **Never visit Silvermoon directly** — all political content arrives through runner messages (Sylvanas POV) or is reported by Vereesa (Ch 19). The Convocation is a felt presence, not a visited location
