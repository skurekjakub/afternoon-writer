**Genre:** Competence thriller with tonal escalation
**Style Target:** `stories/the-plague-road/prose-sample.md` — current prose benchmark for the pipeline: dry, witty, dialogue-forward limited third that tightens into lean thriller pressure as horror escalates
**Tone Curve:** Chapters 1-6 (witty procedural, competence friction) → Ch 7-8 (humor recedes as conspiracy emerges) → Ch 9-14 (Scholomance — the split, the crack, the first test of faith) → Ch 15-16 (collision, tension) → Ch 17-18 (humor dies permanently at Stratholme) → Ch 19-25 (lean thriller, breakneck, exhausted silence)
**POV:** Alternating by default — Sylvanas Windrunner (odd chapters) and Jaina Proudmoore (even chapters) — with dual-POV exceptions in Chapters 22 and 25. Limited third, absolute, in both lanes. Each chapter should be particularly nasty to its POV character — testing and exploring them to their limits.
**Protagonists:** Sylvanas Windrunner (futa, living high elf, Ranger-General) and Jaina Proudmoore (futa, human archmage, 23 years old)

## Premise

An alternate-perspective retelling of the fall of Lordaeron. Sylvanas Windrunner is investigating anomalies at the Quel'Thalas border — missing Farstrider scouts, dead livestock, villages that stopped sending trade caravans — when her patrols intercept an unauthorized human mage inside the first ward boundary: Jaina Proudmoore, Antonidas's protégée, who sensed leyline corruption from afar and followed it north without Kirin Tor backing or authorization. What begins as a hostile interrogation becomes a continent-spanning competence thriller as the two women uncover the Cult of the Damned, race against the Scourge's timetable, witness Arthas Menethil's descent into madness, track him to Northrend and back — and ultimately assassinate him in the throne room of Lordaeron just as he murders his own father. There are no witnesses to Arthas's crime. Only two foreign women standing over two royal corpses with weapons drawn.

## The Engine

Two hyper-competent women — one a military tactician with centuries of combat experience, the other an arcane prodigy in her early twenties — forced into proximity by circumstance and held together by escalating stakes. Their dynamic is competence collision: neither defers to the other, both are used to being the authority in the room, and the friction between martial thinking and arcane thinking produces solutions neither would find alone.

**The core tension is not personality friction — it's threat assessment.** The elves keep tabs on powerful human mages, particularly protégées of the Kirin Tor Council of Six. Jaina, despite her youth, is recognized as a potential danger to Quel'Thalas — not in the martial sense, but in raw magical potential. Finding her unannounced inside the first ward boundary is a sovereign violation. Sylvanas's initial assessment: this is something dangerous that might be currently useful. The arc of their relationship: **threat → asset → partner → essential → the only person she trusts.**

**The partnership arc is the emotional spine of the novel.** It progresses through six phases:

1. **Threat Assessment / Contained Asset** (Chapters 1–4): Sylvanas treats Jaina as a security risk who happens to have useful intelligence. Jaina is tolerated, not trusted. Sylvanas calls her "the mage" internally. Jaina speaks in simplified register, the way academics address non-specialists — which Sylvanas reads as condescension and files accordingly.
2. **Grudging Respect** (Chapters 5–8): Their analyses converge — arcane and tactical reaching the same conclusions through different methods. Sylvanas starts using "Jaina" in internal narration. Jaina stops register-shifting and starts waiting for the tactical angle. The name shift is unremarked — it happens mid-thought during an assessment, as naturally as any other proper noun.
3. **Tests of Faith** (Chapters 9–14 — The Scholomance Arc): Both make mistakes that stem from their core identity. Sylvanas's tactical push walks into a magical trap that kills Lyriel (the military lens has blind spots). Jaina's insistence on analysis delays an exit and causes a Cult ambush (the scholar's blindness to operational time). Neither apologizes. Both change behavior. Then the split: Sylvanas hunts Arthas (lighting the fuse of his descent), Jaina infiltrates Scholomance alone (facing her dark mirror). The crack: Sylvanas finds Jaina intellectually engaged with Kel'Thuzad. The partnership is tested, damaged, and rebuilt — but the scar remains.
4. **Saving Each Other** (Chapters 15–18): The partnership reforms through shared crisis — damaged but functional. Saves become operational, folded into combat. Stratholme is the bonding point: shared witness to atrocity welds the crack shut. The partnership shifts from professional to personal (not romantic — forged in what they've seen together).
5. **Competence Recognition** (Chapters 19–22): Northrend and the race home. Communication in shorthand. Physical coordination without speech. Muradin names it: "You two drill together?" The Frostmourne fight — they lose, but the retreat is immaculate. On the return south, the finding becomes a framework and the framework becomes a combat system.
6. **Peak Competence** (Chapter 23): The throne room. Arthas shrugs off Sylvanas's arrow, batters her down. Jaina invents a three-school spell in two seconds — finds the resonance gap in Frostmourne's field, holds it open for Sylvanas's kill shot. The "dangerous mage" saves the Ranger-General by being exactly what Sylvanas assessed her as. This is Jaina's apex and the partnership's culmination.

The thriller escalates because every time they solve one problem, the next one is bigger, faster, and closer to home — and every escalation forces the partnership deeper.

## The Three Central Plotlines

### 1. External Conflict — The Plague Investigation
The physical/action plot: tracking the Scourge plague from border anomalies to continental conspiracy, dismantling Cult infrastructure, pursuing Arthas from Stratholme to Northrend, and assassinating him in the throne room.

### 2. Main Relationship — The Partnership Arc
The primary emotional throughline: Sylvanas × Jaina from threat → asset → partner → essential → the only person she trusts. Detailed in the six phases above and in `partnership-arc.md`.

### 3. Internal Conflicts — Character Arcs

**Sylvanas's Lie:** "The world beyond the border is someone else's problem." Institutional isolationism internalized as personal doctrine. The Convocation's position lives inside her: Quel'Thalas is safe behind the wards, the plague can't reanimate elves, human affairs are human problems.

**Sylvanas's Truth:** "The border is an illusion. The threat that ignores your walls also ignores your politics." Her arc: forced past the border by the investigation, she discovers that the threat doesn't respect boundaries — and neither can she. The isolationist stance she was raised in is not just wrong but dangerous: complacency built on accurate premises (elven immunity) but catastrophic conclusions (therefore not our problem).

**Sylvanas's Want:** Protect Quel'Thalas from the plague threat.
**Sylvanas's Need:** Accept that protection requires engagement — that the border is a political fiction the threat has already rendered irrelevant.

**Arc beats:**
- Ch 1: The Lie at its peak. She's on the border, doing the work she loves. The border IS the world. Everything beyond it is managed through reports, not personal investment. Lor'themar heading north to the Convocation, Sylvanas heading south to the border — the institutional axis she operates within.
- Ch 3: She crosses physically but not psychologically. She's managing a threat TO the border, not engaging with the world beyond it. Custodial framing: the mage is an asset, not a partner. The dead village is evidence, not a tragedy.
- Ch 5: The contamination network extends far beyond anything the border could contain. She's deep in Lordaeron following a supply chain. Her justification shifts: "protecting Quel'Thalas requires understanding the source." The Lie bends but doesn't break.
- Ch 7: Andorhal — the regional distribution hub. She's investigating continental logistics, a human city's infrastructure. The border justification stretches thin. She's here because the investigation demands it, not because the border is threatened.
- Ch 9: The ambush. The Cult was hunting THEM — the threat knows they're coming and doesn't care about jurisdictions. The border can't protect her from the consequences of what's beyond it. Her blood pays for the fight. The Lie costs something.
- Ch 11: She's hunting a necromancer in Lordaeron. Fully committed to solving someone else's problem. The Lie is a habit she still tells herself, but her actions have already abandoned it.
- Ch 15: Arthas arrives with an army. She has to negotiate with a human prince, manage human politics, operate within human command structures. Political borders collapse alongside geographical ones. The weight: she PUT him on this path in Ch 11. If he breaks, part of the breaking is on her.
- Ch 17: Stratholme. She witnesses "someone else's problem" reach its culmination — the prince purging his own city. The wall between their catastrophe and hers dissolves. She can no longer pretend the border separates her from the consequences. The fuse she lit in Ch 11 is now detonating.
- Ch 19: The Convocation orders her home. The Lie reasserts itself through institutional authority: come back behind the border, let the humans sort it out. She defies the institution that created the Lie. This is the conscious break — the first time she chooses the Truth over the system that raised her.
- Ch 21: Northrend. Literally beyond any border. She followed a human mage to a frozen continent to kill a human prince wielding a cursed sword. "Someone else's problem" is a punchline now. The Truth is lived, even if unspoken.
- Ch 22: She's fighting to save a kingdom that isn't hers, racing to stop a threat that was always going to cross the border regardless.
- Ch 24: The consequences of the Lie — not hers, but the institutions that still believe it. Lor'themar's "too late." The Convocation's refusal to mobilize. The Truth is proven by the cost of the Lie on those who kept faith with it.

**Jaina's Lie:** "Being right is enough — I can do this alone because I'm the one who sees clearly." She left Dalaran because nobody believed her leyline readings. She crossed the border alone. Her intelligence IS her authority, and if the proof is strong enough, people will listen. She doesn't need institutional backing, doesn't need partners, doesn't need permission — she needs data.

**Jaina's Truth:** "Intelligence without trust is just Kel'Thuzad with better intentions." Being right means nothing alone. The thing that separates Jaina from Kel'Thuzad isn't brilliance, conviction, or methodology — they share all three. It's that Jaina found someone who said "Show me" before the proof was complete. A partner who challenges her, covers her blind spots, forces her to operate within operational reality rather than pure analysis. Without Sylvanas, Jaina's trajectory is Kel'Thuzad's: brilliant conviction spiraling inward without friction until it crosses a line it can't see.

**The three-way mirror:**
- **Kel'Thuzad:** Brilliant conviction → acted alone (the Kirin Tor expelled him, the Lich King enabled him) → crossed ethical lines → genocide
- **Arthas:** Brilliant conviction → had partners (Uther, Jaina) → lost them all (Jaina left him years ago, Uther bailed at Stratholme when the moral framework broke) → alone, his certainty escalated without friction → Stratholme, Northrend, Frostmourne, patricide. His journey IS Kel'Thuzad's origin story repeated: the same arc of conviction without counterweight, the same descent into atrocity justified by necessity. He even followed Kel'Thuzad's path literally — tracked him, killed him, and then became the next iteration of the same pattern.
- **Jaina:** Brilliant conviction → started alone → found Sylvanas (someone who trusted her process before the proof was complete, who provides the tactical friction her analytical mind lacks) → the partnership is the difference. Every time her Lie would have led her deeper into Kel'Thuzad's pattern (Ch 9: insisting on analysis during an ambush, prioritizing data over people), the partnership absorbs the cost and teaches her without lecturing.

**Jaina's Want:** Prove the plague is real and stop it.
**Jaina's Need:** Learn that intelligence without trust is powerless — that the partnership (someone who acts on your analysis before it's proven) is what separates her from Kel'Thuzad.

**Arc beats:**
- Ch 2: "Show me" — the moment that separates her path from Kel'Thuzad's, but she doesn't know it yet. She reads it as Sylvanas being pragmatic, not as trust.
- Ch 7: Antonidas's institution delivers useful intel — a crack in "I don't need them." The Lie wobbles.
- Ch 9: Her mistake (insisting on analysis, causing the ambush) — the Lie in action. Data over operational time. The cost is paid by Sylvanas's blood.
- Ch 10: Physical mirror — Kel'Thuzad's research notes look like her handwriting. Same notational system, same margin habits. The Lie confronted.
- Ch 12: Kel'Thuzad dies with scholarly calm. The mirror breaks, the reflection stays. Jaina can never fully condemn his method without condemning her own.
- Ch 16: She spends the plague math in the field. With Sylvanas and Cyndia covering her, she rides ahead into Stratholme and tries to break the grain chain before Arthas's army arrives. The Truth stops being solitary diagnosis and becomes shared labor: being right matters only if someone is there to help you act on it.
- Ch 18: Stratholme. Being right about Arthas changes nothing because nobody listened. The Lie's ultimate failure: she was right, alone, and it accomplished nothing.
- Ch 23: The kill works ONLY because Sylvanas is there for the shot. Intelligence (Jaina's three-school spell) + trust (Sylvanas rising for the kill shot) = the combination that defeats Arthas. Intelligence alone = Kel'Thuzad. Conviction alone = Arthas. The throne room proves the Truth.
- Ch 25: The institutions won't save her. Only the partnership survives. Through the wall, she names it: they didn't stop Arthas because they were right. They stopped him because they were right *together*.

## Divergence from Canon

In Warcraft III canon, Jaina investigates the plague alongside Arthas. In this story, Jaina tracked leyline corruption on her own initiative, crossed into elven territory without authorization, and was intercepted by Farstrider patrols. She has no Kirin Tor backing, no diplomatic credentials — her brilliance is her only credential. Arthas still follows his canonical path (investigation → Stratholme → Northrend → Frostmourne → patricide), but Jaina and Sylvanas shadow it from a different angle — sometimes ahead of him, sometimes behind, sometimes watching from the tree line as events spiral past the point of intervention.

The critical divergence: they kill Arthas. In canon, he survives to become the Lich King. In this story, Sylvanas's arrow and Jaina's magic finish him in the throne room — but too late to save Terenas, and too early for anyone to witness what Arthas did first.

## The Hidden Antagonist

**Mal'Ganis** — a Nathrezim dreadlord — operates as counterintelligence chief for the entire Scourge operation. He runs a two-front deception:

**Front 1: Sanitization.** He appears IN DISGUISE throughout the investigation chapters as different NPCs: a refugee, a merchant, a guard, a villager. He reads their intentions through proximity telepathy and warns Cult cells ahead of the investigation. This is why every site Jaina and Sylvanas reach has been sanitized — not organizational efficiency, but a demon cleaning up in their wake. **Political consequence:** Mal'Ganis's sanitization directly undermines Lor'themar's advocacy in Silvermoon. Every Cult site Sylvanas reports is clean by the time the Convocation sends inspectors. Lor'themar's evidence evaporates on contact with institutional verification. The political subplot and the hidden antagonist are causally linked — Lor'themar is losing his argument because a dreadlord is erasing the evidence.

**Front 2: Distraction.** He manipulates the Blackrock orcs into a series of escalating raids on Lordaeron's southern settlements — starting with Strahnbrad. Prince Arthas is dispatched to deal with the orc crisis, which keeps the Alliance's most prominent military asset occupied with a manufactured threat while the plague spreads unchecked. The orc raids are real violence, real casualties — but they're theater. The point is to keep Arthas chasing orcs instead of investigating grain shipments. This delays Arthas's entry into the plague investigation until AFTER Kel'Thuzad's death (Ch 12), meaning he arrives at the crisis with conviction but no analytical foundation — the worst possible combination for what comes next.

He carries two tells: a dark stone ring (a demonic ward-focus that dampens his aura, explaining why Jaina never detects him) and an ancient Kaldorei farewell — "Kalar'shorel'anor" ("May the stars keep your road") — a pre-Sundering phrase ten thousand years dead. **In disguise, he translates it into Common:** "May the stars keep your road." The Common version is odd but not alarming — Sylvanas notes it as an unusual farewell but doesn't pursue. **In his true form (Ch 25 stinger), he speaks the original ancient elvish:** "Kalar'shorel'anor." This is the trigger — no mortal has spoken Kaldorei in ten thousand years. When Sylvanas hears the elvish, her memory fires backward through every disguise who said the Common translation: the refugee, the merchant, the guard. The same being. Every time.

He does NOT reveal himself during the investigation. The book's final stinger: he appears in his true form to the imprisoned protagonists, gloats that the plan succeeded despite Arthas's death, but does NOT explain what he is, who he serves, or how he operated. The HOW is for Sylvanas to piece together in Book 2. (See: `characters/malganis.md` for full profile.)

## Worldbuilding: Elven Reanimation Immunity

High elves CANNOT be reanimated by the Scourge plague. Something in their genealogy — the millennia of magical saturation, the Sunwell's influence on their biology, the fundamental difference between elven and human physiology — makes the necromantic binding fail. The plague can sicken an elf (theoretically) but cannot create the post-mortem locomotion that turns human corpses into Scourge infantry.

**This is a fact of the AU, not a false belief.** Elves genuinely are immune to reanimation.

**The arrogance is in the conclusion, not the premise.** The Convocation's logic: "The plague cannot reanimate us. Therefore the plague is a human problem. Human problems are not our concern." This ignores the scale — tens of thousands of reanimated human corpses marching in military formation toward Quel'Thalas are dangerous regardless of whether the ward-stones hold. The Convocation treats the Scourge as a curiosity at worst, a human failure at best. They underestimate the threat because they believe their biology and their wards make them untouchable.

**Thematic function:** The immunity deepens the isolationism theme. The elves' dismissal of the plague isn't irrational stupidity — it's rational arrogance. They have evidence that the plague doesn't affect them, and they extrapolate from that evidence a conclusion of safety that ignores everything beyond their borders. This makes the Convocation's position more nuanced (they're not fools, they're complacent) and Sylvanas's arc more powerful (she's fighting against her own people's well-reasoned complacency).

## The Lor'themar Subplot (Political)

**Type:** Institutional subplot — visible through both POVs via messages, runners, and brief appearances
**Theme relationship:** Echoes the main theme (isolation vs. engagement) at the institutional level

Lor'themar Theron is Sylvanas's second-in-command and closest political ally. His job in the novel is to keep Silvermoon's institutional fight visible as a second front: routine governance curdling into advocacy, advocacy hardening into obstruction, and eventual mobilization arriving too late to prevent the throne room but early enough to matter for the aftermath.

### Core function

- **Ch 1–8:** establish the baseline Convocation world, then show Lor'themar shifting from budget arguments to plague advocacy.
- **Ch 9–18:** make Silvermoon an active drag on the field plot — recall pressure, diplomatic panic, and one partial win that proves Lor'themar is losing slowly rather than failing cheaply.
- **Ch 23–25:** pay the thread off as "too late, as always" — the expedition reaches Lordaeron after the kill and becomes leverage for Book 2, not rescue in time.

### Use rules

- Keep all political material filtered through POV characters: runners, letters, brief appearances, overheard consequences.
- Let every Silvermoon update change field decisions, not just reader information.
- Preserve the Lor'themar / Antonidas mirror: two competent advocates losing to systems built to delay.

## The Jaina Subplot (Personal — Brilliance and Authority)

**Type:** Internal subplot — visible through Jaina's POV chapters as interiority, dialogue, and sending-stone exchanges
**Theme relationship:** Complicates the main theme — Jaina IS the thing she's fighting against (brilliant people acting outside institutional authority)

This subplot has three linked lanes, all asking the same question: **what separates heroic brilliance from catastrophic brilliance?**

- **Antonidas / the Kirin Tor:** the mentor who believes Jaina personally but cannot move the system fast enough.
- **Kel'Thuzad:** the methodological mirror — same training discipline, opposite moral direction, same danger of intellectual exhilaration outrunning ethics.
- **Arthas:** the personal foil — conviction without friction, then violence without correction.

### Core function

- Track Jaina's move from "being right is enough" toward "being right only matters if someone can act with you."
- Keep the institutional mirror live through sending-stone contact and late-arrival failure.
- Make the climax cash out all three lanes at once: mentor failure, dark-mirror method, and personal-history kill shot in the same room.

### Use rules

- Keep the subplot visible through Jaina POV, sending stones, and mirror scenes rather than detached summary.
- Preserve the clean structural parallel: Jaina, Kel'Thuzad, and Arthas all act on certainty outside permission; only the partnership keeps Jaina from spiraling into the same shape.
- Let Antonidas and Lor'themar echo each other as advocates trapped inside systems built to stall.

## Thematic Arc

**The cost of being right too early.** Every chapter, Jaina and Sylvanas are ahead of the curve — they identify the plague, they track the Cult, they warn about Arthas. And every chapter, being right accomplishes nothing because the systems around them (diplomacy, military hierarchy, public trust, political inertia) can't process the truth fast enough. The ultimate irony: they finally act decisively, kill the monster, and are punished for it because the world still thinks the monster was a hero.

**Competence vs. systems.** Both women are individually extraordinary. Together they're devastating. But the story is about the limits of individual competence against systemic failure — the Kirin Tor's caution, Silvermoon's isolationism, Lordaeron's faith in its prince, the Alliance's political machinery. Two brilliant women can track a plague, kill a necromancer, and assassinate a death knight. They can't make a tribunal believe them.

**Threat → trust.** The partnership arc is also a story about the transformation of threat assessment into absolute trust. Sylvanas begins by viewing Jaina as a potential danger to Quel'Thalas (raw magical power, Kirin Tor connections, unannounced border crossing). She ends by trusting Jaina with her life — and being right to. The same qualities that made Jaina a "threat" (prodigious magical potential, analytical obsession, willingness to act without authorization) are what save them in the throne room.

**The partnership as the thing that doesn't break.** Every system fails. The Convocation betrays Sylvanas. The Kirin Tor abandons Jaina. Uther's testimony is dismissed. Lordaeron's faith in its prince is misplaced. The Alliance tribunal convicts the innocent. But the partnership — forged through suspicion, tested through failure, hardened through shared witness, proven in the throne room — is the one structure in the story that holds. It survives institutional betrayal, condemnation, and imprisonment. The novel's final image (two voices through a stone wall, then a monster in the dark) is the partnership reduced to its essence: still talking, still connected, still facing the same direction — and now knowing the real enemy.

**Witness to atrocity.** Stratholme (Chapters 17-18) is the emotional center of the book. Everything before it builds toward the question; everything after deals with the answer. The question: what do you do when you're right but you can't stop it? The answer: you keep going, and you carry what you saw.

**The invisible enemy.** Mal'Ganis represents the terrifying truth that the protagonists' failure wasn't a failure of competence — they were outplayed by something they couldn't see. The retroactive revelation that a dreadlord was running interference the entire time reframes every dead end, every sanitized Cult site, every too-helpful stranger. The competence thriller's implicit promise ("smart people solving problems") is honored AND subverted: they ARE smart, they DO solve the problem — but the real problem was never the one they were solving.
