# Subplot: Jaina and Arthas — The Romantic History

**Created:** Morgana Iteration 5
**Purpose:** Backstory document for the Jaina-Arthas romantic history. Enriches the Arthas arc (Ch 15-18) and provides Sylvanas with a new variable for reading the partnership dynamics post-Ch 13.

---

## The Meeting

Dalaran, approximately three years before the story's present. Arthas visited Dalaran as part of a diplomatic tour — Lordaeron's crown prince making the rounds of allied institutions. Jaina was a senior apprentice under Antonidas, already marked as exceptional, already exhibiting the analytical intensity that would define her career.

They met at a formal reception. The attraction was immediate and asymmetric:

- **What drew Arthas to Jaina:** Her intensity. The way she looked at a problem like it was the only thing in the world. In a room full of courtiers and mages performing social competence, Jaina was the one who was *actually thinking* — about the ward architecture in the reception hall, about the diplomatic implications of Lordaeron's grain subsidies to Dalaran, about whatever occupied the specific frequency of her mind at that moment. Arthas was used to being the most important person in any room. Jaina was the first person he'd met who genuinely did not notice that he was important, because she was busy being interested in something that mattered more to her than his title. He found this intoxicating. He mistook it for a challenge.

- **What drew Jaina to Arthas:** His certainty. In a world of academic nuance — Dalaran, where every statement came with three qualifications, where Antonidas taught her to hold seven contradictory hypotheses simultaneously, where the institutional default was deliberation — Arthas DECIDED things. He assessed, he chose, he acted. The speed of his conviction was thrilling to someone who lived in the space between hypotheses. She was drawn to the simplicity of it: here was a person who believed that problems had answers, and that the correct response to an answer was action. She found this clarifying. She did not yet see it as dangerous.

## The Relationship (Six to Eight Months)

Genuine, warm, physical. Not merely political, though the political dimension was always present (Terenas approved; Daelin approved more loudly; the Lordaeron-Kul Tiras alliance would have been strengthened). They spent time together — he visited Dalaran (uncomfortable among mages but charmed by Jaina's willingness to translate the world for him); she visited Lordaeron (fascinated by the machinery of governance, by the way decisions that she analyzed theoretically were executed practically).

**The dynamic that worked:** They covered each other's blind spots. She helped him think before acting — the diagnostic pause, the habit of asking "what are the second-order consequences?" that she'd learned from Antonidas. He helped her act before thinking — the operational momentum, the conviction that at some point analysis must become decision. For six months, the complementarity was genuine. He became more considered. She became more decisive. The relationship made both of them better versions of themselves.

**The dynamic that didn't:** He could not follow her intellectual interests. Not from stupidity — Arthas is intelligent, charismatic, tactically gifted. But Jaina's mind operated at a level of abstraction that his did not reach, and more importantly, did not *value*. When she talked about arcane theory, he listened with the patience of a man who loves someone rather than the engagement of a mind that is interested. She noticed. She filed the gap. She told herself it didn't matter. She was wrong, and she knew she was wrong, and she continued telling herself anyway.

---

## The Gap (The Diagnostic Incident)

A specific incident. Not Stratholme-scale — smaller, diagnostic. A micro-Stratholme that revealed the pattern before the pattern achieved its final, catastrophic expression.

**The incident:** A border dispute between Lordaeron and Alterac — a minor territorial claim over a stretch of farmland near the Alterac Mountains. The local lord had petitioned, the farmers were displaced, the situation required adjudication. Arthas was assigned to mediate (princely duty, standard governance). The situation was complex: the local lord had legitimate legal claim but had exercised it in bad faith, timing his petition to coincide with a harvest that would ruin the farmers if they were displaced. The equitable solution required time — investigation, negotiation, compromise.

Arthas ruled in the lord's favor within hours. The legal claim was clear. The farmers had no title. The ruling was correct on paper and disastrous in practice: thirty families lost their harvest, the local lord gained land he intended to consolidate into a hunting estate, and the displaced farmers became a minor refugee crisis that Lordaeron's governance apparatus had to manage for months afterward.

**What Jaina saw:** The gap between the speed of Arthas's judgment and the complexity of the situation it was applied to. He was not wrong — the legal claim WAS valid. He was not cruel — he authorized relief provisions for the displaced families. But he was FAST in a situation that demanded slowness. He treated ambiguity as an obstacle to be overcome rather than information to be processed. He reached for the clear answer because the clear answer was available, and the availability of a clear answer became, for him, sufficient justification for choosing it.

Jaina recognized the pattern. She had seen it in Dalaran, in different form: the student who reaches for the elegant solution because elegant solutions feel right, and ignores the messy data that doesn't fit. The difference: in Dalaran, the consequence was a failed experiment. In governance, the consequence was thirty families on a road in winter.

She told him. Carefully — analytically, the way she would present a finding to Antonidas. He listened. He did not understand. The ruling was correct. The claim was valid. The situation was resolved. What, specifically, was the problem?

The problem was trajectory. The problem was that a man who ruled this way at twenty would govern this way at thirty, and command armies this way at forty, and the speed that felt like decisiveness was actually the absence of the diagnostic pause that separates good judgment from fast judgment. The problem was the future. The problem was something he hadn't done yet.

She did not say this. She did not have the words yet. She had the feeling — the specific, analytical unease that would crystallize later into certainty. She filed the incident the way Sylvanas would file it: as intelligence. As a data point about trajectory.

## The Breakup

Jaina ended it. Not dramatically — she is analytical, not theatrical. She told him clearly, with the precision of someone who has already run the calculations. The conversation was private (Dalaran, her quarters, the same room where they'd spent their best hours together — the cruelty of geography, of breaking something in the place where it was built).

She said: this is not working. She said: the gap between us is not closing. She said: I see where this goes, and I will not go there with you.

**Arthas's response:** Confusion. Not anger (the anger came later, privately, processed into the specific bitterness that would calcify over years). Genuine, bewildered confusion. Things were GOOD. The relationship was functioning. They were happy. Why would she leave when things were good?

The gap: Jaina saw trajectory. Arthas experienced moments. "Things are good now" was sufficient for him because "now" was the only temporal frame in which he operated. "The trajectory is wrong" was invisible to him because trajectory requires the diagnostic patience he did not possess. She left because of something he hadn't done yet.

That is the cruelest cut. To be left for a sin you haven't committed. To be convicted of a future you don't believe in. Arthas carried this forward: the first thing he wanted that said no. The first experience that could not be resolved by decisiveness, by action, by the charm and conviction that had worked on everything else.

## Political Fallout

Terenas wanted the match. The Menethil-Proudmoore alliance would have strengthened Lordaeron's position with the Kul Tiras naval fleet, reinforced the Alliance's maritime power, and produced heirs with both magical and martial lineage. The breakup was a diplomatic inconvenience that Terenas handled with characteristic grace and Daelin handled with characteristic bluntness. Daelin blamed Arthas (publicly, loudly, at a trade summit where the remark was overheard). Terenas apologized. The incident produced political shrapnel that neither family fully cleaned up.

For Jaina: the political dimension confirmed something she already suspected — that the relationship was never entirely hers. That Lordaeron's interest in her was partially instrumental. That the breakup would cost her politically, and she did it anyway, because the diagnostic data outweighed the political calculus. The cost: reduced influence with Lordaeron. Strained relations with Kul Tiras (Daelin, who approved of the match, interpreted the breakup as Jaina's characteristic inability to commit to anything that wasn't academic). A reputation, in certain diplomatic circles, as "the one who left the prince."

For Arthas: the political dimension was irrelevant. He did not think in alliances and trade agreements. He thought in personal terms: she left. He offered himself — his rank, his future, his certainty — and she said no. The political consequences were his father's problem. The personal wound was his.

## What Arthas Carries Into the Story

Suppressed bitterness under impeccable courtesy. The prince is too disciplined, too trained in public performance, for open resentment. But the memory of being left by someone he cared about — for reasons he STILL does not fully understand — has calcified into a specific blind spot:

**He does not trust intellectual assessment over instinct.** Jaina left because she "thought too much." Therefore, thinking too much is what people do when they want an excuse to leave. Analysis is a tool for cowards who cannot commit. Deliberation is a luxury for people who don't have to act. This feeds DIRECTLY into the Stratholme decision: when the moment comes — the contaminated city, the impossible choice — Arthas will not pause. He will not consult. He will not run the diagnostic. He will act. Because acting is what he does, and the alternative — the careful, analytical, trajectory-assessing alternative — is what Jaina did, and what Jaina did was LEAVE.

**Surface tells:** A stiffness in his courtesy when he addresses Jaina. A slightly too-controlled formality — the warmth has been replaced by a precision that is itself a form of distance. He calls her "Lady Proudmoore" now. He used to call her Jaina. The title is technically correct and emotionally devastating. Every time he uses it, it's a reminder that the familiarity was revoked — by her — and he has accepted the revocation with the grace of a prince and the wound of a man.

**The investiture echo:** When Arthas appears in the story (Ch 15 onward), his interactions with Jaina should carry the specific weight of unfinished emotional business. Not active hostility. Not longing. Something colder: the residue of a connection that was severed cleanly and healed badly. Scar tissue. The stiffness of a joint that works but will never move the way it did before the injury.

---

## What Jaina Carries Into the Story

The memory of a specific judgment gap that proved prescient. Every time she sees Arthas in the story, she sees the gap — wider now, deeper, the thing she noticed in a border dispute now expressed in armies and cities.

**The burden of being right:** She saw this coming. Not the specifics (not Stratholme, not the plague, not Frostmourne) but the trajectory. The speed. The certainty applied to situations that demanded uncertainty. She told him (not in those words, but in the breakup itself — the breakup WAS the warning). He didn't understand. The cruelty: she can SEE the trajectory. She can SEE where it ends. She can DO NOTHING ABOUT IT. Being right is not enough. Being right is NOTHING. The trajectory she diagnosed is executing, and the diagnosis did not change the outcome, and the diagnostic precision that defined her — the quality Arthas could not follow, the quality that made her leave — is revealed as impotent. She can read the data. She cannot change the data. The analyst's curse.

**The guilt:** Not for leaving (the leaving was correct — every subsequent data point confirms this). For not finding the words. For being unable to translate what she saw into language Arthas could process. For being so fluent in abstraction that she could not speak in the concrete terms that might have reached him. The gap between them — his concreteness, her abstraction — was the thing that doomed the relationship AND the thing that prevented her from saving him. The instrument of diagnosis was also the instrument of failure.

**The echo in Scholomance:** Jaina's engagement with Kel'Thuzad (Ch 12-13) rhymes with her engagement with Arthas in a way she is only partially aware of. Both relationships involved intellectual seduction — a mind that complemented hers, that operated in a register she could follow, that offered the specific thrill of being MATCHED. The difference: with Arthas, the gap was between his certainty and her analysis. With Kel'Thuzad, the gap was between his methodology and her ethics. The diagnostic failure was the same in both cases: she could see the flaw, she could not stop herself from following the intelligence to its conclusion. She engaged. She learned. The engagement cost her.

## What Sylvanas Reads

Sylvanas does not have this backstory. She has OBSERVATIONS — the surface data that three centuries of intelligence work have trained her to read.

**Pre-Ch 13:** Sylvanas knows that Jaina and Arthas have history. This is not secret — the relationship was semi-public, the breakup was noted diplomatically, the information is available to anyone with intelligence contacts (which is everyone at Sylvanas's level). She catalogs it as "personal history, unresolved" and files it. It is relevant but not operationally significant — people have histories, histories create dynamics, dynamics can be managed.

**Post-Ch 13:** The Jaina-Arthas history becomes a new variable in the modified trust equation. After the crack — after Sylvanas's assessment of Jaina's engagement with Kel'Thuzad — the backstory acquires weight. Jaina's history with Arthas becomes: another instance of Jaina's engagement with a powerful man whose intellect operated in a register she found compelling. Another data point in the pattern Sylvanas is now constructing (correctly on the surface, incorrectly at the core): Jaina engages. Jaina is drawn to intelligence. Jaina followed Arthas's certainty, Kel'Thuzad's methodology — and now the question: how far does the engagement go? Where does the analytical interest end and the personal begin?

**The operational read:** The Arthas-Jaina backstory gives Sylvanas a second vector of concern for the upcoming Arthas arc. She will be watching Jaina and Arthas interact with the same analytical intensity she brought to the Scholomance room-read — and with the same structural flaw. She will read the surface perfectly (the stiffness, the formality, the "Lady Proudmoore") and miss the interior (the guilt, the diagnostic precision, the impotent prescience). The crack from Ch 13 does not just damage the partnership in the Scholomance arc. It gives Sylvanas a framework — Jaina engages with dangerous intellects — that she will apply to the Arthas arc, where the framework produces new errors.

## Writing Notes for Prose Writers

- **Arthas's voice shifts around Jaina:** The warmth he shows Uther, the easy command he projects with his soldiers — these vanish when he addresses Jaina. Not replaced by coldness (too obvious) but by PRECISION. Every word is correct. Every courtesy is performed. The performance IS the wound.
- **Jaina's tells:** She becomes MORE analytical around Arthas, not less. The diagnostic mode intensifies — she reads his decisions, his body language, his tactical choices with the same intensity she brought to the border dispute. She cannot stop diagnosing. The diagnosis is compulsive. It is also the thing that separated them. She knows this. She does it anyway.
- **The title:** "Lady Proudmoore" vs. "Jaina." Track this across the Arthas arc. If/when the formality breaks — if he says her name, once, in a moment of genuine emotion — it should land like a physical blow. The name is a door he closed. Opening it, even accidentally, exposes the room behind it.
- **The Stratholme echo:** When Arthas makes the Stratholme decision (Ch 19), the diagnostic incident should echo. Jaina will see the pattern complete: the border dispute, scaled to a city. The same speed. The same certainty. The same inability to pause. She will recognize the moment she diagnosed three years ago, executing at full scale, and she will know — with the precision that defines her and the impotence that haunts her — that she cannot stop it.
