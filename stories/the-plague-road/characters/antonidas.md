# Antonidas

**Role:** Jaina's mentor, Kirin Tor authority figure, the voice on the sending stone
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Title:** Archmage; leader of the Kirin Tor; head of the Council of Six
**Age:** 71
**Chapters:** Background presence in all Jaina chapters via sending stone; specific beats in Ch 2, 7, 8, 12, 18, 20, 25-26
**Tier:** Supporting (persistent — runs through every Jaina chapter as a low hum)

---

## The Concept — The Mentor Who Believed Her

Antonidas is the Kirin Tor made human. Not the Kirin Tor at its worst — not the inertia, the caution, the politics. The Kirin Tor at its *best*, and the best is still not fast enough.

He believes Jaina. This is the tragedy's foundation. He is not the mentor who dismisses the student, not the authority figure who refuses to listen, not the gatekeeper who blocks the hero out of arrogance or stupidity. He believes her. He can see the evidence. He wants to help. He has the resources of the most powerful magical body in the world at his disposal. And he cannot mobilize those resources in time, because the Council of Six operates on consensus and deliberation, and the Scourge operates on speed. The gap between trust and action defines every beat he appears in.

He is what happens when a good man leads a cautious system into an existential crisis: the system's strengths become its weaknesses, and the man inside it can see exactly what's going wrong but cannot override the mechanism that's failing. He pushes. He advocates. He spends political capital. He gets results — but the results arrive after the window has closed. Every time.

**The mirror to Lor'themar** is explicit and deliberate. Both men are advocates within failing systems. Both present accurate intelligence to governing bodies that don't want to hear it. Both lose their arguments for the same reason: the system protects itself. Lor'themar fights the Convocation for military resources; Antonidas fights the Council of Six for investigative authority. Both are competent, both are persistent, both win too late. The parallel should land in Ch 25-26 when both fail simultaneously — the Kirin Tor won't extract Jaina, the Convocation disavows Sylvanas. Two capable men, two levers pulled with all available force, the same wall. (See `characters/lorthemar.md` for the full Lor'themar profile and the subplot tracking in `plans/silvermoon-thread.md`.)

## Appearance

Frail in body, formidable in mind. Seventy-one years of library air, lecture halls, and Council chambers have built a body designed for sitting, standing at lecterns, and walking slowly through corridors lined with books. He is thin — not gaunt like Kel'Thuzad, but thin in the way of old men whose appetite was never large and whose energy went elsewhere. His shoulders slope forward from decades of bending over texts. His spine has the permanent curve of a man who spent his thirties hunched over a research desk and his fifties hunched over policy documents and never once thought about posture.

White-bearded, long enough to brush his chest, maintained out of habit rather than vanity. His hair is the same white, still thick, worn long in the Dalaran academic style — tied back when working, loose when at rest. His face is lined in the specific pattern of a man who has spent seventy years thinking: the deep furrows between the brows, the crow's feet from squinting at notation, the parenthetical creases around the mouth from decades of speaking carefully. His eyes are sharp and warm — the combination that made him an effective teacher, an effective politician, and an effective surrogate father to a young prodigy who needed all three.

His robes are Dalaran violet, the full academic regalia of the Council of Six's presiding member. He wears them as identity, not costume — the way Terenas wears his crown. The distinction between the man and the office blurred decades ago. He carries a staff out of tradition rather than necessity; his magic does not require a focus, but the Council's leader carries a staff the way a general wears a sash. His hands are the telling detail: ink-stained at the fingertips from decades of signing documents, the palms soft, the calluses from a pen rather than from lab work. Kel'Thuzad's hands are a researcher's hands — burned, scarred, marked by materials that fought back. Antonidas's hands are an administrator's hands — clean, precise, the damage limited to ink. The contrast tells the story of two careers that started in the same place and diverged.

## Voice Guide

Measured. Paternal. Precise. Antonidas speaks in complete thoughts — not because he is slow, but because he has spent fifty years in rooms where imprecise language caused policy disasters, and the habit of choosing every word has become indistinguishable from his personality. He uses academic qualifiers ("in my judgment," "the evidence suggests," "I would counsel caution") that Jaina finds both comforting and maddening — comforting because they're the sound of home, maddening because the qualifiers are why the Council moves at the speed of consensus rather than the speed of crisis.

### Key Patterns

- **The academic softening:** He hedges. Not from uncertainty — from the political habit of a man who has led a council of six egos for decades. "The Council is considering" means "I'm fighting them." "Progress is being made" means "they voted me down again." "Your findings are being reviewed" means "they don't want to believe you." Jaina has learned to read the gap between what he says and what he means, and the gap widens as the story progresses.

- **The paternal slip:** "Child." He calls her "child" with genuine affection — the word of a man who watched a teenage prodigy grow into the most capable mage of her generation and still sees, underneath the competence, the girl who arrived in Dalaran at fourteen with more talent than she knew what to do with. The word is not condescension. It is the involuntary tenderness of a surrogate father who knows his daughter is in danger and cannot reach her.

- **The silence:** His most expressive tool. The pause on the sending stone when Jaina tells him something that frightens him. The beat of nothing between her report and his response. The sending stone strips out physical cues — no face to read, no hands to watch, no posture to interpret. Only the voice and the silence. When Antonidas goes quiet on the stone, Jaina can hear the shape of what he isn't saying: the fear he won't name, the political reality he can't change, the distance between Dalaran and wherever she is.

- **The measured cadence:** His sentences are complete, balanced, the verbal equivalent of well-drafted policy. He speaks the way he writes — and he writes the way a man writes who knows his documents become law. This is the voice Jaina grew up hearing. This is the voice she associates with authority, competence, home. Its absence, when the stone goes silent, is louder than shouting.

### The Sending-Stone Voice

Most of Antonidas's appearances are through the sending stone — a magical communication device that transmits voice but not visual information. The sending-stone version of Antonidas is slightly different from the in-person version: more careful, more measured, because he knows the stone strips context. In person, his face softens his qualifiers — the warm eyes, the slight smile that says "I'm working on it." On the stone, only the words arrive, and the words alone sound more cautious, more distant, more like the Kirin Tor than like the man behind them. Jaina hears the Kirin Tor's voice on the stone. She remembers the mentor's face behind it. The gap between those two experiences is part of the story's emotional texture.

### Distinction from Kel'Thuzad

Both men are academic voices. The difference is in care. Antonidas speaks *to* his listener — adjusting his vocabulary, softening his conclusions, meeting the other person where they are. He measures his words for the person hearing them. He calibrates. He mentors. Kel'Thuzad speaks *at* his listener — offering the material at the level the material demands, without accommodation. Antonidas would say "the situation is more complex than I can explain on a sending stone." Kel'Thuzad would explain the full complexity regardless of whether the listener could follow.

Both voices are formed by the same training, the same Kirin Tor traditions, the same academic vocabulary. The shared origin makes the divergence sharper: two men who learned to think in the same language, one of whom uses that language to protect and one of whom uses it to destroy.

## Background: The Kirin Tor Career

A genius from childhood — writing advanced arcane theses at age twelve, the kind of prodigy who makes teachers nervous because the questions he asks are better than the questions they prepared. He rose through the Kirin Tor's ranks on intellectual merit: not the fastest, not the most powerful in raw terms, but the most rigorous. His gift was always synthesis — seeing connections between disparate magical disciplines, identifying the principles that unified seemingly unrelated phenomena. The Kirin Tor valued this gift because synthesis is what keeps a body of knowledge coherent across centuries and across thousands of practitioners.

He studied the orcish "lethargy" after the Second War — the mysterious malaise that struck the orcs in their internment camps — with scientific rigor rather than the prevailing prejudice. While most mages dismissed the lethargy as racial weakness or divine punishment, Antonidas approached it as a phenomenon: observable, measurable, potentially explicable. His research was never completed (the political climate shifted; funding was redirected), but the methodology — curiosity without prejudice, observation before conclusion — defined his intellectual character. He sought the truth, not the comfortable answer.

He recognized the necromantic threat within the Kirin Tor's own ranks. Kel'Thuzad's early obsessions were visible to those who looked: the late nights in the restricted stacks, the requisition orders for forbidden texts, the questions in Council sessions that probed the boundaries of acceptable research. Antonidas saw the pattern. He expelled practitioners he considered dangerous — but not quickly enough. By the time the Council voted unanimously to remove Kel'Thuzad, the man had already chosen his path. The expulsion was a door closing behind someone who had already left the building.

The guilt from this failure shapes everything Antonidas does in the story. He is a man who watched a colleague cross a line and responded with committee procedure rather than direct confrontation. He gathered evidence. He built a case. He followed the rules. And by the time the rules produced their result, the damage was already in motion. This is why he trusts Jaina's instincts even when the Council doesn't — because the last time he prioritized consensus over urgency, the result was the Cult of the Damned.

## Key Relationships

### With Jaina — The Surrogate Father

Not just teacher and student — surrogate father and daughter, though neither has named it aloud. He recognized her potential when she arrived in Dalaran at fourteen: raw power that outstripped her training, an analytical mind that outstripped her emotional maturity, and the specific hunger for understanding that he recognized because he carried it himself. He gave her freedom other students didn't receive — access to restricted texts, permission to pursue independent research, the implicit message that her instincts were worth following. This freedom made her the mage she is. It also made her the person who would leave Dalaran without authorization to chase a hunch across a continent.

His pride in her is visible. His fear for her safety is something he tries and fails to hide. In WC3 canon, he sent her to investigate the plague specifically because of her nature: "It's your inquisitive nature that I've come to rely on, child. That crazed fool's convinced that the world is about to end." He trusted her judgment more than his own instincts. He was right to.

The sending-stone conversations with Jaina are the story's version of their relationship: increasingly strained, increasingly honest, the careful language breaking down as the stakes rise. Early calls are mentor and student — he advises, she reports. Later calls are two adults — she briefs, he listens. The final calls are father and daughter — he begs her to come home, she refuses, and the silence on the stone is the silence of a man who knows his child is walking into something he cannot protect her from.

### With Kel'Thuzad — The Colleague He Failed to Stop

Former peers on the Council of Six. They shared the same training, the same academic traditions, the same love of knowledge. They disagreed on where the boundaries of acceptable research should fall, and that disagreement ended in expulsion — but not before years of increasingly tense Council sessions where Antonidas watched a brilliant colleague inch toward a line and tried to pull him back with arguments, with policy, with the tools the system provided.

The question Antonidas carries: could he have stopped it with something other than procedure? Not a committee vote — a conversation. Not an expulsion — an intervention. The answer is probably no. Kel'Thuzad made his choice freely and would have made it regardless. But the question is a weight, and it shapes every sending-stone call to Jaina: the mentor who failed one brilliant student trying desperately not to fail another.

When Kel'Thuzad's name surfaces in Ch 8, the sending-stone silence is the silence of a man hearing the name of his worst failure spoken aloud by the person he most needs to protect from it.

### The Lor'themar Mirror

They never meet in the story. The parallel is for the reader.

Both are competent men operating inside systems that are failing. Lor'themar fights the Convocation of Silvermoon for military resources — troop commitments, supply lines, the permission to act on intelligence that proves an existential threat. Antonidas fights the Council of Six for investigative authority — the mandate to treat Jaina's reports as actionable intelligence rather than the theories of an unauthorized field operative. Both present accurate evidence to governing bodies composed of reasonable people who reach reasonable conclusions that are catastrophically wrong.

Both win. Both win too late. Lor'themar secures the Convocation's mobilization after Sylvanas's Northrend dispatches arrive — but the expeditionary force arrives after the throne room. Antonidas mobilizes Dalaran's political connections to save Jaina — but the Kirin Tor won't extract an accused regicide. Two advocates, two levers, the same wall.

The mirror should land in Ch 25-26 when both fail simultaneously. The reader should feel the parallel even if no character names it.

## The Sending-Stone Thread — Deployment Across Chapters

Antonidas runs through every Jaina chapter primarily through sending-stone exchanges. The progression tracks the story's escalation and the mentor-student relationship's evolution:

- **Ch 2 (First call):** Calm, authoritative, the mentor's voice. He sent her to investigate; he wants to know what she found. The tone is the familiar cadence of a teacher checking on a student's progress. Underneath: relief that she's alive, concern that she's on the elven border, the first hint of worry he won't voice.

- **Ch 7 (Andorhal — the institution delivers):** Antonidas sends intelligence via the stone that proves unexpectedly useful — a detail about Cult methodology from the Kirin Tor archives that Jaina didn't have access to. The information cracks open a dead end in their investigation. For one beat, the Kirin Tor functions as intended: the body of knowledge that Jaina left behind delivers something she couldn't have gotten alone. The moment complicates Jaina's Lie — the system she abandoned has value she's been dismissing.

- **Ch 8 (The name — Kel'Thuzad):** Jaina reports the name. Silence on the stone. The silence of a man hearing his worst failure spoken aloud. When he speaks again, the qualifiers are gone — the careful political language stripped to bare urgency. He knows what that name means. He knows what it means that the name is connected to the plague. He knows, though he will never say it, that the Kirin Tor's fingerprints are on this weapon.

- **Ch 12 (After the conversation):** Jaina contacts Antonidas after the Kel'Thuzad confrontation. She tells him what she learned — the Lich King, the Burning Legion, the true scope. His silence is the longest in the story. The student has surpassed the teacher in understanding the threat, and the teacher knows it. He also knows that Jaina just spent hours in conversation with the man he trained alongside, the man he failed to stop, and the question he cannot ask on a sending stone is: *what did you see in him?*

- **Ch 18 (Before Northrend):** The last call before the ocean. Jaina tells him she's going to Northrend. He urges her to return. She won't. He cannot compel her, cannot order her — she left his authority months ago. The silence at the end of this call is the silence of a mentor who has run out of advice, a father who has run out of arguments, a man watching someone he loves walk toward something he cannot follow.

- **Ch 20 (Intelligence):** A brief exchange — Antonidas relays intelligence about the political situation in Lordaeron. His voice has changed: older, more tired, the political fight wearing him down the way Lor'themar's political fight wears him down. He is losing.

- **Ch 25-26 (The failure):** The Kirin Tor's response to the tribunal. Antonidas mobilizes everything he has — political connections, diplomatic channels, the Kirin Tor's considerable influence. And the Kirin Tor's considerable influence cannot extract an accused regicide from a sovereign kingdom's legal process. The system that was too slow to help is now too cautious to save. Antonidas's final beat in the story is the beat of a man who did everything right within the rules and discovered that the rules were the problem.

## Book 2 Seeds

### The Death That's Coming

In WC3 canon, Antonidas is killed by Arthas when the death knight invades Dalaran to claim the Book of Medivh. This event has not happened yet in this story's timeline — it occurs after the events of Book 1. But the groundwork is being laid: every sending-stone call, every moment of warmth, every flash of the surrogate father's fear for his daughter, is building toward a death that will devastate Jaina in ways the story has spent twenty-six chapters earning.

The mentor who believed her. The mentor who tried. The mentor who failed to mobilize in time but never stopped trying. Killed by the student she couldn't save — Arthas, the prince who was Antonidas's failure made flesh (he trained Kel'Thuzad; the Kirin Tor failed to act on the plague; the system he led produced the conditions that enabled Arthas's fall). The chain of causation loops: Antonidas trained the genius who became the monster, whose plague created the crisis, whose crisis broke the prince, whose fall ends in the mentor's death.

### The Kirin Tor After

With Antonidas gone, the Kirin Tor loses its best advocate, its most capable synthesizer, and the one voice on the Council of Six that consistently prioritized action over caution. The body that was too slow with Antonidas leading it will be slower without him. Jaina inherits not just the grief but the question: is the Kirin Tor worth saving? Can the system that failed — that failed to stop Kel'Thuzad, failed to act on the plague, failed to extract her from the tribunal — be reformed from inside? Or does reform require the same willingness to act outside authority that Jaina has already demonstrated, and that Kel'Thuzad demonstrated before her?

The Lie echoes: the hero version and the villain version of "the system is too slow, I must act alone" are, once again, parallel.

## Prose-Writer Notes

### The Challenge

Antonidas must feel warm without feeling safe. Every scene with him should carry the undertow of "this man is not going to be enough." His love for Jaina is real. His competence is real. His failure to translate either into timely action is also real. The reader should like him, trust him, and feel the slow horror of watching someone they like be insufficient to the crisis.

### The Sending-Stone Constraint

Most of Antonidas's appearances are disembodied voice. The prose writer has no face to describe, no gestures to track, no physical cues to anchor the reader. Everything must be carried by the voice, by the silences, and by Jaina's interiority — what she hears underneath the words, what the pauses mean, what his tone tells her that his vocabulary won't.

This constraint is a strength if used correctly. The sending-stone strips Antonidas to his purest form: the voice. The voice alone. When that voice cracks, or pauses, or goes quiet, the reader has nothing to look at but the silence.

### Voice Traps to Avoid

- **Do not make him doddering.** He is seventy-one and frail, but his mind is razor-sharp. He is not confused, not forgetful, not out of touch. He is a brilliant man trapped in a slow system. The tragedy is clarity without speed, not age without clarity.
- **Do not make him the obstacle mentor.** He is not blocking Jaina. He believes her. He supports her. He is fighting for her within the system. His failure is the system's failure, not his — and the distinction matters because it makes the failure worse. A mentor who refused to listen could be proven wrong. A mentor who listened and still couldn't help is the face of something more frightening: a world where being believed isn't enough.
- **Do not let the sending-stone calls become exposition dumps.** Each call should carry emotional weight as well as plot information. The progression of the calls IS the relationship's arc. Early calls: professional, mentor-student. Middle calls: the warmth breaking through the formality, the fear leaking in. Late calls: raw, the pretense stripped away, two people who love each other unable to help each other.
