# Arthas Menethil — Character Profile

**Role:** Antagonist (tragic)
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Title:** Crown Prince of Lordaeron, Paladin of the Silver Hand
**Age:** Mid-twenties (approximately 24)
**Status in this story:** Pre-Frostmourne at start. His arc traces the full descent: idealistic prince → obsessive commander → death knight.

---

## Appearance

Tall, broad-shouldered, built like the warrior he trained to be. Golden hair, striking blue eyes, the jawline and bearing of someone who has never doubted his right to occupy any space he enters. Handsome in a way that's almost aggressive — the kind of face that makes people trust him before he's said a word, which is exactly the problem. His armor is Silver Hand plate, polished, ornamented with the Menethil crest and the symbol of the Light.

**Light's Vengeance:** His sanctified warhammer, blessed by Uther at his investiture into the Silver Hand. A massive two-handed weapon that glows faintly with the Light when wielded by a worthy paladin. Named for its purpose — to bring the Light's judgment on the undead. Arthas carries it through the entire plague investigation, through Stratholme, through the ship-burning. He only discards it in Frostmourne Cavern, casting it aside when he reaches for the cursed runeblade instead. The hammer lands in the snow outside the cavern, still glowing faintly — a beacon no one will follow. In the later Wrath of the Lich King era, adventurers find it still embedded in Northrend's ice where he threw it. Its abandonment is symbolic but NOT remarked upon in the story — show it hitting the ground, don't editorialize about what it means.

He looks like a hero. Every inch of him looks like a hero.

As the story progresses, the appearance changes: the polish dulls, the armor shows damage he doesn't repair, the eyes develop a fixity that Sylvanas reads as fanaticism and Jaina reads as something worse — the look of a man listening to a voice no one else can hear.

---

## Voice and Register

Commanding, charismatic, raised to lead. Arthas speaks with the assumed authority of someone who has never been denied anything important. He's genuinely intelligent — good tactical instincts, a natural speaker, capable of inspiring real loyalty. But he's also impatient, certain, and fundamentally unable to sit with ambiguity. When he doesn't know the answer, he picks the first answer that feels strong and commits to it absolutely.

**Dialogue patterns:**
- Declarative. Commands disguised as observations. "We need to move" = "I've decided we're moving."
- Interrupts. Not from rudeness — from the genuine conviction that his point is more important than whatever's being said.
- Invokes duty, honor, the kingdom, his father's legacy — not cynically, but as the lens through which he processes every decision.
- Grows shorter and harder as the story progresses. By Northrend, his sentences are clipped, cold, the charisma burned away.

**Key WC3 dialogue (verbatim, for reference):**
- At Stratholme: "Damn it, Uther! As your future king, I order you to purge this city!" / "Then I must consider this an act of treason." / "Lord Uther, by my right of succession and the sovereignty of my crown, I hereby relieve you of your command and suspend your paladins from service." / "I'll hunt you to the ends of the earth if I have to! Do you hear me? To the ends of the earth!"
- To Mal'Ganis at Stratholme: "I won't allow it, Mal'Ganis! Better that these people die by my hand than serve as your slaves in death!"
- Ship-burning (to Falric): "Burn them. All of them." (Blames mercenaries for the burning — a calculated lie.)
- At Frostmourne Cavern: "Then I will make do with what I have. I would sacrifice everything to save my people." (He means it. He does.)
- Throne room: "Succeeding you, Father."

## Background

Born to King Terenas Menethil II and Queen Lianne. Trained from boyhood by Muradin Bronzebeard (combat) and Uther the Lightbringer (faith, tactics, morality). Inducted into the Silver Hand as one of the order's most promising paladins. His early military career was marked by genuine heroism and a sincere desire to protect his people.

He loved three things absolutely: his kingdom, the Light, and the idea of himself as hero-king. The Lich King corrupted him through the third by threatening the first — because Arthas could not accept a world where he failed to save Lordaeron, he would do anything to succeed. Including becoming the very thing he fought.

---

## The Ship-Burning Incident (Northrend)

Critical event between Chapters 8-9. After arriving in Northrend, Arthas secretly hired mercenaries to burn his own ships, then blamed the mercenaries for treachery and executed them — eliminating both the witnesses and his men's escape route. Falric likely knew (or suspected). The men had no choice but to follow Arthas deeper into Northrend, because there was no way home.

This is the point where Arthas stopped being a flawed prince and became something else. Muradin Bronzebeard recognized it: "The lad's not right."

---

## The Frostmourne Scene

In the ice cavern, the guardian spirit warns them to turn back. Muradin reads the inscription — "Whomsoever takes up this blade shall wield power eternal. Just as the blade rends flesh, so must power scar the spirit" — and tells Arthas they need to leave. Arthas doesn't care. He kneels and speaks to the cavern: "I would gladly bear any curse to save my homeland." The ice shatters. Frostmourne is free. A shard strikes Muradin in the chest. Arthas takes the blade without looking back at his fallen friend.

In this story, Sylvanas and Jaina witness this from a concealed position, and Jaina pulls Muradin's unconscious body clear while Arthas walks away with the runeblade, not checking whether the dwarf is alive.

## Personality

- **Core trait:** Certainty. Arthas cannot tolerate not knowing. He cannot tolerate inaction. He cannot tolerate the space between identifying a problem and solving it. This makes him decisive, inspiring, and ultimately catastrophic — because the problems he's facing don't have clean solutions, and his inability to accept that drives him to increasingly monstrous "solutions."
- **Strength:** Genuine courage, genuine compassion (at the start), the ability to inspire loyalty. He means it. The tragedy is that he means every word, right up to and including "Succeeding you, Father."
- **Flaw:** He experiences uncertainty as personal failure. Every moment he doesn't have the answer is a moment people are dying because of him. This makes him the ideal target for the Lich King's manipulation: offer him a weapon that promises certainty, and he'll reach for it.

## Arc in This Story

Arthas appears in Chapters 6 (Strahnbrad), 13-16 (the Arthas block: The Prince → The Split → Stratholme → The Wall), 19-21 (Northrend / Frostmourne), and 24 (The Throne Room). He's never POV — observed from outside by Sylvanas and Jaina, which makes his descent more horrible because it's read entirely through behavioral cues.

**The structural argument:** Arthas's journey IS Kel'Thuzad's origin story repeated. The same arc of brilliant conviction without counterweight, the same descent into atrocity justified by necessity. He even follows Kel'Thuzad's path literally — tracked him, killed him, and then became the next iteration of the same pattern. The difference: Kel'Thuzad started alone (the Kirin Tor expelled him). Arthas had partners — Uther, Jaina — and lost them all. Jaina left him years ago because she saw the gap. Uther bailed at Stratholme when the moral framework broke. Once alone, his certainty escalated without friction. Partnership was the fork. He took the other road.

- **Chapter 6 (Strahnbrad):** The golden prince. Charismatic, commanding, chasing Blackrock orc raids — Mal'Ganis's manufactured distraction. He's busy, certain, utterly convinced the orcs are the real threat. Jaina tries to warn him about the grain. He's polite, dismissive. The personal history makes the dismissal cut deeper.
- **Chapter 13 (The Prince):** Post-Kel'Thuzad. Arrives at the plague crisis weeks late, absorbing in hours what Jaina and Sylvanas built over weeks. Speed of certainty, absence of doubt. The suppressed bitterness from the breakup compounds with professional humiliation — his ex-lover and an elven ranger have intelligence he needs and didn't gather himself.
- **Chapter 14 (The Split):** The fork. Same question as Jaina: "What do you do when the system is too slow?" He answers with escalation — take the army, override authority with force. She answers with persistence. Same conviction, opposite methods.
- **Chapters 15-16 (Stratholme):** The breaking point. He's right about the math (the city will fall to plague) and wrong about the solution (slaughter everyone preemptively). Relieves Uther of command — the last partner gone. The horror isn't that he does it; it's that part of the logic is sound. Both Jaina and Sylvanas know it.
- **Chapter 19 (Frostmourne):** The burned fleet (retroactively revealed — he stranded his own army). The obsessive chase for the blade. He's no longer the prince — something between a zealot and a sleepwalker. Light's Vengeance goes dark in his hands before he casts it aside. He takes Frostmourne without looking back at Muradin.
- **Chapter 22 (The Throne Room):** Whatever Arthas was is gone. Frostmourne doesn't just give him power — it completes the process that Stratholme started. "Succeeding you, Father." The blade goes in. He shrugs off Sylvanas's arrow. Jaina's three-school spell finds the flaw. The second arrow kills him. The journey that repeated Kel'Thuzad's arc ends the same way — destroyed by someone who followed a different path from the same starting point.

## Voice Progression — Phase by Phase

Arthas is never POV, so prose writers only render his voice through dialogue and observed behavior. This section tracks how he sounds at each stage, giving writers concrete cues for dialogue rhythm, word choice, and body language.

### Phase 1: Strahnbrad (Chapter 6)

**Register:** Warm command. The golden prince at his most magnetic.

**Dialogue traits:**
- Full sentences, generous rhythm. He has time for people.
- Genuine humor — teasing, self-aware, the kind of charm that makes soldiers love him.
- Commands wrapped in warmth: "Ride with me" not "Follow." "We finish this before dark" with a grin that says he believes it.
- Addresses people by name. Makes eye contact. Touches shoulders.
- When he disagrees, he's indulgent rather than dismissive — "I appreciate the concern, Lady Proudmoore, but the orcs won't wait for certainty."

**Body language:** Open posture, easy movement, fills space naturally. Laughs. Eats with his men. Light's Vengeance rests across his knees like a trusted tool, not a weapon.

**What's underneath:** Nothing sinister yet. This is the real Arthas — the one people mourn later. The only tell is speed: he decides fast, acts faster, and doesn't revisit decisions once made. At Strahnbrad this reads as confidence. Later it reads as pathology.

### Phase 2: Hearthglen / Investigation (Chapters 13–15)

**Register:** Formal command. The warmth is still there but it's rationed now — deployed tactically, withheld from people who've earned his suspicion.

**Dialogue traits:**
- Shorter sentences. The generous rhythm is tightening.
- "Lady Proudmoore" instead of "Jaina" — the formality is a wall, not courtesy. She left him. He hasn't forgotten.
- Questions that are accusations: "And how long have you known this?" = "Why didn't you tell me sooner?"
- Invokes his title when challenged. "I am the Crown Prince of Lordaeron" is not a boast — it's a load-bearing argument. If his rank doesn't settle the question, nothing will.
- Still capable of gentleness in isolated moments — with Falric, with individual soldiers, with civilians. The warmth hasn't died; it's just retreating to a smaller circle.

**Body language:** Stiffer. Hand rests on Light's Vengeance more often. Paces when receiving reports. Stands when others sit — not from restlessness, but from the need to be above the information, looking down at it.

**What's underneath:** The certainty is hardening into something load-bearing. He's carrying the kingdom's safety as personal debt, and every hour of inaction accrues interest. Jaina and Sylvanas have intelligence he needs, gathered without him, and the compound humiliation — professional and personal — makes every interaction a controlled detonation.

### Phase 3: Stratholme (Chapters 16–17)

**Register:** Stripped command. The charm is gone. What remains is the voice of a man who has done the math and decided that everyone else's moral hesitation is a luxury purchased with other people's lives.

**Dialogue traits:**
- Clipped. Declarative. No questions — questions imply uncertainty.
- "This city must be purged." Not argued. Stated.
- When Uther objects, the response isn't persuasion — it's hierarchy: "As your future king, I order you." He reaches for rank because the argument has failed, and rank is the only tool left.
- The word "must" does heavy lifting. Everything is necessary. Nothing is chosen.
- Interrupts constantly now. Not from conviction that his point matters more — from inability to hear alternatives without experiencing them as threats.

**Body language:** Rigid. Jaw set. Eyes fixed on the middle distance or on the person he's overriding. Light's Vengeance in hand, not sheathed — he's been carrying the hammer like he expects to swing it at any moment. No laughter. No warmth in the eyes. The soldiers who loved him at Strahnbrad are watching a stranger.

**What's underneath:** He's right about the plague math. The city will turn. That's the horror — the logic is sound, and the solution is monstrous, and he can't hold both truths at once, so he collapses them: the math IS the morality. Once that collapse happens, everyone who disagrees is either too weak to face reality or actively complicit in the deaths that will follow. Uther isn't wrong; Uther is a coward. Jaina didn't leave because she saw something true; she abandoned him.

### Phase 4: Northrend / Frostmourne (Chapters 19–21)

**Register:** Mechanical. The prince is gone. What moves through Northrend is an engine of purpose with Arthas's face.

**Dialogue traits:**
- Orders only. No persuasion, no explanation, no warmth.
- Sentences of five words or fewer: "Burn them." "Move." "We press forward."
- Doesn't use names anymore. "You" or nothing.
- The ship-burning lie is delivered flat — no performance, no manufactured outrage. He says the mercenaries betrayed them. If anyone doubts it, he doesn't notice or doesn't care.
- At the cavern: "I would gladly bear any curse to save my homeland." — the last sentence with any rhetorical shape. It's a prayer, spoken to a sword, and he means it the way a drowning man means "air."

**Body language:** Economical. No wasted movement. Doesn't look at people when speaking to them — looks at the direction he's moving. Light's Vengeance has gone dim in his grip; the glow that marked a paladin's blessing is guttering. When he casts it aside for Frostmourne, he doesn't throw it — he opens his hand and lets it fall, the way you drop something you've forgotten you were holding.

**What's underneath:** Whether the Lich King's whispers are audible yet is deliberately ambiguous. What's visible from outside is a man running on fumes of purpose: save the kingdom, find the weapon, end the threat. The connective tissue between those goals — logic, proportion, mercy — has been burned away. Muradin sees it: "The lad's not right." But Muradin follows him anyway, because what else do you do when the prince you helped raise walks into the dark?

### Phase 5: The Throne Room (Chapter 24)

**Register:** Gone. What speaks in the throne room is not Arthas in any meaningful sense.

**Dialogue traits:**
- Two words: "Succeeding you, Father."
- Flat. Functional. The intonation of someone completing an administrative task.
- No emotion in the delivery — not rage, not grief, not triumph. The blade goes in with the same mechanical precision as his Northrend orders. If Terenas answers, Arthas doesn't hear it.
- After the murder, silence. He doesn't announce himself. Doesn't make speeches. Doesn't respond to Sylvanas's first arrow or Jaina's challenge with words. The blade does his talking now.
- If he speaks at all during the fight, it's involuntary — a grunt of impact, a sound forced out by Jaina's spell hitting the seam. Not language. Sound.

**Body language:** Still. Unnaturally still, until he moves — and then the movement is too fast, too precise, the body executing patterns it shouldn't know. He sits the throne after the blade comes out of Terenas, and the posture is wrong — a puppet settled into a chair by someone who has seen chairs but never sat in one. When he stands to fight, it's the same wrongness: the body of the golden prince moving like something else is steering.

**What's underneath:** The question the story refuses to answer: Is Arthas in there? When Jaina's spell finds the seam — the gap in Frostmourne's cycle — does something flicker behind his eyes? Is the grunt of impact pain, or recognition, or nothing? Sylvanas's second arrow goes through the seam Jaina opened. He falls. If there was anything of Arthas left, it dies without speaking. The prose must leave this ambiguous. No internal access. No revelation. Just a body that stops moving, and a sword that hits the floor, and the sound the sword makes when it lands.

### Writing Notes for All Phases

- **Never access his interiority.** He's observed, always. What Jaina sees, what Sylvanas sees, what the soldiers see. The prose writer's job is behavioral precision — the tells that let the reader construct the interior, without the text confirming anything.
- **The voice degrades in a specific direction:** warm → formal → clipped → mechanical → silent. Each stage loses something human. By the end, he communicates through the sword.
- **Charm is a weapon, then a memory.** In Phase 1, his charisma is real. By Phase 3, the ghost of it appears when he needs something — a flash of the old Arthas to rally troops or dismiss opposition. By Phase 4, even the ghost is gone.
- **Light's Vengeance tracks the voice.** The hammer's glow dims as his speech degrades. It's not a metaphor the text should point to — it's a detail that exists in both streams simultaneously, and the reader connects them.
- **"Succeeding you, Father" is the only line that matters.** It echoes WC3 exactly. The prose must not embellish it, contextualize it, or add to it. Two words. Flat delivery. The blade moves. That's everything.
