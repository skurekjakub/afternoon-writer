## King Terenas Menethil II

**Role:** King of Lordaeron, Arthas's father, assassination victim
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Title:** King of Lordaeron, founder of the Alliance of Lordaeron
**Age:** Elderly (late sixties/seventies)

The wise, patient, gentle-but-firm monarch of Lordaeron's golden age. A skilled diplomat who valued the unity of the human kingdoms above all. He founded the original Alliance during the Second War and held it together through peacetime politics, balancing competing egos and national interests with quiet resolve. He is not a warrior — he rules with wisdom and words, not with steel.

**Appearance:** Elderly, dignified. White hair and beard, weathered face, the bearing of a man who has carried a crown for decades. His eyes are kind but tired. He wears the crown and robes of state not as costume but as identity — he has been the king for so long that the distinction between the man and the office has blurred.

**Key relationship with Arthas:** Loving father, increasingly worried. He named his son at birth, watched him grow, sent him to train under Uther. His speech to the infant Arthas (from WC3 cinematic): "My son. The day you were born, the very forests of Lordaeron whispered the name, Arthas… Remember, our line has always ruled with wisdom, and strength. And I know that you will show restraint when exercising your great power. But the truest victory, my son, is stirring the hearts of your people. I tell you this, for when my days have come to an end, you shall be king."

**The Throne Room Scene:**
Terenas sits on the throne as Arthas returns. The court assembled, light streaming through the octagonal window. He is relieved and proud:
- **Terenas:** "Ah, my son. I knew you would be victorious."
- **Terenas:** "What is this? What are you doing, my son?"
- **Arthas:** "Succeeding you, Father."

The blade goes in. The crown falls. It rolls across the white marble floor.

**Voice:** Calm, measured, regal. Speaks with the dignity of long practice. Fatherly warmth underneath formal register. His tone carries both authority and gentleness — the voice of a man who has learned that the best rulers listen more than they speak.

## The Farstrider Escort

### Ranger Lyriel

**Role:** Farstrider scout, escort member — the one who dies
**Species:** High elf (she/her)
**Chapters:** 2–10 (dies in Ch 10, the necromantic trap)

The second escort ranger, riding south with Cyndia from the Chapter 2 release onward. Younger, less experienced on the border but solid in the field — she earned her post through clean fieldwork, not connections. Where Cyndia is watchful and measured, Lyriel is quick — fast to draw, fast to report, fast to move. She and Cyndia fall into a paired formation naturally once Neral peels north in Ch 3: Cyndia left flank, Lyriel right, a rhythm that develops its own wordless coordination over weeks of travel.

She's been walking with them for eight chapters when she dies. That's the point. Not deeply known, not in the way the reader knows the leads — but in the way you know someone who's been at your shoulder for weeks: their habits, their silences, the way they move through terrain. The reader should have stopped noticing her. And then she's gone.

**Function:** Her death in Ch 10 is the cost of Sylvanas's mistake — the tactical advance without Jaina's arcane sweep. The trap that kills her is the kind Jaina would have detected in three seconds. Her death is the first REAL casualty of the partnership's growing pains, and the reason Sylvanas starts waiting for Jaina's sweep before advancing. Every time Cyndia checks the right flank twice from that point forward, the reader is seeing what Sylvanas cost them.

**Voice:** Quick, professional, the clipped efficiency of someone who communicates by pointing and nodding more than by speaking. In the rare moments she does talk — campfire, trail — she's surprisingly wry. A dry humor that doesn't survive her.

### Ranger Neral

**Role:** Farstrider scout, temporary third escort, northbound dispatch rider and survivor escort
**Species:** High elf (she/her)
**Chapters:** 2–3 (rides south in the initial escort, then exits north with the warning dispatch and the Millhaven survivor)

Neral is the third named ranger in the early investigation escort: Cyndia as the experienced border officer, Lyriel as the quick right-flank scout, and Neral as the best road rider in the formation. She matters because the story needs someone Sylvanas can spare once the evidence at Millhaven shows the risen dead are likely mustering toward Quel'Thalas. Neral is that rider — competent, fast, and temporary by design — and she becomes the humane answer to the survivor problem by escorting the Millhaven girl north when the warning dispatch leaves.

Her function is structural and military rather than emotional, but she should have one human edge the others do not: she is quietly good with frightened children. In Ch 3 that matters because she is the first ranger the survivor girl trusts enough to move with. She makes the early escort feel like a real field detail instead of an abstract "some rangers" cloud, and her departure in Ch 3 is what leaves the investigation party at its durable early-war shape: Sylvanas, Jaina, Cyndia, and Lyriel.

**Voice:** Efficient, brief, more road-practical than lyrical. She sounds like someone used to carrying messages that matter more than her opinions about them. Around children, that clipped manner softens into quiet practicality — the kind of ranger who can get a terrified girl moving by talking about safe stables, good feed, and the white horned "unicorn" mounts Silvermoon children grow up romanticizing.

## The Barov Family

**Role:** Minor nobility, Cult collaborators, Jaina's access vector to Scholomance
**Species:** Human
**Chapters:** 10 (primarily)

The Barovs of Caer Darrow: old money, minor nobility, extensive grain holdings across the Eastweald. They gave Kel'Thuzad their ancestral fortress in exchange for the promise of immortality. The family is still alive, still operating their estates, still maintaining a public face. They are not prisoners — they are collaborators. The grain network that's poisoning a continent runs through their lands.

When Jaina arrives with Kirin Tor authority, the Barovs are caught between refusing (confirming something worth hiding) and allowing access (risking exposure). They choose controlled access — a guided tour of what they present as a "scholarly academy." Jaina reads the wards, maps the arcane signatures, and sees through the pretense.

**Function:** (1) The paper-trail endpoint — contaminated grain traces to Barov holdings. (2) Jaina's access vector to Scholomance — she enters through the front door, not by raid. (3) The nobility's complicity in the plague — ordinary people who made an extraordinary deal for understandable (immortality) reasons, from a position of privilege.

### Lord Alexei Barov

**Role:** Head of the Barov family, the man who made the deal
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Age:** Early sixties

Silver-haired, dignified, the bearing of a man whose family has held these lands since before the founding of Lordaeron. His hands are soft — he has never worked a field, never loaded a grain wagon. Other people do that. He manages WEALTH: contracts, holdings, the machinery of generational privilege. He received Kel'Thuzad when the necromancer arrived at his door and saw not a madman but an opportunity — an expelled Kirin Tor genius offering immortality in exchange for a fortress the family barely used. The math was simple. The consequences were someone else's problem.

Jaina meets him briefly in the estate's great hall. He is courteous, unhurried, entirely unsurprised by a Kirin Tor visit — which is the first wrong note. He deflects her questions about the grain operations with practiced ease and delegates her to his daughter. He has done his part. The details are Jandice's department.

**Appearance:** Tall, thin, the kind of elderly that wealth produces — maintained, manicured, the grey at his temples styled rather than neglected. His clothes are finer than rural nobility typically wears — silk lined with Dalaran-imported enchanted thread that regulates temperature. His study has a fireplace large enough to stand in, and it is cold because the walls are heated by enchanted stones. A man who buys magical convenience the way other people buy firewood.

**Voice:** Warm, unhurried, the register of a man who has never been rushed in his life and does not intend to start now. He speaks about the grain contamination with the concern of a good neighbor and the detachment of a man who knows exactly where it came from. His sentences are complete, polished, the verbal equivalent of his silk cuffs — perfectly fitted and hiding the hands underneath.

## Apprentice Corin

**Role:** Kirin Tor contact in Andorhal, Antonidas subplot thread
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Chapters:** 7 (possibly 8)

A junior mage stationed in Andorhal as part of the Kirin Tor's network of regional correspondents — the eyes and ears Dalaran maintains in major cities. He's not powerful, not important, not high-ranking. He's the institutional machinery actually working: the part of the Kirin Tor that functions as intended. He has information, gathered through months of quiet observation, that advances the investigation.

A nerd. Thoroughly, completely, helplessly a nerd. Antonidas personally assigned him to Andorhal — which means the Archmage himself said his name, looked at him, and gave him a task. Corin has been vibrating at a low frequency about this for months. Now Antonidas has sent JAINA PROUDMOORE — the prodigy, the one everyone in Dalaran whispers about — and she's standing in his cramped office above the grain exchange, and she's brought the RANGER-GENERAL OF SILVERMOON with her, and Corin is trying very hard to remember how to form complete sentences. He's the kind of person who prepared a folder. Who has notes. Who organized everything alphabetically and then re-organized it chronologically and then cross-referenced both systems. His hands shake when he hands them over. His intel is excellent.

**Function:** He carries the Antonidas subplot beat for Ch 7: "the institution delivers useful intel." Through him, Jaina sees the Kirin Tor actually functioning — a crack in her "I don't need them" Lie. The institution she's been operating without is, at the ground level, doing useful work. The failure isn't the people in the field; it's the hierarchy above them.

**Voice:** Nervous, precise, over-prepared. The register of someone who has rehearsed this conversation thirty times and is now forgetting every rehearsal. His sentences are too long when he's anxious (which is always) and crystallize into startling clarity when he's talking about his actual work — the data, the patterns, the anomalies he's tracked. He's a better analyst than he knows. Antonidas didn't pick him by accident.

## Captain Aldric Havel

**Role:** Andorhal militia captain, local authority
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Chapters:** 7

The man responsible for keeping order in Lordaeron's agricultural hub. He's not corrupt, not Cult-compromised — he's overwhelmed. Andorhal is a trade city, not a fortress. His militia is trained for drunken merchants and petty theft, not for the kind of threat that's been quietly threading itself through his city's grain supply for months.

**Function:** The face of Andorhal's local authority during the infiltration. He's either an obstacle (suspicious of outside inspectors), an unwitting asset (cooperative but unaware of the Cult's presence), or both. His obliviousness highlights how embedded the Cult's operation is — the militia captain of the city doesn't even know.

**Voice:** Harried, practical, defensive in the way of a man who knows he's out of his depth but can't afford to show it. He talks fast. He lists things he's done to sound competent. He's not stupid — he's just solving the wrong problems, because no one told him what the right problems were.

## Captain Dagny Ironhull

**Role:** Ship captain, Northrend voyage
**Species:** Human (she/her)
**Chapters:** 18

The captain of the merchant vessel Sylvanas and Jaina charter to reach Northrend. Nobody sails to Northrend willingly. She does, because the payment is large enough and because she has enough sense to be frightened and enough courage to go anyway.

**Function:** Outside perspective in the dialogue-heavy voyage chapter. She sees what the reader has been tracking for seventeen chapters — two women who move like a single creature — and provides the angle of someone encountering this partnership without context. Also practical: she's the one who navigates the northern waters, who warns them about ice, who keeps the ship alive while Jaina pushes it with arcane wind.

**Function note:** She also serves as the "civilian witness" — a normal person's reaction to the story Sylvanas and Jaina carry. She sees their faces when they talk about Stratholme and doesn't ask again.

**Voice:** Blunt, salt-weathered, the kind of woman who gives orders by habit and doesn't repeat herself. She calls Sylvanas "Ranger" and Jaina "Mage" because titles are what she uses and names are for people she drinks with. Her ship is her world. She runs it with absolute competence and no patience for passengers who don't understand that the ocean is trying to kill them. Under the gruffness: sharp, observant, the kind of intelligence that keeps a merchant vessel afloat in waters most captains won't sail.

**Title:** Captain of the King's Guard
**Age:** Late twenties/early thirties

## Tribunal Cast — Chapter 25

The tribunal is not a faceless machine. It is specific people making specific decisions, each decision reasonable in isolation, catastrophic in the aggregate. These are the faces of the system that condemns S&J.

### Lord Magistrate Aldous Thornton

**Role:** Presiding judge of the regicide tribunal
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Title:** Lord Magistrate of Lordaeron's High Court
**Age:** Mid-sixties
**Chapters:** 25

The kingdom's most respected jurist, appointed to the tribunal because the crown requires its most credible authority to preside over its most devastating case. Thornton has served on the bench for thirty-one years. He presided over the Alterac treason trials after the Second War. He sentenced Lord Perenolde to exile with a verdict so precisely reasoned that even Perenolde's allies called it fair.

He is not corrupt. He is not lazy. He is not cruel. He is a decent man applying a functional legal framework to a situation that exceeds the framework's capacity. The law knows how to process conspiracy, treason, and regicide. The law does not know how to process a cursed sword that eats souls, an undead army commanded from a frozen continent, or two foreign women who tracked a cosmic invasion force across half the world. Thornton's legal mind processes what it can perceive, and what it can perceive is: evidence, precedent, motive, opportunity. By every metric his training provides, the accused are guilty. His verdict is wrong. His reasoning is impeccable.

**Appearance:** Tall, lean, the severity of a man whose authority comes from precision rather than volume. Grey hair cropped short. A narrow face dominated by the kind of eyes that miss nothing in a courtroom — the movement of a hand, the flicker of a glance, the hesitation before an answer. He wears the Lord Magistrate's robe without ornament: black wool, white collar, the silver chain of office. His hands are ink-stained from decades of writing opinions. He holds his pen the way a soldier holds a weapon — loosely, with practiced certainty.

**Voice:** Measured, exact, the voice of a man who has spent thirty years choosing words the way a mason chooses stones. He does not raise his voice. He does not need to. The courtroom goes quiet when he speaks because the courtroom has learned that when Thornton speaks, the words matter. He addresses witnesses as "ser" or "madam" regardless of rank. He calls the accused "the defendant" even when the room has already convicted them in its mind. He is fair. His fairness is the tragedy: the system working as designed, producing an unjust outcome from valid procedure.

**Key function in Ch 25:** Thornton IS the tribunal. His procedural decisions — what evidence is admitted, which witnesses are heard, how much time the defense receives — shape the outcome. Every ruling he makes is defensible. Every ruling narrows the path to acquittal. The prosecution does not need to cheat. It needs only a fair judge applying law that was never designed for this.

### Lord Prosecutor Reynard Ashcombe

**Role:** Lead prosecutor in the regicide tribunal
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Title:** Lord Prosecutor of the Crown
**Age:** Late forties
**Chapters:** 25

A former military intelligence officer turned crown advocate. Ashcombe spent fifteen years in Lordaeron's intelligence corps before transitioning to prosecution — the same analytical mind, redirected from gathering intelligence to presenting it. He reads evidence the way a commander reads a battlefield: for patterns, connections, the shape of the enemy's plan.

He is smart. He is thorough. He genuinely believes the prosecution's theory. The narrative he builds — two foreign agents who orchestrated Arthas's corruption as a weapon against Lordaeron — is not fabricated. It is the most coherent interpretation of the evidence available to someone who does not know about the Lich King. The facts are real. The connections are documented. The only thing wrong is the direction of causation, and that error is invisible from inside the framework.

**Appearance:** Compact, sharp-featured, the kind of man whose physical presence is defined by energy rather than size. Dark hair silvering at the temples. His eyes are the feature witnesses remember afterward: pale grey, attentive, the gaze of someone who listens to what you are NOT saying as carefully as what you are. He moves in the courtroom the way he once moved in the field: economical, deliberate, every step placed for effect. He carries no notes. He has the case in his head — every document, every timeline, every inconsistency he plans to exploit.

**Voice:** Controlled, precise, the pace of a man who understands that silence between sentences is a weapon. He speaks to the tribunal in the tone of reasoned certainty — not passion, not outrage, but the quiet confidence of someone who believes the evidence speaks for itself and his job is merely to arrange the introduction. When he addresses witnesses, his voice shifts: warmer, inviting, the manner of a man who wants you to feel comfortable telling him everything. When he addresses the accused, the warmth drops to room temperature. Not hostile. Professional. The courtesy of a man who has already convicted you in his analysis and considers the trial a formality.

**Key function in Ch 25:** Ashcombe builds the narrative that turns competence into conspiracy. He does not lie. He does not fabricate. He takes real evidence — the warnings, the investigation, the partnership, the Scholomance knowledge — and arranges it into a picture that makes S&J look like the architects of Lordaeron's destruction. The prosecution's case is his masterpiece, and the masterpiece is built entirely from truth misdirected.

### Captain Gavren Marsh

**Role:** Palace guard captain who arrested S&J in the throne room
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Title:** Captain of the Palace Guard (night watch)
**Age:** Mid-forties
**Chapters:** 24 (unnamed, the guard who enters the chamber), 25 (named, testifying)

A twenty-year veteran of the palace guard. Marsh was the senior officer on duty when the throne room doors opened. He entered the Imperial Chamber and processed what he saw with the professionalism of a career soldier trained for crisis: assess the scene, secure the threats, protect the survivors.

He saw: two dead royals, two foreign women with weapons, a cursed sword on a frozen floor. He made the arrest. He called for reinforcements. He sealed the chamber. He did everything right.

His testimony at the tribunal is the prosecution's foundation — an honest, precise, professional account of what the scene looked like to the first responder. Marsh does not embellish. He does not speculate about motive. He describes what he saw, what he did, and what the scene told a twenty-year veteran about what had happened. What the scene told him was: the two women in the room were the most likely perpetrators.

He is not wrong to interpret the scene that way. Any guard in any kingdom, entering any throne room to find two dead royals and two armed foreigners, would reach the same conclusion. The truth — that the foreigners were the heroes and the dead prince was the villain — requires context that Marsh does not have and that the scene does not provide.

**Appearance:** Broad-shouldered, weathered, the build of a man who has spent two decades in armor. His face has the permanent sunburn of someone who has stood watch on battlements in every season. Brown hair going grey at the temples. A scar on his jawline from a bar brawl fifteen years ago that he does not discuss. His hands are large, calloused, steady — the hands of a man who has drawn a sword a thousand times and never needed to think about the motion.

**Voice:** Clipped, factual, military. He testifies the way he files reports: subject, verb, observation. No adjectives unless they are relevant to threat assessment. "I entered the chamber. I observed two deceased males on and near the throne. I observed two females, one elven, one human, armed, in proximity to the deceased. I ordered the females to stand down. They complied." The precision is damning because it is honest. The scene he describes is the scene the prosecution needs.

**Key function in Ch 25:** Marsh is the prosecution's unimpeachable witness. He has no motive to lie. He has no connection to the accused. He is a career guard who walked into a room and reported what he saw. His testimony cannot be challenged on bias or credibility. It can only be challenged on interpretation — and interpretation requires the tribunal to believe a story more fantastical than anything Lordaeron's legal system has ever processed.

## Book 2 Setup Characters

### Ser Edric Valecrest — The Ambitious Knight

**Role:** Palace guard officer, vault sentry, Frostmourne's most likely next victim
**Species:** Human (he/him)
**Title:** Ser (knighted at eighteen, a courtesy afforded minor nobles entering the guard)
**Age:** Twenty-four
**House:** Valecrest — minor Lordaeron nobility, landed gentry in the Eastweald foothills. The family holds a keep, three tenant farms, and a name that appears in court registries but never in court histories. Their crest is a silver falcon on a blue field. Their wealth peaked two generations ago and has been in polite decline since.
**Chapters:** Offscreen presence in Ch 24-26 (vault duty rosters, guard mentions); named function begins Book 2

### Appearance

Tall for a Lordaeron guardsman — six feet, the kind of height that makes superiors notice you on parade and assign you to visible posts. Dark auburn hair, kept short in the military style. Clean-shaven — he can grow a beard but it comes in patchy, which he considers a personal failing he addresses by shaving twice daily. His features are regular, pleasant, forgettable in the way that second sons of minor houses are forgettable: the face of a man who could be any young officer in any kingdom's guard. Brown eyes with the particular alertness of someone who is always scanning a room for the person whose opinion matters most.

His hands are his tell. When he is at ease, they are still — parade-ground discipline, the guard's resting position. When he is anxious, the right thumb traces the falcon crest embossed on his belt buckle, a nervous habit he does not know he has. The belt buckle is the one piece of his equipment that is not standard issue: House Valecrest's falcon, cast in silver that his mother polished before he left for Capital City. It is the most expensive thing he owns.

He carries himself with the careful posture of a man who learned early that bearing compensates for bloodline. His armor is always immaculate — not because he is vain but because he knows that a minor noble's son cannot afford to look anything less than exemplary. The effort is visible to anyone who has served: too precise, too maintained, the attention of someone performing competence rather than inhabiting it. He will grow out of this. Frostmourne will ensure he does not get the chance.

### Personality

Edric is not a fool and not a villain. He is a competent young officer who happens to have been born with exactly the wrong combination of qualities for proximity to a soul-consuming blade: ambition without ceiling, loyalty without reward, and the particular hunger of a man who has spent his life being adequate when he desperately wants to be exceptional.

He is genuinely good at his job. His patrol reports are thorough. His subordinates respect him — he remembers names, covers shifts without complaint, never pulls rank for personal advantage. He trains hard, fights clean, follows orders with the precision of someone who believes that diligence is the ladder that bloodline denied him. His commanding officers rate him "reliable" and "promising" — words that damn with adequacy, words that tell a second son of a minor house that he will spend his career being almost enough.

The ambition is not corrupt. He does not want power for its own sake. He wants the court to see the falcon on his belt and know the name behind it. He wants his mother to hear that her son was mentioned in dispatches, was trusted with a sensitive post, was recognized. He wants the keep in the foothills to mean something again. The wanting is ordinary. The blade makes ordinary wanting lethal.

### What the Blade Exploits

Frostmourne does not offer Edric power. It offers him *visibility*. The dreams show the throne room — not him sitting on the throne, but him standing before it and being *seen*. The court acknowledges him. His name is spoken by people who matter. The falcon crest gleams on a breastplate that is no longer standard guard issue but something finer, something earned, something that tells the world House Valecrest produced a man worth noticing.

The seduction is precise because it is proportional. A man who dreamed of conquest would dream of armies. A man who dreamed of wealth would dream of gold. Edric dreams of a room full of people who finally know his name. The modesty of the desire is what makes it deadly: he can almost convince himself the dreams are just ambition, just the same wanting he has carried his whole life, just the hunger that any second son of any minor house would recognize. The blade counts on that almost. The distance between "just ambition" and "the sword is warm in my hand" is shorter than Edric thinks.

### The Vault Duty Progression

- **Week 1:** Edric volunteers for vault sentry duty three times. He tells himself it is because the post is a mark of trust — sensitive evidence, high-profile case, the kind of assignment that gets an officer noticed. This is true. It is not the whole truth.
- **Week 2:** He requests permanent vault assignment. His commanding officer approves — the post is cold, boring, and no one else wants it. Edric considers this a victory. The cold does not bother him. (It should.)
- **Week 3:** He extends his shifts. Arrives early, leaves late. Stands in the corridor outside the lead-lined chest and listens to — what? He cannot describe it. Not a sound. A pressure. A sense that the air in the vault has weight and the weight has intention. He does not report this because he is not certain it is real, and because reporting uncertain perceptions is the kind of thing that gets a promising young officer reassigned to gate duty.
- **Week 4 (the claiming):** A night shift. The vault empty. The wards on the chest visibly cracked, the cold seeping through the seams. Edric stands in the cold for hours, and the cold feels like company. His hand moves to the chest's latch. He does not remember deciding to open it. The sword is warm. After weeks of cold, it radiates warmth — the consuming field reversed for one heartbeat, drawing him in the way a fire draws a freezing traveler. By the time the warmth becomes cold again, Ser Edric Valecrest is gone. The wielder has arrived.

### Book 2 Function

Edric is the clock made flesh. Every day that S&J spend imprisoned is a day closer to his hand on the latch. His naming makes the Frostmourne threat specific: not "someone might take the sword" but "this particular young man, with this particular face and this particular mother who polished his belt buckle, is going to be consumed." The reader needs to see the person the blade will eat — to know his name, his habits, his falcon crest — so that when the vault door opens, it is not a plot device but a tragedy.

Lordaeron's second death knight will rise wearing the face of a man who only wanted to be noticed.

**Prose-writer note:** Edric should appear in background details before his claiming — a name on a duty roster, a guard who nods to Jaina in the corridor, a figure standing at the vault door as the POV character passes. The reader should not notice him until it is too late. Build him the way the story built Lyriel: present, professional, unremarkable. Then take him away.
