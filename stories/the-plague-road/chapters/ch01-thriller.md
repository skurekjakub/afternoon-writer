# Chapter 1: The Spire of the Sun

The pin marked a troll incursion near Zul'Aman, and Sylvanas had spent the morning weighing whether to pull it.

Three days since Lor'themar's ambush scattered the raiding party. Scattered wasn't destroyed. She'd watched the Amani regroup in terrain that should've made regrouping impossible often enough to stop trusting the word. The pin stayed. She kept two fingers on it and let her gaze travel the rest of the war table — lacquered ashwood three centuries old, its surface scarred by the colored pins of six ranger-generals before her. Green for active patrols. Amber for pending rotation. Red for the two incursion points she meant to handle personally before the season turned. Supply lines stitched through the map's preservation enchantments in blue thread, so the threads wouldn't rot and the routes wouldn't lie.

Through the narrow window, the Court of the Sun's fountain threw refracted light across the ceiling in slow arcs, the water shaped into helixes by enchantment that hadn't faltered in three centuries. Below the war table, in the leather case she used for field reports, sat the morning's intake: patrol summaries, requisition orders, a formal protest from the Magisters' College about Farstrider incursions into their arcane testing range near the coast. She'd handle that last. The magisters' complaints improved with age, like wine or grudges.

A Farstrider courier's boots sounded on the staircase below, someone bringing a border report she'd never read today.

The knock came before the pin moved.

Lor'themar entered carrying a scroll case and the expression he kept for political assignments: mouth level, eyes careful, the posture of a man who'd learned to deliver bad news by making himself an uninviting target for the reaction. His gauntlet tapped the doorframe as he paused — metal on enchanted stone producing a flat click familiar from a hundred briefings.

"The Convocation has expressed its preference," he said, setting the scroll case beside the map, "that the investigation be handled at the command level."

Sylvanas didn't touch the case. "Which investigation."

"Plague rumors. Near the border." His gaze settled on her hands, the way she hadn't moved them from the troll pin. "The Kirin Tor's requested cooperation. The Convocation wants to appear cooperative." A pause. "They want you. Specifically."

"They want me to hold a human's hand while she sniffs at sick farmers."

"They want you to ensure Silvermoon appears invested without committing resources. Your presence satisfies the diplomatic requirement. One ranger-general costs less than a battalion."

"That's the first honest thing anyone's said about this assignment."

Lor'themar inclined his head. Forty years of service, and the man knew when agreement was the fastest route through a conversation.

---

Magistrix Eressea arrived within the hour in the formal robes of the Convocation's diplomatic corps and delivered the operational brief in a dialect of Thalassian so elaborately courteous that each sentence took three times longer than its content required. Sylvanas leaned against the windowframe and let her talk, pulling out what mattered and discarding the rest.

The plague: villages in Lordaeron's northern provinces going silent over the past month. Trade caravans that had stopped arriving. A pattern the Kirin Tor had flagged, which meant someone in Dalaran was paying attention even if Silvermoon wasn't.

The mage being sent.

Jaina Proudmoore.

Sylvanas straightened from the window.

The name landed with weight. She tracked every significant figure within three kingdoms of her borders, and Proudmoore was near the top of that list. Antonidas's prodigy. The youngest mage ever offered a senior advisory seat in Kirin Tor history, and she'd turned it down to continue her research under Antonidas directly. The Convocation's briefing materials hadn't mentioned the name, or Lor'themar would've led with it. They either didn't know who was coming or didn't think it mattered.

If the Kirin Tor was sending their best, the plague wasn't a rumor.

Eressea set a folded dispatch on the war table, the crimson wax of the Convocation seal cracking under Sylvanas's thumb as she broke it.

"Proudmoore has requested unrestricted access to the border approach," Eressea continued, "including the first ring of ward-stones. The Convocation has authorized limited cooperation, pending your tactical assessment."

"Unrestricted access to our ward network. She asked for that by name?"

"By specific ward designation. She appears to have studied the schematics."

The ward designations were technical documents, maintained by the Farstriders and the magisters jointly, circulated in Thalassian to a restricted list. A human mage who'd gone to the trouble of acquiring them, translating them, and requesting access by designation had done real work. The kind that didn't match a routine diplomatic errand.

"When does she arrive?"

---

Eressea left, and the room improved.

Sylvanas caught Lor'themar's arm before he could follow. "The plague rumors. Not the Convocation's version. What do your rangers actually say?"

He leaned against the doorframe, arms crossed, his shadow cutting the amber light from the corridor. The posture said he'd been waiting for exactly this question.

"Three villages on the Lordaeron side have gone quiet in the last month. Not destroyed. Quiet. Trade stopped. Smoke stopped." He crossed one ankle over the other, settling in. "Cyndia's patrol at the Pass has been filing reports, but command flagged them low priority." He let that sit. Low priority meant the Convocation had read the reports and written off villages on the wrong side of the border as someone else's problem. "She sent Veloren across on an unauthorized sweep six days ago. The nearest village was empty. Food on the tables. Doors open. No bodies. No violence. People just gone."

Sylvanas's fingers found the incursion pin and pulled it from the map. The trolls could wait.

"You want me to believe you're interested in escorting a human mage," Lor'themar said.

"I want you to believe three villages don't go silent without a reason, and the Convocation is too busy being diplomatic to notice."

"Take Cyndia's patrol at the Pass. She's been filing reports nobody reads."

"I'll read them on the ride."

She packed with the speed of someone who'd done it before wars: the recurve bow, its grip worn to her hand from a century of use. Paired short swords in their crossed harness. A quiver of broadhead shafts, fletched with hawk feather for crosswind stability. Two days' provisions, a light pack, field leathers — Farstrider standard, forest green and mithril chain. The formal insignia of her rank stayed on its hook. This wasn't a diplomatic appearance.

Lor'themar stood in the doorway. He didn't say *be careful* — he'd stopped saying that about two centuries ago. He said, "I'll handle the Convocation," and left it at that.

---

Silvermoon unspooled behind her as she rode south.

The city was beautiful. It was always beautiful, the living light suffusing every surface gold, every archway luminous, the whole place arranged like a performance of elven perfection that never broke character. The citizens moved through it with the unhurried grace of people who'd lived inside that beauty long enough to forget it was exceptional. On the Walk of Elders, ancestor statues flanked the avenue in stern procession; one was missing a hand from the Second War, still unrepaired because the Convocation couldn't agree on a sculptor. A hawkstrider handler led three mounts across the Court of the Sun, their clawed feet clicking against the mosaic tiles. A child on a balcony waved.

Sylvanas didn't wave back.

The arch of the southern gate marked the boundary. She rode through and the living light stopped. Natural sunlight replaced it on her skin, thinner, colder, a change of season in a single stride. Beyond the walls the forest was birch and oak turning amber with autumn, the road rutted from cart traffic Silvermoon pretended didn't exist. Out here the world was less golden and more honest.

She preferred it.

---

She read Cyndia's reports in the saddle, one hand on the reins. Missing caravans. A Lordaeron merchant who'd arrived at the Pass in a state of collapse, babbling about grain before anyone could get a coherent word from him. Unusual sounds from across the border at night — rhythmic, heavy, nothing anyone could name. Seven reports filed over three weeks, each more pointed than the last, each receipted by command and answered with nothing.

The Thalassian Pass cut through the mountains where the forest thinned to scrub and rock. The garrison sat built into the cliff face, low stone walls and a lean-to command post, the spare tight organization of an outpost whose commander had been forgotten by her superiors and ran things properly anyway. The ancient ward-stones rose from the ground in a line that marked the border between Quel'Thalas and Lordaeron, their surfaces carrying a faint blue luminescence that pulsed in a slow rhythm. The ward-stones hummed, low enough that she caught it in her teeth more than her ears — the deep pulse of elven magic that had guarded this crossing for millennia. One of Cyndia's scouts sat on a supply crate sharpening arrowheads, the scrape of whetstone on broadhead steady and mechanical. Horses stamped and blew in the picket line, their breath steaming in the crosswind that funneled through the gap.

Cyndia was young for a ranger, barely past her second century, with sharp eyes and the compressed patience of someone who'd been watching something nobody else would look at.

"I've filed seven reports, Ranger-General." She stood at attention, but the words carried an edge the posture couldn't contain. "Seven. The response I received was a logistics request for winter ration projections."

"Tell me what you didn't put in the reports."

Cyndia's jaw worked. "Veloren crossed the border six days ago on patrol. She came back before dawn and asked for a transfer." She stopped. Started again. "When I pressed her, she said the village on the other side of the pass smelled wrong. Not like plague. Like a butcher's floor after closing. Old blood." Her eyes went to the treeline south, where the road bent into Lordaeron and disappeared. "And the village was still occupied."

"Occupied by whom."

"She didn't say by whom." Cyndia's voice dropped low. "She said 'occupied' the way you'd say 'infested.'"

The word sat between them like something heavy set down on stone. Sylvanas turned it against the missing caravans, the sounds at night, the merchant who'd babbled about grain. The picture wasn't whole, but its edges were sharpening, and she didn't like the shape they made.

---

A horse came up the Pass road hard, lathered, at a pace that said its rider had pushed and hadn't enjoyed the experience.

The rider was human, young, wearing the blue-and-silver of the Kirin Tor with the rumpled formality of an apprentice on his first field assignment. He dismounted badly — a foot caught in the stirrup, recovered with the panicked dignity of a man certain every eye in the garrison was on him — and presented himself with a bow that was technically correct and personally terrified.

"Archmage Proudmoore sends her respects and asks that your scouts begin preliminary terrain—"

"She's already at the border."

The courier blinked. "She — yes. She arrived before dawn. She said the ward-stone resonance patterns were best measured during the temperature transition at—"

"She started without me."

"She began preliminary analysis." His voice found the careful pitch of a man choosing each word from a tray of options that all looked dangerous. "She was very clear that she values the cooperation of—"

"Cyndia." Sylvanas turned from the courier. "Saddle up."

Cyndia was already moving. The question on her face wasn't whether — it was where.

"The Kirin Tor's best has decided she doesn't need to wait for an escort to enter a quarantine zone."
