# Chapter 1: The Assignment

As the Ranger-General of Silvermoon, Sylvanas Windrunner could have chosen to spend her mornings in the Convocation's gilded council chamber, debating tariff policy and the decorative arrangement of ancestral statues, like a sensible administrator. However, Sylvanas was not a sensible administrator – she was the highest-ranking military officer in Quel'Thalas, a woman who had spent centuries putting arrows through things that needed arrows put through them, and the only concession she made to governance was attending the quarterly budget review, where she made a point of looking murderous until someone increased the Farstrider allocation.

And so, our story begins: an autumn morning in the Spire of the Sun, Sylvanas's command post in the upper reaches of Silvermoon. The war table dominated the room – lacquered wood, coloured pins marking patrol routes across Eversong and the southern border, a faint hum of preservation enchantments keeping the maps crisp through their third century of active use. Through the narrow window, the Court of the Sun's fountain threw refracted light across the ceiling in slow arcs, the water shaped into helixes by enchantment that hadn't faltered since before any living elf could remember. The city hummed. It always hummed. After a few centuries you stopped hearing it, the way you stopped hearing your own heartbeat.

Sylvanas was reviewing ranger reports from the southern frontier – troll activity near Zul'Aman, supply logistics for the border garrisons, the usual catalogue of incursions and responses that constituted the defence of an immortal civilisation. Her fingertips rested on the pin that marked a troll raiding party she'd been planning to deal with personally. Three days' ride, a clean engagement, home by week's end. The kind of work she was made for.

Then Lor'themar knocked.

Sylvanas recognised the knock. It was Lor'themar's I-am-the-bearer-of-terrible-news-and-it-isn't-my-fault knock, which differed from his standard knock by approximately one degree of hesitation. After decades of shared command, she knew his knocks the way she knew the shifting hum of the Sunwell: without trying, and without getting them wrong.

"Come."

Lor'themar entered. He was wearing the expression she privately called the Convocation Face – carefully neutral, diplomatically blank, the look of a man who knew his commanding officer was about to break something and was calculating the replacement cost.

"The Convocation," said Lor'themar, "has expressed its preference that the investigation of plague rumours near the northern border be handled at the command level."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning you, specifically. The Kirin Tor has requested cooperation, and the Convocation wants diplomatic weight at the table. One Ranger-General, they feel, costs less than a battalion and photographs better."

Sylvanas removed her fingers from the troll pin. Slowly. It was, she thought, rather a lot like having one's dinner taken away and being handed a pamphlet about civic duty instead.

"They want me to hold a human's hand while she sniffs at sick farmers."

"That is a less diplomatic phrasing of it, yes."

"Who's the human?"

"A mage. Kirin Tor. Name of Jaina Proudmoore."

Sylvanas knew the name. Everyone with an intelligence network knew the name. Antonidas's prodigy. The youngest mage ever offered a seat on the Kirin Tor's senior advisory council – an honour she'd declined, which was either admirable humility or breathtaking arrogance, depending on your politics.

This was not a junior researcher being sent to poke at livestock. This was the Kirin Tor's best, and if the Kirin Tor was sending its best, then the plague rumours were rather more than rumours.

Sylvanas did not share this observation with Lor'themar. Not yet.

"And the Convocation's reasoning?" she asked.

"Diplomatic weight. Interracial cooperation at the highest level. The usual vocabulary."

"As in 'appear cooperative without committing resources.'"

"That's the first honest thing anyone's said about this assignment," said Lor'themar. He leaned against the archway, arms crossed, his shadow cutting the amber light that spilled from the corridor. His gauntlet tapped the doorframe – a habit he didn't know he had, metal on enchanted stone producing a flat click she'd heard across a hundred briefings.

The knock on the staircase announced the Convocation's liaison before she appeared. Magistrix Eressea materialised in the doorway with the polished, unhurried deportment of a woman who had been raised to believe that urgency was a commoner's affliction. She spoke in Formal Thalassian – the dialect that used four words where one would serve and seven where four would do – and delivered the full political context of the assignment in the cadence of a woman reciting a particularly tedious treaty.

The plague rumours. The Kirin Tor's concern. Silvermoon's strategic interest in appearing involved without becoming entangled. The name again: Jaina Proudmoore.

Eressea set a folded dispatch on the war table, the wax seal of the Convocation – crimson, stamped with the phoenix crest – cracking as Sylvanas broke it. The mage had requested unrestricted access to the border approach, including the first ring of ward-stones. By specific ward designation.

"She asked for them by name?" said Sylvanas.

"By designation number," said Eressea. "She appears to have studied the schematics."

Sylvanas let that settle. "When does she arrive?"

Eressea told her. Sylvanas dismissed Eressea, who departed with the glacial dignity of a woman constitutionally incapable of being rushed. Then Sylvanas turned to Lor'themar.

"The plague reports," she said. "Not the Convocation's version. The actual ranger reports from the northern patrols."

Lor'themar's Convocation Face dropped. Underneath it was the face she trusted – the working face, the face that had spent decades beside her in the field and wouldn't waste her time with politics.

The reports were troubling. Three villages on the Lordaeron side of the border had gone silent in the last month. Not attacked – silent. Trade caravans stopped arriving. A Farstrider patrol had crossed the border – unauthorised, but Sylvanas didn't reprimand unauthorised intelligence gathering; she rewarded it – and found one of the villages empty. Food on tables. Doors open. No bodies. No violence. People gone, as if they'd simply stood up and walked away from their lives.

Sylvanas's hand returned to the war table. Not the troll pin, this time. The northern border.

"This isn't a diplomatic errand," she said.

"No," said Lor'themar. "It's not."

She selected a light kit: the recurve bow she'd carried for a century, the paired short swords, two days' provisions. She checked the bowstring's tension with the oil cloth drawn between thumb and forefinger – a motion so automatic that she'd done it ten thousand times without thinking and would do it ten thousand more.

"Take the southern patrols," she told Lor'themar. "I'll read Cyndia's reports on the ride."

"You want me to believe you're interested in escorting a human mage."

"I want you to believe three villages don't go silent without a reason, and the Convocation is too busy being diplomatic to notice."

"Take Cyndia's patrol at the Pass. She's been filing reports nobody reads."

"I'll read them on the ride."

~

The ride through Silvermoon was, as always, beautiful.

It was also, as always, insufficient.

Sylvanas rode the Walk of Elders – statues of ancestor-heroes flanking the avenue, their stone faces gilded by the perpetual amber light, one of them missing a hand from the Second War and never repaired because the Convocation couldn't agree on the sculptor. A hawkstrider handler led three mounts across the Court of the Sun, their clawed feet clicking on the mosaic tiles. A child on a balcony waved. Sylvanas did not wave back. She'd long since ceased that particular courtesy – too many children, too many balconies, and the waving never stopped anything that mattered.

The arch of the southern gate was where the living light ended and natural sunlight began. She felt the break on her skin – Silvermoon's magic stopped at the walls, and beyond them the world was colder, harder, less golden.

The road south ran through Eversong's autumn canopy, dappled amber light on the forest floor, the smell of old leaves and running water, the distant sound of a Farstrider patrol moving through the trees in the familiar cadence of elves who'd grown up in this forest and would die in it. She read Cyndia's reports in the saddle, one hand on the reins, her hawkstrider picking its way through roots with the practised indifference of an animal that had made this journey a thousand times.

Cyndia's reports were worse than Lor'themar's summary.

She reached Thalassian Pass by midafternoon.

~

Ranger Cyndia was a young Farstrider with the clipped posture and sharp eyes of someone who had been stationed at the edge of something wrong for three weeks and had filed seven reports about it that nobody read. She saluted when Sylvanas arrived and then, before Sylvanas could dismount, began talking.

"Seven reports, Ranger-General. Seven. The response I received was a logistics request for winter ration projections."

"Tell me what you didn't put in the reports," said Sylvanas.

Cyndia hesitated.

"Veloren crossed the border on patrol six days ago. She came back before dawn and requested a transfer. When I pressed her, she said –" Cyndia stopped. Restarted. "She said the village on the other side of the pass smelled wrong. Not like plague. Like a butcher's floor after closing. Old blood. And the village was still occupied."

"Occupied by whom."

"Just occupied. We couldn't get anything specific from her." Cyndia said. 

Sylvanas let this sit. The border outpost was standard Farstrider construction – half-built into the pass's rock face, ward-stones flanking the road, their faint blue glow pulsing in a rhythm she'd known since childhood. One of Cyndia's scouts sat on a supply crate sharpening arrowheads with a whetstone, the scrape of it rhythmic, mechanical, the task of someone who hadn't slept well in days.

A rider appeared on the road – a human, on a horse that was trailing foam from the bit and had clearly been ridden harder than either horse or rider was accustomed to. A young man, clutching a sealed scroll, looking at the ward-stones with the expression of someone who had just realised that the stories about elven magic were not, in fact, exaggerated.

"Apprentice Maric, ma'am. Archmage Proudmoore sends her respects and asks that your scouts begin preliminary terrain assessment of the road to Millhaven –"

"She's already at the border," said Sylvanas.

Maric blinked. "She – yes. She arrived before dawn. She said the ward-stone resonance patterns were best measured during the temperature transition at –"

"She started without me."

Maric swallowed. He was clearly trying to find a way to frame this that didn't make his employer sound like someone who had ignored the entire point of a cooperative mission. "She began preliminary analysis. She was very clear that she values the cooperation –"

"Cyndia. Saddle up."

Cyndia was already moving.

~

The mage was kneeling in the mud.

This was the first thing Sylvanas noticed, and it set the tone for everything that followed. The Kirin Tor's greatest prodigy, Antonidas's chosen successor, the woman half of Dalaran whispered about – kneeling in the mud on the Lordaeron side of Thalassian Pass, hands pressed flat against a ward-stone, eyes closed, murmuring analytical incantations in a dialect of Thalassian that Sylvanas had not heard from a human mouth before.

Her travelling clothes were practical. Her boots were worn. The knees of her trousers were already muddy, which meant she'd been kneeling for some time – hours, probably. A raven perched on the ward-stone's crown, watching her work with the uncanny stillness of a bird accustomed to her presence.

But the most striking were her hands. Dirt stained, mud embedded deep under fingernails, a sheen of frost -- a leftover from recent frost magic incantations most likely -- gently blanketing each digit.

Jaina finished her spell, opened her eyes, and looked at Sylvanas.

No deference. No formality. No diplomatic overture.

The introduction lasted approximately two minutes before it became an argument.

"Ranger-General. I appreciate Silvermoon's cooperation," said Jaina, rising, brushing mud from her knees with a casualness that suggested she did this often and saw no reason to be embarrassed about it. "I've completed my initial ward-stone analysis and I'd like to proceed to Millhaven before midday. The contamination's residual signature degrades in afternoon thermal –"

"You've been running unsecured diagnostic magic on our ward network since before dawn," said Sylvanas. "In a quarantine zone. Without escort. Without notifying my border patrol."

"I notified your courier. And the contamination's residual signature –"

"Degrades in afternoon heat. You said. The road to Millhaven passes through three kilometres of uncleared forest with no sight lines and no established patrol route. If the village fell to plague, whatever caused it may still be in the area. You don't walk into that alone."

A flicker behind Jaina's eyes. Not hostility – recalculation. "You've already scouted the route."

"My scouts have been monitoring the border for three weeks. The route is not secured, which is why we haven't used it. I don't send people down roads I haven't cleared."

"Then clear it. I need to be in Millhaven before the thermal window closes."

The ward-stones pulsed behind them. The wind through the pass carried the smell of Eversong's golden leaf mulch, already fading into Lordaeron's cold wet stone.

"You're giving me orders," said Sylvanas a moment later.

"I'm prioritizing."

Cyndia, who had been watching this exchange with the expression of a career soldier who has seen officers argue while the enemy advances, said: "Ranger-General. The thermal window Archmage Proudmoore mentioned closes in four hours. If we leave now, we reach Millhaven with two hours to spare. If we continue discussing command structure, we reach Millhaven tomorrow."

Sylvanas looked at Cyndia. "Let's move."

~

The road to Millhaven was wrong.

Sylvanas read it within the first kilometre, the way she read all terrain: automatically, without conscious effort, the way a fish reads current. The silence was wrong. No birdsong. No animal movement. The forest on either side was alive – the trees stood, the leaves turned, the undergrowth grew – but it was unoccupied, emptied of anything with legs or wings. The kind of silence that followed a predator, not a plague.

She said nothing about this. Jaina, walking beside her, was talking – not chattering, but thinking aloud. Analytical, rapid, occasionally lapsing into arcane terminology that Sylvanas didn't know and wouldn't ask about. Two minds on the same road, pointed in different directions: one scanning the tree line for threats, the other reading energy signatures in the soil.

Then Jaina stopped mid-sentence.

She knelt. Hand flat on the road surface, the same gesture she'd used at the ward-stone. Her expression changed – the analytical focus sharpening into alarm.

"Stop."

Sylvanas's hand went to her bow. She scanned the tree line. "What."

"The ground. There's a resonance. Faint – I almost missed it under the ward bleed from the Pass. But it's not natural, and it's not the wards."

"What kind of resonance."

"If I had to characterise it – entropic. It's drawing energy out of the soil. Not much, barely perceptible. But it's been doing it for weeks. That's why there are no animals. Everything alive has moved away from it."

"Everything alive."

"Yes." Jaina met her eyes. "Everything that could move, moved."

Sylvanas sat with the implication. The trees, the soil, the grass – were still here, draining quietly.

The last kilometre was worse. The air had weight. A fence post at the village's edge carried a notice board – a market-day announcement, the ink bleeding from rain, advertising cheese and cider from a farm whose name was no longer readable. A dog's water bowl beside a doorstep, still half-full, green with algae. Cyndia's forward scout crouched behind the fence, one hand raised in the Farstrider hold-signal, the other gripping her bow so hard the knuckles had gone white.

A child's doll on the road. Mud-stained, one button eye missing. Nobody had come back for it.

~

Millhaven was a farming village – thirty buildings, maybe forty families. Sylvanas entered in combat formation, Cyndia's scouts on the flanks, and found a place that had stopped being a place and become a crime scene.

The village was intact. No fires. No broken walls. No signs of battle. The doors were open – not broken open, but left open, the way a room looks when everyone has stepped out for a moment and forgotten to come back. Food on tables, some of it moulded, some barely touched. Candles burned down to puddles of wax. A blacksmith's forge cold for at least a week, a half-finished horseshoe still sitting on the anvil, the hammer on the ground where it fell or was dropped.

A kitchen table set for four. Bread sliced, butter out, a pot of cold stew on the hearth. Four chairs. One was pushed back – someone had stood up quickly. The other three were still tucked in. One person got up. The other three didn't.

Jaina moved through the village with the focused intensity of a woman who had found her research subject and was not about to be separated from it. She took samples – grain from a sack in the general store, water from the well, soil from the garden beds, a smear of the cold stew from the kitchen table. She set up a portable analytical array in the village square: crystals, reagents, a scrying mirror she used for magnification. She uncorked a reagent vial with her teeth, spat the cork onto the cobbles, poured a blue-white liquid over a grain sample in the scrying mirror's basin.

The liquid hissed and turned the colour of old bruises.

"The grain," said Jaina. "The same entropic signature I found on the road, concentrated a hundredfold. It's not in the water. Not in the soil, except where grain was stored. The contamination vector is specific: the grain, and only the grain." She looked at Sylvanas. "Someone poisoned the food supply."

While Jaina analysed, Sylvanas read the tactical picture. The doors open: people left quickly but without panic. No packed bags: they didn't plan to leave. The pushed-back chair: one person moved first. The absence of bodies: wherever they went, they went together. And at the village's north edge, she found what she was looking for.

Tracks. Not footprints – drag marks. Dozens of them, leading out of the village toward the forest. Not the marks of people walking. The marks of people being moved. Or moving wrong. Deep, consistent, the weight of adult bodies being pulled. And orderly. Not the erratic pattern of people fleeing. The measured spacing of people being led.

Cyndia's scouts brought the survivors in twenty minutes later – a married couple found on the south road, half-starved, stumbling toward the border. The wife collapsed onto a crate in the village square and drank from the offered waterskin with the desperate gulping of someone who'd forgotten to bring water when she ran. The husband stood where the scouts left him, arms at his sides, staring at the well. His jaw worked, but no sound came out. He hadn't spoken since they'd left, according to his wife.

The wife talked.

"The grain came same as always. Merchant from Andorhal. Two weeks, maybe, before the sickness. My husband didn't trust new bread – he's always been particular about flour, takes it from Hillsbrad instead – so we didn't eat it. But everyone else did."

"The sickness," said Jaina. "Describe the progression."

"Three days. First the fever. Then their skin went grey, like old meat. Then they stopped talking. Not like they couldn't – like they weren't in there anymore. Empty." The wife's fingers were tearing a crust of bread into smaller and smaller pieces on her lap, not eating it, just working the dry bread apart. "And then they walked out. Middle of the night. All at the same time, like someone rang a bell only they could hear."

"How many," said Sylvanas.

"All of them. Everyone who ate the bread. Thirty families." A shutter on the house across the square slammed open in the wind, banged against the wall, swung shut, slammed open again. Nobody moved to stop it. "They walked north, into the forest, and they didn't come back until the next night."

"And when they came back," said Sylvanas.

The wife's hands stopped moving. "They weren't walking right. Too smooth. Too quiet. And they were collecting the rest of us. The ones who hadn't eaten the bread. Like they knew who had and who hadn't." She was shaking now. "My husband pulled me out the back window and we ran. We ran and we didn't stop."

Jaina was already pacing the village square, her staff striking the cobblestones with each step – crack, crack, crack – running numbers out loud, and the numbers were bad. "If the contamination has been active for two weeks and the distribution reaches the standard route network from Andorhal – that's Lordaeron's agricultural hub, the city that distributes grain to half the northern provinces – this isn't one village. This is hundreds of villages. Minimum."

The wife flinched at the sound of the staff. Behind them, a grain sack in the general store had split further, and the kernels were rolling slowly across the sloped floor toward the open door, accumulating in a crescent against the threshold.

"Cyndia," said Sylvanas. Her voice was level. Her voice was always level when the situation had stopped being an assignment and started being a threat. "You're going back to the Pass. Run. I want Lor'themar to have this report before dawn."

"And the village?"

"The village is a crime scene, not a village. Nobody enters it until we understand what walked out of it."

She followed the drag marks north.

Alone, bow drawn, moving in the hunter's walk she'd learned before she could read. The marks led into the forest, through undergrowth that had been trampled by dozens of feet in formation. Not a mob – a column. Military spacing. Someone was organising whatever was out there.

Three hundred metres in, she stopped.

The forest ahead was different. The trees were the same, but the light was wrong – thinner, greyer, the colour leeched out of the air. The entropic signature Jaina had identified was thick here, concentrated enough that Sylvanas could feel it on her skin: a cold that wasn't temperature, a pressure that wasn't wind. Like standing in front of an open grave, the cold coming up from below. Underfoot, a snapped fence rail from someone's garden, dragged here, caught in the undergrowth, white paint flaking. Further in, a boot sole-up in the leaf litter, laces still tied, the leather crusted with mud and something darker. No foot inside it.

Jaina caught up. She'd finished her sending to Antonidas and had followed the same tracks, because of course she had – the woman was physically incapable of staying where it was safe.

"The entropic field," Jaina said, voice low. "It's exponentially stronger here. Whatever created it is nearby. Hundreds of metres at most."

"I can feel it."

"We should not be pushing into an unknown magical field of this intensity without understanding its properties –"

"This is reconnaissance, not a seminar."

"This is suicide dressed as reconnaissance. You can't fight an energy signature with a bow."

The argument was interrupted by movement in the tree line.

Something was moving between the trees. Not fast – slow, deliberate, patient, the gait of a thing that didn't need to hurry because it was already everywhere. Jaina's hand landed on Sylvanas's forearm. Sudden, unrequested, the grip stronger than her frame suggested.

They saw them.

The villagers of Millhaven, standing in the forest in rows. Motionless. Grey-skinned, eyes open, the fixed stare of the truly vacant. Arranged in formation – military formation, the kind of grid you'd organise soldiers in, not the chaotic scatter of a plague camp. The nearest one was wearing a miller's apron, flour still dusted across the front, the strings trailing in the dirt. Beyond the front row, something glinted – a pitchfork, held upright, its tines dark with something that wasn't soil.

One of them turned its head. Slowly. Just the one, the closest to the tree line. It looked at them with eyes that had nothing behind them, and then it opened its mouth – not to speak, but to emit a sound that wasn't a voice. A low, rattling exhalation, like air escaping a bellows. The others began to turn.

"Back," said Sylvanas. She already had an arrow nocked.

The dead began to move.

~

Tactical withdrawal was the polite term. The practical term was running, but Sylvanas had spent centuries perfecting the art of running backwards while shooting things, which was – she reflected – the defining skill of a Farstrider and one that human military academies regrettably neglected.

The undead pressed forward. Not fast, but relentless, and there were dozens of them. Jaina erected a barrier at the tree line – arcane shielding, translucent, humming with energy. The dead pushed against it with the mindless patience of water against a dam.

Two of them breached it. Jaina's shields, designed for magical threats, were partially permeable to whatever ran on the dark energy that animated these things. Sylvanas put arrows through both. The first fell. The second kept crawling with a shaft through its chest – she put a second through its skull, and it stopped, fingers still twitching in the dirt.

"The barrier's failing," said Jaina. No panic. Just field report.

She switched. Dropped the arcane shielding entirely and went elemental – ice, conjured fast and brutal, not a wall but a field that spread across the forest floor with a sound like cracking knuckles. The dead locked at the ankles. Frost climbed the base of a birch tree, split the bark. One of them still moved its arms, reaching, fingers opening and closing on nothing. The air temperature dropped so sharply that Sylvanas's next exhale came out as a white plume.

The withdrawal opened up. They ran.

Clear of the tree line, Sylvanas counted arrows by touch – six gone. Jaina leaned against a tree, palms flat on the bark, chest heaving, her staff propped in the crook of her elbow. The crystalline focus at the top had gone dark, drained. Her hands were shaking. Frost climbed her own fingers – her ice spell bleeding backward, the magic not fully under control.

From the forest behind them, a cracking sound. Something pulling itself free.

They moved. Fast, south, back toward the village and the road beyond it, with two traumatized civilians and a mage who was unsteady on her feet and holding her staff by pressure rather than coordination because the magical exhaustion had left her hands numb. The husband walked between two scouts, his feet catching on roots, each stumble corrected by a scout's hand on his elbow – mechanical, neither scout looking at him, their eyes on the tree line.

Jaina, recovering as they walked, spoke two sentences that stopped Sylvanas mid-stride.

"The formation. The way they were arranged in the forest. That wasn't the plague. Plague doesn't organise. Someone is commanding them."

"You're telling me someone is giving orders to dead villagers."

"I'm telling you the plague isn't the weapon." Jaina's voice was steady, despite the tremor in her hands. "The plague is the recruitment."

The picture rearranged itself. This woman – kneeling in the mud, shaking from exhaustion, frost-burned by her own spell – had adapted. In combat. Against an unknown threat. She'd seen her barrier fail and invented a solution in the time it took Sylvanas to nock a second arrow.

This was not a mage who needed protection. This was a mage who needed a partner.

Sylvanas said none of this. What she said was: "The road to Andorhal. How fast can you be ready to travel?"

Jaina looked at her. Really looked, for the first time since the border. "You're not going back to Silvermoon."

"My assignment is to escort you during your investigation. Your investigation just expanded."

"The Convocation –"

"The Convocation wanted me to hold your hand while you sniffed at sick farmers. That's not what this is. That hasn't been what this is since we crossed the tree line."

~

They reached the Pass after dark.

The fire pit had been newly stoked, sparks rising into the cold, the ward-stones casting their blue pulse-light across the outpost's low walls. Cyndia's remaining scout boiled water in a dented copper pot. The survivors were given blankets, food, a place by the fire. The wife stopped talking. The husband still hadn't started. His hands rested motionless in his lap, fingers curled around something he'd been holding when he ran – empty air now.

Jaina crouched over her sending stone at the edge of the firelight, the crystal pressed between both palms, murmuring in the subvocal cadence of long-distance arcane communication. Her breath fogged in the cold between each phrase.

She closed the sending. "Antonidas will bring it to the Six. He'll push for immediate deployment."

"How long."

"The Six doesn't have a fast setting. Days. If the political winds cooperate. Weeks if they don't."

"In weeks, every village between here and Andorhal will look like Millhaven."

"I know."

Sylvanas sat by the fire and wrote two letters – the first in the formal script of official correspondence, the facts as observed, the language of military intelligence. The second in the looser hand she used with Lor'themar, the hand of a woman who trusted the recipient enough to write badly.

She was going south with the mage. She was extending the mission unilaterally. The Convocation would not approve. She was asking Lor'themar to hold the southern patrols in her absence and to not ask questions when the angry dispatches started arriving.

Writing the letter forced her to articulate what she knew but hadn't said: this wasn't a border incident. This was an invasion, and she was watching the opening move. If the Convocation couldn't see it from Silvermoon, she'd see it from the road.

She folded the official report with military creases, the parchment cracking at the edges. The personal letter she folded once, quickly, and sealed with a thumb-press of wax she heated over the fire – no signet ring, just the whorl of her thumbprint.

Jaina was at the ward-stones again when Sylvanas found her. Running a different analysis – not diagnostic, but comparative. She'd scratched a rough map in the dirt between the stones with a stick, villages marked as Xs, the contamination's spread shown in sweeping arcs. She was mapping the entropic signature against the ward network, using Silvermoon's own wards as a baseline. Building a detection framework – a way to find the contamination from a distance, to map its spread without walking into every village.

She'd been working since they got back, despite the exhaustion, despite the tremor still in her hands. The ward-stones pulsed brighter when she touched them, the blue light throwing her shadow long and sharp across the ground.

The map was already worse than she'd projected. At least three more villages within the signature range. And the signal got stronger to the southwest. Toward Andorhal.

"The signature isn't decaying," Jaina said, without looking up.

"Meaning."

"Meaning whatever we found at Millhaven isn't the end of something. It's the beginning. And it's moving faster than we are."

An owl called from the Eversong side of the pass – the only animal sound in hours. Distant. Indifferent.

They stood at the ward-stones, both of them looking south into Lordaeron's darkness. The Farstrider camp was behind them. Silvermoon was behind them. Ahead: the grain road, the dead villages, Andorhal, and whatever intelligence was turning Lordaeron's farmers into soldiers.

Sylvanas's hand rested on her bow – not gripping, just resting, the way it sat when she was thinking and her body defaulted to the weapon it knew best. South, past the last of the blue light, the Lordaeron road disappeared into black, and somewhere down that road a wind moved through trees that had no birds in them.
