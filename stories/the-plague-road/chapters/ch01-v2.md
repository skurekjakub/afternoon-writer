# Chapter 1: The Spire of the Sun

The pin marked a troll incursion near Zul'Aman, and Sylvanas had spent the better part of the morning with two fingers resting on its head, weighing whether to pull it.

Three days since Lor'themar's ambush scattered the raiding party into the Eversong canopy. Scattered, not destroyed — a distinction the Convocation would ignore and Sylvanas would not. She'd watched the Amani regroup in terrain that should have made regrouping impossible often enough to stop trusting anyone else's definition of the word. The pin stayed.

She let her gaze travel the rest of the war table. Lacquered ashwood, three centuries old, its surface carrying the stained constellation of six ranger-generals' worth of colored pins: green for active patrols, amber for pending rotation, red for the two incursion points she intended to handle personally before the autumn rains turned the southern approaches to mud. Blue thread stitched supply lines through the map's preservation enchantments, so the threads wouldn't rot and the routes wouldn't lie. Where the thread crossed the Thalassian Pass, someone had added a notation in Lor'themar's tight script — a question mark, nothing more, dated six days ago. She hadn't asked him about it yet.

Through the narrow window behind her, the Court of the Sun's fountain threw refracted light across the ceiling in slow arcs, the water shaped by enchantment into helixes that spiraled upward and dissolved into mist that smelled faintly of ozone and summer rain. The enchantment was older than anyone who maintained it. Below the window, in the distance, a Farstrider drill column wheeled through formation in the courtyard, their synchronized footfalls producing a sound like a heartbeat carried on stone. One-two, one-two. And then, from deeper in the Spire, a courier's boots on the staircase, sharp and uneven, someone climbing fast with a border report she would never read today.

Below the war table, in the leather case she used for field dispatches, sat the morning's intake: patrol summaries from the eastern reaches, a requisition order for replacement broadheads that she'd already approved twice, and a formal protest from the Magisters' College about Farstrider incursions into their arcane testing range near the coast. She'd handle the magisters last. Their complaints improved with age, like wine or grudges.

The knock came before the pin moved.

Lor'themar entered carrying a scroll case and the expression he kept for political assignments: mouth level, eyes careful, the posture of a man who had learned across four decades of service to deliver bad news by making himself an uninviting target for the reaction. He paused at the doorframe, his gauntlet tapping the enchanted stone in the same unconscious rhythm he always used, metal on quartz producing a flat click she'd come to associate with the particular kind of morning where the Convocation had found another way to make her life worse.

He set the scroll case on the table beside the map. It sat there between them like a small declaration of war.

"The Convocation has expressed its preference," he said, "that the investigation be handled at the command level."

Sylvanas didn't touch the case. "Which investigation."

"Plague rumors. Near the border." His gaze settled on her hands, on the fact that she hadn't moved them from the troll pin — which told him where her priorities still sat and where they were about to be relocated. "The Kirin Tor's requested cooperation. The Convocation wants to appear cooperative." A pause, the careful kind, weighted with the thing he hadn't said yet. "They want you. Specifically."

"They want me to hold a human's hand while she sniffs at sick farmers."

"They want you to ensure Silvermoon appears invested without committing resources. Your presence satisfies the diplomatic requirement. One ranger-general costs less than a battalion."

"That's the first honest thing anyone's said about this assignment."

Lor'themar inclined his head. Forty years of shared service, and the man knew when agreement was the fastest route through a conversation.

The scroll case sat untouched. Through the window, the fountain's enchantment shifted its cycle, the helix collapsing into a wide, flat sheet of water that caught the late-morning sun and threw it back as a wall of gold across the ceiling. For a moment the room blazed. Then the helix reformed, and the light settled back into its usual restless arc.

Magistrix Eressea arrived within the hour, announced by the click of formal court shoes on the Spire's upper staircase — a sound as distinct from a Farstrider's tread as porcelain from steel. She wore the layered silks of the Convocation's diplomatic corps, deep crimson over gold, and she delivered the operational brief in a dialect of Thalassian so elaborately courteous that each sentence took roughly three times longer than its content required. The consonants alone could have filled a separate scroll.

Sylvanas leaned against the windowframe, felt the sun-warmed stone through her leathers, and let Eressea talk. She listened the way she'd learned to listen to all Convocation emissaries: selectively, stripping the ornamentation for load-bearing content and discarding the rest.

The plague. Villages in Lordaeron's northern provinces going silent over the past month, not one or two but several, in a pattern that suggested spread rather than isolated event. Trade caravans that had stopped arriving at the border crossing. A cluster of reports from the Kirin Tor's own intelligence network, which meant someone in Dalaran was paying close attention to something Silvermoon had not yet bothered to look at.

And the mage they were sending.

Jaina Proudmoore.

Sylvanas straightened from the window.

The name carried weight. She assigned it to every significant figure within three kingdoms of her borders, kept that map in her head the way she kept the war table: constantly updated, ruthlessly organized, pinned with the colored markers of threat, capability, and political significance. Proudmoore sat near the top. Antonidas's prodigy. The youngest mage ever offered a senior advisory seat in Kirin Tor history, who had turned it down to continue her research under Antonidas directly, a choice that spoke either to genuine scholarly commitment or to a political sophistication advanced enough to recognize that refusing a seat could be more powerful than occupying one.

The Convocation's briefing materials hadn't mentioned the name, or Lor'themar would have led with it. They either didn't know who was coming or didn't think it mattered.

If the Kirin Tor was sending their best, the plague wasn't a rumor.

Eressea set a folded dispatch on the war table. The crimson wax of the Convocation seal, stamped with the phoenix crest, the edges still sharp, cracked under Sylvanas's thumb. The parchment smelled of scribes' ink, sharp and mineral, and underneath it the faint sweetness of the enchanted paper the Convocation used for formal correspondence. She scanned the contents while Eressea continued talking.

"Proudmoore has requested unrestricted access to the border approach," Eressea said, "including the first ring of ward-stones. The Convocation has authorized limited cooperation, pending your tactical assessment."

"Unrestricted access to our ward network." Sylvanas looked up from the dispatch. "She asked for that by name?"

"By specific ward designation. She appears to have studied the schematics."

The ward designations were technical documents maintained by the Farstriders and the magisters jointly, circulated in Thalassian to a restricted list. They were not secret in any meaningful military sense, the wards themselves visible to anyone with arcane sight, but they were obscure, buried in the kind of operational detail that only someone who intended to do real work with them would bother to acquire. A human mage who'd gone to the trouble of obtaining them, translating them from Thalassian, and requesting access by specific designation number had done her homework. The kind that didn't match a routine diplomatic errand.

"When does she arrive?"

Eressea consulted her notes, shuffling through the layered papers with the careful precision of someone who organized her materials the way she organized her sentences, with far more architecture than content required. "Before dawn, at the latest. She indicated that the ward-stone resonance patterns are best measured during the temperature transition at—"

"Before dawn." Sylvanas looked at Lor'themar. Lor'themar's expression didn't change, but the gauntlet tapped the doorframe once. Just once. They'd been holding conversations in single gestures for decades. This one said: *interesting.*

Eressea left, and the room improved.

The fountain's spray caught a shift in the wind and threw a fine mist through the window, cold against Sylvanas's neck, carrying the green smell of the gardens far below. She caught Lor'themar's arm before he could follow.

"The plague rumors. Not the Convocation's version. What do your rangers actually say?"

He leaned against the doorframe, arms crossed, his shadow cutting the amber light from the corridor into a sharp diagonal across the floor. The posture said he'd been waiting for exactly this question. Had been waiting since he set the scroll case on the table and she'd left it unopened.

"Three villages on the Lordaeron side have gone quiet in the last month. Not destroyed. Quiet. Trade stopped. Smoke stopped." He crossed one ankle over the other, settling in. His voice dropped, the way it always did when the information hadn't been sanitized for the Convocation. "Cyndia's patrol at the Pass has been filing reports, but command flagged them low priority." He let that sit. Low priority meant the Convocation had read the reports, weighed Lordaeron's villages against Silvermoon's diplomatic posture, and written off everything on the wrong side of the border as someone else's problem. "She sent Veloren across on an unauthorized sweep six days ago. The nearest village was empty. Food on the tables. Doors open. No bodies. No violence. People just gone."

The question mark on the war table. Six days ago. Lor'themar had been waiting.

Sylvanas's fingers found the troll incursion pin and pulled it from the map. The trolls could wait.

"You want me to believe you're interested in escorting a human mage," Lor'themar said.

"I want you to believe three villages don't go silent without a reason, and the Convocation is too busy being diplomatic to notice."

"Take Cyndia's patrol at the Pass. She's been filing reports nobody reads."

"I'll read them on the ride."

She packed with the speed of someone who'd done it before wars. The recurve bow came first, its grip worn to the exact shape of her hand from a century of use, the limbs refinished twice, the sinew string checked by thumb-pressure along its full length in a single practiced draw. Paired short swords in their crossed harness, the leather dark from oil. A quiver of broadhead shafts, fletched with hawk feather for crosswind stability, thirty-two arrows because thirty-two was the number she'd carried since her first season as a ranger and she had not found a reason to change it. Two days' provisions, a light pack, field leathers, Farstrider standard, forest green and mithril chain, the clasps worn smooth from decades of buckling in the dark.

The formal insignia of her rank stayed on its hook by the door. This wasn't a diplomatic appearance.

Lor'themar stood in the doorway. He didn't say *be careful* — he'd stopped saying that about two centuries ago, around the same year she'd stopped pretending she might. He said, "I'll handle the Convocation," and left it at that.

---

Silvermoon unspooled behind her as she rode south.

The city was beautiful. It was always beautiful, the living light suffusing every surface gold, every archway luminous, the whole place arranged like a performance of elven perfection that never broke character. Sylvanas had spent centuries inside it and could admit the beauty without being moved by it, the way a soldier admits the quality of a fortification without being comforted by walls. The citizens moved through the streets with the unhurried grace of people who'd lived inside that beauty long enough to forget it was exceptional: a pair of magisters in their red and silver arguing in the garden district, their hands shaping the air with the habitual gestures of people who cast spells as punctuation; a hawkstrider handler leading three mounts across the Court of the Sun, their clawed feet clicking against the mosaic tiles in an irregular rhythm, the birds' crests fanning with the mild irritation of creatures who'd been groomed recently against their preference. On the Walk of Elders, ancestor statues flanked the avenue in stern procession. One was missing a hand — taken by an orc axe during the Second War, still unrepaired because the Convocation couldn't agree on a sculptor.

A child on a balcony waved. Sylvanas didn't wave back.

The southern gate was the boundary. She rode through, and the living light ended. Natural sunlight replaced it on her skin, thinner, sharper, stripped of the magical warmth that Silvermoon kept like a second atmosphere. The change was immediate and physical: a temperature drop, a shift in the quality of the air she felt in her lungs as much as on her face. Beyond the walls the forest was birch and oak turning amber with autumn, the leaves dry enough to whisper against each other in a low, persistent murmur that the living light had smothered inside the city. The road south was rutted from the cart traffic Silvermoon pretended didn't exist: the merchants, the grain haulers, the supply chains that kept the golden city fed while it pretended to be self-sufficient.

Out here the world was less golden and more honest. She preferred it.

She read Cyndia's reports in the saddle, one hand on the reins, the hawkstrider's gait smooth enough on the packed earth to hold the parchment steady. The reports were written in the clipped, precise Thalassian of a ranger who had run out of patience for the bureaucratic euphemisms the Convocation preferred. Missing caravans — three in three weeks, on routes that had been reliable for a decade. A Lordaeron merchant who'd arrived at the Pass in a state of collapse, smelling of road sweat and something else, something sour, babbling about grain before anyone could get a coherent word from him. Unusual sounds from across the border at night, rhythmic, heavy, a resonance that didn't match wind or wildlife or any natural source anyone in the garrison could name. Seven reports filed over three weeks, each more pointed than the last, each receipted by border command and answered with nothing.

The land changed as she rode. The golden birch thinned to oak and scrub, the road climbing as the mountains grew ahead, and the air cooled by degrees until she could see her hawkstrider's breath steaming in the late afternoon shadow. The Thalassian Pass cut through the mountains where the forest gave way entirely to grey rock and wind, a natural corridor carved by geological patience millennia before the elves had poured their wards into its walls.

The garrison sat built into the cliff face: low stone walls and a lean-to command post, functional in the way that remote outposts become functional — the spare, tight organization of a commander forgotten by her superiors who ran things properly anyway. The ancient ward-stones rose from the ground in a line that marked the border between Quel'Thalas and Lordaeron, their surfaces carrying a faint blue luminescence that pulsed in a slow, subdermal rhythm, like something breathing underground. The hum was low enough that she caught it in her teeth more than her ears, the deep vibration of elven magic that had guarded this crossing for millennia. One of Cyndia's scouts sat on a supply crate sharpening arrowheads, the scrape of whetstone on broadhead steady and mechanical, a sound that grounded the whole outpost in its essential purpose. Horses stamped and blew in the picket line, their breath steaming in the crosswind that funneled through the gap, and the wind carried the smell of rock dust and cold stone and, faintly, from across the border, something else, something flat and sweet that didn't belong to the mountains.

Cyndia was young for a ranger, barely past her second century, with sharp eyes and the compressed patience of someone who'd spent three weeks staring at something nobody else would look at.

"I've filed seven reports, Ranger-General." She stood at attention, but the words carried an edge the posture couldn't contain. "Seven. The response I received was a logistics request for winter ration projections."

"Tell me what you didn't put in the reports."

Cyndia's jaw worked. The wind pushed through the Pass behind her, flattening her cloak against her side, carrying that sweet wrongness from the south. "Veloren crossed the border six days ago on patrol. She came back before dawn and asked for a transfer." She stopped. Started again. "When I pressed her, she said the village on the other side of the pass smelled wrong. Not like plague — she'd been in the field long enough to know plague, and this wasn't that. Like a butcher's floor after closing. Old blood." Her eyes went to the treeline south, where the road bent into Lordaeron and disappeared between the mountains. "And the village was still occupied."

"Occupied by whom."

"She didn't say by whom." Cyndia's voice dropped low. "She said 'occupied' the way you'd say 'infested.'"

The word sat between them, heavy as the ward-stones' hum. Sylvanas turned it against the missing caravans, the sounds at night, the merchant babbling about grain. The picture wasn't whole. But its edges were sharpening, and the shape they were making had nothing to do with a simple plague.

---

A horse came up the Pass road at a pace that said its rider had pushed hard and hadn't enjoyed the experience, galloping on loose shale, from the sound of it, with more enthusiasm than skill.

The rider was human, young, wearing the blue-and-silver of the Kirin Tor with the rumpled formality of an apprentice on his first field assignment. His robes were dusty at the hem, his face flushed from the ride and the altitude, and he dismounted badly — a foot caught in the stirrup, recovered with the panicked dignity of a man certain every eye in the garrison was on him. He wasn't wrong. Cyndia's scouts had stopped sharpening to watch. Even the horses turned their heads.

He presented himself with a bow that was technically correct and personally terrified.

"Archmage Proudmoore sends her respects and asks that your scouts begin preliminary terrain—"

"She's already at the border."

The courier blinked. "She — yes. She arrived before dawn. She said the ward-stone resonance patterns were best measured during the temperature transition at—"

"She started without me."

"She began preliminary analysis." His voice found the careful pitch of a man choosing each word from a tray of options that all looked dangerous. "She was very clear that she values the cooperation of—"

"Cyndia." Sylvanas turned from the courier. "Saddle up."

Cyndia was already moving. The question on her face wasn't whether — it was where.

"The Kirin Tor's best has decided she doesn't need to wait for an escort to enter a quarantine zone."
