# Chapter One: The Assignment

As the Ranger-General of Silvermoon – a title that came with its own office in the Spire of the Sun, two adjutants, a view of the Eversong canopy that would have made a poet weep, and an amount of paperwork that would have made that same poet hang herself – Sylvanas Windrunner had developed a finely tuned instinct for when her morning was about to go sideways.

The instinct had nothing to do with magic. It was older, more dependable: the sound of Lor'themar Theron's boots in the corridor outside her door.

Lor'themar had a particular stride when he brought good news – unhurried, the weight even, sometimes accompanied by a whistle. He had a different stride entirely when the news was bad: faster, heavier, the gait of a man who wanted to deliver his cargo and leave before it detonated. But the worst stride of all – the one that made Sylvanas set down her pen and reach for the nearest weapon – was the slow, even-footed approach that meant Lor'themar was about to be diplomatic.

Lor'themar being diplomatic meant the Convocation was involved.

"No," said Sylvanas, before he had finished opening the door.

Lor'themar stepped into her office with the expression of a man who had been expecting this response and had brought exactly no countermeasures. He was holding a scroll case in one hand and a second, smaller scroll in the other. The scroll case bore the Convocation's seal – crimson wax, sunburst crest, the sort of stamp that made every Farstrider's jaw tighten on instinct.

"You haven't heard what it is yet," he said.

"I heard your boots."

Lor'themar set the scroll case on her desk with the care one reserves for live ordinance. "The Convocation wants a Farstrider escort for a Kirin Tor mage. They want you."

Sylvanas looked at the scroll case and did not pick it up. "Since when does the Convocation assign escort duty to the Ranger-General?"

"Since the investigation involves the southern border and they want someone who won't start a diplomatic incident." Lor'themar paused. "The implication being that the last three Farstriders who encountered Alliance operatives near the border almost started one."

"Ranger Thalion didn't start anything. The human drew first."

"The human drew first because Thalion shot an arrow at his horse."

"At the ground near his horse."

"The horse disagreed."

Sylvanas picked up the scroll case, because it was clear that Lor'themar had no intention of taking it back, and cracked the seal. The Convocation's orders were written in the ornate formal script that Silvermoon's bureaucrats employed to make everything sound simultaneously urgent and tedious. She skimmed past the honorifics – there were four – and the jurisdictional throat-clearing – there was a full paragraph of it – and arrived at the substance.

The Kirin Tor had identified contamination in the grain supply along the southern border. Plague contamination. A mage had been dispatched to investigate the source, and the Convocation – in its infinite generosity – was providing a military escort to ensure the investigation proceeded without incident.

The smaller scroll was the Kirin Tor's formal request. It was shorter, cleaner, and notably lacked the four honorifics. It named the mage.

"Proudmoore," said Sylvanas.

Lor'themar's eyebrow climbed half an inch. "You know her?"

"Everyone knows the name." Sylvanas set the scroll down on her desk, aligning it precisely with the edge of the wood. Jaina Proudmoore. Antonidas's protégée, current darling of the Kirin Tor. There were perhaps half a dozen names in the current political landscape that could make Silvermoon's Convocation sit up and pay attention, and Proudmoore was on at least two of the lists.

"Eressea will brief you on the route and the forward post," said Lor'themar, already moving toward the door. He knew when to retreat. It was one of his better qualities.

"Lor'themar."

He stopped.

"When the horse business is settled," said Sylvanas, "I want Thalion's incident report on my desk. The real one, not the version he wrote for the Convocation."

"Already on your desk. Under the intelligence dispatches."

"You buried it."

"I prioritised." He offered a smile that stopped well short of his eyes and closed the door behind him.

Eressea's briefing was efficient – Sylvanas appreciated that – and thorough, as expected. Eressea was the best aide-de-camp a Ranger-General could ask for: competent, sharp-eyed, in possession of the rare quality of knowing when to stop talking. She had prepared a dossier on the mage that was only slightly thicker than the Convocation's scroll, suggesting she'd been working on this for at least a day before Lor'themar decided to break the news.

"Archmage Jaina Proudmoore," said Eressea, flipping open the dossier on the campaign table between them. "Twenty-nine. Human. Theramore-born, Dalaran-trained. Primary specialisation in arcane theory – specifically, magical contamination and entropic decay. She published a paper two years ago on the arcane signatures of plague agents that was apparently quite well received among people who read that sort of thing."

"Academic."

"Extremely." Eressea turned a page. "She's also a combat mage, trained in frost and arcane disciplines. Dalaran doesn't turn out field mages who can't hold their own in a fight, although her combat record is – theoretical, as far as Silvermoon's intelligence can determine."

"Theoretical meaning she hasn't been in one."

"Theoretical meaning if she has, it wasn't on any battlefield we monitor."

Sylvanas considered this. A combat-trained academic who wrote papers about plague contamination and had been personally dispatched by the Kirin Tor to investigate the southern border. Either the contamination was worse than the Convocation's scroll suggested, or Proudmoore had drawn the short straw. Given that the Kirin Tor didn't generally dispatch its rising stars on errands, the former seemed far more likely.

"When does she arrive at the border?"

Eressea glanced at the second page of the dossier. "Two days ago."

Sylvanas paused. "She's already there?"

"She arrived at the Farstrider outpost at Quel'Danil Pass the day before yesterday. Lieutenant Cyndia sent word this morning – the mage is at the pass, she's set up a field station, and she's waiting for her escort." A beat. "Impatiently, from the tone of Cyndia's dispatch."

Of course she was.

Sylvanas folded the Convocation's orders into the inside pocket of her coat, left the Kirin Tor's request on the campaign table for Eressea to file, and went to pack.

~

The ride from Silvermoon to Quel'Danil Pass took the better part of a day – south through the Eversong canopy, where the light came through the leaves in long amber shafts and the air smelled of cedar and warm stone, and then down through the thinning forest where the quel'dorei wards began to fray and the sunlight changed colour. You could feel the border before you saw it. The Farstrider wards made the air hum – a low, steady vibration that settled into the bones – and where the wards ended, the hum stopped, and what replaced it was the ordinary silence of a country that had no magic in its soil.

Sylvanas had ridden this road a thousand times. The transition never bothered her, exactly, but she noticed it the way a sailor notices the change from river current to open sea: a different quality of resistance.

Lieutenant Cyndia met her at the outpost gate with a salute that was slightly too sharp – trouble – and a report that was slightly too detailed.

"The Kirin Tor mage arrived at sixteen hundred on the ninth," said Cyndia, falling into step beside Sylvanas as they crossed the outpost yard. "She inspected the ward-stones, set up a field station in the east barracks, and sent a courier south to the contamination site at Millhaven. I offered a Farstrider escort for the courier. She declined."

"Of course she did."

"The courier returned this morning with soil and grain samples. The mage has been running analyses since dawn." Cyndia paused, and the pause was doing a great deal of work. "She's efficient, General. I'll give her that."

"But?"

"But she acts like the escort is a courtesy, not a requirement. She sent her own courier into contested territory without clearance. If I hadn't had scouts in the area already, she'd have had a human riding through Farstrider patrol zones with no identification and no protection." Cyndia's jaw was set in that way it got when she was restraining herself from saying something far less polite. "And this morning, a second courier arrived from the Kirin Tor. With a letter. Addressed to you."

She handed Sylvanas a sealed envelope. The paper was heavy – Dalaran stock, pale blue, with the Kirin Tor eye stamped into the wax. Inside, the letter was written in a precise, economical hand.

*Ranger-General Windrunner – I have been informed of your assignment to escort this investigation. As I have already begun preliminary analysis at the border site and at Millhaven, I see no reason to delay the field investigation pending your arrival. The contamination is not waiting for us and I would prefer that we not wait for it. I will brief you on my findings upon your arrival. – J. Proudmoore, Archmage, Kirin Tor*

Sylvanas read the letter twice. The first time for the content. The second time for the tone, which was precisely the tone of someone who considered the escort a mild inconvenience imposed upon her by people who didn't understand the urgency of the work.

"When you say she acts like the escort is a courtesy," said Sylvanas, folding the letter and slipping it into her coat, "what you mean is she's been doing exactly what she wants and politely informing us after the fact."

"That is a diplomatic way to put it, General."

"I'm told I'm here to be diplomatic."

Cyndia, wisely, said nothing.

The mage's field station was not, as Sylvanas had expected, a hastily assembled tent full of Kirin Tor apparatus. It was the east barracks – an entire building – which the mage had apparently commandeered without asking and converted into a working laboratory. Glass vials stood in racks along the window ledges. A map of the southern border covered the far wall, pinned at the corners with small arcane anchors that pulsed faintly blue. There were soil samples in labelled jars on every flat surface, and the air smelled of ozone and a sweeter, organic scent that Sylvanas recognised from battlefield triage stations. Rot. Very faint, but present.

Jaina Proudmoore was standing at the map with her back to the door when Sylvanas entered. She was taller than the dossier had suggested – or perhaps she simply held herself taller, the way mages did when they were drawing on the ambient. Her hair was pulled back from her face and secured with a clasp that had nothing decorative about it. Her robes were working robes – practical, travel-stained.

"Ranger-General," said the mage, turning from the map. "I was told to expect you yesterday."

"You were told to wait for your escort," said Sylvanas.

"I was told a great many things." The mage's eyes – the colour of deep ice – met Sylvanas's without flinching. "Most of them weren't relevant to the investigation."

Sylvanas let the silence hold. She had met this kind before – the ones who hadn't lost a war yet and walked as though the possibility hadn't occurred to them. They were either very good or very lucky, and she'd find out which on the road.

"I've read your letter," said Sylvanas, pulling the chair out from the desk – the mage's desk, in the Farstrider's barracks, commandeered without so much as a requisition form – and sitting down without being invited. Two could play at territorial. "Brief me."

The mage blinked at the chair. The blink lasted half a second longer than it needed to. Then Proudmoore turned back to the map and began.

She was, Sylvanas had to admit, thorough. The contamination was in the grain supply – wheat, specifically, distributed through the autumn trade routes to at least four villages south of the border. The grain carried a magical signature consistent with engineered plague agents – deliberate introduction, precise and targeted. The signature was entropic in nature, breaking things down at the cellular level, keyed to organic matter – everything that ate the grain was affected. The village of Millhaven, twelve miles south, had reported the first symptoms three weeks ago. The reports had stopped ten days ago.

"Stopped," said Sylvanas.

"Stopped." The mage didn't elaborate.

"You're telling me this in short sentences," said Sylvanas. "Is that for my benefit, or yours?"

The mage's chin came up half an inch. "I beg your pardon?"

"You've been simplifying your explanations since I walked in. Shorter clauses, smaller vocabulary, fewer qualifications What you're saying to me now is the version you'd give a field officer who needed the summary." Sylvanas leaned back in the chair. "I've read Dalaran's published research on entropic contamination. I have questions about your methodology. I'd prefer the answers in full."

The silence that followed was, to Sylvanas's ear, extremely satisfying.

"I wasn't –" The mage stopped herself. Started again. "The entropic signature in the grain samples shows a decay rate of approximately fourteen percent per day in organic tissue, accelerating upon direct consumption. The vector appears to be a modified strain of arcane blight, but the modification is unusual. Conventional plague agents dissipate after the host material is consumed. This one embeds. It rewrites the necromantic potential of the tissue itself." She met Sylvanas's gaze. "In practical terms, anything that eats enough of the grain doesn't just die. It becomes available for reanimation."

"And Millhaven?"

"I need to see Millhaven in person. The courier's samples are suggestive, but field analysis requires proximity to the contamination site." She paused. "That's why I started without the escort. Because every day we wait is a day the contamination spreads further south."

It was, grudgingly, a reasonable answer. Sylvanas would have done the same in the mage's position. She would have done it faster, and without the courtesy of a letter.

"We leave at dawn," said Sylvanas. "I'll have Cyndia prepare a four-ranger detail."

"I don't need –"

"You need an escort. That's why I'm here." She stood. "We leave at dawn. Bring your sampling kits."

The mage opened her mouth, closed it, and – with what appeared to be considerable effort – said nothing.

Sylvanas left the barracks and went to find Cyndia.

~

The road to Millhaven wound south through country that couldn't decide what it wanted to be – the tail end of the Eversong wards fading into Lordaeron scrubland, pine forests giving way to fields that had been tended once and now languished unattended, the whole landscape settling into the grey-green middle ground between elvish territory and human farmland. The morning was cold and clear. Birds sang in the canopy behind them -- the ward-stones setting a clear boundary past which wildlife was unwiling to tread.

Sylvanas rode at the front. The mage rode behind her, flanked by two Farstrider scouts. Two more brought up the rear. Nobody spoke.

The mage, however, did not appear to be enjoying the silence. She rode with her head turning every few minutes – scanning the tree line, the road surface, the sky. It looked like vigilance. It was curiosity.

"Your scouts," said the mage, after the first mile.

"My scouts are doing their job."

"They're scanning the canopy every thirty seconds. Is there a threat overhead?"

Sylvanas glanced back. The mage was watching her with an expression of genuine inquiry – brows up, chin level. "Harpies," said Sylvanas. "This stretch of the border had a nest two seasons ago. The nest was cleared. The scouts check because nests return."

"Ah." The mage considered this, and Sylvanas waited for the follow-up question about harpy migration patterns or nesting behaviours or whatever ancillary detail a Kirin Tor academic would want. The follow-up never came. Instead, the mage shifted her attention to the road surface and said, "The topsoil here is different from the samples my courier brought back. The mineral content is wrong."

Sylvanas said nothing. She had learned, over the centuries, that the most efficient way to draw information from someone who wanted to give it was to stay quiet and let them talk.

"The Millhaven samples had a high calcium carbonate content – chalky, consistent with the riverbed geology in that area. But the grain distributed through the autumn trade routes passed through three different soil regions, and the contamination signature was identical in all of them, which means the plague agent was introduced after the grain was milled, not before." The mage paused. "Someone is poisoning the flour."

"Not the wheat."

"The wheat is clean. I checked the samples against pre-harvest records from the nearest Lordaeron agricultural post. The contamination enters at the milling stage – after the wheat is processed but before it reaches the villages."

The full picture, for the first time. The mage was no longer simplifying. Whether she'd taken Sylvanas's comment to heart or had simply forgotten to condescend in the excitement of her own analysis, the effect was the same.

"The mills are in Andorhal," said Sylvanas.

"Yes," said the mage. "They are."

The rest of the ride passed in a silence that was more productive than the earlier one. Sylvanas turned the information over. Andorhal was the grain hub for the entire western reach of Lordaeron – a market town grown fat on the wheat trade, population ten thousand at last count, with granaries, mills, and distribution networks that fed a hundred villages between the capital and the border. If the contamination was being introduced at the milling stage, it wasn't an accident. It was infrastructure. Someone was using Lordaeron's own supply lines as a weapon.

Millhaven announced itself by smell.

~

The village was wrong the way a painting is wrong when someone has shifted the perspective – everything in its place, nothing where it should be. Houses stood in their rows. Fences lined their yards. The road ran straight through the centre, hard-packed earth marked with wheel ruts from the harvest carts. All of it correct, all of it empty.

The smell was grain. Rotting grain. The mage dismounted before Sylvanas could tell her not to and stood in the road with her eyes closed, both hands raised, palms flat against the air.

"Don't touch anything," said Sylvanas, swinging down from her mount. She signalled the scouts to spread. "Cyndia – perimeter. Full sweep. I want to know if anything in this village is still moving."

"General." Cyndia and three rangers fanned into the village.

The mage was already walking toward the grain stores.

Sylvanas followed – partly because the mage needed supervision, although she would deny it, and partly because the tactical picture was building itself in her head and she wanted to see what the woman saw alongside it. The grain stores sat at the centre of the village, a long wooden building with a loading dock on the south side. The doors were open. Inside, the light came through gaps in the roof slats in pale, dusty columns, and the smell was thick enough to chew.

The mage crouched at the edge of the grain pile – wheat, gone grey and soft, the kernels swollen with moisture they shouldn't have held. She pulled a glass vial from her satchel, scraped a sample from the top layer, held it to the light. The vial pulsed faintly – a sick green-white – and her face went very still.

"This is the same signature," she said. "Identical to the courier samples. But the concentration here is –" She stopped. Tilted the vial and muttered a few incantations, breathing faster and faster as she worked. "An order of magnitude higher than what I measured at the border." She finally said.

"Meaning the closer we get to the source, the stronger it gets."

"Meaning the contamination is cumulative. The grain doesn't just carry it. It concentrates." She stood, brushing grey dust from her palms, visibly trying to calm herself down. "The people who ate this were exposed to a dose that's –"

Sylvanas left the mage to her samples and walked the village.

The story was in the ground. She'd read battlefields since before this mage's grandmother was born, and Millhaven was a battlefield – just not the kind anyone taught at an academy. The drag marks told the first chapter: heavy, irregular, from the houses to the road. Furniture shoved aside, door frames scuffed at hip height, the marks of bodies moving through spaces too narrow for the way they were moving. The footprints told the second: dozens of them, all facing south, close-packed, the spacing uniform. A column.

A formation.

The people of Millhaven had walked out of their houses in the middle of the night and lined up in the road like soldiers on a parade ground. Then they had walked south.

Cyndia found the survivors in the last house on the eastern edge – a man and a woman huddled in a root cellar, the trapdoor barred from the inside. The woman spoke, eventually. The man had not spoken since the night it happened. He sat beside the fire that the Farstriders built for them and stared at his hands, which were curled around nothing, fingers still shaped to grip a handle or a tool he'd dropped when he ran.

"The bread," said the woman. Her voice was flat, scraped clean by two weeks of waiting in a cellar. "We ate the bread and then – my husband. He stood up. In the middle of the night." She pulled the blanket tighter around her shoulders. The wool caught on a splinter in the crate she was sitting on, pulling a loose thread. "He stood up and he walked to the door and I tried to stop him and he – he didn't see me. He looked at me and he didn't see me. And I followed him out and they were all –"

She stopped. The fire popped – sap in the green wood – and both of them flinched, the woman's hand shooting to her husband's arm, gripping, then slowly releasing.

"They were standing in the road," she said. "In a line. Facing south."

Sylvanas let the woman rest. She crossed the village to the grain stores and found the mage wrist-deep in the contaminated wheat, vials and instruments spread across the floorboards around her, entirely consumed by the work.

"They walked south," said Sylvanas. "In military formation. The entire village."

The mage looked up. Her hands were dusted grey with grain. "Military formation?"

"Uniform spacing. Directional alignment. Single-file lines converging into a column on the south road." Sylvanas crouched beside the nearest set of drag marks, visible through the open doors. "That isn't a crowd. That's an organised movement. Someone called them."

The mage stood slowly. Her face went through a sequence – surprise, confusion, and then the dangerous one. Fascination.

"The Andorhal mills," said the mage. "The contamination is introduced there. The grain is distributed through the trade routes. The people eat the bread, and then –"

"Something calls them." Sylvanas straightened. "Something south, with the ability to command a hundred civilians in coordinated movement."

"That's not a plague," said the mage.

Sylvanas agreed.

~

The trail south of the village led into a stand of old-growth oaks that had no business being this quiet. No birds. No insects. The canopy cut the light to a grey-green twilight at ground level, and the temperature dropped as soon as they crossed the tree line.

Sylvanas had her bow in hand before the first of them came into view.

They stood among the trees in rows – three deep, perhaps sixty total, spaced at arm's length like soldiers in a drill formation. Millhaven's missing. They wore the clothes they'd been wearing when they walked out of their houses two weeks ago: nightshirts, work trousers, bare feet. Some of them still had flour on their hands. All of them were standing perfectly still, facing south, and all of them were dead.

Dead and wrong. No bloating after two weeks in the open. No scavenger damage. They were preserved – hollow-eyed, upright, waiting. The entropic contamination had done exactly what the mage said it would. It hadn't just killed them. It had made them available.

"Back," said Sylvanas, very quietly, to the patrol.

The nearest corpse turned its head.

The movement was wrong – mechanical, a pivot rather than a turn. Its eyes were open, filmed white, and they tracked Sylvanas with the blank precision of a predator that doesn't need to think about what it's hunting. Then the second moved. The third. The ripple spread through the formation like wind through wheat, head after head swivelling toward the Farstrider patrol, and the sound that came from sixty dead throats was a low, grinding exhalation, like the first breath of a bellows.

"Fall back," said Sylvanas. "Now. Don't run. Fifty-yard intervals. Cyndia – left flank. Eressea – right. I'll hold the centre."

She put the first arrow through the nearest one's eye socket at forty yards, drew and nocked and released before it hit the ground. The second arrow took the one behind it in the throat. The third missed – the corpse moved laterally, a shuffle that no dead thing should have known how to execute, and the arrow buried itself in the oak behind it.

They were learning. Or something was learning for them.

The patrol fell back through the trees in the disciplined, silent withdrawal that Farstriders trained for years to execute – firing, retreating, covering, firing. Behind them, the dead came forward with the slow patience of a tide. They didn't charge. They advanced in formation, sixty corpses moving as one body directed by a mind that wasn't in any of them.

The mage caught up to the retreat at the tree line.

She'd been running. Her face was flushed and she had both hands raised and the air between her palms was white with frost.

"You left without me," she said.

"You were taking samples."

"Move," said the mage, and for the first time her voice carried the flat, hard certainty of someone who knew what she was about to do and knew it would work. She stepped past Sylvanas, planted her feet on the bare ground between the last oaks and the open field, and brought both hands down.

The ice hit the ground in a sheet, spreading from the mage's feet in a circle that cracked and sang as it expanded, flowing between the tree trunks, slicking the roots, climbing the bark. The dead kept coming, stepping onto the ice with the same mechanical steadiness they'd used through the trees, and the first rank went down – already dead, they couldn't be killed again, but the ice locked around their ankles, their shins, climbed their legs and held them, and behind them the second rank collided with the first and went down too, the formation collapsing into a heap of frozen limbs and working jaws.

It wouldn't hold. Sylvanas knew it and the mage knew it – you could see it in the set of her shoulders, the way she kept her hands raised, feeding power into the ice. The dead were already cracking the sheet, the ones at the back climbing over the ones at the front.

"That bought us four minutes," said the mage, dropping her hands. A tremor ran through her fingers. "Perhaps five."

"Then we use them." Sylvanas turned to Cyndia. "Double time. Back to the road. Now."

They ran. Behind them, the sound of cracking ice and the low exhalation of sixty dead throats followed them out of the trees and onto the open road, where the sunlight hit like a wall and the world snapped back to sky, road, distance.

They didn't stop until the tree line was half a mile behind them and Cyndia confirmed that the dead had stopped at the forest edge. They hadn't pursued into the open. A boundary held them to the trees, or a command called them back, and Sylvanas didn't know which was worse.

The mage was breathing hard. The tremor in her hands hadn't stopped. She looked at Sylvanas and Sylvanas looked at her and neither of them said anything for a moment that stretched, because what was there to say? They had both seen the same thing. They had both arrived at the same conclusion.

"Military formation," said the mage. "You said military formation."

"I said it because that's what it was."

"A plague agent that kills civilians and reanimates them into organised units." The mage's voice was steady, which was impressive given that her hands were not. "That's not a disease. That's a weapon."

"Yes."

"Whoever is contaminating the grain in Andorhal isn't poisoning a food supply. They're building an army."

"Yes."

The mage went quiet. Then she said, "I need to send a message to the Kirin Tor. Antonidas should know immediately."

"Agreed. I'm sending a dispatch to Silvermoon." Sylvanas began walking north, and after a moment the mage fell into step beside her. Not behind – beside. The positioning was new. "You adapted the ice spell on the field."

"It was designed for structural containment. I modified the casting for area denial."

"In the middle of a combat situation."

"The original application wasn't going to work. The situation required a different approach." The mage glanced at her, and there was a question in the glance that she didn't ask.

"That was good work," said Sylvanas.

The mage's stride missed half a beat. She recovered. "Your archery is – also impressive."

"Also impressive. You could put that on my headstone."

The mage's mouth twitched – half a degree, barely there – the first crack in the wall of Kirin Tor composure she'd seen from Jaina Proudmoore since the barracks.

They walked in silence after that. A different kind of silence.

~

Night fell over Quel'Danil Pass with the slow certainty of a curtain drawn across a stage. The camp was set. The fires were lit. The dispatch riders had gone north to Silvermoon and south to find a sending station with enough range to reach Dalaran. Cyndia's scouts held the perimeter in rotating shifts, and the survivors from Millhaven sat beside the nearest fire wrapped in blankets, saying nothing. The husband's hands were still curled around empty air.

Sylvanas wrote two letters.

The first was for the Convocation – clinical, the language of military intelligence. The facts as observed. Contaminated grain supply, confirmed. Reanimated civilians in organised formation, confirmed. Source likely Andorhal. Threat level: significant. Recommended response: immediate reinforcement of the southern border and dispatch of a full investigation team.

The second was for Lor'themar alone. She wrote it in the loose, fast hand she used for personal correspondence – the handwriting of the woman, not the title. She was going south with the mage. She was extending the mission unilaterally. She knew the Convocation wouldn't approve. She was asking him to keep the southern patrols running in her absence and not to ask questions when the angry dispatches started arriving from the Spire.

Writing it forced her to put into words what she already knew: this wasn't a border incident. This was an invasion – patient, deliberate, using bread as the weapon and farmers as the soldiers. If the Convocation couldn't see it from Silvermoon, she would see it from the road.

She folded the official report with military creases, sharp, the parchment cracking at the edges. The personal letter she folded once, quickly, and sealed with a thumbpress of wax heated over the fire. No signet ring. Just the whorl of her thumbprint in the cooling red.

She found Jaina an hour later at the ward-stones.

Jaina was crouched between the two largest ward-stones with her hands flat against the engraved faces. Somewhere between the ice field and the road north. The blue light of the wards pulsed brighter where her skin touched stone. She had scratched a rough map in the dirt with a stick – villages marked as Xs, sweeping arcs showing the contamination's spread, the stick discarded beside her knee with its tip dark and wet.

She'd been working since they got back. The tremor was still in her hands. She was running a new analysis – comparative, using Silvermoon's wards as a baseline to map the entropic signature across the border network. Building a way to find the contamination from a distance, to chart its spread without walking into every village to check.

Sylvanas recognised the instinct, because it was her own. The mission over the body. The objective over rest. She had seen it in Farstriders who stayed at their posts through injury, through exhaustion, through every sensible reason to stop. She had never expected to see it in a Kirin Tor academic.

"How far?" said Sylvanas.

Jaina didn't look up. "Three more villages, at minimum. The contamination extends well past Millhaven." Her fingers traced one of the arcs in the dirt. "And the signal gets stronger to the southwest."

"Andorhal."

"Andorhal." Jaina sat back on her heels. The ward-stones pulsed once more and dimmed. She looked at the map she'd drawn – rough, already drying at the edges, the X marks shrinking as the moisture left the soil. "The signature isn't decaying."

Sylvanas waited.

"Meaning," she said.

"Meaning whatever we found at Millhaven isn't the end of something." Jaina looked up, and in the blue light of the fading wards her face was sharp and tired and certain. "It's the beginning. And it's moving faster than we are."

Sylvanas looked south. Past the ward-stones, past the last pulse of blue light, the Lordaeron road disappeared into darkness. Somewhere down that road the wind moved through trees that had no birds in them, and sixty dead farmers stood in formation waiting for orders from something she couldn't see.

"Then we move faster," she said, hand resting on her bow.

Neither of them went back inside.
