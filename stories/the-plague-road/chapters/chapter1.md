# Chapter 1: The Assignment

The war table occupied the center of Sylvanas's command post like a second commander, and she preferred its company to most of the Convocation's. Lacquered ashwood, three centuries old, its surface scarred by the pins of six ranger-generals before her. The current spread covered the southern border in miniature: colored pins for patrol routes — green for active, amber for pending rotation, red for the two incursion points near Zul'Aman where the Amani had been testing the perimeter since midsummer. Supply lines in blue thread, stitched through the map's preservation enchantments so the thread wouldn't rot and the lines wouldn't lie. Sylvanas rested two fingers on the red pin marking the eastern incursion and considered removing it. The troll raiding party had scattered after Lor'themar's ambush three days ago, but scattered wasn't destroyed, and she'd learned long before her first century that the Amani had a gift for regrouping in terrain that should have made regrouping impossible.

The knock came while she was still deciding whether the pin stayed or moved.

Lor'themar entered carrying a scroll case and wearing the expression he reserved for political assignments — mouth neutral, eyes careful, the face of a man who'd learned to deliver bad news by making himself an uninviting target for the reaction.

"The Convocation has expressed its preference," he said, placing the scroll case on the table beside the map, "that the investigation be handled at the command level."

Sylvanas didn't reach for the case. "Which investigation."

"Plague rumors near the northern border. Kirin Tor has requested cooperation. The Convocation wants to appear cooperative." He paused, and the pause held the shape of the thing he wasn't saying yet. "They want you specifically."

"They want me to hold a human's hand while she sniffs at sick farmers."

"They want you to ensure Silvermoon appears invested without actually committing resources. Your presence satisfies the diplomatic requirement. One ranger-general costs less than a battalion."

"That's the first honest thing anyone's said about this assignment."

Lor'themar inclined his head. He'd served under her for forty years, long enough to recognize when agreement was the fastest route through a conversation.

---

Magistrix Eressea arrived within the hour, immaculate in the formal robes of the Convocation's diplomatic corps, and proceeded to deliver the operational brief in a register of Thalassian so elaborately courteous that each sentence took three times longer than its content required. Sylvanas stood by the window and let the words wash past her like ornamental water, catching only the facts that mattered.

The plague rumors: villages in Lordaeron's northern provinces going silent over the past month. Trade caravans that stopped arriving. A pattern the Kirin Tor had flagged as anomalous, which meant someone in Dalaran was paying attention even if Silvermoon wasn't.

The mage being sent: Jaina Proudmoore.

Sylvanas turned from the window.

"Proudmoore has requested unrestricted access to the border approach," Eressea continued, "including the first ring of ward-stones. The Convocation has authorized limited cooperation, pending your tactical assessment."

"Unrestricted access to our ward network. She asked for that by name?"

"By specific ward designation. She appears to have studied the schematics."

Sylvanas said nothing for a moment. The ward designations were technical documents, maintained by the Farstriders and the magisters jointly, circulated in Thalassian to a restricted list. A human mage who'd bothered to acquire them, translate them, and learn enough about the system to request access by designation had done real work. That was either very careful preparation or a very particular kind of arrogance, and both were more interesting than a routine diplomatic errand.

"When does she arrive?"

---

Eressea left, and the room improved. Sylvanas pulled Lor'themar back before he could follow.

"The plague rumors. Not the Convocation's version. What do the ranger reports say?"

He leaned against the doorframe, arms crossed, and the posture told her he'd been waiting for this question.

"Three villages on the Lordaeron side of the border have gone quiet in the last month. Not destroyed — quiet. Trade stopped. Smoke stopped. Cyndia's patrol at the Pass has been filing reports, but command flagged them as low priority." He let that sit. Low priority meant the Convocation had read the reports and decided that villages on the wrong side of the border weren't Silvermoon's concern. "She sent Veloren across on an unauthorized sweep six days ago. The village nearest the Pass was empty. Food on the tables. Doors open. No bodies. No violence. People just gone."

Sylvanas's fingers found the eastern incursion pin and pulled it from the map. The trolls could wait.

"You want me to believe you're interested in escorting a human mage," Lor'themar said.

"I want you to believe three villages don't go silent without a reason, and the Convocation is too busy being diplomatic to notice."

"Take Cyndia's patrol at the Pass. She's been filing reports nobody reads."

"I'll read them on the ride."

She selected her kit with the methodical speed of someone who'd done it before wars: the recurve bow she'd carried for a century, its grip worn to the shape of her hand; the paired short swords in their crossed harness; a quiver of thirty shafts, broadhead, fletched with hawk feather for stability in crosswind. Two days' provisions. A light pack. She dressed in field leathers — Farstrider standard, forest green and mithril chain, the formal insignia of her rank left on its hook. This wasn't a diplomatic appearance. This was a reconnaissance.

---

Silvermoon unwound behind her as she rode south, and she watched it go with the detached assessment of a woman who spent most of her time at its edges rather than its center. The city was beautiful. It was always beautiful — the living light that suffused the architecture turned every surface golden, every arch luminous, every courtyard a study in elven perfection. The citizens moved through it with the unhurried grace of people who'd lived inside that beauty long enough to forget it was exceptional. The Convocation governed from chambers so exquisite they could mistake governance for aesthetics.

The arch of the southern gate marked the boundary. She passed through it, and the living light stopped. Natural sunlight, thinner, colder, replaced it on her skin like a change of season in a single stride. Beyond the walls, the forest was just forest — birch and oak, turning amber with the first bite of autumn, the road rutted from cart traffic that Silvermoon's citizens rarely thought about. Out here, the world was less golden and more honest, and Sylvanas preferred it.

The ride to Thalassian Pass took the better part of the morning. She read Cyndia's reports in the saddle, one hand on the reins, the other holding parchment that grew increasingly unsettling as the pages turned. Missing caravans. A Lordaeron merchant who'd arrived at the Pass in a state of collapse, babbling about grain before the border guards could get a coherent statement from him. Unusual nighttime sounds from across the border — rhythmic, heavy, unidentifiable. Seven reports, filed over three weeks, each one more pointed than the last, each one receipted by command and answered with silence.

---

Cyndia met her at the Pass garrison, a Farstrider outpost built into the rock face where the forest thinned and the ancient ward-stones rose from the ground in a line that marked the border between Quel'Thalas and Lordaeron. The ward-stones hummed — a low, subsonic vibration Sylvanas registered in her teeth more than her ears, the deep pulse of elven magic that had guarded this crossing for millennia.

Cyndia was young for a ranger, barely past her second century, with the sharp eyes and compressed patience of someone who'd been watching something no one else would look at.

"I've filed seven reports, Ranger-General. Seven." She stood at attention, but the words carried an edge that attention couldn't quite contain. "The response I received was a logistics request for winter ration projections."

"Tell me what you didn't put in the reports."

Cyndia's jaw tightened. "Veloren crossed the border on patrol six days ago. She came back before dawn and requested a transfer. When I pressed her, she said the village on the other side of the pass smelled wrong." A pause, and the pause had weight. "Not like plague. Like a butcher's floor after closing. Old blood. And the village was still occupied."

"Occupied by whom."

"She didn't say by whom. She said 'occupied' the way you'd say 'infested.'"

The word sat between them like a stone dropped in still water. Sylvanas filed it, cross-referenced it with the missing caravans and the nighttime sounds, and arrived at a picture she didn't like.

A horse came up the Pass road at a pace that suggested its rider had pushed hard and hadn't enjoyed it. The rider was human, young, wearing the blue-and-silver of the Kirin Tor with the rumpled formality of an apprentice on his first field assignment. He dismounted badly, caught his foot in the stirrup, recovered with the panicked dignity of someone who knew he'd been watched, and presented himself to Sylvanas with a bow that was technically correct and personally terrified.

"Archmage Proudmoore sends her respects and asks that your scouts begin preliminary terrain assessment of the road to Millhaven, the first—"

"She's already at the border."

The courier, Maric, blinked. "She — yes. She arrived before dawn. She said the ward-stone resonance patterns were best measured during the temperature transition at—"

"She started without me."

"She began preliminary analysis." Maric's voice found the careful register of a man selecting each word from a tray of options that all seemed dangerous. "She was very clear that she values the cooperation—"

"Cyndia." Sylvanas turned from the courier. "Saddle up. The Kirin Tor's best has decided she doesn't need to wait for an escort to enter a quarantine zone."

---

Jaina Proudmoore was kneeling in the mud on the Lordaeron side of the Pass, both palms pressed flat against the base of the nearest ward-stone, her staff planted upright in the dirt beside her, her eyes closed. She was murmuring. The sound was analytical, rhythmic — an incantation, Sylvanas realized, in a dialect of Thalassian she hadn't heard from a human mouth. The pronunciation was imperfect but the cadence was right, the kind of fluency that came from studying the language as a technical instrument rather than a living tongue.

She was not what Sylvanas had expected. The expectation had been a court mage — formal robes, ceremonial staff, the soft posture of someone who'd spent their career in libraries and lecture halls. What she found was a woman in traveling clothes that had already seen hard use, leather boots worn at the heels from actual walking, the knees of her trousers muddy from hours of kneeling on wet ground. Her hair was pulled back in a braid that had started the morning neat and surrendered to the conditions by midday. Her hands, pressed flat on the ward-stone's surface, had dirt under the nails and calluses on the fingertips — the marks of staff-work and practical spellcraft, not academic demonstration.

She finished the incantation, opened her eyes, and looked up at Sylvanas with an expression that held no deference, no anxiety, no particular accommodation for the fact that the Ranger-General of Silvermoon was standing three paces away with a bow on her back and a grievance she hadn't yet decided how to deliver. It was the expression of someone surfacing from deep concentration, still halfway inside the work, assessing the interruption's relevance before committing to engaging with it.

"Ranger-General." She stood, wiping her palms on her trousers. "I appreciate Silvermoon's cooperation. I've completed my initial ward-stone analysis, and I'd like to proceed to Millhaven before midday. The contamination's residual signature degrades in afternoon thermal conditions, and I need—"

"You've been running unsecured diagnostic magic on our ward network since before dawn. In a quarantine zone. Without escort. Without notifying my border patrol."

A beat of silence. Jaina's eyes were blue, very blue, and they held the particular steadiness of someone who was not accustomed to being interrupted and was deciding how much patience the interruption deserved.

"I notified your courier. And the contamination's residual signature—"

"Degrades in afternoon heat. You said. The road to Millhaven passes through three kilometers of uncleared forest with no sight lines and no established patrol route. If the village fell to plague, whatever caused it may still be in the area. You don't walk into that alone."

Something shifted in Jaina's expression. A reassessment, quick and clinical, the look of a researcher encountering a variable she'd underestimated.

"You've already scouted the route."

"My scouts have been monitoring the border for three weeks. The route is not secured, which is why we haven't used it. I don't send people down roads I haven't cleared."

"Then clear it. I need to be in Millhaven before the thermal window closes."

The silence between them stretched, thinned, acquired an edge.

"You're giving me orders."

"I'm establishing priorities."

Behind Sylvanas, Cyndia cleared her throat with the precision of a soldier who has watched superior officers waste daylight before and has long since stopped finding it instructive.

"Ranger-General. The thermal window Archmage Proudmoore mentioned closes in four hours. If we leave now, we reach Millhaven with two hours to spare. If we continue discussing command structure, we reach Millhaven tomorrow."

Sylvanas looked at Cyndia. The look contained several things, not all of them appropriate to deliver in front of the Kirin Tor's representative, but the primary content was the grudging acknowledgment that her scout had found the obvious solution to a problem that two senior officers had been too busy measuring each other to solve.

"Move."

---

The road to Millhaven was a cart track, rutted and poorly maintained, cutting through forest that should have been alive with autumn activity — squirrels in the canopy, woodpeckers working the dead birch, the constant low rustle of small things going about the business of being small. Cyndia took point with two scouts. Sylvanas walked behind them with Jaina, and the walk established their dynamic faster than any negotiation could have.

Jaina talked while she moved. Not chatter — processing, her thoughts running parallel to her feet, analytical observations about the contamination measurements she'd taken at the ward-stones. The residual signature was entropic, she said. It drew energy from the surrounding environment the way a fire drew air, pulling heat and vitality out of the soil, the vegetation, the ambient magical field. She'd measured it at the ward-stones because elven wards provided a stable reference baseline, and the deviation from that baseline told her the contamination had been active for weeks, spreading outward from a source to the south and west.

Sylvanas listened and watched the tree line. The two activities ran in parallel — she tracked the analytical thread while her eyes and ears did the work her body had been trained for since before Jaina's grandparents were born. The forest was wrong. She'd registered it within the first hundred meters and had been cataloguing the wrongness ever since: no birdsong, no animal movement in the underbrush, no insects. The canopy was intact, the leaves still turning amber and copper, but beneath the visual normality the forest was empty. Every living thing that could leave had left. The silence was the kind that followed a predator, not a plague.

Jaina stopped mid-sentence. She knelt, pressed her palm flat against the road surface, and her expression changed — the analytical fluency replaced by something sharper, more focused, the look of someone who'd found something unexpected buried in familiar terrain.

"Stop."

Sylvanas's hand went to her bow. "What."

"The ground. There's a resonance. Faint — I almost missed it under the ward bleed from the Pass." She pressed harder, fingers splaying against the dirt, and a faint shimmer of arcane light rippled across the back of her hand. "It's not natural, and it's not the wards."

"What kind of resonance."

"Entropic. It's drawing energy from the soil. Not much, barely perceptible, but it's been doing it for weeks." She lifted her hand and looked north along the road. "That's why there are no animals. Everything alive moved away from it."

"Everything alive."

Jaina met her eyes, and for the first time since the border, the analytical distance between them collapsed into something that was simply one person telling another person a hard truth.

"Yes. Everything that could move, moved."

They covered the last kilometer in silence that neither of them chose to break. The road worsened — deeper ruts, weeds growing between the tracks, the signs of a route that hadn't seen regular traffic in weeks. The air thickened. A weight to it, a density that sat in the lungs and made each breath feel marginally less productive than the last. A child's doll lay in the mud at the road's edge, one button eye missing, its cloth body stained and waterlogged. Dropped, and not retrieved. Nobody had come back for it.

Cyndia's signal came from ahead: two sharp bird calls, the code for "objective in sight, no movement." Sylvanas crested the low rise and saw Millhaven.

---

The village was intact. That was the first wrong thing.

Thirty buildings, give or take — stone cottages with thatched roofs, a general store, a blacksmith's forge, an inn with a faded sign Sylvanas couldn't read at this distance. The architecture of a Lordaeron farming community, plain and functional, built for weather rather than aesthetics. No fires, no broken walls, no scorched earth, no siege marks. The doors were open. Not broken open — ajar, left that way, the casual openness of doors that had been passed through and not returned to.

Sylvanas entered in combat formation, Cyndia's scouts flanking wide to cover the approach from the north and east. She moved through the village with her bow drawn, an arrow nocked, checking each building with the systematic thoroughness of a soldier clearing a position she expected to be occupied. The buildings were empty. The village was empty. Everything else remained.

A kitchen: table set for four, bread sliced on a board, butter congealed in its dish, a pot of stew on the cold hearth. The stew had gone grey, a film of mold on its surface. The bread was harder than bread should be after a week, softer than bread that had sat for a month. The smell was stale food and cold ash and something else, underneath, that Sylvanas couldn't name and didn't want to investigate with her nose alone. Four chairs surrounded the table. One was pushed back from its place, angled as if someone had stood up quickly. The other three were tucked in. One person had moved. The other three hadn't.

She checked the bedroom. The beds were made. A child's shoes sat beside the smaller bed, neatly paired. Whoever had lived here had eaten dinner, put their children to sleep, and vanished between one ordinary evening and the next.

Jaina moved through the village with a different kind of focus. She carried a leather satchel that she opened in the village square, producing reagent vials, a scrying mirror no larger than her palm, and a set of crystalline focusing elements that she arranged on the ground with the quick, practiced economy of someone who had done this fieldwork before. She took samples — a fistful of grain from an open sack inside the general store, water from the well, soil from a garden bed, a scraping from the cold stew. Each sample went into a separate vial. Each vial was sealed, labeled, and placed in the array.

Sylvanas left her to it and read the village.

The doors, open: a departure, not a flight. No bags packed, no valuables taken, no sign that anyone had planned to leave. The cold forge: the blacksmith had banked his fire for the night and never lit it again. The general store: grain sacks open, some spilled on the floor, but no looting — the spill was careless, not desperate. The church, small, white-painted, its door standing wide: the altar candle had burned down to nothing, wax pooled across the altar cloth in a frozen spill that mapped the hours since someone had lit it and no one had come back to tend it.

She found the tracks at the north end of the village.

Not footprints. Drag marks — parallel grooves in the mud, dozens of them, leading from the village's northern edge toward the tree line two hundred meters away. The grooves were evenly spaced, uniform in depth and width, heading in the same direction with the kind of regularity that bodies fleeing in panic never produced. This was orderly. This was organized. Whatever had left these marks had moved in formation.

Cyndia appeared at her shoulder, looked at the tracks, and said nothing. The silence was professional. A soldier didn't need to narrate the obvious when the obvious was lying in the mud in front of them.

A shout from the south road — one of Cyndia's scouts, signaling contact.

---

The survivors were a married couple, Lordaeron humans in farm clothes, stumbling north along the road Sylvanas's party had just traveled. The woman was thin in the way that comes from days of walking without stopping to eat properly, her face drawn, her hands trembling even after Cyndia wrapped a blanket around her shoulders. The man sat beside her and stared at the ground. His hands rested in his lap, curled loosely around nothing, and he hadn't spoken.

"He stopped talking the night we left," the woman said. She held a cup of water Cyndia had given her and hadn't drunk from it. "He hasn't said a word since."

Sylvanas sat across from them at the village inn's front table — the closest sheltered space to the south road — and let Jaina take the lead. The mage had a way of asking questions that was direct without being aggressive, precise without being cold, the manner of someone who understood that a traumatized witness needed structure more than sympathy.

"The grain," Jaina said. "Tell me about the grain."

"Came same as always. Merchant from Andorhal, same one who's been running the route since my husband's father's time. Two weeks before the sickness, maybe a bit more. Same sacks, same seals, same flour." The woman's voice was steady in the way that exhaustion makes things steady — not calm, but too tired for the emotion the words deserved. "My husband didn't trust new bread. He's always been particular about flour, takes it from Hillsbrad instead. So we didn't eat it. But everyone else did."

"The sickness. Describe the progression."

"Three days." The woman looked at her cup. "First the fever. Then their skin went grey, like old meat left too long. Then they stopped talking." She swallowed. "Not like they couldn't talk — like they weren't in there anymore. Like the person behind the face had gone somewhere and left the body standing."

"And then?"

"They walked out. Middle of the night, all at the same time. Every person who'd eaten the bread stood up from wherever they were — their beds, their chairs, the floor — and walked north, into the forest. Like someone rang a bell only they could hear. The rest of us tried to stop them. Eadric, the blacksmith, he grabbed his wife's arm and she threw him across the room." The woman's hands shook. "She was a weaver. Half his size. She threw him like he was made of straw."

Sylvanas glanced at the drag marks visible through the inn's open door. "How many."

"All of them. Everyone who ate the bread. Thirty families, near enough."

"And when they came back."

The woman's breath caught. Her husband's hands tightened in his lap, the first movement he'd made since sitting down.

"They weren't walking right. Too smooth, too quiet, like the joints didn't work the same way anymore. And they were collecting the rest of us." Her voice dropped. "The ones who hadn't eaten the bread. They knew. They knew exactly who had and who hadn't. My husband pulled me out the back window and we ran. We ran and we didn't stop."

Jaina's hands were still on the table, but Sylvanas could see the tension in them — the fingers pressed flat against the wood, the knuckles white. The mage was doing arithmetic. Sylvanas watched her do it and arrived at the same number by a different route.

"Andorhal distributes grain to the standard route network," Jaina said, and her voice had changed, the analytical fluency stripped back to something rawer, something that held the shape of a woman who had just understood the size of what she was looking at. "If the contamination has been active for two weeks and the distribution reaches the full network... this is hundreds of villages. Minimum."

"Cyndia." Sylvanas stood. "You're going back to the Pass. Run. I want Lor'themar to have this report before dawn."

"And the village?"

"The village is a crime scene, not a village. Nobody enters it until we understand what walked out of it."

---

Jaina composed her sending to Antonidas in the village square, her staff grounded, her eyes closed, the arcane words running from her lips in a murmur that Sylvanas didn't interrupt. The sending would reach Dalaran within the hour. Whether it would produce action within the week was another question, and one Sylvanas couldn't answer by standing over a mage waiting for her to finish.

She followed the drag marks north.

The hunter's walk was the first thing the Farstriders taught, and it lived in her body now the way breathing did — weight forward on the balls of the feet, each step placed rather than landed, the bow at half-draw, the eyes tracking movement while the ears tracked sound. The drag marks led through underbrush that had been trampled by dozens of bodies moving in the same direction, branches broken at uniform height, the forest floor compressed into a path that shouldn't have existed a month ago. The spacing of the marks was regular. Column formation. Military discipline, applied to bodies that had been farmers and weavers and blacksmiths a fortnight before.

Three hundred meters in, Sylvanas stopped.

The forest ahead was the same forest — the same birch, the same oak, the same autumn canopy — but the light was wrong. Thinner, greyer, as though the air itself had been diluted. The color leached from the leaves in a gradient she could track: amber to ash to something that wasn't a color at all, an absence, the visual equivalent of the silence she'd been walking through since the Pass. The entropic field Jaina had identified on the road was concentrated here, and for the first time Sylvanas could feel it without arcane training — a cold that had nothing to do with temperature pressing against her exposed skin, a pressure on her chest that wasn't wind and wasn't weight but sat somewhere between the two, the sensation of standing too close to something vast and patient and fundamentally wrong.

She held her position. The drag marks continued into the grey.

A footstep behind her — not a ranger's step, but quiet enough that the person who'd made it had been trying. Jaina arrived at the tree line with her staff in hand and her sending completed, and the change in her expression when she crossed the field's threshold told Sylvanas everything the mage's analytical vocabulary would have taken three sentences to communicate. Jaina felt it. Jaina felt it worse.

"The field is exponentially stronger here." Jaina's voice was low, controlled, pitched for proximity rather than distance. "Whatever's generating it is close. Hundreds of meters, not miles."

"I'm pushing forward."

"Into an unknown magical field of this intensity? Without understanding what it does to living tissue at high concentration?" Jaina shook her head. "That's not reconnaissance. That's exposure."

"Reconnaissance is accepting exposure in exchange for intelligence. The tracks—"

Movement. In the tree line, between the grey trunks, something shifted. Slow. Deliberate. The patient adjustment of something that had been standing still and had decided to stop.

Sylvanas drew to full, the motion so fast it existed outside conscious decision, her fingers finding the string and the arrow already nocked from her walk through the village. Jaina raised her staff. The crystal at its tip brightened.

They were standing in rows.

The villagers of Millhaven, arranged in the forest in a grid. Motionless, grey-skinned, their eyes open and fixed on nothing — the flat, vacant stare of faces that had been emptied and not refilled. They stood with military spacing, evenly distributed across the forest floor, shoulders square, arms at their sides. Not the huddled scatter of plague victims or the chaos of a massacre's aftermath. A formation. Someone had arranged them this way, and they'd been standing here, in the cold, in the grey, waiting.

The closest one turned its head.

Slowly. The movement was mechanical, too smooth, the head rotating on the neck with the precision of something that didn't need to consider where to look before looking. Its eyes found Sylvanas. They were human eyes in a human face, and there was nothing behind them — no recognition, no aggression, no fear. The mouth opened, and what came out was not a voice. A low, rattling exhalation, air pushed through lungs that still functioned as mechanisms while everything that had made them worth functioning had departed. The sound carried no meaning. It was the sound of a body announcing that it was still running.

The others began to turn.

---

"Back," Sylvanas said. "Now."

She released. The arrow took the nearest body in the chest, and it staggered backward a step — not from pain, from force — and then continued forward. The shaft jutting from its sternum didn't slow it. It didn't bleed.

Jaina's staff carved an arc of white-blue light, and an arcane barrier snapped into existence across the tree line, a shimmering wall of compressed energy that hummed like a plucked wire. The undead reached it and pressed. No charge, no rush, no battle cry. They walked forward until their bodies met the barrier and then they continued walking, leaning into it with the mindless, patient pressure of water against a dam.

The barrier held. Then it didn't.

Two of them came through — not breaking the barrier but passing through it, the arcane energy parting around their bodies the way it would part around something that existed on a frequency the barrier hadn't been built to stop. Jaina's shields were designed for magical threats, for combat spells and offensive wards, and whatever animated these bodies was something else, something the arcane architecture of a Kirin Tor barrier treated as ambient rather than hostile.

Sylvanas put an arrow through the first one's eye. It dropped. The second she hit in the chest, and it kept coming, crawling forward on its hands when the shaft through its ribs made walking impractical, dragging itself toward her with the mechanical persistence of something that didn't understand the concept of stopping. Her second arrow found its skull, and it went still.

"The barrier won't hold them," Jaina said, and her voice was tight but controlled, the voice of someone who had just watched her primary defense fail and was already building the next one. She planted her staff, dropped to one knee, pressed both palms flat against the ground, and the air temperature plunged.

Ice erupted from the soil in a wave that raced toward the tree line, not a wall but a field — a spreading sheet of frost that locked the undead in place at the ankles, freezing the mud around their feet into solid ground that gripped and held. The ones that had been pressing the barrier stopped. They tugged, mechanically, their bodies still trying to move forward while their legs refused to come free. The ones further back were caught mid-stride, locked in position like figures in a grotesque tableau.

Jaina stood. Her hands were shaking — a fine tremor that ran from her fingers to her wrists, and frost was climbing her knuckles, her own ice spell bleeding backward along the channels of her magic in a way that said the entropic field was pulling at her power, eating it as fast as she could channel it. She'd burned through more energy in sixty seconds than most mages spent in a day, and the field was taking a tax on top of the expenditure.

"Move," Sylvanas said, and Jaina moved, and they retreated through the village without speaking, past the open doors and the cold stew and the blacksmith's dead forge, past the inn where the survivors sat wrapped in blankets, past the cart tracks and the child's doll and the ruts in the road that led south toward the border and safety.

They ran.

Not a rout — Sylvanas didn't rout. A controlled withdrawal, covering Jaina and the survivors, her bow ready, her ears tracking the forest behind them for the sound of ice cracking and bodies pulling free. The sound didn't come. The ice held, for now.

Jaina recovered as they moved, her breath evening out over the first kilometer, her stride steadying, her hands stuffed in her pockets where Sylvanas couldn't see whether they'd stopped shaking. She was quiet for half the distance back to the border, and when she spoke, her voice had the flat precision of someone who'd finished being frightened and started being furious.

"The formation. The grid spacing. The coordinated turning when they detected us." She was walking fast, matching Sylvanas's pace, her eyes fixed on the road ahead. "That isn't pathological behavior. It's command behavior."

"You're telling me someone is giving orders to dead villagers."

"I'm telling you the plague isn't the weapon." Jaina looked at her, and the blue eyes held something that Sylvanas recognized because she saw it in her own officers after their first real engagement — the cold, clear fury of someone who has just understood the shape of the enemy. "The plague is the recruitment."

---

They reached the Pass after dark, the ward-stones humming their deep subsonic welcome, the garrison fires visible through the trees like a promise of something that still made sense. The survivors were given blankets, food, a fire, and the careful attention of soldiers who knew not to ask questions the answers to which they weren't ready to hear. The wife drank water for the first time since the inn. The husband sat beside her and stared at the flames and held his hands in his lap, still curled around nothing.

Jaina sent a second message to Antonidas — longer, more detailed, the full scope of what they'd found. Sylvanas sat across the garrison's main table from her and listened to the mage compose the sending in murmured Thalassian, the words precise and rapid and weighted with the kind of urgency that academic language wasn't designed to carry but was being forced to convey.

The response, when it came, was careful. Antonidas believed her. He would bring it to the Council of Six. He would push for immediate deployment.

"How long," Sylvanas said.

"The Six doesn't have a fast setting. Days, if the political winds cooperate." Jaina's voice was tired, and the tiredness was honest in a way that the analytical composure hadn't been. "Weeks if they don't."

"In weeks, every village between here and Andorhal will look like Millhaven."

"I know."

Sylvanas left the garrison table and found the desk Cyndia's patrol used for reports. She wrote two letters. The first was a formal intelligence brief for Silvermoon Command: the threat confirmed, the contamination vector identified, the scale assessed, the recommendation unambiguous. She wrote it in the clipped, precise register the Convocation expected from its military officers, and she made sure every sentence landed with the weight of a fist on a table.

The second letter was for Lor'themar alone, and she wrote it in the looser hand she used for the man rather than the rank. She was going south with the mage. She was extending the mission unilaterally. She knew the Convocation wouldn't approve. She needed him to keep the southern patrols running, handle whatever political dispatches arrived in her absence, and not ask questions about where she'd gone or why, because the answers would take longer to explain than the situation had time for.

She sealed both letters and gave them to the garrison runner. The wax cooled under her thumb, and the weight of it surprised her — not the wax, but everything the seal committed her to. She'd crossed past the boundary of the assignment into territory she hadn't been ordered to enter and wouldn't be thanked for finding.

---

She found Jaina at the ward-stones again, kneeling in the same position she'd held that morning, palms flat on the ancient surface, but running a different analysis. Not diagnostic this time — comparative, mapping the entropic signature against the ward network, using Silvermoon's wards as a stable baseline to triangulate the contamination's spread and direction. She'd been working since they returned, despite the tremor in her hands, despite the exhaustion that showed in the set of her shoulders and the deliberate way she moved, each gesture rationed.

Sylvanas stood beside her and said nothing for a moment, watching the faint arcane light pulse across the ward-stone's surface in patterns she couldn't read but that Jaina's fingers followed the way a cartographer's fingers followed a coastline.

"The map is worse than I projected," Jaina said, without looking up. "The contamination extends at least three villages beyond Millhaven. The signal strengthens to the southwest."

"Andorhal."

"Andorhal." Jaina pulled her hands from the stone and sat back on her heels, and the weariness in the gesture made her look her age for the first time since Sylvanas had met her — young, too young for what she'd seen today, too young for the arithmetic she'd done in a dead village with stew going grey on the table. Then the weariness passed, and what replaced it was harder, clearer, the expression of someone who had looked at the numbers and decided that being too young was a problem she didn't have time to solve. "The signature isn't decaying. It's strengthening. Whatever we found at Millhaven isn't the end of something. It's the beginning."

Sylvanas looked south. The forest was dark, the road invisible beneath the tree cover, Lordaeron's autumn night settling over the landscape with the kind of cold that came from the season and the kind of cold that came from something else, something that lived in the soil and the silence and the drag marks of a hundred empty bodies walking north in perfect formation.

"The road to Andorhal," she said. "How fast can you be ready to travel?"

Jaina looked at her. The analytical distance was gone. What remained was something Sylvanas recognized from her own officers — the clear-eyed assessment of a colleague rather than a stranger, a person being weighed not by rank or reputation but by what they'd done in the last twelve hours and whether the person doing the weighing wanted them at their side for the next twelve.

"You're not going back to Silvermoon."

"My assignment is to escort you during your investigation. Your investigation just expanded."

"The Convocation—"

"The Convocation wanted me to hold your hand while you sniffed at sick farmers. That's not what this is." Sylvanas held the mage's gaze, and the words came without heat, without performance, flat and final. "That hasn't been what this is since we crossed the tree line."

Jaina looked at her a moment longer. Then she stood, brushed the mud from her knees, and picked up her staff.

"Dawn," she said. "I'll be ready at dawn."

The ward-stones hummed between them, and beyond the ward-stones the darkness of Lordaeron waited, and somewhere south of where they stood — past the dead villages and the silent roads and the grain that turned farmers into soldiers — the source of the contamination was still working, still distributing, still recruiting. And it was moving faster than they were.
