# Chapter 1: The Mortifying Ordeal

The thing about being very good at your job was that people kept giving you more of it.

Draco Malfoy sat in his cubicle on the fourth floor of the Ministry of Magic, boots on the desk, chair tipped back at an angle that would have given his mother palpitations, and watched Tonks work her way down the aisle. Monthly assignments. The parade of human misery, packaged in Manila folders and distributed to those unlucky enough to have proven themselves competent. It was, in Draco's estimation, the single greatest argument against professional ambition he'd ever encountered.

Tonks had a system. She started at the far end, with the junior Aurors who got the dull work: stakeouts, missing persons, low-level jinx-breakers who'd turned their talents toward petty extortion. The folders were thin. The juniors were grateful. Then she moved to the middle ranks, and the folders got thicker, and the gratitude got thinner, and by the time she reached the senior desks the whole exercise had the air of a sentencing hearing.

Fernsby received a folder the size of a phonebook and went a shade of green that clashed memorably with his robes. Proudfoot was given something involving necromancy in the Hebrides and looked like she'd been asked to holiday there. Savage got vampires in Yorkshire and actually laughed, which told Draco everything he needed to know about Savage's home life.

The Lanark Lunatic. That was why Tonks was saving him for last. He'd resolved the Lanark business in nine days — the previous record was six weeks — and the reward for efficient work was, as always, more work. He could see it in the way she held the last two folders, one in each hand, weighing them like a greengrocer with two dubious melons.

She arrived at his cubicle. Set both folders on his desk. Smiled the smile of someone delivering bad news with professional relish.

"Two options," she said. "First is a series of inappropriate acts with trolls in the Wye Valley. Thirteen incidents over three months, escalating in severity and, I'm told, creativity." She tapped the thicker folder. "Second is a Ministerial protection assignment. Long-term, low action, high tedium. Shacklebolt's personal request."

Draco picked up the troll folder and opened it.

The photograph on the first page was — he closed the folder. Set it down. Pushed it three inches away from himself with two fingers, the way one might handle a dead animal found behind the sofa.

"I'll take the protection assignment."

"Wise." Tonks collected the troll folder and tucked it under her arm with a satisfaction that bordered on indecent. "I'll let Fernsby know the good news."

From three cubicles down, a sound like a man being slowly deflated.

Draco opened the remaining folder. Shacklebolt's seal, which meant budget, which meant someone important enough for the Minister to stick his considerable neck out. The first pages were standard: security audit, warding assessment, escorting as needed, ongoing surveillance at the discretion of the assigned Auror. Comprehensive. Thorough. The kind of assignment that would eat his calendar for months and leave him bored enough to start alphabetizing his sock drawer.

He flipped to the Principal's page.

The photograph blinked at him once, seemed to form an opinion, and walked out of the frame.

Hermione. Bloody. Granger.

He sat with that for approximately four seconds, which was three seconds longer than he'd have liked, then stood up and walked to Tonks's office with the controlled stride of a man who was absolutely not rattled and would appreciate it if his legs would stop moving quite so fast.

"We have a history," he said, standing in her doorway. "A rather unhappy one."

Tonks was arranging folders in a filing cabinet that appeared to be arranged by some system known only to her and possibly God. "Do you."

"She and I — during the war — there were incidents."

"Incidents." Tonks closed the cabinet drawer. It stuck. She kicked it. It closed. "You mean you called her a slur for six years and she punched you in the face and you spent most of seventh year trying not to get everyone killed and failing."

"I was going to phrase it more diplomatically."

"Were you." She turned. Her eyes had gone that hawkish yellow they went when she was done being patient, which was, in Draco's experience, roughly ninety seconds after any conversation began. "Malfoy. You're the best I've got, and Shacklebolt asked for the best. If you'd prefer, I can swap you with Fernsby. He's just opened his folder, and from the sounds coming from his cubicle, he'd welcome any alternative up to and including death."

"I don't have a complex about Granger."

"Good." Her eyes stayed yellow. "Because I won't have your complex about Granger domino its way through the entirety of my department. Take the assignment. Do the work. Be professional." She looked at the door. The door swung shut, squeezing him into the corridor.

The corridor felt emptier than before. The Foe-Glass on Tonks's wall had three silhouettes pressed against it, leaning in, transparent faces alight with interest. One of them appeared to be grinning.

Back at his desk. Past Fernsby's cubicle, where the sounds had evolved from deflation to something closer to existential crisis. He paused. Considered the swap. Heard another strangled gurgle from within.

Fine. He'd do the Granger thing. It was, at any rate, not troll pornography.

---

He composed a letter that evening. Professional, pleasant, the tone of a man who harboured no resentment whatsoever about childhood incidents that he had largely instigated and bore full responsibility for, a position he had reached through extensive therapy and several strongly worded conversations with his mother.

_Dear Dr Granger,_ he wrote, because he'd done his research and she had more letters after her name than the alphabet had to spare, _I have been assigned as your protection detail by the Minister for Magic. I would appreciate an opportunity to meet at your earliest convenience to discuss the parameters of this assignment._

Signed. Sealed. Sent. He opened a bottle of wine.

Her reply arrived the following morning. The handwriting was small, precise, and slanted slightly to the right in a way that suggested decades of taking notes faster than anyone should. The tone was — well, it was typey. That was the word. Typey for Granger.

_Mr Malfoy. The Minister is overreacting. I have told him so. Kindly disregard the assignment and enjoy your afternoon._

He enjoyed his afternoon. He did not tell Tonks.

Three days later, a second letter arrived. The handwriting was the same. The tone had shifted from dismissive to resigned.

_Mr Malfoy. The Minister has not budged. Would you be available Thursday? Nine o'clock. The Granger Laboratory. Trinity College, Cambridge._

He tossed the letter into the fire and watched it curl. Cambridge. Of course. How could anyone expect anything less from Hermione Granger than Cambridge bloody University, the most painfully appropriate place she could have landed short of building her own tower and filling it with books. 

He finished his wine. Stared at the fire. Went to bed and did not think about Thursday, or Granger, or the photograph that had taken one look at him and left.

---

Thursday. Cambridge.

He Apparated to a lane behind King's Parade and walked the rest. October, but mild enough — the sun was pale and thin behind a scrim of cloud, the kind of English light that couldn't commit to being either grey or golden. Students in gowns crossed the lawns, which helped; robes drew no second looks here. The architecture was old in a way that didn't need to announce itself, stone and mullioned glass and ivy that had been climbing the same walls since before his family had arrived on the island, which was saying something.

Trinity College's porter didn't glance twice at his robes. Academics wore gowns. But when Draco said the name "Granger," the man's expression shifted, corrective rather than hostile.

"Doctor Granger?"

"Doctor Granger," Draco repeated, with the conviction of a man who had been about to say it all along.

The porter waved him through. "Great Court, through to Nevile's Court, left past the Wren Library. We call them courts at Cambridge." A pause. "Not quadrangles."

"Wouldn't dream of it."

He crossed the court. Ancient flagstone, immaculate lawn, a fountain that sounded like it had been running since the Reformation and had no plans to stop. Students everywhere, carrying stacks of books and the expression of people who believed very strongly that what they were doing mattered. He turned a corner, then another, following the porter's directions into an older part of the college where the stone was darker and the tourists thinned out.

The plaque was small. Bronze, green with age, mounted on a wall that appeared to be solid stone: KING'S HALL — FOUNDED 1317. He drew his wand, tapped the plaque twice, and the stone rippled like water.

A building materialised. Medieval, narrow, the kind of architecture that was all vertical ambition and no horizontal sense: turrets, mullioned windows, buttresses that served no structural purpose he could identify. Gargoyles along the roofline, one of which turned its head to follow him. He gave it two out of ten on his security assessment. The entrance was visible from the Muggle side if you knew where to look, the concealment charm was standard Ministry-issue with no personal modifications, and the gargoyle was about as threatening as a garden ornament. At least Muggles couldn't find her. That was one thing.

Inside was warmer, darker, and cluttered with the particular chaos of academic magic. Scholars in robes brushed past him, carrying things that glowed, things that steamed, and in one case a birdcage containing something that was definitely not a bird. The ceiling was dark carved oak, the walls lined with portraits that whispered as he passed, and the staircases, there were five of them, branching in directions that seemed to change depending on which one you looked at.

He chose wrong. Twice. The third staircase deposited him in a corridor lined with doors, each bearing a brass nameplate. He found a student, young, spots, the expression of someone who had been awake for forty hours and was operating on a substance that probably wasn't tea.

"I'm looking for Granger."

The student blinked. "Professor Granger?"

Professor. He filed that. Doctor, Professor, the woman collected titles the way his father had collected Dark artefacts. "Professor Granger."

"Third floor. End of the corridor. Past the small zoo."

The small zoo was, in fact, a small zoo. Terrariums, cages, a tank of something luminescent that pulsed in rhythm with the building's wards. Draco walked past it, turned right, and found the door.

GRANGER. RING FOR ATTENTION.

Huh, I guess she's not all that. Draco though.

The door opened. A figure in a white coat stood in the gap, translucent face-coverings, goggles pushed up on the forehead, the flat expression of someone interrupted mid-task and unhappy about it.

"Healer Granger doesn't take walk-ins."

Or maybe she is. His security assessment dropped to one out of ten: the door was answered by a junior researcher with no apparent screening protocol, no identification request, no wand check. Any lunatic could ring and be let in.

"I'm from the Ministry. Auror Malfoy."

The researcher looked at him. Looked at his badge. Looked at him again with the squinting assessment of someone trying to determine if he was worth the interruption. Then the door opened wider.

He stepped through.

The office was smaller than he'd expected. Bookshelves on three walls, floor to ceiling, the kind that sag in the middle from years of bearing too much weight. A desk that appeared to have been buried in paperwork sometime around the turn of the century and had given up hope of rescue. Two chairs, one of which was occupied.

Granger looked up.

Fifteen years compressed into the distance between a desk and a chair. Six years of school, a war, a trial where she'd spoken in his defence for reasons he still couldn't fully account for, and then nothing. Not a word, not a letter, not a nod across a crowded Ministry atrium. Fifteen years of nothing, and here she was, behind a desk in Cambridge, looking at him as though he were a form she wasn't sure she needed to fill in.

"Malfoy."

"Granger."

Equal eloquence. Neither moved. The room held its breath, or he did, someone was holding something, and it might have been the researcher in the white coat, who was standing behind him and clearly wondering whether to intervene.

She looked different. She would, fifteen years was fifteen years, but the specifics caught him. The hair was the same impossible mass, but she'd pinned it up, coiled at the crown of her head, and the architecture of it suggested either significant magic or significant patience. Her face was thinner, the lines of it sharper, the baby fat of school long gone. She was wearing a white lab coat over black jeans, so Muggle, so thoroughly, deliberately Muggle, and trainers that had seen better days. She sat with one leg crossed over the other, and her knee bounced once, a quick restless motion she stilled the moment she caught herself doing it.

She was cataloguing him too. He could feel it: the eyes moving from his hair (shorter, no longer slicked back, a concession to age and common sense) to his face (harder, he knew, the jawline more pronounced) to the Auror insignia on his chest to the cut of his robes to his boots. Neither of them was subtle about it. The mutual assessment had the flavour of two predators circling a watering hole, each waiting for the other to drink first.

"This is an absolute waste of Auror resources." No preamble, no pleasantries. She uncrossed her legs and leaned forward, elbows on the desk. "I've told the Minister. I've told him twice. He is overreacting, and you are the physical manifestation of that overreaction."

"Charming." Draco sat in the empty chair without being invited. The cushion was flat. The armrests were worn to the wood grain. This chair had absorbed a great many uncomfortable meetings and was resigned to absorbing more. "Give me a bit more to go on and I can make a case to Shacklebolt to withdraw. I'd rather not be here either."

Something flickered behind her eyes, surprise, maybe, that he wasn't fighting. She straightened. "It's a research project. Not under Ministry purview. Not Ministry-funded. I shared some — progress — with Kingsley, as a friend, and he decided the progress warranted protection. I've told him repeatedly that it doesn't."

"What kind of progress?"

"The kind that has implications for a certain segment of the population."

"Which segment?"

"I'm not at liberty to discuss that."

"Granger."

"Malfoy."

They stared at each other. Somewhere in the building, the luminescent tank pulsed, and the light coming through the window changed colour briefly, blue, then back to grey. The researcher in the white coat had vanished at some point, closing the door quietly behind them, which was either discretion or self-preservation.

"Isn't it possible," Draco said, settling deeper into the terrible chair, "that Shacklebolt is right? That the work you're doing has attracted the kind of attention that warrants a security presence?"

"If I told you I'd solved world famine, would you pause to worry about a few naysayers?"

"One naysayer would be enough to dispatch a do-gooding researcher," he said. "Especially one who keeps her laboratory secured with a third-rate locking charm and some chicken wire."

Her knee bounced. She stilled it again. A cat-tail twitch, involuntary, the body betraying what the face would not.

"Here's what I propose," he said. "A penetration test. I attempt to breach your security. If I fail, we take the results to Shacklebolt and make the case that you don't need me. If I succeed, you accept the protection detail without further complaint."

Her eyes hardened. Challenge recognised, assessed, and accepted in the space between one heartbeat and the next. "When will this penetration test take place?"

"I'm not giving you a warning."

"I do love surprises."

They did not shake hands. She did not see him out. He found his own way back through the five staircases, past the small zoo, past the portraits that whispered, and out into the Cambridge light, which had committed to grey in his absence and looked better for it.

---

Shacklebolt's office smelled of woodsmoke and old paper. The fire was low, the curtains half-drawn, and the Minister himself was standing at the window with the posture of a man carrying more on his shoulders than the robes accounted for. He turned when Draco entered and gestured to a chair. Draco didn't sit.

"Infuriating woman," Draco said.

"She is that."

"She wouldn't tell me what she's working on. Will you?"

"I cannot." The word was careful. Not won't. Cannot. There was a distinction, and they both heard it.

"Vow of Secrecy?"

Shacklebolt raised his hands, palms up. The universal gesture of a man who could not speak and wanted you to understand the precise nature of that inability. Answer given.

"The work is important?" Draco said.

"She could change the lives of hundreds. Thousands, perhaps, for the better." Shacklebolt moved to his desk and sat down, the chair groaning under him. "And she is completely, characteristically incapable of recognising her own vulnerability. She sees the research. She does not see the woman carrying it."

"I see."

"I know you won't hesitate to get nasty, Malfoy, if any malicious individuals were to make a move against her." The backhanded compliment landed with the precision of a man who had survived twenty years in politics by knowing exactly how to phrase a threat as a favour. "That is why I requested you specifically."

Draco inclined his head. A mock bow, a quarter-inch of deference that acknowledged the compliment and refused to be flattered by it. "I'll need full access to her security infrastructure. Warding schematics, entry logs, personnel files for anyone with laboratory access."

"You'll get nothing." Shacklebolt's smile was tired. "She's locked it all down. You'll have to earn it."

"I'm very good at earning things."

"I know. That's the other reason I requested you."

Draco took a pinch of Floo powder from the pot on the mantel and tossed it into the fire. The flames turned green, roaring up.

"Cambridge."

He stepped through. He'd given her long enough.

---

Monday. Late. King's Hall was quiet, the corridors dark, the portraits dozing, the small zoo giving off a faint bioluminescent glow that cast moving patterns on the walls. Draco stood at the laboratory door and drew his wand.

Before he could cast a single spell, footsteps. Quick, coming from the staircase behind him. He turned.

Granger rounded the corner at speed, out of breath, her hair escaping its coil in three places. The white lab coat was gone, replaced by Healer robes, the formal kind, midnight blue with silver clasps, which meant she'd come from somewhere that required credentials. Her wand was in her hand. Her cheeks were flushed.

"Malfoy."

"Granger."

He filed her timely arrival. Too clever for coincidence: she'd known the moment he entered the building, or close to it. But he'd detected nothing. No alarm had sounded, no ward had triggered, nothing magical had brushed against him on his way in. Which meant her perimeter security was either much better than he'd assessed or she had a source of information he couldn't identify.

"Don't mind me," she said, and drew her wand in a short, practiced motion. The air shimmered. A hairpin materialised in her off hand, and she Transfigured it, a flick, a turn, two syllables of Latin, into a glossy wooden chair with carved legs and a padded seat. She set it against the wall, sat in it, crossed her legs, and looked at him with the expression of someone settling in to watch something entertaining.

"By all means."

He didn't mind an audience. Especially one he was about to systematically dismantle.

The door wards were layered. He started from the outside and worked in, reading the magical signatures the way a locksmith reads tumblers: pressure, direction, resonance. The outermost layer was a Caterwauling Charm, the kind you'd cast on a garden shed to discourage teenagers. Insulting. He dismantled it in four seconds.

Basic intruder alarms beneath. Salvio Hexia, solid but standard. Foribus Ignis, which would have set the door on fire if he'd tried brute force, clever, that one, because most wizards defaulted to brute force. He traced the Ignis lattice with his wand tip and found the seam where it joined the Salvio. Separated them. Peeled the Ignis away like a label from a jar.

Custos Portae. A hair-trigger Confundus that would have left him standing in the corridor babbling about soup for an hour. A Blinding Hex, keyed to the door handle, decidedly mean. Beneath the Blinding Hex, a Balding Jinx, which had no tactical value whatsoever and could only have been placed there out of spite. Unsportsmanlike.

And concealed behind all of it, tucked into the door handle itself like a viper in a glove, a Confringo.

He paused. The Confringo was beautiful work, seamlessly woven into the handle's magical signature, invisible to standard detection. If he'd simply grabbed the handle after removing the surface wards, his hand would be — not there any more. He traced the enchantment's edges, found the release thread, and pulled. Carefully. Sweat beaded at his temples. His fingers didn't shake, but they thought about it.

The Confringo unravelled. The door clicked. Four minutes, twenty-three seconds.

Sweat cooled on his forehead. He wiped it with the back of his hand and turned to Granger with a look that he hoped communicated professional satisfaction and not the relief of a man who still had all his fingers.

"At least there's a Healer nearby," he said. "In case my face gets blown off."

"I would have put you back together." She paused. "Mostly."

The door swung open.

Stone. A solid wall of grey stone, flush with the doorframe, not a gap or a crack in it. Draco stared at it. Pressed his palm to the surface. Cold, solid, real. Three detection spells. Nothing. It was stone. It was just stone.

Behind him, Granger uncrossed her legs.

"An impeccable decoy," she said, with the tone of someone narrating a nature documentary. "The real door is a metre down the corridor. Behind a Finite Incantatem that any of my undergraduate staff can manage."

He didn't turn around. Not immediately. He let the information settle: the four minutes of meticulous work, the sweat, the Confringo that could have killed him, all of it spent on a door that went nowhere. She'd built an elaborate puzzle box and put nothing inside it.

"I needed my staff able to get in," she added, and there was something in her voice that might have been an apology and was more likely amusement.

His neck stiffened. He turned. She was smiling, a small thing, controlled, but her eyes were bright with it. The real door was, as advertised, one metre to the left. He opened it with Finite Incantatem, feeling like a man who had picked the world's most complicated lock only to find the house had a cat flap.

The laboratory was another country. Sterile white, fluorescent lighting that hummed at a frequency just below conscious hearing, steel surfaces that reflected his face back at him pale and slightly distorted. The smell was chemical, cleaning solutions, something astringent, something organic beneath it. She'd built a Muggle research facility inside a sixteenth-century wizarding building, and the collision of aesthetics was profoundly disorienting.

He searched methodically. Shelves first, organised, labelled, everything in its place. Muggle literature lined one wall: journals, textbooks, ring binders stuffed with printouts. Words without meaning jumped out at him: cytokines, monoclonal antibodies, chimeric antigen receptors, T-cells. He waved his wand at everything. No magical traces, no hidden caches, no concealment charms. Nothing useful unless you held twelve doctorates, which, given the woman standing behind him, was a plausible scenario.

"Gamma delta T cells," Granger said. She'd followed him in and was leaning against a counter, arms loosely folded. "Antigens: MART-1, Tyrosinase, GP100, Survivin. All of magical provenance, but not otherwise noteworthy. Very few people in the world would know what to do with what's in this room."

"I see," said Draco. He did not see at all. The words were English, technically, but they'd been arranged in a configuration that his education, extensive, expensive, and entirely wizarding, had not prepared him for. By "very few people," she meant herself.

The computers.

The machines were easy to miss. They occupied the far corner of the laboratory, glowing screens, blinking lights, cables snaking across the floor to a humming box that radiated a low heat. His eyes had slid past them on the first sweep, and he'd circled back to the potions stores and the reference shelves, because his instincts were trained on magical signatures, and computers had none. She might as well have cast a Notice-Me-Not; his own pureblood habits did the work for her.

Something shifted in Granger's posture when he walked toward them. The loosely folded arms tightened, just a fraction, and her chin lifted.

Getting somewhere.

"Computers," Draco said, pulling up a distant memory from Muggle Studies — the class he'd taken because the alternative was Divination, and he'd refused to sit in a room that smelled of sherry and listen to a woman predict his death.

"Well done," Granger said, with the tone one takes to praise an especially slow child who has correctly identified a barn animal. "Would you like a sticker?"

The screens told him nothing. Lines of text, graphs, columns of numbers that could have been anything, star charts or stock prices or the complete works of Shakespeare for all he could determine. Not the faintest sodding clue where to go from here. His wand was useless. His training was useless. The machines sat there glowing, placid and impenetrable, and he understood, with a clarity that bordered on physical pain, that she had built her security around the one thing no pureblood Auror could breach.

"I'd need to bring in a Muggle-born."

"Or," she said, "a hacker."

"A hacker."

The word offered no self-explanation. It sounded violent, which was appropriate, given the circumstances.

"What's to stop me from destroying the lot?"

"It's all in the cloud."

"The cloud."

"Yes."

"The — cloud."

Stymied. Completely, thoroughly stymied by Muggle infrastructure. The wards had been an elaborate puzzle, entertaining, challenging, ultimately decorative. She'd known there was nothing behind them worth protecting. The real work lived in machines he couldn't read, stored in a cloud he couldn't reach, written in a language he didn't speak. She'd wanted to see if he was as good as they said. The answer was yes, and it didn't matter.

The corner of her mouth twitched. She was enjoying this. He could see it, the satisfaction of a woman who had spent four minutes watching the best Auror in Britain disarm a door that opened onto nothing.

"So," Draco said, and his voice was steady, because he was a professional and a Malfoy and both of those things meant something even when you'd just been thoroughly humiliated by a Muggle-born with a hairpin. "No evidence of concealment. No written findings. Computers. Clouds." He turned from the machines and faced her. "If I'm a would-be attacker who needs the information in this laboratory, what do I do?"

Granger looked at him. The amusement was still there, but something else had appeared behind it, a flicker of attention, an alertness she hadn't shown before.

"Hypothetically," she said.

"Hypothetically."

"You can't break the wards. You can't breach the computers. You can't find the research in any physical form."

"No."

"Then what's left?"

He raised his wand. Her eyes widened, just a fraction, just enough, and the spell hit her in the chest.
