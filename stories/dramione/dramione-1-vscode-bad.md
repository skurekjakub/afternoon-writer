# Chapter One: The Mortifying Ordeal

The Auror Office at Monday morning was the same colour as regret — greying cubicles in a greying light, the smell of cold coffee and damp robes and low-grade institutional despair. Draco had worked here for eleven years. He was, he felt, well-placed to identify the fragrance.

He'd chosen Auror work deliberately, the way he chose everything after the war: strategically, with an eye to optics, and with a private acknowledgment of which options would keep him sane. The legal proceedings had taken two years. The slow rebuilding of the Malfoy name had taken a year more. The eventual realization that politics would bore him into an early grave had taken approximately three weeks in a ministerial anteroom watching Shacklebolt sign things while Draco sat very still and thought about whether this was what the rest of his life was going to look like. He needed fieldwork. He needed to be somewhere crimes were still being committed, preferably in his vicinity, and he needed a job that would produce just enough heroics for positive coverage and just enough Ministry-sanctioned license to get very unpleasant when the occasion demanded. Auror work ticked all three boxes. It was, he admitted privately and to no one, also the only remaining profession at which he was genuinely very good.

The year after the war there'd been people who took exception to his presence in the training programme. He'd outlasted them.

He had, in the twelve years since, apprehended a ring of Dark artifact smugglers operating out of Knockturn Alley; disrupted a Lethifold-trafficking operation with connections to four countries; and shut down the Lanark Lunatic — a particularly productive month, that one, which had earned him twelve days of paperwork and a commendation Tonks had presented with the air of someone swallowing something unpleasant. He'd also, in that same period, been stabbed twice with non-magical implements (undignified), Splinched once, partially (harrowing), and bitten by a Blast-Ended Skrewt (unacceptable on every level, and he attributed it to faulty intelligence rather than his own decision-making, which was a position he maintained and had never examined). The commendations were in a drawer somewhere. The scars were on his forearm and left calf, which was worse, as drawers went.

His cubicle was in the corner. He'd fought for it — not openly, there was nothing so gauche as open office politics, not at his level — but the previous occupant, Wickham, had found himself reassigned to the Orkney Islands in January and Draco had moved his things in before the desk was cold. The corner had natural light from two directions. It also had a sight-line directly to Tonks' office door.

He noticed the door open at half eight and drank the rest of his coffee.

---

Tonks' grip on the assignment stack was the sort that suggested she had developed opinions about its contents. She moved through the cubicles with the purposeful air of someone distributing small disasters, and the office had learned to track her progress the way prey tracks a predator across open ground — much peripheral attention, no direct eye contact.

"Ashton." A folder dropped. The hag coven murders. Ashton was good at hag covens.

"Pemberton." Necromancy in Shropshire — unregistered zombie, probably just some second-year who'd got hold of a library book they shouldn't have. Pemberton brightened.

"Finch. Yorkshire vampires." Finch muttered something about it always being Yorkshire and received no sympathy from anyone.

"Goldsmith." Transmogrifian Torture, Northumberland. Goldsmith accepted with the calm of a man who had long since made his peace with the knowledge that his career would be unpleasant.

Draco turned back to his reading.

Tonks stopped at his desk.

He'd known she would. She always saved him for last, which was a compliment of sorts — the cases she held for last were the awkward ones, the ones that needed someone she trusted to be unpleasant about them in a professional capacity. The compliment was implicit and never acknowledged. This was the correct nature of their working relationship, and they both understood it.

She set two folders on his desk. Neither landed with the comfortable thud of something manageable.

"Two options," said Tonks, "due to your efficient handling of the Lanark situation." She said *efficient* the way one says *unfortunately*. "Take your pick."

He opened the first folder.

Then he closed it.

He had, in twelve years, examined crime scenes that would have turned a lesser Auror grey. He had catalogued horrors in the patient methodical way that the work required and done it without flinching because flinching was impractical and the first time had been a very long while ago. He had never, in all of that, closed a folder this quickly.

"What," he said, "is Transmogrifian Torture, compared to this."

"The other one is Ministerial protection," said Tonks.

He opened the second folder.

The assignment was substantial — full security audit, warding consultation, escort protocols, ongoing surveillance. Shacklebolt had apparently written *everything* at the top of the brief and underlined it, which Draco found irritating in the way that only administrative enthusiasm can irritate someone who preferred their cases to involve fieldwork and no reports afterwards. He flipped past the brief with the gathering impatience of a man who had requested a simple case and received instead a curriculum. He didn't mind work. He minded bureaucratized work disguised as fieldwork. He minded the escort dimension particularly, which was, he felt, a role for people who hadn't made better use of their time at Hogwarts.

He reached the Principal's page.

The photograph blinked at him once. Then, registering his expression — or perhaps just possessing reasonable instincts for self-preservation — it left the frame entirely.

Draco set the folder down on his desk very carefully. Centred it. Square to the edges.

"No," he said.

"That's a feeling, not a position."

"I have," he said, with considerable precision, "a rather unhappy history with the Principal in question."

"I'm aware."

"We have —" He stopped. Restarted. "It would be more appropriate to assign someone else."

Tonks' eyes had gone hawkish yellow. He hadn't seen that happen before, or perhaps he had and it had meant something different. "I won't have your complex about Granger domino its way through the entirety of it," she said, and the door of her office opened and then closed, smoothly, in the manner of a door performing an act it had been trained to perform at precisely this moment. He found himself on the other side of it with no clear recollection of having moved.

---

He stood in the corridor for a moment.

Across the office, from inside Fernsby's cubicle, came a sustained gurgling horror that had no clear endpoint. The troll folder lay open across the partition wall. Fernsby's visible hand had the particular stillness of someone experiencing something they had not consented to.

Draco looked at the hand.

He looked back at the folder still tucked under his arm.

He walked back to his cubicle, sat down, and opened the protection file to the Principal's page again.

Fine. He'd do the Granger thing. It was, at any rate, not troll pornography. It was, he could acknowledge, a rather low bar to clear, but it was clearing it nonetheless, and he had cleared lower.

---

He wrote the note that afternoon.

*Malfoy, D. (Auror, Level Seven, Specialist Operations) to H.J. Granger — re: Ministerial Protection Detail, Case No. 7731-B. Per the Ministry's request, I'd like to arrange a preliminary assessment at your earliest convenience. I'm available Tuesday through Thursday of this week. Please advise.*

He read it over. Professionally pleasant. Conveyed its information cleanly. The tone was, he felt, calibrated correctly — formal without warmth, which was the appropriate register for corresponding with someone he had spent the previous fifteen years actively not thinking about.

Her reply arrived the following morning.

*The Minister is overreacting. Please disregard the request. — H.G.*

He spent the afternoon in his flat with a good whisky and a detective novel.

Her second letter came Thursday.

*The Minister has, apparently, not changed his position. Would you be available on Thursday next? Nine o'clock. My laboratory. Trinity College, Cambridge.*

Draco held the letter over the fireplace for a moment.

"Cambridge," he said to the empty sitting room. "Of course." He thought about it. "How could we expect anything less from Hermione Granger."

He dropped it into the fire and went to find his travel robes.

---

Trinity College in October morning was the sort of place with opinions about itself — old stone and deliberate greenery and archways placed just so, the quiet institutional confidence of somewhere that knew exactly what it was and had known for longer than most of the surrounding city had been standing. Hogwarts had something similar. He preferred Hogwarts. Hogwarts didn't make him feel like it was assessing him back.

The porter didn't glance twice at his robes. Academics wore gowns; eccentricity was occupational at places like this.

He said Granger's name.

"Doctor Granger," said the porter, without looking up from his newspaper. "Third court, second staircase, follow the signs to the Cavendish Annexe, then the corridor marked King's Hall. Mind the step."

He noted the correction. He said nothing.

The court — and a clipboard-bearing postdoc crossing his path at the second turning informed him they were called *courts* at Cambridge, not quads, that was Oxford — opened onto a quad of considerable charm, which he refused to acknowledge. He navigated it via instinct and ended up approximately where he'd intended. This he counted a victory.

The King's Hall plaque was set into a section of wall between a painted bicycle and a cheerful poster advertising a public lecture series on quantum field theory. He tapped it with his wand. There was a sound like stones settling, and the seam of the door appeared between the plaque and the bicycle, and when it opened the smell that came out was candle wax and old mineral stone and the specific dusty signature of magical concealment laid in careful iterations over a long period. Someone had been maintaining this for decades. The layers smelled like work.

Draco stepped through and stood for a moment in the corridor.

He began running the assessment.

The concealment from the Muggle side was functional and the depth of the magical layers was respectable, but the entry point was findable. A trained nose and ten minutes of patience and a clear Thursday morning — he'd located it in eight minutes, and he wasn't trying particularly hard. Two points to the debit column. He moved them and kept walking.

Inside, the stone corridor widened into a hall from another century: dark carved wood, candlelight in iron brackets, the worn flags of a floor that had been walked by several generations of people convinced of their own significance. Scholarly witches and wizards moved through it with the preoccupied air of people thinking about something considerably more interesting than their current location. One of them walked directly into a standing lectern and apologised to it.

He navigated five staircases.

At the top of the fifth he emerged onto a landing he was reasonably certain he'd been on before, approached from a different direction.

"Excuse me," he said to a spotty young man carrying a stack of parchment that was threatening to fold him double.

"Yeah?"

"I'm looking for Granger's office."

"*Professor* Granger." The young man pointed upward with the authority of someone who had delivered this correction many times. "Third floor. Follow the smell of formaldehyde."

Draco climbed.

---

The third floor had offices and communal rooms and, behind a door marked *Please confirm no fauna before entering*, something that sounded like a conversation between several entities of differing opinion. He passed this door at a pace he would not describe as hurried.

The formaldehyde smell was real and navigable.

The door at the far end of the corridor read:

*GRANGER*
*Ring for attention*

He noted the absence of the accumulated titles, and filed that under something — something he didn't immediately categorize. He rang.

The door was opened by a Being in a white laboratory coat, translucent face coverings, and goggles that enlarged the Bearer's eyes to an unsettling degree. The Being consulted a clipboard without looking at him.

"Healer Granger doesn't take walk-ins," said the Being.

He closed his eyes. Opened them. "Malfoy. From the Ministry. I have an appointment."

"Mm." A mark on the clipboard. "She's in the lab. I'll let her know." The door closed.

He stood in the corridor.

He revised his security assessment to one out of ten, which was as low as he went on the scale.

---

Granger's office smelled like woodsmoke and coffee and the faint metallic underlayer of spell-work recently cast. It was smaller than he'd expected and denser — not disordered, nothing that Granger arranged was disordered, but layered with the particular precision of someone who knew exactly where every object was and regarded any implication to the contrary as a personal insult. Books floor to ceiling, the shelves packed double in places. A desk buried in papers he would have laid very confident odds were filed in a system legible only to their owner. A window catching the flat grey October light.

She was standing by it.

They looked at each other.

Fifteen years sat between them in the middle distance — the war, the trials, school, the six years before the war that neither of them had been able to use the way they might have, the three years during it that neither of them talked about. Fifteen years of accumulated not-spoken-to compressed into the floor space between the desk and the door, a piece of furniture no one had bothered to move.

"Malfoy," said Hermione Granger.

"Granger," said Draco Malfoy.

A moment of equal eloquence stretched out and lay down between them.

She catalogued him: Auror insignia on the left breast, silver pip indicating the level, robes that were neither flashy nor cheap, the hair shorter than she'd probably last seen it and a shade or two greyer at the temple. Her gaze moved across him with the thoroughness of a professional inventory, and he let it pass, because he'd done the same and it would be hypocritical to object. The curling mass of her hair was pinned up at the crown with what appeared to be a quill pushed through the knot, dark copper at the temples where the morning light caught it, escaping in two or three places in the way of something that had given up on being controlled. The face was thinner than he remembered — sharper-cornered, less of the soft certainty she'd had at seventeen. White lab coat over black jeans, so Muggle he could hear the automatic commentary in his own head and chose not to examine its source. Chalk or something pale-powdered across the toe of the left trainer.

He sat down in the chair across from her desk. She sat down opposite.

"This," she said, without preamble, "is an absolute waste of Auror resources."

"Good morning, Granger."

"I told the Minister. I'm telling you. Whoever conducted the threat assessment doesn't understand what they're looking at. I don't need a minder. I particularly don't need a minder turning up in my laboratory to reassure himself that I'm not doing anything interesting, when I very clearly am."

"Give me a bit more to go on," said Draco, settling back in the chair in the deliberate way of someone who intended to be comfortable regardless of his reception, "and I can make a case to Shacklebolt to withdraw." He paused. "That would, I imagine, be the preferred outcome for both of us."

The challenge hit something in her expression — she'd been expecting the argument and hadn't been expecting the offer. A small recalibration.

"It's a research project," she said, after a moment. "Not under Ministry purview. Not Ministry-funded. I shared some preliminary results — positive results — with the Minister in a personal capacity, as a friend, and he appears to have decided this warrants the full apparatus of Specialist Operations descending on my laboratory."

"Which is me," said Draco.

"Which is, mercifully, only you." She folded her hands on the desk. "The project has implications for a certain segment of the population. The nature of which I'm not disclosing."

"That's comprehensive."

"It's necessary."

He let the silence run for a moment. Her knee, he noticed — involuntarily, the way you notice things when you've spent a decade training yourself to notice movement — had started bouncing under the desk's lip, the barely-visible oscillation of someone exercising considerable restraint.

"Isn't it possible," he said, "that Shacklebolt's concern is legitimate? If your research has the kind of implications you're describing —"

"If I told you I'd solved world famine," she said flatly, "would you pause to worry about a few naysayers?"

"One naysayer would be sufficient," said Draco, "to dispatch a do-gooding researcher. Particularly one who keeps her laboratory secured with a third-rate locking charm and some chicken wire."

The knee stopped. The stillness was a different quality of tension — she'd been struck somewhere accurate and she knew it, which meant she already knew the security was insufficient, which meant she'd made a calculation about acceptable risk, which meant there was a reason she hadn't improved it. He filed that.

"I have significantly better security than that," she said.

"I haven't seen it."

"You won't," she said, "until you're specifically looking for it."

Draco looked at her. There was, he noticed without quite intending to, something almost clean about this — the intelligence across the desk operating at full capacity, no longer carrying the muffled quality of a clever teenager who hadn't finished becoming herself. He'd spent fifteen years not thinking about her specifically enough that he'd apparently forgotten what she looked like when she had.

He didn't examine this.

"Penetration test," he said.

The look she gave him was instantaneous and immovable.

"I come back," he said, steadily, "unannounced. Test your security. If it holds — or fails in ways that are contained — I establish what you need added, I put it in writing for Shacklebolt, and we make the case together that the current risk level doesn't warrant an ongoing detail." He watched her listen. "If it doesn't hold, we have a different conversation."

The hardness in her eyes had cooled into something more calculated — the specific look of someone who has recognized a chess move and is assessing the board behind it.

"When will this penetration test take place?"

"I'm not giving you a warning."

The corner of her mouth moved. "I do love surprises."

They did not shake hands. She did not see him out.

---

The Minister's office later that week was warm in the fire-and-dark-wood way of offices that have been occupied for a long time by people with things to think about. Shacklebolt received him across a desk that was, Draco noted with the part of his attention that catalogued irrelevancies, approximately twice the size of Granger's. He had never decided whether this said more about the Minister or about what the Ministry considered worth accommodating.

"She told you very little," said Shacklebolt, which was not a question.

"She told me it's a research project with implications for a segment of the population she declined to name." Draco paused. "She also told me the security is better than it looks. I plan to verify that."

"I see."

"What can you tell me?"

The Minister looked at him for a long moment. His expression was the considered kind — not evasive, exactly, but weighted. "She could change the lives of hundreds of people for the better," he said. "Possibly thousands."

Draco waited for the rest.

The rest did not arrive.

"Did she make you take a Vow of Secrecy?" he said.

Shacklebolt raised both hands, palm out, and held very still and said nothing.

"Right," said Draco.

"I know you won't hesitate," said the Minister, "to get nasty, Malfoy, if any malicious individuals were to make a move against her."

"You've always been generous with your assessments of my character."

"I prefer to think of it as deploying the right tool for the right job."

Draco executed a fractional mock-bow, the minimum necessary for plausible deniability as a gesture of deference, and reached for the Floo powder on the mantelpiece. He tossed it into the fire and said *Cambridge* and stepped through.

He'd given her long enough.

---

King's Hall at ten past nine on a Monday evening was a different creature — the scholarly bustle compressed down to the occasional distant footstep, candlelight lower in the brackets, the stone corridors running quiet in the particular way of old buildings that are used to patience. His own footsteps sounded purposeful in a space that wasn't expecting purposeful.

He stood at the laboratory door.

He hadn't drawn his wand yet. He always looked first — mapped the visible architecture before the invisible one, noted the static details that might read differently in context. The door was unremarkable: wood, iron handle, the plaque still reading *GRANGER. Ring for attention*. The ring-pull was on a slightly different angle than he remembered. This meant nothing necessarily. He noted it anyway and began to read.

The Caterwauling Charm surfaced first. He dismantled it with the practised contempt he reserved for wards that were technically functional and utterly predictable — the kind a clever third-year would lay if a clever third-year had access to the right textbook. "Insulting," he said quietly, to no one, and removed it cleanly.

A basic intruder alarm followed it. Less insulting, more professional — the sort wired to a Foe-Glas somewhere in the building. He took it apart carefully, leaving nothing behind that would ping as recently disturbed. Salvio Hexia came next, dense and properly layered, three weeks' work at minimum. He gave it the respect it deserved. Then Foribus Ignis — unusual on a laboratory door, sensible in context — and Custos Portae, which was standard.

The Confundus was hair-triggered and he nearly walked into it.

He stepped back. Breathed. Approached it from a lower angle, changed his grip on the wand, came at the trigger mechanism from the side instead of the front. Two minutes that felt like considerably more. When it came apart he held the dissipated charm in his mind for a moment, mapping the construction so he could replicate the technique later if he needed to, then set it down.

The Blinding Hex was, he decided, deliberately mean. Positioned to catch someone who'd lowered their guard after the Confundus, which was either very good instinct or very good instruction. Possibly both.

The Balding Jinx, knee-height, calibrated for someone who'd been crouching: unsportsmanlike. He said so. There was no one to hear it, which did not diminish the accuracy of the assessment.

The Confringo on the handle itself he nearly missed altogether.

It was concealed behind the Balding Jinx — behind it, folded into the gap between the ward's termination point and the iron of the handle — and it was not the decorative kind. It was the kind that removed faces from people who weren't careful about their hands. He found the root of it by accident, felt the thermal signature under his palm, and went very still for perhaps three seconds before beginning to carefully, carefully unravel it. When the last thread of the charm came apart, his hands were warmer than they should have been and he was aware of a thing he didn't normally allow himself to be aware of during fieldwork, which was the proximity of his own face to something that had wanted to remove it.

He straightened. Rolled his neck. Looked at the door.

Four minutes.

He put his hand on the iron handle and pushed.

—

The stone wall behind the door was very smooth and very old and did not move.

He stood there.

From somewhere in the corridor behind him — to his left, perhaps a metre — came a sound that was not quite a laugh and was also not not a laugh. A very small, very contained sound, in the register of someone exercising a degree of self-control that impressed him in spite of himself.

He turned.

She was perched on a chair that had no business being a chair — he could see it was a hairpin in every line but the shape, the Transfiguration clean enough to hold but too recent to lose the tension in the wood grain. Her Healer robes were the ones for actual use rather than display, slightly rumpled, the collar still settling. Her hair had come down around the crown. She was slightly out of breath, in the way of someone who had been walking fast for some time and had recently stopped.

He noted: too convenient for coincidence. He'd detected nothing that should have told her he was moving. He stored these two facts in the same mental folder and did not close it.

"The real door," she said pleasantly, "is a metre down the corridor." She nodded to indicate direction. "Behind a Finite that my undergraduate staff can manage, since they need access to the laboratory."

Draco looked at the stone wall. Looked at the door he had spent four minutes and a Confringo's worth of sweat on.

"The Confringo," he said.

"Was on the decoy," she said. "I thought it seemed only fair, since it was the most interesting ward in the set." A pause. "Genuinely, I was curious if you'd find it."

His neck had gone rigid. He chose not to reach for it.

He walked to the real door. The Finite on it dissolved in three seconds. He walked through without looking back.

---

The laboratory was everything the office wasn't, and his instincts objected to it on a level he recognized and chose to ignore. White and steel and glass, the antiseptic chill of Muggle medical infrastructure, fluorescent light instead of candles. Too bright, too flat, too stripped of anything that suggested the space had absorbed personality over time. He did not like it and he understood that this was his problem.

He moved through it methodically. Shelves of glass-stoppered bottles with her handwriting across the labels: the close particular script from six years of shared classrooms, unchanged. Cupboards of equipment he couldn't name and wasn't wasting time trying to. Stacked Muggle literature in binders along the east wall, the spines dense with terminology from another discipline entirely — *Immunotherapy Review*, *Nature Methods*, *Cell* — words that fell through his comprehension without catching. He waved his wand at everything. No Dark traces. No magical constructs embedded in the surfaces. No hidden caches. The laboratory was clean with the particular cleanliness of someone who knew exactly which things needed to not be findable.

He stopped at one shelf.

She'd followed him in, silent in the manner of someone who'd learned to observe from the edges before moving to the centre. He didn't turn.

"Gamma delta T cells," he said. The labels were specific: *MART-1. Tyrosinase. GP100. Survivin.* Below, in her hand: *Magically-derived. Batch three.* "Antigens. They're of magical provenance." He turned then. She was standing in the interior doorway, her expression technically neutral. "But not otherwise noteworthy."

"Very few people," she said, "would identify those as magical in origin from a label."

"I see," said Draco.

He did not see. He hadn't the faintest idea what any of it meant and his taxonomy of magical antigen research was effectively non-existent, so he had identified the words *magical provenance* and worked from there, which was not the same thing as understanding. The modesty in *very few people* was, he could hear, load-bearing. She meant no one currently alive.

He moved toward the back wall.

He nearly didn't.

The near-miss was invisible to him in the moment — he registered it only as a thought that had drifted off toward the centre of the room instead of the corner, a priority-triage his attention had performed automatically, logging the bench as equipment-storage and moving on. Fifteen years of Auror instinct, and his own deep-grained assumptions about which categories of objects were capable of posing a threat, had collaborated without consulting him. The Notice-Me-Not she hadn't cast had been cast anyway, by twenty-seven years of being pureblood-raised and three OWLs in Muggle Studies that he had not followed up.

Three large computers on a low bench. Glowing screens, graphs and text in fonts too small to read from here, a row of small lights indicating something operational in progress. He walked toward them.

Behind him, he heard her attention shift — a minuscule adjustment, the intake of breath of someone who has been watching for a specific development and has just seen it arrive.

She'd wanted to know if he'd get here. She'd been waiting.

"Computers," said Draco, surfacing the distant memory from Muggle Studies alongside the near-certainty that whatever he said next would be wrong.

"Well done," said Hermione Granger, in the tone one uses with a slow child who has looked at a cow and correctly identified it as large.

He looked at the screens. Then he looked at them more carefully, in case looking carefully helped. It did not help. The screens continued to display information at him in the complete confidence that he did not have the tools to do anything with it.

"I'd need to bring in a Muggle-born," he said.

"Or a hacker."

"The term requires —"

"Someone who breaks into computers professionally, or for sport," she said. "They exist. There are quite a lot of them."

He looked at the machines for a moment longer. The wards on the decoy door had been interesting: a genuinely well-constructed problem, layers he'd had to think about, the Confringo a nicely unsporting touch. But that — that had been a puzzle he had the tools for. This was a lock made for a different kind of key and he didn't own the key and she had known he wouldn't, and she had built her security around that knowledge, which was either very clever or very arrogant, and the two were not mutually exclusive.

"What's to stop me from destroying the lot?" he said.

She looked at him with the patience of someone who has anticipated this question and prepared for it. "It's all in the cloud."

He waited.

"Remote storage," she said. "External servers. The hardware is access, not repository. Destroying those machines doesn't touch the data."

He stood with this for a moment.

The wards were a puzzle she'd been comfortable letting him solve, because there was nothing behind them she couldn't afford for him to find. The computers were a puzzle she'd known he couldn't solve, because the tools to solve them weren't in his formation. She hadn't secured her research in the laboratory at all. She'd secured it in infrastructure he didn't have a framework for, in a way that was invisible to him not through magic but through assumption. She'd wanted to know if he was as good as they said. He was good enough to find the corner. Not good enough to know what to do with it.

He turned.

"No concealment," he said, evenly. "Nothing in writing I can access. Computers, clouds." He looked at her across the steel bench. "If I'm a threat to you — someone who needs what you know — what do I do next?"

She met his eyes. He watched the calculation happen: recognition, then a second calculation about whether to answer. Her chin tilted, very slightly. Inquiring. Interested in the way of someone watching a chess board reach the square they'd been anticipating.

"I go after you," he said.

He raised his wand.

The spell caught her in the chest and she stumbled back two steps and her hand came up automatically, reflexively, already moving toward her own wand — and then the shield hit the wall behind her and she stopped, and the light faded, and she was looking at him with something in her face that wasn't quite what he'd expected.

He lowered his wand.

"The Protego Totalum in your office doorframe," he said, "is good work. From outside the room, I couldn't have that conversation with you safely. You'd need to be in my custody or I'd need a clear approach." He let that sit for a moment. "You're right that the laboratory wards are a distraction. You're wrong that the distraction is sufficient."

Granger straightened her robes. Her expression had settled into something careful and assessing, the same particular quality it had held across the chess board at eleven years old except that she had since had decades to refine the capacity to conceal what she was thinking behind it.

"I'm not wrong," she said. "I'm incomplete."

"That's a distinction that would be very interesting to your next of kin."

She said nothing for a moment.

"Thursday," she said. "Come back Thursday. I'll have something to show you."

He picked up his outer robe from where he'd slung it over the steel shelving.

"I'm not giving you a warning," he said.

"You just did."

He left her standing in the white laboratory with the computers glowing behind her and the overhead light catching the particular angle of her face and his neck still very slightly rigid, which was something he chose not to think about on the walk back to the Floo.
