# White Room Syndrome & Sensory Grounding in AI Fiction

## Summary
"White room syndrome" describes scenes where characters interact in a featureless void — no setting, no sensory detail, no environmental friction. AI-generated prose is especially prone to this because LLMs generate dialogue and action as abstract token sequences rather than embodied physical events.

## Core Problem: AI Prose Defaults to Abstraction

### The Symbol Grounding Problem
LLMs learn statistical relationships between words but have never experienced physical reality. They know "apple" relates to "red" and "sweet" but have never bitten into one. This produces prose that is:
- Syntactically correct but physically unmoored
- Full of generic atmosphere words ("tension filled the room") instead of concrete sensory detail
- Weighted toward visual description, neglecting touch, smell, sound, taste, temperature
- Prone to describing psychological states abstractly instead of through physical manifestation

### Six Deadly Sins of AI Prose (from grounding primer)
1. **Rhythm Destruction (Bloat)**: AI adds detail by appending clauses, destroying staccato tension
2. **Subtext Spoon-feeding**: AI names emotions explicitly instead of staging them physically
3. **Wiki-Speak**: Characters explain their own world to each other unnaturally
4. **Jargon Fatigue**: Functional verbs replaced with domain-specific metaphors
5. **Preemptive Exposition**: Object details appear before character interacts with them
6. **Melodramatic Tropes**: Austere reality replaced with hyperbolic genre clichés

## Grounding Approaches That Work

### 1. The 1:1 Replacement Rule
Ground text by SWAPPING generic nouns for specific ones without adding syllables:
- "sword" → "Tyrell-corp blaster" (keeps rhythm, adds world)
- "chalk" → "alchemical chalk" (minimal expansion, maximum specificity)
- "floor" → "flagstone" (one word, completely changes the material reality)

This is the most AI-safe approach because it prevents bloat while adding specificity.

### 2. The Contact Rule
Material details emerge ONLY at the moment a character interacts with an object:
- Don't describe forge-marks until the sword is drawn
- Don't describe the crack in the glaze until the cup is touched
- Don't describe the desk's wood grain until the character sits at it

This prevents preemptive exposition (Sin #6) and keeps grounding in motion.

### 3. POV-Filtered Specificity
Details enter through the POV character's expertise:
- A soldier notices defensive positions, weapon condition, terrain
- A mage notices arcane materials, ward-lines, chalk residue
- A merchant notices trade goods, material quality, pricing
- A noble notices social signals, heraldry, protocol violations

This prevents generic "world-building spray" and keeps grounding characterful.

### 4. Sensory Rotation
Prevent visual-only grounding by rotating through senses:
- **Touch**: stone temperature, leather texture, metal cold, paper grain
- **Sound**: distant bells, footsteps on specific flooring, wind direction, voices through walls
- **Smell**: preservative chemicals, cooking smoke, horse sweat, lake-damp, old paper
- **Temperature**: lake-cold through boot-soles, forge-warm, evening chill from an open window
- **Taste**: metallic adrenaline, chalk dust, last meal residue

Goal: at least 3 different senses per scene, with touch and temperature most under-used by AI.

### 5. The Palimpsest Rule (Wear-and-Tear)
Environments and objects show history:
- Armor has field repairs, not pristine finish
- Stone has worn grooves from generations of foot traffic
- Books have cracked spines, folded corners, annotation in different hands
- Buildings have repurposed fittings from previous occupants

This grounds the world in time, preventing the "museum exhibit" feel where everything is new.

### 6. Institutional Friction
When institutions collide, their material cultures clash:
- Different uniform standards
- Incompatible paperwork
- Conflicting protocols for the same situation
- Different terminology for the same rank or procedure

This generates grounding detail organically from worldbuilding.

### 7. Temporal/Ecological Anchoring
Replace generic time and weather with world-specific systems:
- "Morning" → "second bell" or "grey-hour" or the specific liturgical hour
- "Trees" → named species typical to the region
- "Rain" → the specific quality of rain in this biome at this season
- "Wind" → wind carrying specific smells from a specific direction

## Grounding Checklist (Scene-Level)

After grounding a scene, verify:
1. [ ] Can the reader sketch the room/setting from memory within 5 seconds?
2. [ ] Can they point to the exit, the obstacle, and the working surface?
3. [ ] Does at least one object keep doing work after being introduced (not just mentioned and forgotten)?
4. [ ] Is the POV character thinking about the live problem rather than narrating from outside?
5. [ ] Are details entering through character interaction, not narrator exposition?
6. [ ] At least 3 different senses represented across the scene?
7. [ ] Environment shows wear, history, or institutional specificity?
8. [ ] Every proper noun sourced from materials, not invented?
9. [ ] Grounding density roughly even across the scene (not front-loaded)?
10. [ ] High-tension passages preserved at original rhythm (1:1 swaps only, no expansion)?
