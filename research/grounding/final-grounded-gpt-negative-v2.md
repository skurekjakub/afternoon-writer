# Chapter 12: The Necromancer

Kel'Thuzad's sanctum sat at the bottom of Caer Darrow's old crypt spine.

Jaina had expected vulgarity. A throne. Black tapers. Some parody of sorcery she could brace against. The room offered shelves instead, crowded with folios and specimen jars, a broad oak desk under a chalk-filmed slate, green grave-fungi washing brass retorts and stoppered glass in drowned light. The air was cold and dry, sharp with preserving salts. Under that sat the sweeter rot that had followed her through Scholomance's lower halls.

Antonidas's hand was on every surface.

His habits, rather. Margin stars. Cross-braced proofs. The little hooked correction mark he used in the Violet Citadel when a student reached the right answer by an ugly route. Jaina had spent half her life arranging thoughts for that red pen. Here those marks covered the deepest room under Scholomance, copied and extended by the man who had taken the same lessons and kept going.

At the threshold, Jandice kept one hand on the sanctum door. Proud. Careful. Her excitement still showed in the angle of her chin.

Kel'Thuzad turned a vellum page, settled a river-stone paperweight on it, and raised his head.

Grey-haired, gaunt, scholar-thin. His robe still carried the bones of Kirin Tor cut beneath cloth dark enough to swallow the old violet trim. In another room he might have been any senior archmage at work.

"Mistress Barov," he said, "you have done enough."

Jandice's fingers tightened on the latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore followed the upper sequence farther than I expected. She caught the false rings at once--"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad said, looking at Jaina at last. Pale eyes. Steady. Interested. "I can see that."

Warmth touched Jandice's face and vanished. She bowed and left.

The door shut with a thick stone sound. The wards under the floor hummed once, a sound that reached the chair through stone before it reached the ear.

Kel'Thuzad folded his hands on the desk. "You have come a long way for the missing step."

Jaina stayed where Jandice had left her, seated across from him at the long table that cut the room in two. A stick of chalk lay near her right hand. White dust marked her thumb.

"I came because the body was doing work my field reads could not finish," she said. "Your grain carries the strongest signature because you want it to. Every site answered there first. Every site stalled there too."

"Good." Plain as breath. "Then begin there."

He rose and crossed to the nearest slate. Chalk clicked once against stone.

"The grain is only the carrier," he said.

He drew a circle, then a second around it. Outer shell. False ring. No need to write the words. The pattern said them.

"You know that much," Jaina said.

"You know more than that much. You know the treated grain survives storage, wagon travel, even oven heat, because the preserving work is simple and brutal. You know repeated ingestion matters more than any single loaf. You know the host is altered before death. What you lacked was the final relation."

Jaina went still. Host. Altered. He used the words like furniture.

Kel'Thuzad wrote a second chain below the first, deeper and tighter, each mark tucked inside the next.

"The outer shell teaches nothing," he said. "It only arrives intact. The lesson begins in the body."

Chalk rasped across the slate. Prepared host. Repeated teaching. The body keeping the lesson. He said it all in the language of wards, bindings, and prepared vessels, and Jaina could follow every step.

"It settles," she said. "Waits. The body carries it farther than the bread can."

His mouth changed. Approval, there and gone.

"Exactly."

Heat climbed the back of her neck. She hated the flicker of pleasure his approval drew from her. Jandice had been fast. Kel'Thuzad was faster.

He tapped the deeper chain with the chalk. "Your field work at the farmhouse was close. The body does not merely suffer contamination. It is taught by it. Repetition lays down an answerable shape. Death does not interrupt the sequence. Death releases it."

Jaina looked at the slate, then at the specimen jars on the shelf beyond him. A child's hand in one. Two preserved eyes in another.

"The body becomes the answering chamber," she said quietly.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head.

The phrase completed too much at once. The villages. The gut-first spread. Why the strongest readings in Andorhal and the Eastweald had never yielded the whole thing.

She stood and crossed to the slate. Cold had settled into her knees during the descent. She took the chalk from the ledge and added a branching line under his.

"The first waking is crude," she said. "Because the body has been taught enough to answer, not enough to remain itself."

"Precisely."

She drew another branch.

"A village fed together dies together," Kel'Thuzad said behind her. "A village that dies together does not rise as a pile of accidents. It rises in relation. Appetite. Direction. Response. Enough to muster. Enough to become useful under a competent hand."

Her hand stayed raised over the slate.

"The Cult acolytes in those villages," she said. "They do not create that from nothing."

"No."

"They catch it."

"And harden it."

She set the chalk down with great care. White dust clung to her fingertips.

"Then the dead in those villages never had a chance," she said.

Kel'Thuzad turned the chalk between his fingers. "The villages had many chances. They baked them."

The flatness of the answer struck harder than outrage. She turned back to him.

"To hear you tell it, routine becomes consent."

"I speak because routine is the strongest force available to any large design. You of all people should respect repetition."

He set the chalk aside and moved back to the desk. The shift from slate to oak changed the room with him. Proof done. Argument beginning.

"There is still a difference," she said. "What rises in a village and what you are doing here."

"At last."

He drew one calfskin folio nearer and opened it. Notes. Diagrams. A lattice of circles bound around a single central mark. Finer work than the village mechanisms. Costlier. Made for one body at a time.

"Your village dead keep their function," he said. "Motion. Appetite. Rough answerability. Their pattern survives in outline because outline is enough for war. The finer work requires finer structure."

Jaina leaned over the page despite herself. Ink lines. Layered binding. Different words, same ideas underneath.

"This is what the villages do not receive."

"This is what the villages cannot afford."

The distinction opened under her feet. Low village dead. Rough answerables. And above them, a second aim.

"Preserved selfhood," she said.

Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened with pleasure. "There. Yes."

She stepped back from the desk.

"That is what you want for yourself."

"Of course."

Mild surprise that the point needed saying aloud.

"You built a machine big enough to eat a kingdom to prove a private theorem."

He sat and steepled his fingers. "I built a kingdom-wide engine because the theorem was true whether anyone liked it or not. My private interest lies in carrying that truth farther than your teachers permitted."

"The Council of Six banned necromancy," she said.

"Yes."

"Because it destroys the line between a body and a person."

"Yes."

"Because it collapses consent."

"Yes."

"Because it turns every household, every healer, every grave into a point of violation."

Approval warmed his face again. "Much better. You have stopped objecting at the level of taste."

Anger hit clean and fast.

"Do not mistake clarity for agreement."

"I have never required agreement from a serious mind."

Jaina put both hands on the table. Cold wood. Smooth from use.

"The fact that the theorem holds," she said, "does not absolve what it does."

"Absolution is a priest's word," he said. "The work needs no absolution. It needs to be named."

"Naming it is not enough when the method consumes whole villages."

"And prohibition is not enough when the method remains sound."

"Your Council never disproved necromancy," he said. "It forbade it. There is a difference. They found the line where valid method touched living society and became intolerable. Persons. Consent. Continuity of self. Civic trust. They were right to fear that line. Fear, however, is not refutation."

Jaina opened her mouth. Nothing useful arrived. The Council had never called the art nonsense. The Council had called it forbidden.

"That is your answer? That because the logic survives the ban, the ban can be ignored?"

"My answer is that the ban names the cost more honestly than the scholars who still invoke it as proof the art itself was impossible."

"You think this makes you brave."

"Bravery is for men about to face the unknown," Kel'Thuzad said. "I did something certain and accepted the result."

"The result is famine, plague, and mothers dragged out of their own kitchens by what used to be their sons."

"Yes."

"You let the word stand where judgment should."

"It is sufficient to name what occurred."

"You built an atrocity and then taught yourself to say it cleanly."

Kel'Thuzad tipped his head a fraction. "And your response has been what, exactly? You crossed borders without sanction, pursued a plague no one had authorized you to pursue, and followed your private analysis into this room because you were certain the answer waited below."

"Do not compare us."

"I have no need to force the comparison. You made it yourself the moment you entered."

Jaina's hands curled at her sides. Chalk dust marked one cuff. Roads, village rooms, grain dust, Sylvanas's blood tacky on her own fingers at the ridge.

"I crossed a border to stop this."

"Yes."

"You crossed one to spread it."

"Direction matters. I have never denied that."

"Then what are you claiming?"

"Only this." He touched the desk once with two fingers, the way a lecturer marked the true line of a problem. "We were both made by the same institution. We were both taught to follow pattern where it led. We both found the world outside our studies too slow for the work at hand."

"You call your trespass duty because the object of your conviction flatters your ethics. I call mine scholarship because it flatters my honesty. The underlying habit is the same."

"You're wrong."

"You left a note in the Violet Citadel and rode north alone because you trusted your reading over orders. Admirable. So did I, once."

"I did not build a machine for stripping personhood out of a kingdom."

"No. You are earlier in the chain."

She turned away from him before she could stop herself. The nearest shelf carried labeled boxes in a careful hand, each marked by subject and date. One had Antonidas's old sigil for translational instability scratched into the corner and crossed out later, replaced by the cleaner notation Jaina had seen in Jandice's logbooks upstairs.

She put her palm against the shelf. Dry wood. Cool. Solid.

"You are wrong."

"About your intentions? I do not doubt them. About your methods? I doubt them less with every minute."

"If I had had another trained mind with me in Andorhal," she said, "the work would have gone faster."

"Would it have gone differently?"

"Yes."

"Because that second mind would have introduced better ethics? Or because it would have forced you to price time, risk, and consequence against your hunger to complete the pattern?"

The questions arrived in a voice that was not in the room. How long. How many. Which door. Who pays if you stay another minute.

When she opened her eyes, Kel'Thuzad was watching with fresh interest.

"There," he said. "Now you are asking the useful question."

"No," Jaina said. "Now I am remembering the human one."

"Human concerns are always invoked at the point where disciplined minds become inconvenient."

"Human concerns are the point."

"To governments. To priests. To frightened committees."

"To anyone who has ever buried someone they loved."

That reached him. The last warmth went out of him.

"Love is an argument from attachment," he said. "Attachment makes poor policy."

"And your policy is to make every body in Lordaeron answerable to an intelligence it never consented to serve."

"Your outrage still lives in ethics rather than method. I understand. It is the one ground on which the old ban can still stand."

"That ban stands because once your work touches actual bodies, the category changes. You stop moving dead flesh. You assault the self. You turn a person into an instrument without their leave."

"Only because you insist on treating the self as sacred at the point where it becomes worth studying."

"Worth studying." The phrase left iron in her mouth. "That is what the Eastweald villages are to you."

"The villages are proof," Kel'Thuzad said.

"Dalaran did not lose its nerve," she said. "It found the place where your method became public butchery and named it correctly."

Kel'Thuzad nodded once, formal enough to insult.

"That is the strongest version of the argument," he said. "Antonidas never put it so cleanly."

"Do not speak of him like you knew him better than I do."

"I knew him before the Violet Citadel trusted you with a staff."

"He taught you the same discipline he taught me. The difference is that he understood the cost, and you chose not to."

Kel'Thuzad studied her. The green fungal light caught the hollow planes of his face. "Perhaps. And perhaps he understood the cost only because he lacked the will to bear it."

The argument had reached its floor. Standing across from him felt too much like taking instruction. Jaina turned and went back to the table. The heavy oak chair scraped harshly against the flagstone when she pulled it, but she sat, letting lake-cold bleed up through the soles of her boots.

Kel'Thuzad let the silence stretch. He did not hurry to follow her. When the quiet had held long enough to make the point, he took his own chair.

He folded one vellum page over another, exposing a new diagram. The structure widened. Multiple runic chains descended across the page, pulling inward from the margins to one black center mark. Jaina stared at the ink. The density of the lattice made her stomach tighten. The thing widened past Andorhal, past the Eastweald, past Lordaeron itself.

"You came for mechanism," he said. "You have it. You came for scale as well, though you did not know that when you entered."

"Say what sits above you," she said.

"Above the work."

He placed one fingertip on the uppermost mark in the chain.

"A will in Northrend," he said. "Bound. Vast. Learned in ways your libraries only gesture toward. I did not derive this design alone, Jaina. No mortal mind in Lordaeron could have derived it alone. The patronage came first. The revisions after."

The word hit first.

"Patronage."

"What else should I call it? I was given a frame larger than Dalaran ever offered me. A mind willing to answer the questions your teachers banned at the point of discomfort. Power equal to the labor required."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the table edge.

"Its name."

He met her eyes.

"The Lich King."

The new name reordered the whole conversation. Patron. Northrend. Voice in the dark.

"King of what," she asked.

"Of the dead, eventually." The smallest shrug. "Titles arrive before they are fully earned."

Jaina stayed still.

"The plague is logistics," she said, more to herself than to him. "Preparation."

"Yes."

"For an invasion."

That earned another degree of warmth. "You are quick when you stop arguing for the room."

"The Scourge is preparation," he said. "The force above it is older and far less patient."

He did not wait for her to ask the second name.

"The Burning Legion."

Jaina sat rigid.

Grain roads. Bakehouses. Scholomance. And behind them, farther north, a named intelligence feeding revisions into the work.

"You expect me to accept that on your word."

"You have accepted harder things on my word in the last hour."

"Do not become careless enough to mistake me for your only source of truth. I offer you scale because scale is already bearing down on your province. Whether your institutions can digest the names in time is another matter."

Antonidas would hear the first name and understand too much. The Council would hear the second and slow to the crawl of classification. Either way, the roads would keep moving grain.

"How much of this design is yours?" she asked.

"Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only honest one available. I built the mortal apparatus. Grain routes. Cult cells. Instruction. Deployment. I refined the host work because mortal scholarship is still useful when properly guided. Some edges belong to higher authorship."

All the villages already buried under that answer.

"It interests anyone trying to stop you."

"Then stop the machine you can touch. Leave cosmology for later."

She pushed back from the table and crossed to the shelves. Green bars from the grave-fungi lay across the floor. At the far wall the stone carried a colder thrum. Old ward lines. Stronger.

"Why tell me any of this?" she asked.

"Because you came this far."

"That is vanity."

"Partly." He did not deny it. "Mostly economy. There is no point withholding names once pressure reaches the threshold where names become unavoidable."

"And if I carry them out?"

"You will carry out a truer understanding of your defeat."

He reached for the last folio on the desk and opened it with care that bordered on reverence. The diagram inside was smaller than the rest. Tighter. Its lines nested toward a single center and held there.

"There remains one distinction you have not fully accepted," he said.

Jaina came back to the desk in spite of herself.

"You mean the difference between your villagers and your own plans."

"Yes."

He touched the central mark.

"The village dead are rough continuities. Useful enough for the field. The self remains only in outline. For them, recurrence is low and cheap."

The word landed in her stomach with a colder force than resurrection would have. Plainer. More worked through.

"For you," she said.

"For me, the preserving structure is made for one mind. Costly. Deliberate. Designed to hold memory, intellect, priority, will."

Jaina looked at the page and understood before she wanted to. He had already priced his own death.

"You think this solves mortality."

"It solves enough of it."

"Enough."

"You still frame this as faith," he said. "It is not. I have no interest in crude village survival for myself. The rough answerables proved what they needed to prove. The finer question was always whether the self could be carried intact through a prepared threshold."

"And you intend to be your own proof."

"Every serious scholar should prefer first-hand verification where possible."

"You made villages into rehearsal for your private continuation."

"The villages proved one branch of the theorem. My own work proves another. Do not collapse scales that differ in purpose because outrage prefers the shorter line."

Jaina looked down at the folio and the understanding came over her in one clean, intolerable sweep.

Carrier. Host. Answering chamber. Low recurrence. High preservation.

For one heartbeat she followed it all the way through. The body taught to hold its ending. The threshold approached and crossed. The self not extinguished, only transferred along a prepared line.

The ward-line under the floor shivered with the chair's blow against Caer Darrow flagstone.

Jaina jerked back from the desk so hard the chair struck behind her. Air hit her lungs too fast. The room swam once, sharply, then steadied.

"Do not," she said.

"I left the inference open," Kel'Thuzad said.

"You did not need to."

Her voice sounded wrong in her own ears. Thin where she wanted steel.

"Understanding is not surrender," he said.

"No." Jaina forced breath into a shape that held. "But it is enough to wound."

She understood the structure. She hated it. The hatred did not erase the understanding.

Jaina made herself sit because her legs had gone unreliable and she refused to let him see more than he already had.

"You said Antonidas lost his nerve," she said.

"I said he stopped where the work offended the room."

"He stopped where it touched bodies."

"Bodies were always where the work was heading."

"They were people."

"Yes." This time impatience flashed clean and brief. "That is exactly what made the question worth asking."

There. At last. His composure slipped and showed appetite beneath.

"That is the line," she said.

"One line."

"It is the only one that matters."

Kel'Thuzad turned one page corner between his fingers. When he spoke again his voice had lost the last warmth entirely.

"You have a partner," he said. "A friction your mind did not produce. I had a Council. They preferred order to inquiry. The outcomes differ."

Jaina did not answer. No one here had cut through elegance to ask what it did in a village with ovens and actual dead.

"Do not romanticize your distinction from me," he said. "Understand it precisely. Intelligence answers whatever hand directs it. Method does the same. What governs either is relation. Attachment. The bonds that still pull at you. I cut mine elsewhere."

Jaina sat with that because refusing too quickly would have been cowardice. Acceptance never entered it. Her mind ran toward pattern by default. Mercy required someone else in the room.

Neither of them spoke. Wards cycled under stone. Beyond the wall, Darrowmere water.

Jaina looked at the unfinished notes near his elbow.

Her own copied pages from the farmhouse lay there too, where he had set them beside his work, her hand in the same stack as his. Oilcloth edges. Her cramped field script. The sight of them in this room made her recoil harder than any specimen jar had.

"You kept those."

Kel'Thuzad followed her gaze. "They were worth keeping."

The compliment fouled the air.

"If I leave here with this," she said, "and speak it exactly, no one in Dalaran will know what to do with the truth."

"Likely."

"Antonidas might."

"Antonidas will understand it. Understanding and action have never been identical."

She thought of the sending stone going thin and blue with distance. Antonidas's voice stripped of everything but urgency. The Council compressing horror into orderly speech. The roads south where grain still moved whether any chamber believed in the names attached to it or not.

She wiped the chalk from her fingers onto her sleeve. White dust against Dalaran blue.

Kel'Thuzad's head turned before hers did. Small motion. Attention lifting.

The wards answered a beat later.

The Caer Darrow flagstone took the disturbance first. A ward-pulse. Then another, farther up the crypt spine. Scholomance's old protections passed the descent downward in order, each threshold yielding cleanly to the next.

Kel'Thuzad stood. "Interesting," he said.

Jaina was already on her feet.

The next ripple came clearer. Flagstone. Stair. Vault turn. Stair again. Whoever was coming moved through hostile ground and kept their pace through it.

Kel'Thuzad looked not at the door but at her. She understood why a moment later. Recognition had crossed her face before she could master it.

"Someone expected?" he asked.

Jaina did not answer.

Another pulse. Closer now. The ward-stones answered one after another to leather boots driving over them without waste. A pause at one choke point. Angle checked. Then movement again, cleaner than before.

Kel'Thuzad's eyes narrowed. "You know the approach."

She did.

That cadence. Light armor crossing flagstone without drag, every blind corner cleared on the draw. Only one mind moved through a hostile building that way.

Above them, a heavy blow struck Barov stone. Then silence. Then the next threshold answered.

Relief nearly folded her knees. Dread came with it at once. Whoever entered next would see only aftermath: the chair knocked back, chalk on her hands, Kel'Thuzad alive and calm at the center of the room.

Kel'Thuzad took one step toward the door and stopped. He was listening in earnest now.

"Remarkable," he said.

The next ripple settled it.

Sylvanas.
