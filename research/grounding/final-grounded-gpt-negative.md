# Chapter 12: The Necromancer

Kel'Thuzad's sanctum was an office at the bottom of a grave.

Caer Darrow sat above it in old Barov weight, all stone and buried privilege, with Darrowmere pressing cold somewhere past the walls. Jaina had expected vulgarity from the last room in that descent. A throne. Black candles. Some theater she could brace against. The sanctum gave her shelves instead, oak darkened by salt, crowded with folios, labeled specimen jars, brass fittings gone green at the seams, a broad mahogany desk under a chalk-filmed slate board. Green fungi washed brass and glass in drowned light. The air was cold and dry, salted hard against rot. Under the salt lay the sweeter reek that had followed her down through Scholomance: preservative spirit, old linen, the faint cooked note of worked flesh.

Antonidas's hand was on every surface.

His habits, rather. Margin stars. Cross-braced proofs. The little hooked correction mark he used in Violet Citadel seminar rooms when a student reached the right answer by an ugly route. Jaina had spent half her life arranging thoughts for that red pen and that dry cough behind it. Here the same habits covered the deepest room in Scholomance, carried forward in chalk and iron-gall by the man who had taken the same lessons and kept walking.

At the threshold, Jandice Barov kept one hand on the iron strap of the door. Barov burgundy at the sleeve, pale fingers tight on dark metal. Proud. Careful. Her excitement still showed in the angle of her chin. She had brought Jaina farther than anyone was meant to go, and the set of her mouth made it hard to tell whether she expected triumph or rebuke.

Kel'Thuzad turned a page, set a brass paperweight on it, and raised his head.

Grey-haired, gaunt, scholar-thin. Reagent had roughened the first two fingers of the hand on the paper. His robe still carried the old Kirin Tor line through the shoulders and folded sleeves under darker cloth, Dalaran violet buried under soot-black wool. In another room, at another hour, he might have been any senior archmage at work over a difficult problem.

"Mistress Barov," he said, "you have done enough."

Jandice's fingers tightened on the latch. "Master, Lady Proudmoore followed the upper sequence farther than I expected. She caught the false rings on the first pass--"

"Yes," Kel'Thuzad said, looking at Jaina at last. Pale eyes. Steady. Interested. "I can see that."

Warmth touched Jandice's face and went out again. She bowed and withdrew.

The iron-banded door shut with a thick stone sound. Under the flagstone, the buried wards hummed once, low enough that another listener might have missed it. Jaina did not. The room sealed itself around the two of them.

Kel'Thuzad folded his hands on the desk. "You have come a long way for the missing step."

Jaina stayed where Jandice had left her, seated across from him at the long oak table that cut the room in two. A stick of fine white chalk lay near her right hand. Dust from the descent and the boards above marked her thumb and the blue cuff at her wrist.

"I came because the body was doing work my field reads could not finish," she said. "Your grain carries the strongest signature because you want it to. Every site answered there first. Every site stalled there too."

"Good." Plain as breath. "Then begin there."

He rose and crossed to the nearest board. Chalk clicked once against slate. Good chalk, denser than the travel sticks Jaina carried on the road, the kind the Violet Citadel reserved for seminar rooms and copied diagrams. It cut a clean white line instead of crumbling.

"The grain is only the carrier," he said.

He drew a circle, then a second around it. Outer shell. False ring. He did not write the words. The pattern said them for him.

"You know that much," Jaina said.

"You know more than that much. You know the treated grain survives storage, cart-rattle, river damp, even baking, because the preserving work is simple and brutal. You know repeated ingestion matters more than any single meal. You know the host is altered before death. What you lacked was the final relation."

Jaina went still. Host. Altered. He used the words the way another man might have said kettle or chair.

Kel'Thuzad wrote a second chain below the first, deeper and tighter, each mark tucked inside the next with the old Kirin Tor habit of making elegance do the work of compression.

"The outer shell teaches nothing," he said. "It only arrives intact. The lesson begins in the body."

Chalk rasped. Prepared host. Repeated teaching. The body keeping the lesson. He said it all in the language Antonidas had trained into her until it lived in her wrist and jaw: receptive medium, carried charge, recurrence through prepared structure. Jaina could follow every step.

"Not poison," she said.

"No. Poison spends itself."

She answered before she could stop herself. "This settles. Waits. The body carries it farther than the bread can."

His mouth changed toward a smile.

"Exactly."

Heat climbed the back of her neck. She hated the brief clean lift his approval drew from her. Jandice had been fast. Kel'Thuzad was faster.

He tapped the deeper chain with the chalk. "Your field work at the farmhouse north of the trade road was close. The body does not merely suffer contamination. It is taught by it. Repetition lays down an answerable shape. Death does not interrupt the sequence. Death releases it."

Jaina looked from the board to the specimen jars on the shelf beyond him. A child's hand floated in clear reagent in one, little finger curled inward. Two preserved eyes drifted in another. Labels in Kel'Thuzad's careful hand hung from both on waxed cord.

"The body becomes the answering chamber," she said.

Kel'Thuzad inclined his head.

The phrase completed too much at once. Andorhal's granaries. Millhaven's dead livestock. The villages where bread had gone ordinary and then fatal. Why the strongest readings had never yielded the whole thing. The gut-first spread. The way the plague had gone quiet inside daily life and then answered from beneath it.

She stood and crossed to the board. Cold had settled into her knees during the descent. She took the chalk from the ledge and added a branching line under his.

"The first waking is crude," she said. "Because the body has been taught enough to answer, not enough to remain itself."

"Precisely."

She drew another branch, smaller, testing the relation at the edge.

"A village fed together dies together," Kel'Thuzad said behind her. "A village that dies together does not rise as a pile of accidents. It rises in relation. Appetite. Direction. Response. Enough to muster. Enough to become useful under a competent hand."

Her hand stayed raised over the slate.

"The acolytes in those villages," she said. "They do not create that from nothing."

"No."

"They catch it."

"And harden it."

She set the chalk down with great care. White dust clung to the grooves of her fingertips.

"Then the dead in those villages never had a chance," she said.

Kel'Thuzad turned the chalk between his fingers. "The villages had many chances. They baked them."

The flatness of the answer struck harder than outrage. Jaina turned back to him.

"You speak of routine as consent."

"I speak because routine is the strongest force available to any large design. You of all people should respect repetition."

He set the chalk aside and moved back to the desk. The shift from board to table changed the room with him. Proof done. Argument beginning.

"There is still a difference," she said. "What rises in a village and what you are doing here."

"At last."

He drew one folio nearer and opened it. Vellum, not cheap paper. Tight ink lines. A lattice of circles bound around a single central mark, finer work than the village mechanisms and costlier by an order Jaina could see at a glance. He had built this for a single mind, not a crowd.

"Your village dead keep their function," he said. "Motion. Appetite. Rough answerability. Their pattern survives in outline because outline is enough for war. The finer work requires finer structure."

Jaina leaned over the page despite herself. Iron-gall ink. Layered binding. Different words, same ideas underneath. The diagram had the terrible cleanliness of something revised many times and never once repented.

"This is what the villages do not receive," she said.

"This is what the villages cannot afford."

The distinction opened under her feet. Low village dead. Rough answerables. And above them, a second aim, narrow and dear.

"Preserved selfhood," she said.

Kel'Thuzad's gaze sharpened with pleasure. "There. Yes."

She stepped back from the desk.

"That is what you want for yourself."

"Of course."

He looked mildly surprised that the point needed speaking aloud.

"You built a kingdom-scale slaughter to prove a private theorem."

"No." He sat and steepled his fingers. "I built a kingdom-scale apparatus because the theorem was true whether anyone liked it or not. My private interest lies in carrying that truth farther than your teachers permitted."

"Dalaran banned necromancy," she said.

"Yes."

"Because it destroys the line between a body and a person."

"Yes."

"Because it collapses consent."

"Yes."

"Because it turns every hearth, every healer, every grave into a point of violation."

Approval warmed his face again. "Much better. You have stopped objecting at the level of taste."

Anger hit clean and fast.

"Do not mistake clarity for agreement."

"I have never required agreement from a serious mind."

Jaina put both hands on the table. Cold oak. Smooth from years of sleeves and papers passing over it. One corner still held a faint burn mark where some lamp or careless alchemical flame had once bitten the finish.

"The fact that the theorem holds," she said, "does not absolve what it does."

"Absolve?" he repeated. "No. That is a priest's word. The work needs no absolution. It needs to be named."

"Naming it is not enough when the method consumes whole villages."

"And prohibition is not enough when the method remains sound."

He said the next words with the patience of a lecturer correcting something elementary. "The Council of Six never disproved necromancy. It forbade it. There is a difference. They found the line where valid method touched living society and became intolerable. Persons. Consent. Continuity of self. Civic trust. They were right to fear that line. Fear, however, is not refutation."

Jaina opened her mouth. Nothing useful arrived. Those arguments had lived for years in locked drawers and restricted lectures under the Violet Citadel. Dalaran had never called the art nonsense. Dalaran had called it forbidden.

"That is your answer?" she said. "That because the logic survives the ban, the ban can be ignored?"

"No. My answer is that the ban names the cost more honestly than the scholars who still invoke it as proof the art itself was impossible."

"You think this makes you brave."

"No," Kel'Thuzad said. "Bravery is for men about to face the unknown. I did something certain and accepted the result."

"The result is famine, plague, and mothers dragged out of their own houses by what used to be their sons."

"Yes."

"You say it like the word were sufficient."

"It is sufficient to name what occurred."

"You built an atrocity and then taught yourself to say it cleanly."

Kel'Thuzad tipped his head a fraction. "And your response has been what, exactly? You crossed borders without sanction, pursued a plague no one had authorized you to pursue, and followed your private analysis into this room because you were certain the answer waited below."

"Do not compare us."

"I have no need to force the comparison. You made it on your own the moment you entered."

Jaina's hands curled at her sides. Chalk dust marked one cuff. Road grit still lived in the hem of her robe. Sylvanas's blood from the ridge above Millhaven had dried under her nails two days ago and been scrubbed away, but her body remembered it.

"I crossed a border to stop this."

"Yes."

"You crossed one to spread it."

"Direction matters. I have never denied that."

"Then what are you claiming?"

"Only this." He touched the desk once with two fingers, the way a lecturer marked the true line of a problem. "We were both made by the same institution. We were both taught to follow pattern where it led. We both found the world outside our studies too slow for the work at hand."

"You call your trespass duty because the object of your conviction flatters your ethics. I call mine scholarship because it flatters my honesty. The underlying habit is the same."

"No."

"You left a note and rode north alone because you trusted your reading over orders. Admirable. So did I, once."

"I did not build a machine for stripping personhood out of a kingdom."

"No. You are earlier in the chain."

She turned away from him before she could stop herself. The nearest shelf carried labeled boxes in a careful hand, subject and date inked on each lid. One still bore Antonidas's old sigil for translational instability scratched into the corner and crossed out later, replaced by Kel'Thuzad's cleaner notation. Jaina put her palm against the shelf. Dry wood. Cool. Solid.

"You are wrong."

"About your intentions? I do not doubt them. About your methods? I doubt them less with every minute."

"If I had had another trained mind with me in Andorhal," she said, "the work would have gone faster."

"Would it have gone differently?"

"Yes."

"Because that second mind would have introduced superior ethics? Or because it would have forced you to price time, risk, and consequence against your hunger to complete the pattern?"

The questions came in a voice that was not in the room. How long. How many. Which door. Who pays if you stay another minute. A ridge above Millhaven. Farstrider blood on her fingers. Sylvanas looking at the dead with no patience for elegance.

When Jaina opened her eyes, Kel'Thuzad was watching with fresh interest.

"There," he said. "Now you are asking the useful question."

"No," Jaina said. "Now I am remembering the human one."

"Human concerns are always invoked at the point where disciplined minds become inconvenient."

"Human concerns are the point."

"To governments. To priests. To frightened committees."

"To anyone who has ever buried someone they loved."

That reached him. The last warmth went out of him.

"Love is an argument from attachment," he said. "Attachment makes poor policy."

"And your policy is to make every body in Lordaeron answerable to an intelligence it never consented to serve."

"Your outrage remains ethical rather than analytical. I understand. It is the one ground on which the old ban can still stand."

"That ban stands because once your work touches actual bodies, the category changes. You stop moving dead flesh. You assault the self. You turn a person into an instrument without their leave."

"Only because you insist on treating the self as sacred at the point where it becomes worth studying."

"Worth studying." The phrase left iron in her mouth. "That is what villages are to you."

"The villages are proof," Kel'Thuzad said.

"Dalaran did not lose its nerve," she said. "It found the place where your method became a civic atrocity and named it correctly."

Kel'Thuzad nodded once, formal enough to insult.

"That is the strongest version of the argument," he said. "Antonidas never put it so cleanly."

"Do not speak like you knew him better than I do."

"I knew him before you had a staff to hold."

"He taught you the same discipline he taught me. The difference is that I accepted where it led."

"No." Jaina's voice came quieter now, but harder. "The difference is that he understood the cost where you chose not to."

Kel'Thuzad studied her. "Perhaps," he said. "And perhaps he understood the cost only because he lacked the will to bear it."

She went back to the table because standing still came too close to yielding. The chair scraped over flagstone when she pulled it. She sat. The lake-cold in the floor came up through the wood and her boots.

Kel'Thuzad remained standing another beat, then took his own chair again.

He folded one page over another and exposed a new diagram. Wider structure. Multiple chains descending toward one marked point. Jaina saw the turn before he spoke. This was no village mechanism and no personal continuation. This was scale.

"You came for mechanism," he said. "You have it. You came for scale as well, though you did not know that when you entered."

Jaina stared at the new diagram.

"Say what sits above you," she said.

"Not above me. Above the work."

He placed one fingertip on the uppermost mark in the chain.

"A will in Northrend," he said. "Bound. Vast. Learned in ways your libraries only gesture toward. I did not derive this design alone, Jaina. No mortal mind in Lordaeron could have derived it alone. The patronage came first. The revisions after."

The word struck before the rest.

"Patronage."

"What else should I call it? I was given a larger frame than Dalaran ever offered me. A mind capable of answering the questions your teachers banned at the point of discomfort. A power equal to the labor required."

Jaina's fingers tightened on the table edge hard enough that the nail of her thumb hurt.

"Its name."

He met her eyes.

"The Lich King."

The name reordered the whole conversation. Patron. Northrend. Voice in the dark. The plague had just acquired intention farther away than any road she could ride in time.

"King of what," she asked.

"Of the dead, eventually." The smallest shrug. "Titles arrive before they are fully earned."

Jaina did not smile.

"The plague is logistics," she said, more to herself than to him. "Preparation."

"Yes."

"For an invasion."

That earned another degree of warmth. "You are quick when you stop arguing for the room."

"The Scourge is preparation," he said. "The force above it is older and far less patient."

He did not wait for her to ask the second name.

"The Burning Legion."

Jaina sat very still.

Plague villages. Grain roads. Scholomance under Caer Darrow. And behind them, farther north, a named intelligence feeding revisions into the work while Lordaeron argued with itself over terms, jurisdiction, and proof.

"You expect me to accept that on your word."

"You have accepted harder things on my word in the last hour."

"Do not become careless enough to mistake me for your only source of truth. I offer you scale because scale is already bearing down on your province. Whether your institutions can digest the names in time is another matter."

Antonidas would hear the first name through a sending stone and understand too much at once. The Council would hear the second and slow into the crawl of classification. Either way, the bakehouses would keep working and grain would keep moving south under ordinary hands.

"How much of this design is yours?" she asked.

"Enough."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only honest one available. I built the mortal apparatus. Grain. Cult. Instruction. Deployment. I refined the host work because mortal scholarship is still useful when properly guided. Some edges belong to higher authorship."

Jaina thought of all the villages already buried under that sentence.

"It interests anyone trying to stop you."

"Then stop the machine you can touch. Leave the cosmology for later."

She pushed back from the table and crossed to the shelves again. Green bars from the fungi lay across the floor. At the far wall the stone carried a colder thrum where older ward-lines fed up through the foundations of Caer Darrow. This room had been built on work older than either of them.

"Why tell me any of this?" she asked.

"Because you came this far."

"That is vanity."

"Partly." He did not deny it. "Mostly economy. There is no point withholding names once pressure has reached the threshold where names become unavoidable."

"And if I carry them out?"

"You will carry out a truer understanding of your defeat."

He reached for the final folio on the desk and opened it with care that bordered on reverence. The diagram inside was smaller than the others. Tighter. Personal. Its lines nested inward toward a single center and held there with an intimacy more obscene than the village charts. This one had not been designed for counties or granaries. It had been designed for one soul and one death.

"There remains one distinction you have not fully accepted," he said.

Jaina came back to the desk in spite of herself.

"You mean the difference between your villagers and your own plans."

"Yes."

He touched the central mark.

"The village dead are rough continuities. Useful enough for the field. The self remains only in outline. For them, recurrence is low and cheap."

The word landed in her stomach with colder force than resurrection would have. Plainer. Worked through. A term one could use in a ledger without ever having to look at the body.

"For you," she said.

"For me, the preserving structure is made for one mind. Costly. Deliberate. Designed to hold memory, intellect, priority, will."

Jaina looked at the page and understood before she wanted to. He had already priced his own death. The calculations had been sitting on this desk waiting for conditions to ripen around them.

"You think this solves mortality."

"It solves enough of it."

"Enough," she repeated.

"You still frame this as faith," he said. "It is not. I have no interest in crude village survival for myself. The rough answerables proved what they needed to prove. The finer question was always whether the self could be carried intact through a prepared threshold."

"And you intend to be your own proof."

"Every serious scholar should prefer first-hand verification where possible."

"You made villages into rehearsal for your private continuation."

"The villages proved one branch of the theorem. My own work proves another. Do not collapse scales that differ in purpose because outrage prefers the shorter line."

Jaina looked down at the folio and the understanding came over her in one clean, intolerable sweep.

Carrier. Host. Answering chamber. Low recurrence. High preservation.

For one heartbeat she followed it all the way through in the same clear way she had once followed tide tables, ward chains, and teleport lattices in Antonidas's study. The body taught to hold its ending. The threshold approached and crossed. The self not extinguished, only borne along a prepared line laid in advance.

The chair struck flagstone behind her hard enough to ring the ward-line under the floor.

Jaina jerked back from the desk. Air hit her lungs too fast. The room swam once, sharply, then steadied.

"Do not," she said.

Kel'Thuzad did not move.

"I said no such thing."

"You did not need to."

Her voice sounded wrong in her own ears, too thin for the fury she wanted in it.

"Understanding is not surrender," he said.

"No," Jaina said, and forced breath into a shape that held. "But it is enough to wound."

She understood the structure. She hated it. The hatred did not erase the understanding.

Jaina made herself sit because her legs had gone unreliable and she refused to let him see more than he already had.

"You said Antonidas lost his nerve," she said.

"I said he stopped where the work offended the room."

"He stopped where it touched bodies."

"Bodies were always where the work was heading."

"They were people."

"Yes." This time the answer came with unmistakable impatience, brief and real. "That is exactly what made the question worth asking."

There. At last. His composure slipped and showed appetite beneath, lean and cold and completely sincere.

"That is the line," she said.

"That is one line."

"It is the only one that matters."

Kel'Thuzad turned one page corner between his fingers. When he spoke again his voice had lost the last warmth entirely.

"You have a partner," he said. "A friction your mind did not produce. I had a Council. They preferred order to inquiry. The outcomes differ."

Jaina did not answer. No one in this room had ever cut through elegance to ask what the mechanism did in a village with ovens, winter stores, and actual dead. Sylvanas had. Sometimes before Jaina finished her sentence.

"Do not romanticize your distinction from me," he said. "Understand it precisely. Intelligence answers whatever hand directs it. Method does the same. What governs either is relation. Attachment. The bonds that still pull at you. I cut mine elsewhere."

Jaina sat with that because refusing too quickly would have been cowardice. Acceptance never entered it. Her mind ran toward pattern by default. Mercy had entered the work from outside her.

Neither of them spoke. Wards cycled under stone. Beyond the wall, Darrowmere pressed its cold into the foundations.

Jaina looked at the unfinished notes near his elbow.

Her own copied pages from the farmhouse lay there too, where he had set them beside his work, her hand in the same stack as his. Oilcloth edges. Road grit at the corners. Her cramped field script beside Kel'Thuzad's narrow, patient lines. The sight of them in this room made her recoil harder than any specimen jar had.

"You kept those."

Kel'Thuzad followed her gaze. "They were worth keeping."

The compliment fouled the air.

"If I leave here with this," she said, "and speak it exactly, no one in Dalaran will know what to do with the truth."

"That is very likely."

"Antonidas might."

"Antonidas will understand it. Understanding and action have never been identical."

She thought of the sending stone going thin and blue with distance. Antonidas's voice stripped of everything but urgency. The Council compressing horror into orderly speech while the roads kept moving whether any chamber was ready for the names attached to them or not.

Then the thought turned toward the missing correction she had left outside this room. Not comfort. Not agreement. The harder thing. How long. How many. What does it do outside this room.

She wiped the chalk from her fingers onto her sleeve. White against blue.

Kel'Thuzad's head turned before hers did.

Small motion. Attention lifting.

The wards answered a beat later.

The floor took the disturbance first. A pulse. Then another, farther out, passed hand to hand through the buried chain under Scholomance. The old protections took the descent in order. No fumbling at the locks. No clumsy magical shove. Each threshold answered and handed the motion downward.

Kel'Thuzad stood.

"Interesting," he said.

Jaina was already on her feet.

The next ripple came clearer. Stair. Landing. Stair again. Whoever was coming knew how to move through hostile ground and kept their pace through it.

Kel'Thuzad watched her.

A moment later she knew why. The change had shown on her face before she could school it.

"Someone expected?" he asked.

Jaina did not answer.

Another pulse. Closer now. The wards answered one after another to a body driving through them without waste. A pause at one choke point. Choice made. Then movement again, cleaner than before.

Kel'Thuzad's eyes narrowed. "You know the approach."

She did.

She knew that shape. Pressure crossing defended space without waste, every angle priced on the way through. She knew what kind of mind had entered Scholomance and kept moving.

Above them, a heavy blow struck stone. Then silence. Then the next ward answered.

Relief nearly folded her knees. Dread came with it at once. Whoever entered next would see only the aftermath: the chair knocked back, chalk on her hands, Kel'Thuzad alive and calm at the center of the scene.

Kel'Thuzad took one step toward the iron door and stopped. He was listening now in earnest.

"Remarkable," he said.

Jaina heard the next ripple and knew.

Sylvanas.
