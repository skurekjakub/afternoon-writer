# Texture Transformations

Concrete patterns for rewriting sentences. Each shows a before and after.

## 1. Compound → Participial

When two independent clauses share a subject and the second action flows from the first, convert the compound join into a participial phrase. This simultaneously reduces compound% and increases participial%.

**Pattern:** `Subject verbed X, and [subject] verbed Y` → `Subject verbed X, verbing Y`

She drew her sword, and she turned to face the beast.
→ She drew her sword, turning to face the beast.

He rounded the creature, and he picked his way more carefully across the battlefield.
→ He rounded the creature, picking his way more carefully across the battlefield.

Kalak whispered the words, and he stepped up and gripped the other man's arm.
→ Kalak whispered the words, stepping up and gripping the other man's arm.

The clerk was a thin man with ink stains past his wrists, and he reached for the documents with the automatic hand of someone who had read a thousand identical sheets that week.
→ The clerk was a thin man with ink stains past his wrists, reaching for the documents with the automatic hand of someone who had read a thousand sheets that week.

Jezrien hesitated, and he looked at the sword, then bowed his head and turned away.
→ Jezrien hesitated, looking at the sword, then bowed his head and turned away.

## 2. Compound → Period split

When two clauses are independent thoughts, split them. Short second sentence punches harder.

A boy moved down the line selling water from a clay jug, and two of the drivers were arguing about whose seal would clear first.
→ A boy moved down the line selling water from a clay jug. Two of the drivers were arguing about whose seal would clear first.

Their paddock was sound, and they had not asked questions about the four women on Thalassian mounts.
→ Their paddock was sound. They hadn't asked questions about the four women on Thalassian mounts.

The sixth man's relief would come from that direction, and whoever ran the detail had put the watcher where he could see arrivals.
→ The sixth man's relief would come from that direction. Whoever ran the detail had put the watcher where he could see arrivals.

## 3. Participial insertion — adding connective tissue

Add a participial phrase to connect an action to its surroundings. The phrase should carry sensory or spatial information.

**Pattern:** `Subject verbed.` → `Subject verbed, Ving [sensory/spatial detail].`

Szeth stood and began to pick his way through the room.
→ Szeth stood, picking his way through the room.

His own Blade formed in his hands, appearing from mist, wet with condensation.
(Already has a participial. Leave it.)

He sat on a bench at the back. → He sat on a bench at the back, watching the dancers blur past the fire.

She studied the chalk map. → She studied the chalk map, tracing the two feeder routes with one fingernail.

He walked away. → He walked away, pulling his coat against the wind.

## 4. Leading participial

Open a sentence with the participial for variety. Use sparingly — one per page at most.

**Pattern:** `Ving [detail], subject verbed.`

Rounding the last granary wall, she caught the smell before she saw the yard.

Gripping the railing with both hands, Kalak looked down at the field of corpses.

Picking up the pace, she angled for the alley mouth where the shadows pooled deepest.

## 5. DO NOT convert

Some compound joins are right. Leave these alone:

**Lists of three or more actions** — these are rhythmic, not flabby.
He stamped the papers, pressed a second mark below the first, and handed them back. ✓

**True contrast** — "but" or "yet" carrying surprise or contradiction.
She expected fury, but his voice came out flat and tired. ✓

**Parallel structure for emphasis.**
They danced, and drank, and yelled, and sang. ✓

**Short punchy compounds under 15 words.**
He looked up, and she was gone. ✓

## 6. Sanderson in situ — what the target looks like

These are real passages from the style target. Notice how participial phrases braid action, setting, and interiority into single sentences without sounding ornamental.

> He rounded the creature, picking his way more carefully across the battlefield. The plain was a place of misshapen rock and stone, natural pillars rising around him, bodies littering the ground.

Three participials in two sentences: *picking*, *rising*, *littering*. Each carries spatial or sensory information.

> Kalak whispered the words, stepping up and gripping the other man's arm. "I can't."

The participial lets the physical action overlap with the speech — simultaneous, not sequential.

> Those fires, those hooks, digging into his flesh again and again.

Fragment built around a participial. Raw and visceral.

> The heir's feasting companions were unimportant. Szeth stayed far from the heir, skirting the sides of the room, passing the drummers. Musicspren zipped through the air around them, the tiny spirits taking the form of spinning translucent ribbons.

Two participials in the middle sentence (*skirting*, *passing*), one in the last (*taking*). Continuous motion through a space.

> Holding his breath, he clung to the Stormlight. He could still feel it leaking out.

Leading participial. Short, controlled.

> He sat in a large stone room, baked by enormous firepits that cast a garish light upon the revelers, causing beads of sweat to form on their skin as they danced, and drank, and yelled, and sang.

One trailing participial (*causing*) followed by a legitimate parallel compound list (*danced, and drank, and yelled, and sang*). Both constructions in the same sentence — that's the target register.
