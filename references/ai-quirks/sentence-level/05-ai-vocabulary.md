# AI Vocabulary

AI models have a vocabulary fingerprint — a set of words they reach for disproportionately often. These words share traits: Latinate, polysyllabic, vaguely literary, emotionally imprecise. They signal "I'm writing well" without actually doing the work of vivid description. A human writer might use "tapestry" once in a career for a specific, earned metaphor. AI reaches for it every third page because it sounds writerly.

The replacement is always the same move: get concrete. Get specific. Use the Anglo-Saxon word. Use the word that makes the reader SEE something rather than vaguely FEEL impressive.

**Hard limit:** Any word from the lists below — max 1 use per 3,000 words, and ONLY when the literal meaning is intended (e.g., "tapestry" for an actual tapestry on a wall). The figurative use is always what to cut.

---

## Latinate Abstractions

### 1.
**Bad:**
She delved into the ancient texts, searching for answers.

**Good:**
She dragged the next book toward her, cracked the spine, and started reading.

**Why:** "Delved" is AI's favourite verb for investigation. It sounds scholarly but says nothing about the physical act. The replacement gives us weight (dragged), sound (cracked), and action (started reading).

### 2.
**Bad:**
The city was a tapestry of cultures and traditions.

**Good:**
Three languages on every street sign. Four different kinds of bread in the market. Temple bells, call to prayer, and someone's grandmother yelling about fish prices, all at once.

**Why:** "Tapestry of cultures" is the ultimate AI cliché — decorative, vague, and says nothing specific about what makes this city interesting. The replacement gives us concrete sensory details.

### 3.
**Bad:**
Her skill with a blade was a testament to years of training.

**Good:**
Twenty years of holding a sword, and it showed. The grip was automatic, the stance habitual, the way she moved her weight a thing the body did without being asked.

**Why:** "Testament to" is a phrase that sounds like a museum plaque. The replacement shows the training in the body — automatic, habitual, unconscious.

### 4.
**Bad:**
The battlefield was a mosaic of victory and defeat.

**Good:**
Bodies. Theirs and ours, difficult to tell apart in the mud. A banner still standing at the north end, ours. One burning in the ditch, also ours.

**Why:** "Mosaic of victory and defeat" aestheticizes carnage. The replacement is ugly and specific — mud, bodies, banners — the way a battlefield actually looks.

### 5.
**Bad:**
The juxtaposition of her gentleness and her strength made her compelling.

**Good:**
She could break a collarbone and set one. She'd done both in the same afternoon. That was the thing about her — the violence and the tenderness lived in the same hands, and neither apologised for the other.

**Why:** "Juxtaposition" belongs in a college essay. The replacement shows the contradiction through specific actions and gives it a voice.

### 6.
**Bad:**
She delved deeper into the mystery, each clue unraveling another layer.

**Good:**
Another dead end. Another question growing where an answer should have been. She pushed on anyway, because the alternative was admitting she didn't know what she was doing.

**Why:** "Delved deeper" + "unraveling" + "layer" — three clichés in one sentence. The replacement gives us frustration, stubbornness, and honesty.

### 7.
**Bad:**
The landscape was a testament to nature's resilience.

**Good:**
Trees growing out of the ruins. Roots cracking stone, moss eating mortar. Give it another century and you'd never know anyone had built here.

**Why:** "Testament to nature's resilience" tells us what to think. The replacement shows the specific evidence and lets us draw our own conclusion.

### 8.
**Bad:**
Their relationship was a complex tapestry of shared experiences.

**Good:**
Fourteen years. A war, a famine, two children, one betrayal, and one very long winter spent in a house too small for the silence between them.

**Why:** "Complex tapestry of shared experiences" is meaningless. The replacement is a specific, devastating list.

### 9.
**Bad:**
She delved into the depths of her own consciousness.

**Good:**
She closed her eyes and went looking for the place where the fear lived.

**Why:** "Delved into the depths of her consciousness" sounds like a self-help book. The replacement is simpler, more direct, and more frightening.

### 10.
**Bad:**
The mosaic of alliances made the political landscape treacherous.

**Good:**
Everyone was allied to someone who hated someone else's ally. Draw the lines on a map and they looked like a child's scribble.

**Why:** "Mosaic of alliances" and "political landscape" are both dead metaphors. The replacement makes the complexity visual and specific.

## False-Poetic

### 11.
**Bad:**
An ephemeral sense of peace washed over her.

**Good:**
Peace — for a breath. Maybe two. Then the world remembered where she was and came crashing back.

**Why:** "Ephemeral" is AI reaching for a pretty word when "brief" would do. The replacement dramatises the brevity through counting breaths.

### 12.
**Bad:**
The forest had an ethereal quality in the morning mist.

**Good:**
Mist in the trees. The light came through sideways, blue-white, turning the trunks to columns and the canopy to cathedral glass. Everything wet. Everything quiet.

**Why:** "Ethereal quality" is a label. The replacement builds the ethereal feeling through specific sensory details — the light angle, the colour, the wetness, the quiet.

### 13.
**Bad:**
The beauty of the moment was ineffable.

**Good:**
She didn't have words for it. Later, she'd try — she'd describe the light, the way the water moved, the sound of Maren laughing — and none of it would be right. You had to be there. She was glad she had been.

**Why:** "Ineffable" is a word that describes its own failure. The replacement shows the ATTEMPT to describe and the failure, which is more honest and more moving.

### 14.
**Bad:**
She moved through the room with sublime grace.

**Good:**
She moved through the room the way water moves through a channel — finding the path, taking it, wasting nothing.

**Why:** "Sublime grace" tells us to be impressed. The replacement shows us what the movement looks like.

### 15.
**Bad:**
She existed in a liminal space between sleep and waking.

**Good:**
Half-asleep. Sounds reached her like they were crossing a long room — Maren's voice, footsteps, the scrape of a chair. She let them stay distant. Not ready to surface yet.

**Why:** "Liminal space" is academic jargon. The replacement gives us the specific texture of being half-conscious.

### 16.
**Bad:**
The melody was hauntingly beautiful.

**Good:**
The melody lodged in her chest. Three notes, four, a phrase that climbed and never quite resolved — she'd be humming it for days, she knew. The kind of song that follows you home.

**Why:** "Hauntingly beautiful" is a wine-label adjective. The replacement shows what "haunting" means: the melody doesn't leave.

### 17.
**Bad:**
Her ethereal beauty seemed to transcend the mundane world around her.

**Good:**
She looked wrong for this place. Too clean for the mud, too bright for the gloom, like something carried here from a painting and set down still wet.

**Why:** "Ethereal beauty" + "transcend" + "mundane" — triple AI vocabulary. The replacement uses contrast with the setting and a specific visual metaphor (wet paint).

### 18.
**Bad:**
The ephemeral nature of their happiness made it all the more poignant.

**Good:**
It wouldn't last. They both knew it wouldn't last. That made the mornings sharper, the evenings longer, the space between them in bed feel like something borrowed and due back soon.

**Why:** "Ephemeral nature" + "poignant" = two AI words. The replacement dramatises impermanence through how it CHANGES their experience.

### 19.
**Bad:**
The dawn cast a sublime glow across the valley.

**Good:**
Dawn. The valley filled slowly, gold rolling down the hillsides like something poured, thick and warm, until the shadows retreated to the creases between the hills.

**Why:** "Sublime glow" is a postcard. The replacement gives the light physical properties (poured, thick, warm) and shows where the darkness goes.

### 20.
**Bad:**
She felt an ineffable connection to this place.

**Good:**
Something in her knew this place. The smell of the stone, the angle of the light through the windows, the way the floor dipped in front of the hearth — her body remembered things her mind had lost.

**Why:** "Ineffable connection" dodges the work. The replacement specifies WHAT she recognises and makes the memory physical (body vs. mind).

## Emotional-Vague

### 21.
**Bad:**
The tension in the room was palpable.

**Good:**
Nobody moved. Three women at a table, three cups of untouched tea, and silence thick enough to lean on.

**Why:** "Palpable" is AI's favourite adjective for tension. It SAYS the tension is physical without making it so. The replacement shows it through stillness, untouched tea, and the silence's weight.

### 22.
**Bad:**
Her words resonated deeply with everyone present.

**Good:**
Maren stopped chewing. Tiku put her cup down. Even the dog looked up. Whatever she'd said, it had landed.

**Why:** "Resonated deeply" is a TED talk phrase. The replacement shows the impact through individual reactions.

### 23.
**Bad:**
There was a visceral quality to her fear.

**Good:**
Her stomach tried to climb her throat. Her hands went cold. The kind of fear that lives in the gut, not the head — the kind you can't think your way out of.

**Why:** "Visceral quality" tells us the fear is physical. The replacement shows it: stomach, hands, gut. Actually visceral.

### 24.
**Bad:**
The scene was deeply poignant.

**Good:**
She found the doll in the rubble. Small, cloth, one arm missing. Someone had sewn the dress by hand — tiny, careful stitches, the kind that take an hour for something that would fit in a palm.

**Why:** "Deeply poignant" instructs the reader to feel moved. The specific detail (hand-sewn dress, careful stitches, tiny garment) does the job without instruction.

### 25.
**Bad:**
A wistful smile played across her lips.

**Good:**
Her mouth moved — almost a smile, not quite. The kind of expression that arrives when the body remembers something the mind is trying to leave alone.

**Why:** "Wistful" is the narrator labelling the emotion. The replacement describes the expression and lets the reader feel the wistfulness.

### 26.
**Bad:**
The nuanced dynamics of their relationship were difficult to navigate.

**Good:**
They'd never been simple. Not enemies, not allies, not friends, not lovers — or maybe all of those, depending on the day and who was asking. She'd stopped trying to name it. The name always turned out to be wrong.

**Why:** "Nuanced dynamics" and "navigate" are corporate-speak. The replacement shows the actual difficulty through the futility of labelling.

### 27.
**Bad:**
The atmosphere was palpable with unspoken truths.

**Good:**
Three things nobody was saying. She could feel them in the air — the shape of them, the weight, the way conversation kept steering around them like water around rocks.

**Why:** "Palpable with unspoken truths" stacks two vague phrases. The replacement gives the unspoken things physical properties and shows the avoidance in action.

### 28.
**Bad:**
She felt a visceral connection to the land.

**Good:**
This dirt. These stones. She'd bled on this ground, slept on it, buried things in it. Her feet knew its slopes the way they knew stairs.

**Why:** "Visceral connection to the land" is a tourism brochure. The replacement is specific acts of inhabitation — bleeding, sleeping, burying, walking.

### 29.
**Bad:**
The music resonated with something deep inside her.

**Good:**
The music caught her somewhere below the ribs. Not a feeling she could name — just pressure, warmth, the sense of something loosening that had been tight for so long she'd forgotten it was there.

**Why:** "Resonated with something deep inside" is empty. The replacement locates the sensation physically (below the ribs) and describes it in physical terms (pressure, warmth, loosening).

### 30.
**Bad:**
His words carried a poignant weight.

**Good:**
She'd remember those words. Not the meaning — the meaning was obvious — but the way they sat in the air after, heavy and slow to dissipate, like smoke from a blown-out candle.

**Why:** "Poignant weight" labels the effect. The replacement gives the words a physical afterlife.

## Movement-Pretty

### 31.
**Bad:**
The flag unfurled in the wind.

**Good:**
The flag caught the wind and snapped open — all at once, like a hand unclenching.

**Why:** "Unfurled" is AI-pretty. "Snapped open" has violence and speed, and the simile adds physical specificity.

### 32.
**Bad:**
Their fates were deeply intertwined.

**Good:**
She couldn't untangle herself from this woman. Every road out circled back. Every decision she made landed in Maren's lap and vice versa, as if the universe had decided they were a package deal and stopped accepting returns.

**Why:** "Deeply intertwined" is decorative. The replacement dramatises the inability to separate.

### 33.
**Bad:**
Emotions cascaded through her.

**Good:**
It came in waves. Anger first, hot and clean. Then shame, slower, arriving the way shame always does — late, thorough, settling in for a long visit.

**Why:** "Cascaded" makes emotions sound like a waterfall — pretty, one-directional, smooth. Real emotions are messy, sequential, varied. The replacement gives each emotion a personality.

### 34.
**Bad:**
Lights danced across the surface of the water.

**Good:**
Light on the water. Scattered, restless, breaking and reforming with each ripple — she couldn't track any single reflection before it split and became two others.

**Why:** "Danced" is the default AI verb for light on surfaces. The replacement describes what the light actually DOES and adds the observer's experience.

### 35.
**Bad:**
Shadows unfurled across the courtyard as the sun set.

**Good:**
The shadows crept. Slow, patient, eating the courtyard stone by stone as the sun dropped behind the western wall.

**Why:** "Unfurled" again — AI reaches for it constantly. "Crept" gives the shadows intent. "Stone by stone" gives the process specificity.

### 36.
**Bad:**
Their conversations always intertwined the personal and the political.

**Good:**
They couldn't talk about the border without talking about the night at the river, and they couldn't talk about the night at the river without talking about the border. Everything was personal. Everything was political. The same sentence, usually.

**Why:** "Intertwined the personal and the political" is a thesis statement. The replacement shows the actual conversational trap.

### 37.
**Bad:**
Ideas cascaded through her mind.

**Good:**
One thought led to another, then another, too fast to catch — she was three connections ahead of her ability to articulate any of them, grasping at threads that kept multiplying.

**Why:** "Cascaded through her mind" is stock. The replacement captures the speed and frustration of rapid ideation.

### 38.
**Bad:**
Smoke unfurled from the chimney.

**Good:**
Smoke rose from the chimney in a grey column, then lost its nerve and went sideways, shredded by the wind.

**Why:** "Unfurled" makes smoke sound deliberate and graceful. Smoke doesn't unfurl. It rises and gets torn apart.

### 39.
**Bad:**
Joy danced in her eyes.

**Good:**
Her eyes were bright. Not wet — bright, the way eyes get when the person behind them has decided to be happy and committed to it fully.

**Why:** "Joy danced in her eyes" is a stock phrase that means nothing specific. The replacement describes what the eyes actually look like and adds the implication of deliberate joy.

### 40.
**Bad:**
The river cascaded over the rocks.

**Good:**
The river hit the rocks and came apart — white spray, mist, the roar of water disagreeing with stone.

**Why:** Even for literal water movement, "cascaded" is limp. The replacement gives the impact a personality (disagreeing).

## Dramatic Overreach

### 41.
**Bad:**
Emboldened by her victory, she pressed forward.

**Good:**
The win sat warm in her chest. She was probably being stupid. She pressed forward anyway.

**Why:** "Emboldened" is a narrator word — it labels the internal state from outside. The replacement puts us inside: the warmth of confidence, the self-awareness of recklessness.

### 42.
**Bad:**
A serendipitous encounter changed everything.

**Good:**
She wasn't supposed to be there. Neither was Maren. Later, she'd trace every consequence back to that hallway, that door, that specific hour — but at the time it was just a wrong turn.

**Why:** "Serendipitous" sounds like a book review. The replacement shows the randomness through specific details and adds the character's retrospective awareness.

### 43.
**Bad:**
The labyrinthine corridors confused even the guards.

**Good:**
The corridors didn't make sense. Right turns that led left. Stairs that went up and arrived lower than they started. Even the guards carried chalk — white marks on every corner, their own private language for "this way back."

**Why:** "Labyrinthine" tells us the corridors are maze-like. The replacement SHOWS the confusion through specific impossible geometry and the chalk marks.

### 44.
**Bad:**
The cacophony of the battle was overwhelming.

**Good:**
Too loud to think. Steel on steel, someone screaming, the thud of bodies hitting ground — all of it at once, all of it close, the kind of noise that gets inside your skull and hammers.

**Why:** "Cacophony" is a pretty word for an ugly thing. The replacement itemises the sounds and makes the noise physical (gets inside your skull).

### 45.
**Bad:**
A myriad of emotions crossed her face.

**Good:**
Her face did three things in two seconds — surprise, then anger, then something harder to name that might have been grief or might have been recognition. She settled on blank.

**Why:** "A myriad of emotions" is vague and ornate. The replacement counts them, names them (with uncertainty), and shows the character choosing to shut it down.

### 46.
**Bad:**
Emboldened by the revelation, she confronted the truth.

**Good:**
So that was it. The truth sat in her lap like something dead. She'd asked for it. Now she had to figure out what to do with it.

**Why:** "Emboldened by the revelation" + "confronted the truth" = two dramatic overreaches in one sentence. The replacement is blunt and darkly comic (truth as dead animal in the lap).

### 47.
**Bad:**
The myriad stars above seemed to mock her loneliness.

**Good:**
Stars. Thousands of them, clear and sharp and completely indifferent. The sky didn't care that she was alone. The sky didn't care about anything. She found that oddly comforting.

**Why:** "Myriad" + "seemed to mock" = AI vocabulary + pathetic fallacy. The replacement subverts the cliché — the sky's indifference is comforting rather than cruel.

### 48.
**Bad:**
The serendipitous nature of their meeting lent it a quality of fate.

**Good:**
Coincidence. Call it fate if you want — she didn't. Fate was for people who needed reasons. She preferred the honest version: dumb luck, bad timing, and a door that should have been locked.

**Why:** "Serendipitous nature" + "quality of fate" is AI stacking ornate abstractions. The replacement gives the character a voice — sceptical, practical, specific.

### 49.
**Bad:**
The labyrinthine plot had finally come to light.

**Good:**
The whole scheme, laid out. It was simpler than she'd expected — most conspiracies are, once you strip away the paranoia. Three women, one lie, and a lot of patience.

**Why:** "Labyrinthine plot" + "come to light" = two dead metaphors. The replacement punctures the drama with the insight that conspiracies are usually simple.

### 50.
**Bad:**
A cacophony of thoughts assailed her mind.

**Good:**
Too many thoughts, too fast. She grabbed one — the nearest, the loudest — and held on. Everything else could wait.

**Why:** "Cacophony of thoughts assailed her mind" uses a sound metaphor for thinking and a violent metaphor for experiencing one's own thoughts. The replacement is simpler: overwhelmed, she picks one and shelves the rest.
