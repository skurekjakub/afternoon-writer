# Punctuation Tics

AI models have distinctive punctuation habits that mark prose as machine-generated. Em dashes in clusters. Semicolons that make narration read like an essay. Zero contractions, giving every character the diction of a formal letter. Sentences that march in uniform 15-20 word lockstep. Ellipses trailing off into nothing. Colons introducing explanations nobody asked for. Each tic alone is survivable; together, they create a recognisable AI texture — over-punctuated, rhythmically flat, and weirdly formal.

**Hard limits:**
- Semicolons in narration: max 1 per 500 words
- Ellipses: max 1 per 1,000 words
- Colons in narration (non-dialogue): max 1 per 1000 words

## Em Dash Overuse

### 1.
**Bad:**
She crossed the room — quickly, silently — and pressed her ear to the door — the thick oak door that had been here longer than anyone alive — and listened.

**Good:**
She crossed the room — quickly and silently — and pressed her ear to the door. Old oak, thick as her forearm. She listened.

**Why:** Three em dashes in one sentence. The second pair is a parenthetical insert that could be a separate sentence. Fix: use commas for the first aside, a new sentence for the description.

### 2.
**Bad:**
The fire — the only source of warmth — had died to embers — dull, orange, barely alive — and the room was cooling fast.

**Good:**
The fire had died to embers, dull and orange and barely alive. The room was cooling fast. The fire had been the only warmth, and now there wasn't any.

**Why:** Four em dashes creating two nested parentheticals. This makes the reader hold too many open brackets in their head.

### 3.
**Bad:**
Maren — tall, lean, her hair cut short since the last time — stood in the doorway — not quite inside, not quite out — as if deciding whether the room deserved her presence.

**Good:**
Maren stood in the doorway. Taller than last time, leaner, her hair cut short. Not quite inside, not quite out, as if deciding whether the room deserved her.

**Why:** Four em dashes. The description of Maren works better as its own sentence. The threshold hesitation works better as a comma-joined continuation.

### 4.
**Bad:**
She reached for the knife — her last one — and tested the edge — sharp enough — and tucked it into her belt.

**Good:**
She reached for the knife, her last one, tested the edge. Sharp enough. She tucked it into her belt.

**Why:** Three em dashes for what is a sequence of simple actions. Commas and short sentences handle this better.

### 5.
**Bad:**
The answer — the real answer — was something she'd known for years — long before anyone had asked — but saying it out loud was different — harder — than she'd expected.

**Good:**
The answer was something she'd known for years. Long before anyone had asked. Saying it out loud was harder than she'd expected.

**Why:** Five em dashes. The sentence is trying to do too much in one breath. Breaking it into three sentences gives each thought room.

### 6.
**Bad:**
She walked — no, stumbled — toward the bed — the narrow, creaking bed that smelled of dust — and collapsed.

**Good:**
She stumbled toward the bed. Narrow, creaking, smelling of dust. She collapsed onto it.

**Why:** The first em dash pair creates a self-correction that works better as a simple verb choice (stumbled instead of walked). The description parenthetical works better as a fragment.

### 7.
**Bad:**
Maren's expression — careful, composed, the kind of face she wore when she was lying — told her everything — and nothing — all at once.

**Good:**
Maren's face was careful. Composed. The kind of face she wore when she was lying. It told her everything and nothing.

**Why:** Four em dashes. The dash-heavy style tries to create emphasis but creates congestion instead. Short sentences achieve the same rhythm without the visual clutter.

### 8.
**Bad:**
The city — what remained of it — stretched before them — broken walls, empty streets, the smell of old fire — and she tried to remember it whole.

**Good:**
The city stretched before them, what remained of it. Broken walls. Empty streets. The smell of old fire. She tried to remember it whole.

**Why:** Three em dashes. The ruins work better as fragments (broken walls. empty streets.) which create a different rhythm — choppy, devastated, matching the content.

## Semicolons in Narration

### 9.
**Bad:**
The rain had stopped; the clouds were clearing; a thin light pushed through the canopy like something cautious.

**Good:**
The rain had stopped. Clouds clearing. A thin light pushed through the canopy, cautious, like something venturing out after a storm.

**Why:** Two semicolons create a list structure that reads like a report. The fix uses a period and a fragment to vary rhythm, then gives the light simile more room.

### 10.
**Bad:**
She was tired; her muscles ached; the wound on her shoulder had started bleeding again.

**Good:**
She was tired. Her muscles ached. The wound on her shoulder had opened again, spotting the bandage rust-brown.

**Why:** Semicolons create a compound list of complaints. Periods separate them into individual facts, each with its own weight. Adding visual detail (rust-brown) to the last one creates hierarchy.

### 11.
**Bad:**
The market was empty; the stalls stood abandoned; cloth snapped in the wind like small, surrendering flags.

**Good:**
The market was empty, stalls abandoned, cloth snapping in the wind like small flags of surrender. Nobody. The kind of empty that meant they'd left in a hurry.

**Why:** The semicolons create equal weight for unequal observations. The simile is the strongest image and deserves its own clause. Adding "Nobody" and the inference gives the observation a mind behind it.

### 12.
**Bad:**
She trusted Maren; she didn't trust anyone else; this was both a comfort and a problem.

**Good:**
She trusted Maren. Nobody else. That was either a comfort or a problem, depending on who you asked.

**Why:** Three semicolons turning an emotional truth into an academic observation. The fix is more direct and adds voice ("depending on who you asked").

### 13.
**Bad:**
The room was dark; the fire had gone out; she could hear breathing that wasn't her own.

**Good:**
Dark room. Dead fire. And breathing — not hers. Someone else's, close, regular, the breathing of a person pretending to sleep.

**Why:** Three semicolons create a surveillance-report cadence. The fragments and the dash create suspense. Adding the detail about pretended sleep turns a list into a scene.

### 14.
**Bad:**
Her hands were steady; her voice was calm; nothing about her suggested fear. She was terrified.

**Good:**
Hands steady. Voice calm. Nothing about her suggested fear.

She was terrified.

**Why:** The semicolons undermine the buildup. Fragments create a checklist (steady, calm, no fear) that the single-line paragraph break demolishes.

### 15.
**Bad:**
She knew the way; she'd walked it a hundred times; every stone was familiar. Yet something felt wrong; the air was different; the shadows fell at odd angles.

**Good:**
She knew the way. A hundred times she'd walked it, every stone familiar. But something was off. The air, different. Shadows falling wrong, at angles that didn't match the light.

**Why:** Five semicolons in two sentences. The fix varies sentence structure — a short statement, a rearranged clause, then fragments for the wrongness, which makes the wrongness feel more disorienting.

### 16.
**Bad:**
The wine was bitter; she drank it anyway; some things you do for the ritual, not the taste.

**Good:**
The wine was bitter. She drank it anyway — some things you do for the ritual, not the taste.

**Why:** One em dash where it earns its place: connecting the action to the philosophy. The semicolons made this read like three separate thoughts instead of one flowing moment.

## No Contractions

### 17.
**Bad:**
She did not know what to say. It was not that she did not have opinions; she simply could not find the right words. She would have to think about it.

**Good:**
She didn't know what to say. Not that she didn't have opinions — she just couldn't find the words. She'd have to think about it.

**Why:** Zero contractions in narration make prose sound like a legal document. Limited third narration should match how the character thinks, and people think in contractions.

### 18.
**Bad:**
"I do not trust her," she said. "I have not trusted her since the incident at the gate. She is not what she appears to be."

**Good:**
"I don't trust her." She set down her cup. "Haven't since the gate. She's not what she looks like."

**Why:** Dialogue without contractions makes every character sound like a robot. Real speech contracts.

### 19.
**Bad:**
It would not be long now. She could not afford to wait. If they did not move, they would not survive the night.

**Good:**
It wouldn't be long. She couldn't afford to wait — if they didn't move, they wouldn't last the night.

**Why:** Four "not"s without contractions. The fix contracts them all and the prose immediately sounds like a person thinking instead of a machine narrating.

### 20.
**Bad:**
She was not angry. She was not sad. She was not anything she could name, and that was perhaps the most frightening thing of all.

**Good:**
She wasn't angry. She wasn't sad. She wasn't anything she could name, and that scared her more than either would have.

**Why:** The uncontracted "was not" repeated three times creates a formal, distant register. Contracting and replacing "perhaps the most frightening thing of all" (wordy) with a direct statement brings it closer.

### 21.
**Bad:**
"I cannot believe you have done this. You should not have come here. We are not safe. They will find us."

**Good:**
"I can't believe you did this. You shouldn't have come. We're not safe. They'll find us."

**Why:** Four uncontracted constructions in panicked dialogue. Nobody speaks this formally when afraid. Contract everything.

### 22.
**Bad:**
It is a strange thing, to stand in a place you have not visited for years and find that it has not changed while you have. She had not expected the memories to be this sharp.

**Good:**
Strange, standing here. Years gone, and the place unchanged — while she'd changed plenty. She hadn't expected the memories to be this sharp.

**Why:** "It is" + "you have not" + "it has not" + "you have" + "She had not" — five uncontracted forms that create an essayistic register. The fix contracts and tightens.

### 23.
**Bad:**
He — she was not sure how long she had been standing there. It could not have been more than a minute, but it felt as though hours had passed.

**Good:**
How long had she been standing here? Couldn't have been more than a minute. Felt like hours.

**Why:** Three uncontracted forms. The fix also converts the indirect question to direct and uses fragments for compression — matching the disorientation of lost time.

### 24.
**Bad:**
She would not go back. She could not go back. There was nothing there for her, and she would not pretend otherwise.

**Good:**
She wouldn't go back. Couldn't. There was nothing there for her, and pretending otherwise had stopped working somewhere around the third year.

**Why:** Three "not"s without contractions. The fix contracts and replaces the vague "would not pretend" with a specific, time-stamped failure ("stopped working around the third year").

## Sentence Length Uniformity

### 25.
**Bad:**
She walked through the marketplace in the early morning light. The stalls were just beginning to open for the day. Vendors arranged their goods on tables made of rough wood. The air smelled of spices and fresh-baked bread from somewhere. She paused to examine a display of silver rings near the corner.

**Good:**
The marketplace, early morning. She moved through it before the crowds — stalls just opening, vendors arranging goods on rough wood tables, the air thick with spice and bread smoke. She stopped at a ring seller's table. Silver. Cheap, probably, but pretty in the half-light.

**Why:** Five sentences, all 10-14 words, all following Subject-Verb-Object. Monotonous. The fix varies from 3 words to 25, mixes fragments with long flowing sentences.

### 26.
**Bad:**
The castle rose above the treeline in the grey afternoon. Its walls were made of the same grey stone as everything. The windows were narrow and set deep into the thick walls. Guards patrolled the battlements with a bored and practiced air. A flag bearing the house crest hung limply from the highest tower.

**Good:**
The castle. Grey stone, grey sky, the walls so much a part of the landscape they looked grown rather than built. Narrow windows, set deep. Guards on the battlements, patrolling with the bored efficiency of people who'd been doing this since before it mattered. A flag hung from the highest tower, limp. No wind today. No wind for days.

**Why:** Five uniform sentences replaced with varied lengths — a one-word fragment, a long sensory sentence, a compact detail, a longer character observation, then three short finishing sentences.

### 27.
**Bad:**
Maren sat at the edge of the bed and looked at her hands. Her fingers were callused from years of handling rope and leather. She flexed them slowly and felt the stiffness in her joints. The room was quiet except for the sound of rain outside. She wondered if the rain would stop before they had to leave.

**Good:**
Maren sat at the bed's edge, looking at her hands. Callused, stiff. Rope and leather and twenty years of pretending that didn't hurt. She flexed them.

Rain. Steady. The kind that settles in for the day. They'd have to leave in it.

**Why:** Five uniform sentences. The fix varies from one word (Rain) to a long sentence with three clauses. The paragraph break mirrors a shift from body to environment.

### 28.
**Bad:**
She climbed the stairs to the tower with growing anticipation. Each step brought her closer to the answers she had sought. The stairwell was narrow and lit by occasional torches on the wall. Her breath came faster as she neared the top of the stairs. At last she emerged into a circular room filled with books.

**Good:**
She climbed. Narrow stairwell, torch-lit, the steps worn concave by centuries of feet. Halfway up, her breath started to labour. Near the top, her thighs burned.

Then — the room. Circular, books on every surface, books stacked on the floor, books open on the table as if someone had just stepped out. She stood in the doorway and forgot about her legs.

**Why:** Five sentences of near-identical length and structure. The fix: a two-word sentence, a long descriptive one, two medium physical ones, then a dramatic reveal paragraph with a long listing sentence.

## Ellipsis Overuse

### 29.
**Bad:**
She looked at the horizon... wondering what lay beyond... The wind picked up... carrying with it the scent of rain... She sighed...

**Good:**
She looked at the horizon. Whatever lay beyond it, she'd find out soon enough. The wind picked up, carrying rain-smell, and she pulled her collar tighter.

**Why:** Five ellipses in one paragraph, each one trailing into nothing. Ellipses create a dreamy vagueness that says "I can't commit to this thought." The fix commits.

### 30.
**Bad:**
"I thought..." She paused. "Never mind..."

**Good:**
"I thought—" She stopped. Shook her head. "Never mind."

**Why:** Ellipses for interrupted speech should usually be em dashes (which indicate a break) rather than trailing off. And the second ellipsis is just hedging the "never mind."

### 31.
**Bad:**
The room was empty... or was it... She could sense something... a presence... watching from the shadows...

**Good:**
The room looked empty. She knew better. Something in the shadows — she couldn't see it, but she could feel it watching, the way you feel a hand hovering near your skin.

**Why:** Five ellipses creating a breathless, melodramatic sequence. The fix replaces vagueness with specific sensation.

### 32.
**Bad:**
She wanted to tell her... everything. But the words... they wouldn't come. Not now... maybe not ever...

**Good:**
She wanted to tell Maren everything. The words wouldn't cooperate. They sat in her throat like stones, heavy and wrong-shaped for the mouth.

**Why:** Four ellipses for what is a simple emotional beat: she wants to speak and can't. The fix gives the inability a physical metaphor.

### 33.
**Bad:**
"Do you remember...?" she asked softly.

**Good:**
"Do you remember?"

That was all. No object. No specifics. Do you remember — as if there was only one thing between them worth remembering, and they both knew what it was.

**Why:** The ellipsis makes the question trail off, but the power of the question is in its incompleteness. The fix addresses that directly — the lack of a direct object IS the point.

## Colon Overuse in Narration

### 34.
**Bad:**
She knew one thing for certain: she wasn't going to wait any longer. The choice was clear: act now or lose everything. There was only one path forward: the one through the fire.

**Good:**
She wasn't going to wait any longer. The choice was act now or lose everything. And the only path forward went straight through the fire.

**Why:** Three colons in three sentences, each introducing a restatement. Colons in narration create an explanatory, essay-like register. The fix makes direct statements.

### 35.
**Bad:**
The evidence pointed to one conclusion: betrayal. Everything made sense now: the missed meetings, the locked doors, the letters she wasn't supposed to see.

**Good:**
Betrayal. That's what it added up to. The missed meetings, the locked doors, the letters she wasn't supposed to see — all of it, one word.

**Why:** Two colons, each introducing a revelation. The fix leads with the conclusion (Betrayal.) and then lists the evidence.

### 36.
**Bad:**
She had two options: fight or run. Fighting meant one thing: death. Running meant something worse: survival on their terms.

**Good:**
Fight or run. Fighting was death. Running was survival on their terms, which was its own kind of death.

**Why:** Three colons. The fix eliminates the introductory clauses entirely ("She had two options:", "Fighting meant one thing:", "Running meant something worse:") and makes direct statements.

### 37.
**Bad:**
There was one quality Maren possessed above all others: patience. It wasn't the passive kind: it was the patience of someone who has decided to win and doesn't care how long it takes.

**Good:**
Maren was patient. Not the passive kind — the kind that's decided to win and doesn't care how long it takes.

**Why:** Two colons creating an explanatory structure. The fix is more direct and uses an em dash for the clarification (one dash, properly placed, vs. two colons).

## Parenthetical Asides

### 38.
**Bad:**
She crossed the courtyard (empty now, though it had been full this morning) and approached the door (locked, as she'd suspected) with the key she'd taken (stolen, really) from Maren's room (which Maren would discover soon enough).

**Good:**
She crossed the empty courtyard — full this morning, desolate now — and tried the door. Locked. She'd suspected as much. The key from Maren's room fit the lock on the first try. Stolen, that key. Maren would notice soon enough.

**Why:** Four parenthetical asides in one sentence, each one breaking the flow. The fix distributes the information across multiple sentences where it can breathe.

### 39.
**Bad:**
The bridge (or what remained of it) crossed a gorge (deep, jagged, the kind that doesn't forgive a misstep) and led to the temple (abandoned for decades, if the moss was any indication).

**Good:**
The bridge — what remained of it — crossed a gorge. Deep. Jagged. The kind that doesn't forgive a misstep. On the far side, the temple, abandoned for decades if the moss was any judge.

**Why:** Three parentheticals. The gorge description works better as fragments (the short, sharp sentences matching the danger). The temple detail integrates into the sentence naturally.

### 40.
**Bad:**
Maren (who had, despite everything, remained loyal through the worst of it) handed her the map (hand-drawn, inaccurate in places, but the best they had) without a word (which was, in itself, a kind of eloquence).

**Good:**
Maren handed her the map without a word. Hand-drawn, inaccurate in places, the best they had. She took it. The silence between them said enough — Maren had stayed through the worst of it, and that loyalty needed no narration.

**Why:** Three parentheticals. The fix distributes: map description becomes its own fragment, Maren's loyalty becomes the closing thought.

## Combined Tics

### 41.
**Bad:**
She did not know what to make of it — the silence; the emptiness; the sense that something had gone terribly, irrevocably wrong. It was not the kind of feeling one could simply ignore... She would have to act — soon — before it was too late.

**Good:**
She didn't know what to make of it. The silence, the emptiness, the sick feeling that something had gone irreparably wrong. She couldn't ignore it. Had to act. Soon.

**Why:** Uncontracted "did not" + em dashes + two semicolons in a list + ellipsis + another dash pair + "it was not." Six different tics. The fix: contractions, commas for the list, no ellipsis, fragments for urgency.

### 42.
**Bad:**
The morning — grey and cold; the kind of morning that does not invite early rising — found her already awake... She had not slept; she could not sleep; the events of the previous night had left her in a state she would not — could not — describe.

**Good:**
Grey morning. Cold. The kind that doesn't invite early rising. She'd been awake for hours. Couldn't sleep. The night before had left her in a state she couldn't name and didn't want to try.

**Why:** Em dashes, semicolons, ellipsis, no contractions, another em dash pair — the paragraph has every AI punctuation tic. The fix: fragments, contractions, direct statements.

### 43.
**Bad:**
She could not help but wonder — was it fate; was it coincidence; or was it something else entirely? She did not know... She might not ever know. The uncertainty — the not-knowing — was, she supposed, the worst part; or perhaps the best.

**Good:**
Was it fate? Coincidence? Something else? She didn't know. Might never. The not-knowing was either the worst part or the best, and she didn't have enough information to decide which.

**Why:** Em dashes, semicolons, ellipsis, no contractions, and wordy constructions. The fix: direct questions, contractions, and a single sentence for the ambiguity.

### 44.
**Bad:**
"I have been thinking..." she said — quietly; almost to herself — "about what you said. I am not sure I agree; I am not sure I disagree. It is... complicated."

**Good:**
"I've been thinking about what you said." Quiet, almost to herself. "I'm not sure I agree. Not sure I disagree, either. It's complicated."

**Why:** Ellipsis, em dash pair, two semicolons, three uncontracted forms — all in dialogue, where they're especially wrong because people don't speak this way.

### 45.
**Bad:**
The garden — once beautiful; now overgrown — lay before her. She did not recognize it; it did not recognize her. They had both changed — irrevocably; completely — and neither was what the other remembered... if they remembered anything at all.

**Good:**
The garden lay before her. Once beautiful, now overgrown. She didn't recognise it. Fair enough — she'd changed too, and neither of them was what the other remembered.

**Why:** Two dash pairs, four semicolons, one ellipsis, two uncontracted forms. The fix: periods, commas, one em dash where it earns its place.

### 46.
**Bad:**
It was — she realized with a start — the same melody; the one from her childhood; the one her mother had hummed while braiding her hair... She had not heard it in years; perhaps decades. The recognition hit her: sudden; fierce; undeniable.

**Good:**
That melody. Her mother's, the one she'd hummed while braiding hair. She hadn't heard it in decades.

The recognition hit all at once — fierce, undeniable, and entirely unwelcome.

**Why:** Em dash pair, three semicolons, ellipsis, colon, three more semicolons. The fix: a fragment for the recognition beat, one dash for the emotional qualities.

### 47.
**Bad:**
She would not — could not — forgive this; not now; not ever. The betrayal was complete: final; irreversible... and she was alone with it.

**Good:**
She wouldn't forgive this. Couldn't. The betrayal was complete, final, irreversible. She was alone with it.

**Why:** Every tic: dash pair, two semicolons, colon, ellipsis, uncontracted forms. Fix: contractions, periods, commas.

### 48.
**Bad:**
The path ahead — narrow; treacherous; barely visible in the fading light — was not the one she would have chosen. But it was the only one left... and she was not in a position to be selective.

**Good:**
The path ahead was narrow, treacherous, barely visible in the failing light. Not the one she'd have chosen. But the only one left, and she wasn't in a position to be picky.

**Why:** Dash pair with semicolons inside, ellipsis, uncontracted forms. Fix: comma list, contractions, simpler diction ("picky" vs. "selective").

### 49.
**Bad:**
"We should not have come here," she whispered — her voice barely audible; trembling — "but it is too late now... Is it not? It is too late."

**Good:**
"We shouldn't have come." Barely a whisper, trembling. "But it's too late now, isn't it."

Not a question. A verdict.

**Why:** Dash pair inside dialogue attribution, semicolon, ellipsis, three uncontracted forms — all in two sentences of speech. The fix contracts, removes punctuation tics, and adds the observation that the question isn't really a question.

### 50.
**Bad:**
The sky — grey; endless; the colour of old iron — pressed down on the land. It was not a sky that promised anything; it was not a sky that threatened. It simply... was. And she — standing beneath it; small and insignificant — felt nothing at all; which was, she supposed, its own kind of answer... or perhaps a question.

**Good:**
Grey sky. The colour of old iron, pressing down. Not promising, not threatening. Just there.

She stood beneath it and felt nothing. That was either an answer or a question, and she couldn't be bothered to decide which.

**Why:** The paragraph has every possible tic: four em dashes, six semicolons, an ellipsis, uncontracted forms, and a trailing final ellipsis. The fix: fragments, one comma list, contractions, and character voice.
