# Body Language Clichés

Stock physical gestures that convey generic emotion instead of character-specific reaction. AI reaches for these because they're the statistically dominant way to show anger/surprise/nervousness/etc. in its training data. Each was effective once. Now they're semantic white noise — the reader's eye slides over them without registering, and they homogenize every character into the same puppet running the same gesture library.

The fix is never "don't show body language." The fix is: make it THIS character's body language, in THIS specific environment, doing something that tells the reader who they are before the reader checks the name.

**Hard limit:** Max 1 use per chapter for any item on this list. If you reach for one, replace it with a gesture tied to the character's history, profession, or nervous habits.

---

## "Raised an eyebrow"

### 1.
**Bad:**
She raised an eyebrow. "Is that so?"

**Good:**
"Is that so?" Her tone didn't change, but something in her posture shifted — a subtle settling, the way a cat goes very still before it moves.

**Why:** The raised eyebrow is the single most common "skepticism" indicator in AI prose. The cat-stillness conveys skepticism through body tension rather than facial semaphore.

### 2.
**Bad:**
Maren raised an eyebrow at the suggestion.

**Good:**
Maren's head tilted a degree to the left. She did that when she was deciding whether someone was stupid or brave.

**Why:** The replacement turns a generic expression into a character-specific habit with stakes — the reader wonders which category the speaker falls into.

### 3.
**Bad:**
She raised an eyebrow in amusement.

**Good:**
She looked at Tiku the way you'd look at a dog that had taught itself to open the gate.

**Why:** "Raised an eyebrow in amusement" is a label. The simile conveys amusement, surprise, and grudging respect simultaneously.

### 4.
**Bad:**
"Really?" She raised an eyebrow.

**Good:**
"Really." Not a question. A dare.

**Why:** The eyebrow raise tries to make "really" sarcastic. Reformatting it as a statement with "not a question, a dare" does it sharper.

## "Crossed her arms"

### 5.
**Bad:**
She crossed her arms defensively.

**Good:**
She tucked her hands inside her elbows, a fold that had nothing to do with the cold.

**Why:** "Crossed arms" is the universal AI signal for defensiveness. The replacement describes the same physical position but reframes it as self-comfort, which is more nuanced and human.

### 6.
**Bad:**
Maren crossed her arms and leaned against the wall.

**Good:**
Maren posted herself against the doorframe — one boot up, arms folded, the exact posture of a woman who planned to watch rather than participate.

**Why:** The replacement keeps the crossed arms but embeds them in a full-body tableau that reveals attitude: she's a spectator, not a combatant.

### 7.
**Bad:**
She crossed her arms, clearly annoyed.

**Good:**
She pulled her collar higher and turned half away — not leaving, but not facing it either.

**Why:** "Crossed arms + clearly annoyed" is double-telling. The half-turn shows annoyance through avoidance, which is more psychologically real.

### 8.
**Bad:**
"No," she said, crossing her arms.

**Good:**
"No." She planted her feet. The "no" had weight to it, the kind that came from the floor up.

**Why:** The replacement conveys refusal through stance (grounding) rather than a barrier gesture (arms).

## "Clenched her jaw"

### 9.
**Bad:**
She clenched her jaw, fighting back anger.

**Good:**
Her back teeth met. She held them there, a pressure that grounded her, kept the words locked behind her tongue.

**Why:** "Clenched her jaw" is the go-to anger suppression gesture. The replacement takes the same physical action and goes INSIDE it — what the clenching feels like, what it's holding back.

### 10.
**Bad:**
Maren clenched her jaw and looked away.

**Good:**
Maren's nostrils flared — a small, involuntary thing, the only crack in the composure she wore like armor.

**Why:** Jaw-clench is the first thing AI reaches for. Nostril flare is less common, equally physical, and the "only crack" framing tells us Maren is controlling everything else.

### 11.
**Bad:**
She clenched her jaw in determination.

**Good:**
She squared her shoulders the way she'd seen her captain do it — chest out, chin up, as though determination were a matter of geometry.

**Why:** "Clenched jaw = determination" is a stock equation. The replacement shows determination as a learned posture, copied from a mentor, which adds character history.

### 12.
**Bad:**
Her jaw clenched as she watched them leave.

**Good:**
She watched them leave. Her hands, at her sides, curled into fists she didn't remember making.

**Why:** Unconscious fists are a more visceral anger signal than jaw tension, and "didn't remember making" adds the layer of the emotion bypassing conscious control.

## "Let out a breath she didn't know she was holding"

### 13.
**Bad:**
She let out a breath she didn't know she'd been holding.

**Good:**
Her lungs unlocked. She'd been bracing without realising — ribs tight, shoulders up around her ears — and now, all at once, she wasn't.

**Why:** This is the single most mocked cliché in fiction. The replacement does the same work (relief after unconscious tension) but describes the tension first and the release second, making the reader feel the physical state rather than narrating a breath.

### 14.
**Bad:**
She released a breath she hadn't realized she was holding.

**Good:**
The first full breath she'd taken in minutes burned going in. Cold air, too much of it, too fast.

**Why:** Same cliché, different phrasing. The replacement focuses on the QUALITY of the breath — cold, too much, burning — instead of the meta-observation about not knowing.

### 15.
**Bad:**
A breath she didn't know she'd been holding escaped her lips.

**Good:**
Her hands were shaking. She pressed them flat against her thighs and breathed — in, out, in — until they stopped.

**Why:** The cliché is a single-moment relief. The replacement shows a process of self-regulation that takes time and effort, which is more physically real.

### 16.
**Bad:**
She exhaled, releasing a breath she'd been unconsciously holding.

**Good:**
Done. Over. She sat down on the nearest surface — a crate, she thought, or maybe a barrel — and breathed like someone who had just remembered how.

**Why:** The "unconsciously holding" is the tell. The replacement conveys post-crisis relief through the character's indifference to what she's sitting on and the simile about remembering breathing.

## "Rolled her eyes"

### 17.
**Bad:**
She rolled her eyes. "Obviously."

**Good:**
"Obviously." The word dripped with the particular patience she reserved for people who were catching up.

**Why:** Eye-rolling is a visual cliché that reads as adolescent. The replacement conveys the same exasperation through the quality of the speech.

### 18.
**Bad:**
Maren rolled her eyes and groaned.

**Good:**
Maren made a sound — low, theatrical, the kind of groan that was half performance and half genuine suffering.

**Why:** "Rolled her eyes and groaned" is two stock gestures stacked. The replacement picks ONE (the groan) and makes it specific: theatrical, half-performed.

### 19.
**Bad:**
She rolled her eyes at Tiku's dramatics.

**Good:**
She watched Tiku's performance with the flat expression of someone who'd seen all four acts and knew exactly when the curtain call was coming.

**Why:** The replacement turns exasperation into familiarity — this is someone who knows Tiku well enough to predict the pattern, which builds relationship.

### 20.
**Bad:**
"Sure," she said, rolling her eyes.

**Good:**
"Sure." Said the way you'd agree to carry someone's pack when they'd already dropped it at your feet.

**Why:** The simile conveys resigned exasperation more specifically than an eye-roll, and it adds stakes (the obligation is already imposed).

## "Heart pounded in her chest"

### 21.
**Bad:**
Her heart pounded in her chest.

**Good:**
Her pulse hammered at the base of her throat — she could feel it there, exposed, obvious.

**Why:** "In her chest" is always redundant (where else?). Moving the pulse to the throat makes it vulnerable and externally visible, adding stakes.

### 22.
**Bad:**
She could feel her heart pounding.

**Good:**
Everything was too loud — her breathing, her blood, the creak of her leather glove as her hand tightened on the hilt.

**Why:** "Could feel her heart pounding" is a filter word + cliché combo. The replacement distributes the panic across multiple sensory channels.

### 23.
**Bad:**
Her heart hammered against her ribs.

**Good:**
Something in her chest was doing its best to climb out through her sternum.

**Why:** "Hammered against her ribs" is a slight variation of the cliché. The replacement is visceral and slightly grotesque, which matches the intensity of real fear better than percussion metaphors.

### 24.
**Bad:**
Her heart rate spiked as she heard the sound.

**Good:**
The sound came and her whole body went taut — wrists, jaw, the tendons in her neck standing out like wire.

**Why:** "Heart rate spiked" is clinical. The replacement shows the startle response distributed across the body.

## "Bit her lip"

### 25.
**Bad:**
She bit her lip nervously.

**Good:**
She caught herself pressing her thumbnail into the pad of her finger — hard enough to leave a crescent.

**Why:** Lip-biting is the default nervousness/desire gesture. Thumbnail-into-fingertip is a less common self-stimulating behavior that feels more specific and real.

### 26.
**Bad:**
Maren bit her lower lip, considering.

**Good:**
Maren ran her tongue along the inside of her cheek, the way she did when numbers weren't adding up.

**Why:** Lip-biting is generic. Tongue-against-cheek is a character-specific thinking habit linked to calculation.

### 27.
**Bad:**
She bit her lip to hold back a moan.

**Good:**
The sound that wanted out of her she trapped behind her teeth, swallowed down, felt it hum in her chest instead.

**Why:** In explicit scenes, "bit her lip" is even more overused. The replacement traces the suppressed sound through the body — trapped, swallowed, humming — which is more sensory.

### 28.
**Bad:**
She bit her lip, trying not to cry.

**Good:**
She pressed her tongue to the roof of her mouth and breathed through her nose and thought about absolutely nothing until the burning behind her eyes dimmed.

**Why:** The replacement shows the WORK of not crying — the specific techniques a person uses to hold it together. This feels earned rather than performative.

## "Stomach dropped / flipped"

### 29.
**Bad:**
Her stomach dropped.

**Good:**
The ground under her shifted — not literally, but her balance went wrong for a second, as though the floor had opinions about what she'd just heard.

**Why:** "Stomach dropped" is the generic dread response. The replacement externalizes the sensation as a balance disruption, which is how dread actually feels physically.

### 30.
**Bad:**
She felt her stomach flip.

**Good:**
A coldness pooled in her gut, slow and certain, like ink dropped in water.

**Why:** "Stomach flip" is vague. The simile "ink in water" gives the dread a visual quality and a speed (slow, spreading, staining).

### 31.
**Bad:**
Her stomach lurched when she saw the letter.

**Good:**
The letter was face-up on the table. She recognised the seal before she recognised the dread — the dread came second, an aftershock.

**Why:** Instead of describing a stomach sensation, the replacement shows the temporal experience of recognition preceding emotion, which is psychologically accurate.

---

## "Tears pricked her eyes"

### 32.
**Bad:**
Tears pricked her eyes.

**Good:**
Her vision went bright at the edges. She blinked it back.

**Why:** "Tears pricked" is the most common pre-crying cliché. The replacement describes the visual effect of tears forming without naming them.

### 33.
**Bad:**
She felt tears prick the corners of her eyes.

**Good:**
Her throat closed. The tightness started at the base of her jaw and worked downward, and she had about three seconds before it won.

**Why:** "Felt tears prick" is a filter word + cliché. The replacement focuses on the throat tightness that precedes tears, which is the body's actual warning signal.

### 34.
**Bad:**
Tears pricked her eyes, but she refused to let them fall.

**Good:**
She tipped her head back and stared at the ceiling until the world went flat and manageable again.

**Why:** "Refused to let them fall" is narrating willpower. The replacement shows the specific physical technique she uses to prevent crying (head back, ceiling focus).

### 35.
**Bad:**
Hot tears pricked behind her eyelids.

**Good:**
Something was building behind her face — pressure, heat, the kind that came with the realisation that she'd been right to be afraid.

**Why:** "Hot tears" is a redundancy (tears are body temperature). The replacement builds the pre-crying sensation as pressure and heat, linked to an emotional realisation.

---

## "A smile tugged at her lips"

### 36.
**Bad:**
A smile tugged at her lips.

**Good:**
She turned away before her face betrayed her.

**Why:** "Smile tugged" personifies the smile as an external agent. The replacement shows the same near-smile through the character's active suppression of it.

### 37.
**Bad:**
A reluctant smile tugged at the corner of her mouth.

**Good:**
She tried to keep her face stern. Failed. Felt the failure, warm and traitorous, spreading through her chest.

**Why:** The replacement takes the physical event (fighting a smile) and adds emotional dimension — she FEELS the failure of composure, and it's warm rather than unwelcome.

### 38.
**Bad:**
She couldn't stop the smile that tugged at her lips.

**Good:**
Something in her face cracked open. Not a smile — not yet — but the ground shifting before one.

**Why:** "Couldn't stop" + "tugged at" is double passivity. The geological metaphor (ground shifting) gives the near-smile tectonic weight.

---

## "Blood ran cold"

### 39.
**Bad:**
Her blood ran cold.

**Good:**
The temperature in the room hadn't changed. She was the one who'd gotten colder.

**Why:** "Blood ran cold" is a dead metaphor. The replacement literalises the sensation and pins it to the character rather than the blood.

### 40.
**Bad:**
She felt her blood run cold when she saw the mark.

**Good:**
The mark. She knew it. She knew it and her body knew it before her mind caught up — skin tightening, breath stopping, a stillness she hadn't chosen.

**Why:** Filter word + dead metaphor. The replacement shows the fear response as body-leading-mind, with specific physical reactions.

### 41.
**Bad:**
A chill ran through her veins.

**Good:**
The cold that came wasn't weather. It started in her fingertips and worked inward, and she knew this cold — it was the kind that meant she'd understood something she wished she hadn't.

**Why:** "Chill ran through her veins" is the anatomical version of the cliché. The replacement gives the cold direction (fingertips to center) and meaning (unwanted understanding).

---

## "Furrowed her brow"

### 42.
**Bad:**
She furrowed her brow in confusion.

**Good:**
She read the passage again. Then once more, lips moving. The words were clear enough individually; it was the arrangement of them that refused to resolve.

**Why:** "Furrowed brow in confusion" is telling twice. The replacement shows confusion through the action of re-reading and the nature of the difficulty.

### 43.
**Bad:**
Maren furrowed her brow, deep in thought.

**Good:**
Maren went quiet — the dangerous kind of quiet, the kind that meant she was dismantling something in her head and would emerge with either a plan or an argument.

**Why:** The replacement turns a facial expression into a personality-revealing silence. "Dangerous quiet" tells us more about Maren than a furrowed brow ever could.

## Stacking Multiple Clichés in One Paragraph

### 44.
**Bad:**
She clenched her jaw, her heart pounding in her chest. Tears pricked her eyes as her stomach dropped. She crossed her arms and took a breath she didn't know she'd been holding.

**Good:**
Everything hit at once. She sat down — not because she chose to but because her knees made the decision for her. The chair caught her. The room swam. Someone was talking and she couldn't parse the words over the noise inside her own skull.

**Why:** Five clichés in three sentences. The replacement shows overwhelm through loss of physical control (knees giving out), sensory overload (room swimming), and cognitive impairment (can't parse words). Each detail is concrete and none are stock.

### 45.
**Bad:**
Her blood ran cold as she bit her lip, her heart hammering against her ribs. She clenched her fists, tears pricking the corners of her eyes.

**Good:**
The letter lay on the table between them and neither reached for it. Her hands felt strange — distant, as though they belonged to someone else. The ink blurred. She realised she was crying only when she tasted salt.

**Why:** Four clichés in two sentences. The replacement shows fear through dissociation (hands feeling distant), and sadness through the surprising discovery of her own tears via taste rather than the stock "pricking" sensation.

### 46.
**Bad:**
Maren raised an eyebrow, arms crossed. Tiku smiled nervously, biting her lip. Yanna frowned, her stomach in knots.

**Good:**
Maren leaned back and waited — patient as a judge, giving nothing. Tiku's fingers found her collar and started worrying the fabric, a habit she'd never managed to break. Yanna stood very still, but the kind of still that cost something.

**Why:** Three characters, three cliché packages. The replacement gives each a distinctive response: Maren's deliberate composure, Tiku's fabric-worrying tic, Yanna's expensive stillness.

### 47.
**Bad:**
She raised an eyebrow, then frowned. Her heart skipped a beat. A smile tugged at her lips before her stomach dropped. She clenched her jaw.

**Good:**
Three emotions in the space of a breath: surprise, then something sharp and pleased, then the bottom falling out. She closed her eyes. When she opened them, whatever had shown on her face was gone.

**Why:** Five clichés in rapid succession trying to convey emotional whiplash. The replacement names the emotional sequence abstractly, then shows the character COMPOSING herself — which is the actually interesting part.

### 48.
**Bad:**
Tears sprang to her eyes. She clenched her fists. Her jaw tightened. Her heart pounded.

**Good:**
It hit her like a wall. Not the news — she'd expected the news, had been bracing for it for days — but the way the room went sharp-edged and too bright afterward, as though someone had turned up the contrast on everything. She sat very still and waited for the world to dull again.

**Why:** Four clichés in a row creating a checklist of emotional responses. The replacement conveys shock through a single vivid sensory shift (the world going sharp and too-bright) and shows the character's coping strategy (waiting for it to pass).

### 49.
**Bad:**
She bit her lip, stomach flipping, pulse racing. She crossed her arms and tried to steady her breathing.

**Good:**
Her body was doing all the things bodies do when they're afraid and she wasn't interested in cataloguing them. She gripped the edge of the table and held on.

**Why:** Meta-aware dodge: the character refuses to itemize her own fear response, which is actually more psychologically honest than the cliché checklist. Then one concrete action (gripping the table) grounds the scene.

### 50.
**Bad:**
She clenched her jaw and balled her fists, her breath coming fast. Her eyes narrowed and her nostrils flared. She bit the inside of her cheek hard enough to taste copper.

**Good:**
Rage was a slow thing in her — never fast, never clean. It started as a heat in her chest and climbed, vertebra by vertebra, until it sat at the base of her skull and hummed. By the time it reached her hands, she was already moving.

**Why:** Five physical anger signals listed in sequence, reading like a police report. The replacement traces anger as a single rising sensation with a specific path through the body, building to action. The "vertebra by vertebra" makes it embodied and specific.
