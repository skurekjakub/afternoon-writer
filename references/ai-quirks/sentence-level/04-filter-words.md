# Filter Words

In limited third POV, everything described IS what the character perceives. Adding "she saw," "she heard," "she noticed," "she realized" inserts a cognitive step between the reader and the experience — like watching a movie through a window instead of being in the room. The filter word is almost always deletable. Remove it, and the sentence gets closer to the reader's face.

The broader family: noticed, realized, saw, heard, watched, decided, wondered, knew, observed, thought, remembered. Each creates the same distance. Each is the narrator saying "the character perceived X" instead of just giving us X.

**Hard limit:** Any filter word from this list — max 3 uses per chapter total (not per word — total combined). Every use must survive the test: "Does this filter word add information, or can I delete it and lose nothing?"

---

## "She noticed"

### 1.
**Bad:**
She noticed the candles had been lit.

**Good:**
The candles were lit. Someone had been here.

**Why:** Deleting "she noticed" loses nothing — we're in her POV, so everything described is what she notices. The follow-up thought ("someone had been here") adds inference the filter word didn't.

### 2.
**Bad:**
She noticed a scar running along Maren's collarbone.

**Good:**
A scar ran along Maren's collarbone — pale, thin, the kind left by something sharp and deliberate.

**Why:** The scar is more vivid without the filter. Adding specific detail (pale, thin, deliberate) does more work than "she noticed" ever could.

### 3.
**Bad:**
She noticed that the room smelled different today.

**Good:**
The room smelled different today. Smoke, and under it something greener — herbs, maybe, or rain-soaked wood.

**Why:** "She noticed that" is five words of nothing. Cut them, add sensory detail.

### 4.
**Bad:**
She noticed Tiku hadn't touched her food.

**Good:**
Tiku's plate sat untouched, the stew cooling to a skin.

**Why:** The observation is more pointed without the filter. The cooling stew adds time (it's been sitting there a while).

### 5.
**Bad:**
She noticed the way Maren's hand trembled.

**Good:**
Maren's hand trembled. A fine, almost invisible vibration, the kind you only caught if you were watching for it.

**Why:** "She noticed the way" is a double filter (noticed + the way). The replacement commits to the tremor and adds the detail that it's subtle — implying she WAS watching closely, which says more about the relationship.

### 6.
**Bad:**
She noticed a change in the air.

**Good:**
The air shifted. Heavier, warmer, carrying the copper scent of something she didn't want to name.

**Why:** "She noticed a change" is vague. The replacement tells us what changed and adds ominous specificity.

## "She realized"

### 7.
**Bad:**
She realized she was holding her breath.

**Good:**
She was holding her breath. Had been for — how long? She forced her lungs to work.

**Why:** "She realized" frames a body state as a cognitive event. The replacement stays in the body — the held breath, the uncertainty about duration, the deliberate release.

### 8.
**Bad:**
She realized the door was locked.

**Good:**
The door didn't move. She tried again, harder. Locked.

**Why:** "She realized" tells us the conclusion. The replacement shows the discovery process — the attempt, the resistance, the single-word verdict.

### 9.
**Bad:**
She suddenly realized what Maren had been trying to tell her.

**Good:**
It hit her — late, stupid late, the kind of late where you wonder how you missed it. Maren hadn't been warning her. She'd been saying goodbye.

**Why:** "Suddenly realized" is a telling shortcut. The replacement makes the lateness of the realization part of the character's self-assessment and then delivers the content of the realization as its own punching line.

### 10.
**Bad:**
She realized she had been wrong about everything.

**Good:**
The whole picture rotated. What she'd taken for loyalty was fear. What she'd called caution was cowardice. The shapes were the same; the labels had all been wrong.

**Why:** "Realized she'd been wrong about everything" is abstract. The replacement shows WHAT she was wrong about, using a visual metaphor (rotation) and specific reframings.

### 11.
**Bad:**
She realized with a start that she was crying.

**Good:**
Salt on her lips. When had that started? She wiped her face with the back of her hand, angry at the evidence.

**Why:** "Realized with a start" is a filter plus a shock modifier. The replacement discovers the crying through taste (salt), which is more sensory and more surprising.

### 12.
**Bad:**
She realized they were being followed.

**Good:**
The footsteps behind them had matched their pace for three streets. When they stopped, the footsteps stopped. When they turned, so did the footsteps.

**Why:** "She realized" collapses the discovery into a single moment. The replacement dramatizes the evidence chain — how she came to know — which is more tense.

## "She saw / watched / observed"

### 13.
**Bad:**
She saw the blood on the floor.

**Good:**
Blood on the floor. Smeared, not pooled — someone had been dragged.

**Why:** "She saw" is the most basic filter. Cut it, add the forensic detail that makes the blood tell a story.

### 14.
**Bad:**
She watched Maren cross the room.

**Good:**
Maren crossed the room — slowly, deliberately, each step placed like she was walking on something breakable.

**Why:** "She watched" is passive observation. The replacement shows HOW Maren crossed, which reveals Maren's state (careful, tense) without filtering through the watcher.

### 15.
**Bad:**
She watched the sun set over the valley.

**Good:**
The valley filled with gold, then amber, then a deep rust that turned the river to copper. By the time the light died, she was cold and hadn't moved.

**Why:** "She watched" makes the character a passive observer. The replacement puts us inside the sunset and adds time-compression through the character's stillness and cold.

### 16.
**Bad:**
She observed that Tiku kept touching her belt.

**Good:**
Tiku's hand kept finding her belt — the empty place where her knife should have been. Five times in the last minute.

**Why:** "She observed that" is clinical. The replacement adds the KEY detail (the empty sheath) and a count that shows how closely the POV character is tracking this.

### 17.
**Bad:**
She saw Maren flinch.

**Good:**
Maren flinched. Tiny, contained, the kind of flinch you train yourself out of and never quite manage.

**Why:** "She saw" adds nothing. The expansion after the flinch (trained but imperfect composure) adds a paragraph's worth of characterization.

### 18.
**Bad:**
She could see the outline of the fortress from here.

**Good:**
The fortress. A dark shape against the darker sky, close enough now that she could pick out individual towers.

**Why:** "She could see" is a filter + could-construction. The replacement places us at the character's vantage point and adds specificity (individual towers = real proximity).

## "She heard"

### 19.
**Bad:**
She heard footsteps approaching.

**Good:**
Footsteps. Approaching from the corridor, unhurried.

**Why:** "She heard" is the filter; the sentence works as a fragment with more tension. "Unhurried" adds characterization of whoever is approaching.

### 20.
**Bad:**
She heard a voice she recognized.

**Good:**
A voice — and recognition hit before the words did, before she could even place the pitch. She knew that voice the way she knew the sound of her own name.

**Why:** "She heard a voice she recognized" compresses a complex emotional moment into a flat statement. The replacement slows down the recognition process, making it visceral.

### 21.
**Bad:**
She heard the crack of thunder in the distance.

**Good:**
Thunder — distant, a low roll that ran along the horizon and died. The storm was moving east. Away from them, for now.

**Why:** "She heard the crack of" is filter + filler. The replacement puts us in the sound and adds tactical assessment (moving east).

### 22.
**Bad:**
She heard Maren mutter something under her breath.

**Good:**
Maren muttered something. Too quiet to catch, but the tone carried well enough — sharp and thoroughly disgusted.

**Why:** "She heard" removed, but the important information preserved: she couldn't make out the words but could read the tone.

### 23.
**Bad:**
She could hear the river from here.

**Good:**
The river. Close enough to hear the water working at the stones, a sound that should have been soothing and wasn't.

**Why:** "She could hear" is a filter + could construction. The replacement describes the specific quality of the sound and the character's response to it.

### 24.
**Bad:**
She heard a door slam somewhere above her.

**Good:**
A door slammed — somewhere above, two floors up, maybe three. Then silence, the particular silence that follows violence done to furniture.

**Why:** The fragment "A door slammed" has more impact than "She heard a door slam." The follow-up adds wit ("violence done to furniture").

## "She knew"

### 25.
**Bad:**
She knew this wasn't going to end well.

**Good:**
This wasn't going to end well. She could feel it the way you feel weather turning — a pressure change behind the eyes, a wrongness in the wind.

**Why:** "She knew" tells us about her certainty. The replacement shows us what certainty FEELS like in the body.

### 26.
**Bad:**
She knew Maren was lying.

**Good:**
Maren was lying. The tell was in the hands — still, deliberately still, the way they only went when Maren was working to keep them calm.

**Why:** "She knew" states a conclusion. The replacement shows the evidence that leads to the conclusion.

### 27.
**Bad:**
She knew she should leave, but something kept her rooted.

**Good:**
Leave. She should leave. Her legs didn't agree.

**Why:** "She knew she should" is the narrator summarising an internal argument. The replacement dramatises it — the imperative, the acknowledgment, the body's refusal.

### 28.
**Bad:**
She knew it was a trap.

**Good:**
A trap. Everything about it — the open door, the unguarded chest, the silence where there should have been guards — screamed trap. She went in anyway.

**Why:** "She knew" is flat. Listing the evidence and then walking in anyway creates tension through the character's choice to proceed despite knowing.

### 29.
**Bad:**
She knew without looking that Tiku was behind her.

**Good:**
Tiku. Behind her and to the left, close enough that she could smell the sword oil. She didn't need to look.

**Why:** "She knew without looking" is the narrator explaining a cool moment. The replacement shows it through another sense (smell) and THEN states the confidence.

## "She decided"

### 30.
**Bad:**
She decided to tell the truth.

**Good:**
The truth, then. She was tired enough for it.

**Why:** "She decided" reports a decision like a meeting minute. The replacement frames the decision as an act of exhaustion-driven honesty, which is more interesting.

### 31.
**Bad:**
She decided it wasn't worth arguing about.

**Good:**
Not worth it. She let the argument die in her mouth, swallowed it down with the last of her wine, and changed the subject.

**Why:** "She decided" is a cognitive summary. The replacement shows the decision through action — swallowing the words, the wine, the topic change.

### 32.
**Bad:**
She decided to take the risk.

**Good:**
The risk sat in front of her like a dare. She reached for it.

**Why:** "She decided" is passive. The metaphor (risk as dare, reaching for it) adds physicality and agency.

---

## "She wondered"

### 33.
**Bad:**
She wondered what would happen next.

**Good:**
What happened next was anyone's guess. She settled in to wait, and tried not to make too many of her own.

**Why:** "She wondered" is the blandest possible form of uncertainty. The replacement gives the uncertainty personality (deliberately avoiding speculation).

### 34.
**Bad:**
She wondered if Maren felt the same way.

**Good:**
Did Maren feel the same? Impossible to read — that face, that careful, composed face, which gave away nothing except that nothing was exactly what it wanted to give.

**Why:** "She wondered" reports a question. The replacement ASKS the question and then engages with why it's unanswerable.

### 35.
**Bad:**
She found herself wondering about the woman who had built this place.

**Good:**
The woman who had built this place — who was she? The kind of person who chose stone over wood, windows that faced east, a staircase wide enough for two. Every choice had a voice; she just couldn't read the language yet.

**Why:** "Found herself wondering" is a double filter (found + wondering). The replacement lets the wondering happen through specific observations about the architecture.

---

## "She remembered"

### 36.
**Bad:**
She remembered the last time she'd stood in this room.

**Good:**
The last time she'd stood in this room, the fire had been lit and Maren had been alive and neither of those things had seemed remarkable. Now both felt like miracles that had expired.

**Why:** "She remembered" announces a flashback. The replacement drops us directly into the memory and uses the contrast (then vs. now) to create emotional impact.

### 37.
**Bad:**
She remembered her mother's voice.

**Good:**
Her mother's voice — low, smoky, the kind that carried lullabies and warnings in the same register. It came unbidden, the way it always did, in the quiet moments she forgot to guard.

**Why:** "She remembered" is flat. The replacement gives the voice specific qualities and then adds the insight that these memories ambush her.

### 38.
**Bad:**
She remembered being afraid of this place as a child.

**Good:**
As a child, this place had been the end of the world — dark corridors, ceilings too high to see, echoes that sounded like voices. Now it was just stone. The fear was still in the walls, though. She could feel it, faint, the way you feel an old bruise when you press it.

**Why:** "She remembered being afraid" is a summary. The replacement recreates the childhood fear in sensory detail and then shows how it lingers as a physical trace.

## Filter Word Chains

### 39.
**Bad:**
She saw the door was open. She heard footsteps inside. She noticed blood on the threshold. She realized someone had beaten her here.

**Good:**
The door, open. Footsteps inside — still moving, whoever it was. Blood on the threshold, a smear going in. Someone had beaten her here.

**Why:** Four filter words in sequence creates a surveillance-report narration. The replacement drops us into the scene through fragments and lets each observation land directly.

### 40.
**Bad:**
She heard the bell toll. She watched the crowd turn. She noticed the fear on their faces. She knew what was coming.

**Good:**
The bell tolled. The crowd turned — all of them, at once, like a flock startled from a field. Fear on every face. The same fear. The kind that meant they knew what was coming before she did.

**Why:** Four filters replaced with direct observations. The bird simile for the crowd's movement adds something the filter words never could.

### 41.
**Bad:**
She realized the room was dark. She noticed the fire had gone out. She felt a chill and heard the wind outside.

**Good:**
Dark. The fire — out, cold, not even embers. Wind shouldered its way through the shutters, carrying the kind of chill that settled in the bones.

**Why:** Four filters in a row create the sense of a character discovering basic facts about their environment one at a time, like a tourist. The replacement is immediate and atmospheric.

### 42.
**Bad:**
She watched Maren pour the wine. She noticed Maren's hand was steady. She thought about how different things might have been.

**Good:**
Maren poured the wine. Steady hand, steady pour, not a drop wasted. A year ago, those hands had shaken so badly she couldn't hold a cup. Neither of them mentioned it.

**Why:** Three filters removed. The "neither mentioned it" does more emotional work than "she thought about how different things might have been" because it shows the shared awareness without the narrator stepping in to explain.

### 43.
**Bad:**
She heard the silence first. Then she noticed the stillness. She realized something was wrong.

**Good:**
Silence. Not the absence of sound — the presence of it, a thick, deliberate hush that pressed against her ears like cotton. Nothing moved. Not the curtains, not the dust, nothing. Wrong. Everything about this was wrong.

**Why:** The three filters create a false progression (heard → noticed → realized). The replacement makes the silence itself the character, gives it physical qualities, and lets "wrong" land as a verdict.

### 44.
**Bad:**
She saw Tiku reach for her sword. She heard Maren's sharp intake of breath. She felt her own pulse spike.

**Good:**
Tiku's hand moved to her sword — fast, fluid, the gesture of someone who'd done it a thousand times and hoped to stop at a thousand. Maren's breath caught. The pulse in her own throat kicked hard enough to taste.

**Why:** Three filters narrating three different people's reactions. The replacement gives each reaction a physical detail that the filter word was blocking.

### 45.
**Bad:**
She could see the city below. She could hear the sounds of the market. She could smell the river. She thought about how long it had been.

**Good:**
The city spread below her — rooftops, smoke, the clatter of the market drifting up like birdsong. The river stank. She'd forgotten that, somehow, in the years away. You could romanticise a lot from a distance, but the river wouldn't cooperate.

**Why:** Four "she could" filters. The replacement merges sight and sound, gives the river's smell character (the stink refuses romance), and threads in the time-away without the narrator announcing it.

### 46.
**Bad:**
She noticed the guards were gone. She realized the gate was unbarred. She decided it was a trap. She knew she'd go anyway.

**Good:**
No guards. Gate unbarred. A trap so obvious it was practically waving. She went anyway. What else was there?

**Why:** Four filters replaced with four punchy fragments. The personality comes through in "practically waving" and the final rhetorical shrug.

### 47.
**Bad:**
She remembered his — her face. She felt the familiar ache. She knew it would pass. She decided to let it.

**Good:**
Her face. It came back the way it always did — not the whole of it, just pieces. The jaw. The way one eye crinkled more than the other. The scar. The ache was old by now, a dull thing that lived in her chest and mostly behaved. She let it be. Some pains you keep because putting them down means putting down everything attached to them.

**Why:** Four filters creating a mechanical emotional sequence. The replacement lets the memory fragment (specific pieces of the face), gives the ache a personality (mostly behaved), and adds the philosophy of keeping pain — all without a single filter word.

### 48.
**Bad:**
She thought about what to do next. She considered her options. She decided on the simplest approach.

**Good:**
Three options. Two of them clever, one of them blunt. She went with blunt. Clever was for people with time.

**Why:** Three filters narrating a decision process. The replacement shows the same process in four short sentences with personality.

### 49.
**Bad:**
She saw the letter. She noticed it was addressed to her. She felt a cold dread settle in her stomach. She knew what it contained.

**Good:**
The letter. Her name, in handwriting she recognised. She didn't open it. She didn't need to. The seal was enough — the seal, and the fact that they'd sent it at all.

**Why:** Four filters walking through a discovery sequence. The replacement creates tension through what she DOESN'T do (doesn't open it) and what she already knows (the seal tells her everything).

### 50.
**Bad:**
She heard the approaching footsteps. She saw the shadow under the door. She realized she'd been found. She felt her body tense.

**Good:**
Footsteps. Shadow under the door. Found.

Her body knew before her mind caught up — muscles locking, breath stopping, every instinct pulling her toward the window.

**Why:** Four wordy filter sentences collapsed to three words. The body's response gets its own paragraph, showing it as a separate, faster system than conscious thought.
