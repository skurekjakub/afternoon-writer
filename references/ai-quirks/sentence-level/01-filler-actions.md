# Filler Actions

Characters do things between lines of dialogue. The problem is when they always do the SAME things — nod, sigh, smile, shrug, glance, frown — five or six puppet-string gestures that convey nothing specific about who this person is, where they are, or what they're feeling beyond the broadest strokes. These are the body-language equivalent of "said" — except "said" is invisible and these aren't. They draw the eye, promise characterization, and deliver nothing.

AI defaults to these because they're statistically dominant in training data. They're safe, universal, and require no knowledge of the character or the environment. That's exactly why they fail.

**Hard limit:** Any single filler verb (nodded, sighed, smiled, shrugged, glanced, frowned) — max 3 uses per chapter total. Combined filler verbs from this list — max 8 per chapter.

---

## Nodded — Agreement Padding

### 1.
**Bad:**
She nodded. "That makes sense."

**Good:**
"That makes sense." She was already pulling the map closer, tracing the route with one finger.

**Why:** The nod adds nothing — the dialogue already conveys agreement. The replacement shows agreement through action that also advances the scene.

### 2.
**Bad:**
Maren nodded slowly. "I think you're right."

**Good:**
Maren turned the idea over for a moment, her thumb pressed to her lower lip. "I think you're right."

**Why:** "Nodded slowly" tries to convey thoughtfulness but reads as stage direction. The replacement shows the thinking through a specific, personal gesture.

### 3.
**Bad:**
"We leave at dawn," Tiku said.
Yanna nodded.

**Good:**
"We leave at dawn," Tiku said.
Yanna was already checking her pack straps.

**Why:** A wordless nod is the most invisible kind — the reader's eye slides over it. A character-specific action (checking gear) conveys agreement AND reveals who this person is (someone who prepares rather than talks).

### 4.
**Bad:**
She nodded in understanding.

**Good:**
Her expression shifted — the tight line between her brows easing as the pieces clicked.

**Why:** "Nodded in understanding" is telling twice: the nod AND the explanation. The replacement shows the moment of understanding landing physically.

### 5.
**Bad:**
They both nodded in agreement.

**Good:**
Neither argued. Tiku reached for her boots; Yanna was already sheathing her blade.

**Why:** "They both nodded" is synchronised puppetry. The replacement gives each character a distinct response to the same decision.

### 6.
**Bad:**
She nodded, her expression serious.

**Good:**
She held the gaze a beat longer than comfortable. Said nothing. Didn't need to.

**Why:** "Nodded, her expression serious" stacks a filler action with an emotional label. The replacement creates weight through silence and eye contact.

### 7.
**Bad:**
"Understood," she said, nodding.

**Good:**
"Understood." She folded the letter and slipped it inside her gauntlet.

**Why:** The dialogue already says "understood" — the nod is redundant. The replacement shows her securing the information physically.

### 8.
**Bad:**
He — she nodded. "I'll handle it."

**Good:**
"I'll handle it." Her hand was already on the door.

**Why:** When a character says they'll do something and simultaneously moves to do it, the reader trusts the commitment without a nod.

## Sighed — Resignation / Exhaustion Default

### 9.
**Bad:**
She sighed heavily. "Not again."

**Good:**
She pressed two fingers to the bridge of her nose. "Not again."

**Why:** "Sighed heavily" is the default AI sound effect for weariness. The pinched bridge is specific, visual, and conveys the same exhaustion through a concrete physical act.

### 10.
**Bad:**
Maren sighed and leaned back in her chair.

**Good:**
Maren let her head fall against the chair back, staring at the ceiling as if it owed her something.

**Why:** The sigh is generic. The specific posture and the "as if" observation give this character a personality — someone who deflects frustration through mild dramatics.

### 11.
**Bad:**
She let out a long sigh.

**Good:**
The breath she released was slow enough to count.

**Why:** Same physical event, different camera angle. "A long sigh" is a label; "slow enough to count" puts the reader inside the experience.

### 12.
**Bad:**
"Fine," she sighed.

**Good:**
"Fine." The word came out flat, emptied of fight.

**Why:** "She sighed" after dialogue is a mood indicator that the dialogue should carry on its own. Describing HOW the word sounded achieves more.

### 13.
**Bad:**
She sighed with relief.

**Good:**
Her shoulders dropped an inch. Two.

**Why:** "Sighed with relief" is telling the reader what to feel. The physical detail — shoulders dropping, the deliberate "an inch. Two." pacing — lets the reader feel the relief through the body.

### 14.
**Bad:**
With a weary sigh, she stood up.

**Good:**
She stood like someone who'd been sitting too long and would be sitting again soon.

**Why:** "Weary sigh" is an adverb wearing a noun costume. The replacement conveys weariness through the quality of the movement.

### 15.
**Bad:**
She sighed, running a hand through her hair.

**Good:**
She scraped her hair back from her face with both hands and held it there, staring at the wall.

**Why:** "Sighed + hand through hair" is two stock gestures in one sentence. The replacement is specific — both hands, holding the pose, staring — and conveys overwhelm without naming it.

### 16.
**Bad:**
"I know," she sighed.

**Good:**
"I know." Quietly, like she'd known for a while and had been hoping someone else would say it first.

**Why:** The attribution "she sighed" flattens the moment. The follow-up line gives us her internal state through inference, not label.

## Smiled — Positive-Emotion Catch-All

### 17.
**Bad:**
She smiled. "Thank you."

**Good:**
"Thank you." The warmth in it was real — the first real thing she'd said all evening.

**Why:** A smile is the most overused positive signal in fiction. The replacement skips the facial expression entirely and tells us something about the character's state AND the scene's history.

### 18.
**Bad:**
Maren smiled at her reassuringly.

**Good:**
Maren squeezed her wrist — quick, firm, gone before she could object.

**Why:** "Smiled reassuringly" is telling the emotion and the intent simultaneously. Physical contact conveys reassurance more viscerally than a facial expression.

### 19.
**Bad:**
She couldn't help but smile.

**Good:**
Something loosened in her chest. Just a fraction.

**Why:** "Couldn't help but smile" is one of the most common AI constructions. The replacement moves INWARD — to the physical sensation of happiness arriving — rather than outward to the facial expression.

### 20.
**Bad:**
A small smile played on her lips.

**Good:**
Her mouth did something complicated — not quite a smile, not quite anything she'd admit to.

**Why:** "A small smile played on her lips" is purple cliché. The replacement gives the expression ambiguity and personality.

### 21.
**Bad:**
She smiled sadly.

**Good:**
The corner of her mouth lifted, but it didn't reach anywhere useful.

**Why:** "Smiled sadly" is an oxymoron shortcut. The replacement shows the disconnect between the expression and the emotion through spatial language.

### 22.
**Bad:**
They shared a smile.

**Good:**
Their eyes met. A look passed between them that neither would have been able to name and both understood perfectly.

**Why:** "Shared a smile" is a greeting card. The replacement preserves the wordless connection without reducing it to a single facial muscle.

### 23.
**Bad:**
She smiled knowingly.

**Good:**
She said nothing, but there was something in the tilt of her chin that suggested she'd already worked out the ending.

**Why:** "Smiled knowingly" is an adverb problem. The replacement shows knowledge through posture and implication.

### 24.
**Bad:**
He — she smiled warmly at the younger woman.

**Good:**
She watched the younger woman fumble with the clasp and felt the old, familiar tug — half amusement, half tenderness — that meant she was about to do something patient.

**Why:** "Smiled warmly" labels the emotion. The replacement takes us inside the POV character's experience of that warmth as it happens.

## Shrugged — Indifference Signal

### 25.
**Bad:**
She shrugged. "I don't care."

**Good:**
"I don't care." She turned back to the window, already elsewhere.

**Why:** The shrug and the dialogue say the same thing. The replacement shows indifference through disengagement rather than a stock gesture.

### 26.
**Bad:**
"Maybe," she shrugged.

**Good:**
"Maybe." A non-answer that did the work of a door closing.

**Why:** Attributing dialogue to "shrugged" is a said-bookism problem AND a filler problem. The metaphorical description conveys the dismissal more precisely.

### 27.
**Bad:**
She shrugged noncommittally.

**Good:**
She made a sound that could have meant anything and went back to sharpening her blade.

**Why:** "Shrugged noncommittally" is a redundancy — shrugs are inherently noncommittal. The replacement shows avoidance through action (returning to a task).

### 28.
**Bad:**
"Who knows?" She shrugged.

**Good:**
"Who knows?" She picked at a loose thread on her sleeve, not meeting anyone's eyes.

**Why:** The shrug is wasted visual real estate. The replacement uses a small, avoidant gesture that suggests the character knows more than she's saying.

### 29.
**Bad:**
Yanna shrugged and took another bite.

**Good:**
Yanna chewed, considering. Swallowed. "Pass the salt."

**Why:** The shrug is a filler between two actions. The replacement keeps the food (environmental grounding) and adds a deliberate avoidance of the topic through a mundane request.

### 30.
**Bad:**
She shrugged helplessly.

**Good:**
Her hands opened at her sides — palms up, empty. She had nothing.

**Why:** "Shrugged helplessly" is an adverb on a filler. The replacement describes the same physical gesture with more specificity and pathos.

## Glanced — Attention Redirect

### 31.
**Bad:**
She glanced at the door.

**Good:**
The door. She couldn't stop checking it.

**Why:** "Glanced at" is the most generic way to direct a character's attention. The replacement conveys the same eye movement plus anxiety through the admission "couldn't stop checking."

### 32.
**Bad:**
She glanced over at Maren.

**Good:**
Maren was doing that thing with her jaw again — the tight clench she probably didn't know she did.

**Why:** Instead of "she looked at Maren," we skip to what she SAW when she looked. The observation carries more characterization than the act of looking.

### 33.
**Bad:**
He — she glanced nervously around the room.

**Good:**
The exits. She counted them without meaning to. Two doors, one window, the window too high.

**Why:** "Glanced nervously" tells us the emotion. The replacement shows the nervous behavior — compulsive threat assessment — and reveals this character's background (military/survival training).

### 34.
**Bad:**
She glanced down at her hands.

**Good:**
Her hands were shaking. When had that started?

**Why:** The glance is a camera direction that adds nothing. Jumping to what she sees (and her surprise at seeing it) is tighter and more immersive.

### 35.
**Bad:**
They glanced at each other.

**Good:**
A look passed between them — the kind that had replaced words sometime around the second year.

**Why:** "They glanced at each other" is stage direction. The replacement gives the look history and weight.

### 36.
**Bad:**
She glanced at the clock and frowned.

**Good:**
Late. Later than she'd thought. The afternoon had slipped past while she wasn't paying attention.

**Why:** Two filler actions stacked ("glanced" + "frowned"). The replacement skips the looking and frowning entirely — we get the information (late) and her reaction (surprise at lost time) in one move.

## Frowned — Negative-Emotion Catch-All

### 37.
**Bad:**
She frowned. "That doesn't make sense."

**Good:**
"That doesn't make sense." She turned the coin over in her fingers, as though the answer might be on the other side.

**Why:** The dialogue already conveys confusion — the frown is redundant. The replacement adds a fidget that's both character-specific and thematically linked (examining the problem literally).

### 38.
**Bad:**
Maren frowned deeply.

**Good:**
Maren's whole face tightened — not anger, exactly. Closer to the expression of someone doing difficult math.

**Why:** "Frowned deeply" is an intensity modifier on a stock gesture. The comparison to "difficult math" gives us a specific quality of displeasure that a frown can't convey.

### 39.
**Bad:**
She frowned in confusion.

**Good:**
She read the line again. Then a third time, slower, as if the words might rearrange themselves into something that made sense.

**Why:** "Frowned in confusion" is telling twice. The replacement shows confusion through the specific action of re-reading.

### 40.
**Bad:**
"Hmm," she frowned.

**Good:**
"Hmm." A sound that meant she was chewing on something and hadn't decided whether to spit it out.

**Why:** Attributing a sound to "frowned" is a tag error. The replacement gives the "hmm" character through metaphor.

### 41.
**Bad:**
He — she frowned at the map.

**Good:**
The map was wrong. She could feel it — some proportion off, some distance lying — but she couldn't yet point to where.

**Why:** "Frowned at the map" is a camera direction. The replacement takes us inside her assessment process, giving us both the problem and her instinct.

### 42.
**Bad:**
She frowned, clearly unhappy with the answer.

**Good:**
The answer sat between them like a dead fish. Neither touched it.

**Why:** "Frowned, clearly unhappy" is triple-telling (frown + clearly + unhappy). The metaphor conveys displeasure AND the social dynamic of avoidance.

## Multiple Fillers in Rapid Succession

### 43.
**Bad:**
She sighed and glanced at Maren, who shrugged. Tiku frowned and nodded slowly. Yanna smiled.

**Good:**
The silence after the question was its own kind of answer. Maren wouldn't meet her eyes. Tiku's jaw worked, chewing on words she wasn't ready to say. Only Yanna seemed unbothered — but Yanna was always unbothered, and that meant nothing.

**Why:** Five filler actions in two sentences. The replacement gives each character a distinct reaction that reveals personality rather than performing generic emotion.

### 44.
**Bad:**
She nodded and smiled. "Good plan."
Maren shrugged and glanced away.
Tiku sighed and frowned.

**Good:**
"Good plan." She meant it, which surprised her — she'd walked in ready to argue.
Maren had already moved on, pulling her coat from the peg by the door.
Tiku said nothing. The quiet had a weight to it, the kind that meant she'd bring this up later, privately, when it would be harder to deflect.

**Why:** Three lines of puppet-string gestures replaced with three lines of distinct characterization. Each woman responds differently, and the responses reveal relationship dynamics.

### 45.
**Bad:**
She smiled and nodded. "Sounds good."
"Great," Maren smiled back.
Tiku nodded too. "Let's do it."

**Good:**
"Sounds good." She was already pulling her hair back, winding it into the knot she wore for work.
"Great." Maren drummed her fingers once on the table — her version of excitement, barely contained.
Tiku cracked her knuckles and stood. "Let's do it."

**Why:** Smile-nod-smile-nod is a tennis rally of stock gestures. The replacement gives each character a physical habit: hair-knot, finger-drum, knuckle-crack. Now the reader can tell who's acting without checking names.

## Same Filler Repeated Across a Scene

### 46.
**Bad:**
She sighed. [paragraph] ... She sighed again and rubbed her eyes. [paragraph] ... With another sigh, she turned back to the fire.

**Good:**
First use: She pressed her palms flat against the table and breathed out — long, deliberate, controlled.
Second use: She rubbed her eyes hard enough to see sparks.
Third use: The fire needed tending. She knelt beside it, feeding kindling one stick at a time, and tried to let the small task settle her.

**Why:** Three sighs in one scene becomes a tic. The replacement uses three different physical expressions of the same underlying exhaustion, each grounded in the environment.

### 47.
**Bad:**
Maren nodded. [paragraph] ... Maren nodded again. [paragraph] ... "I know," Maren nodded.

**Good:**
First use: Maren tipped her chin — go on.
Second use: "Mm." Maren's hand found the hilt of her sword, not drawing, just resting there the way she did when she was thinking.
Third use: "I know." Quiet. The kind of quiet that meant she'd already moved three steps ahead and was waiting for the others to catch up.

**Why:** Three nods from the same character in one scene is a characterization failure. The replacement builds a portrait of Maren through varied physical behaviors that all say "I'm with you" differently.

### 48.
**Bad:**
She smiled at the joke. [paragraph] ... She smiled when Tiku walked in. [paragraph] ... A smile crossed her face as she read the letter.

**Good:**
First use: The joke landed. She let it — a huff of air through her nose, almost a laugh.
Second use: Tiku in the doorway, rain-soaked and swearing. Something in her chest unclenched.
Third use: The letter. She read it twice, the second time slower, and when she set it down her hands were steady for the first time in days.

**Why:** Three smiles strip the expression of meaning through repetition. The replacement shows three distinct positive emotional moments — amusement, relief, hope — each conveyed through a different physical channel.

### 49.
**Bad:**
She glanced at the door. [paragraph] ... She glanced at her sword. [paragraph] ... She glanced at Maren, then back at the door.

**Good:**
First use: The door. Shut, bolted, but she could feel the night pressing against it.
Second use: Her sword leaned against the chair, close enough to grab. Not close enough.
Third use: Maren caught her eye and gave the barest shake of her head. Not yet.

**Why:** "Glanced at X" repeated three times is a security-camera narration style — tracking eye movements without conveying what they mean. The replacement shows what the character SEES and FEELS about each object.

### 50.
**Bad:**
She sighed. Maren nodded. Tiku shrugged. Yanna frowned.
[Later:] She nodded. Maren sighed. Tiku smiled. Yanna glanced away.

**Good:**
The four of them sat with it — the decision and its weight. Nobody spoke. Maren cracked a walnut with her bare hands, a trick she did when she needed to occupy her fingers. Tiku was cleaning under her nails with a knife point, a habit that looked casual and wasn't. Yanna stared at the fire, but her eyes were unfocused, seeing something none of them could.

She was the one who finally moved. Chair pushed back. Hands flat on the table. "Dawn, then."

**Why:** The bad example runs the entire cast through the filler rotation — twice. The replacement gives each character a distinctive physical behavior rooted in personality (Maren's hands, Tiku's knife, Yanna's thousand-yard stare) and lets the POV character break the silence with decisive action.
