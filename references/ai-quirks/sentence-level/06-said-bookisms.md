# Said Bookisms & Dialogue Attribution

AI models are terrified of "said." They reach for exclaimed, murmured, breathed, intoned, retorted — words that draw attention to themselves, that tell the reader HOW the line was delivered instead of trusting the line to do that work. They also over-attribute, attaching a tag or action beat to every line even when two characters are trading back and forth and the reader knows perfectly well who's speaking.

Good dialogue attribution is invisible. "Said" disappears. A well-placed action beat grounds the speaker in the scene. And sometimes — often — no tag at all is the strongest choice.

**Hard limit:** Said bookisms (any dialogue tag verb other than said/asked) — max 2 per chapter. Adverb-on-tag — max 1 per chapter. Action beats on every line — never more than 3 consecutive attributed lines.

---

## Said Bookisms

### 1.
**Bad:**
"We need to move," she exclaimed urgently.

**Good:**
"We need to move." She was already on her feet.

**Why:** "Exclaimed urgently" is redundant — the dialogue and the action convey urgency. The action beat (already on her feet) is more vivid than any tag.

### 2.
**Bad:**
"I never agreed to this," Maren declared firmly.

**Good:**
"I never agreed to this," Maren said.

**Why:** "Declared firmly" tells us Maren is resolute. The line itself tells us that. "Said" gets out of the way and lets the words land.

### 3.
**Bad:**
"You're lying," she hissed through clenched teeth.

**Good:**
"You're lying." Quiet. The kind of quiet that's worse than shouting.

**Why:** "Hissed through clenched teeth" is a stock construction. The replacement lets silence be the delivery method — more threatening, more specific.

### 4.
**Bad:**
"Perhaps we should reconsider," she murmured softly.

**Good:**
"Perhaps we should reconsider." She said it to her hands, not to the room.

**Why:** "Murmured softly" is redundant (murmuring is already soft) and tells us nothing. "Said it to her hands" shows us the speaker's posture and confidence level.

### 5.
**Bad:**
"That's enough!" Tiku bellowed across the room.

**Good:**
"That's enough!" The words hit the far wall. Everyone stopped. Tiku didn't raise her voice often — when she did, even the furniture seemed to pay attention.

**Why:** "Bellowed across the room" is a flat description. The replacement shows the EFFECT of the volume and adds characterization (she rarely yells).

### 6.
**Bad:**
"I suppose you're right," she conceded reluctantly.

**Good:**
"I suppose you're right." The words tasted like gravel.

**Why:** "Conceded reluctantly" tells us the manner and the attitude. The metaphor (words tasting like gravel) puts us in the speaker's body.

### 7.
**Bad:**
"Get out," she growled menacingly.

**Good:**
"Get out." Two words. Flat, low, with nothing behind them except the absolute certainty that she meant it.

**Why:** "Growled menacingly" stacks a bookism with a redundant adverb. The replacement is more threatening because it describes the QUALITY of the delivery.

### 8.
**Bad:**
"How fascinating," she intoned drily.

**Good:**
"How fascinating," she said, in a tone that made clear nothing about this was fascinating.

**Why:** "Intoned drily" is showing off. The replacement achieves the same deadpan effect with plainer language and a touch of humour.

### 9.
**Bad:**
"I didn't mean—" she stammered nervously.

**Good:**
"I didn't mean—" She stopped. Started again. Stopped again. Whatever she meant, she couldn't find the shape of it.

**Why:** "Stammered nervously" tells us about the delivery. The replacement shows the stammer in action and adds the character's frustration with herself.

### 10.
**Bad:**
"Run," she breathed against Maren's ear.

**Good:**
"Run." Barely a word — more a shape made with her mouth against Maren's ear, warm and urgent.

**Why:** "Breathed" as a dialogue tag is AI-literary. The replacement keeps the intimacy (mouth against ear) while making the whisper physical.

### 11.
**Bad:**
"You always do this," she retorted sharply.

**Good:**
"You always do this," she said. And there it was — the fight they'd been circling for an hour, finally arriving.

**Why:** "Retorted sharply" tells us the line is a counter-attack. The replacement uses "said" and then adds the real information: this argument was inevitable.

### 12.
**Bad:**
"I'll find another way," she vowed solemnly.

**Good:**
"I'll find another way." She said it like a prayer, or a threat, or both.

**Why:** "Vowed solemnly" is a narrator label. The replacement gives us ambiguity about whether the statement is devotion or menace — more interesting.

### 13.
**Bad:**
"Watch yourself," she warned ominously.

**Good:**
"Watch yourself." She smiled when she said it. That was the worst part.

**Why:** "Warned ominously" tells us to be scared. The smile does that job better, because threats delivered with smiles are more frightening than threats delivered with frowns.

### 14.
**Bad:**
"Come here," she whispered seductively.

**Good:**
"Come here." Low, slow, the kind of voice that pools in the stomach.

**Why:** "Whispered seductively" tells us the intent. The replacement puts the effect in the listener's body (pooling in the stomach).

### 15.
**Bad:**
"Is that all?" she queried with raised eyebrows.

**Good:**
"Is that all?" One eyebrow. It did more work than the question.

**Why:** "Queried" is a bookism for "asked." The replacement makes the eyebrow the star — a single eyebrow is more specific (and more withering) than two.

## Adverb-on-Tag

### 16.
**Bad:**
"I'm fine," she said tiredly.

**Good:**
"I'm fine," she said. She sounded like someone who hadn't been fine in a long time.

**Why:** The adverb tells us HOW she said it. The replacement shows us the gap between the words and the delivery.

### 17.
**Bad:**
"Don't touch me," she said coldly.

**Good:**
"Don't touch me." Flat. Not angry — flat, which was worse.

**Why:** "Coldly" labels the emotional temperature. "Flat, which was worse" shows it and adds the POV character's assessment.

### 18.
**Bad:**
"I love you," she said hesitantly.

**Good:**
"I love you." A pause before it, and a longer one after. As if the words were a bridge she wasn't sure would hold.

**Why:** "Hesitantly" is a single-word summary. The replacement dramatises the hesitation through pauses and adds a metaphor that earns its place.

### 19.
**Bad:**
"We leave at dawn," she said authoritatively.

**Good:**
"We leave at dawn." She didn't phrase it as a question, and no one treated it as one.

**Why:** "Authoritatively" tells us she's in charge. The replacement shows authority through the group's response — no one questions her.

### 20.
**Bad:**
"I'm sorry," she said quietly.

**Good:**
"I'm sorry." Barely audible. She said it the way people say things they need to get out but don't want heard.

**Why:** "Quietly" is the most common adverb-on-tag. The replacement gives us the contradiction of speaking but not wanting to be heard.

### 21.
**Bad:**
"That's not fair," she said angrily.

**Good:**
"That's not fair." Her voice cracked on "fair." She cleared her throat and didn't repeat it.

**Why:** "Angrily" labels the emotion. The cracking voice on a specific word shows where the anger lives and the throat-clearing shows her trying to regain control.

### 22.
**Bad:**
"You shouldn't have come," she said sadly, shaking her head.

**Good:**
"You shouldn't have come." She looked at Maren the way you look at something beautiful and breakable that someone has placed too close to the edge.

**Why:** "Sadly, shaking her head" is tell + cliché gesture. The simile shows the mix of appreciation and dread without naming either.

### 23.
**Bad:**
"Follow me," she said confidently, striding forward.

**Good:**
"Follow me." She didn't look back to check if they would. They did.

**Why:** "Confidently, striding forward" narrates the confidence. Not looking back SHOWS it. "They did" confirms the authority.

### 24.
**Bad:**
"It's over," she said bitterly.

**Good:**
"It's over." She said it the way you say a word in a language you've just stopped speaking — carefully, with the accent already fading.

**Why:** "Bitterly" is a one-note emotion. The metaphor (a word in a dying language) captures something more complex: loss, distance, the beginning of forgetting.

### 25.
**Bad:**
"I remember," she said wistfully.

**Good:**
"I remember." A pause. She turned her cup in her hands, watching the dregs circle. "I remember everything."

**Why:** "Wistfully" labels the nostalgia. The cup-turning, the pause, the repetition — these show a woman sitting with memory.

## Action Beat Overload

### 26.
**Bad:**
"The gate is open." She drew her sword. "That shouldn't be possible." She checked the perimeter. "Someone's been here." She knelt to examine the tracks.

**Good:**
"The gate is open." She drew her sword. "That shouldn't be possible."

Tracks in the mud — fresh, two sets, heading in and not coming out. She knelt. "Someone's been here."

**Why:** A beat on every line creates a mechanical rhythm: speak-act-speak-act. The fix groups beats and lets some lines stand alone.

### 27.
**Bad:**
"I know what you're going to say." Maren crossed her arms. "Don't bother." She turned away. "I've heard it before." She moved to the window. "All of it."

**Good:**
"I know what you're going to say." Maren crossed her arms. "Don't bother. I've heard it before — all of it."

She turned to the window. Whatever she saw there, she preferred it to this conversation.

**Why:** Beat on every line + short fragments creates a staccato that feels like stage directions. Combining the dialogue and moving the action to its own paragraph gives the moment breathing room.

### 28.
**Bad:**
"We should go north." She pointed at the map. "The pass is still open." She traced the route with her finger. "We can make it in three days." She looked up. "If we leave now."

**Good:**
"We should go north." She traced the route on the map — the pass, the ridge, the descent into the valley. "Three days, if we leave now."

**Why:** Three action beats (pointed, traced, looked up) for what is essentially one continuous gesture. Combine them.

## Over-Attribution

### 29.
**Bad:**
"Where are you going?" Maren asked.
"Out," Tiku said.
"Out where?" Maren asked.
"Just out," Tiku said.
"That's not an answer," Maren said.
"It's the only one you're getting," Tiku said.

**Good:**
"Where are you going?"
"Out."
"Out where?"
"Just out."
"That's not an answer."
"It's the only one you're getting."

**Why:** Two speakers, rapid exchange, no ambiguity about who's saying what. The tags add nothing and slow the rhythm. Strip them all.

### 30.
**Bad:**
"Do you remember the river?" Maren asked.
"Which river?" Tiku replied.
"The one in Kaelith," Maren said.
"With the bridge?" Tiku asked.
"The bridge that collapsed," Maren clarified.
"I remember," Tiku said.

**Good:**
"Do you remember the river?"
"Which river?"
"The one in Kaelith. With the bridge."
"The bridge that collapsed."
"I remember." Tiku's hand found the scar on her knee without looking. She always touched it when Kaelith came up. A reflex, like pressing a bruise.

**Why:** Over-attributed and over-tagged. The fix strips tags, combines fragmented lines, and saves one action beat for the end — where it does real character work.

### 31.
**Bad:**
"We need to talk," Maren said.
"About what?" she asked.
"About last night," Maren said.
"What about it?" she asked.
"You know what," Maren said.

**Good:**
"We need to talk."
"About what?"
"About last night."
She knew. She asked anyway. "What about it?"
"You know what."

**Why:** Five tags, none necessary. The fix inserts one beat of interiority ("She knew. She asked anyway.") which is more informative than all five tags combined.

### 32.
**Bad:**
"Ready?" Maren asked.
"Ready," Tiku confirmed.
"On three," Maren instructed.
"Skip three," Tiku suggested. "Just go."

**Good:**
"Ready?"
"Ready."
"On three."
"Skip three." Tiku grinned. "Just go."

**Why:** Four tags for four lines. The only one that needs a beat is Tiku's grin, which adds personality.

### 33.
**Bad:**
"It's not safe," Maren warned.
"Nothing is safe," she responded.
"This is different," Maren insisted.
"How?" she challenged.
"Because this time, I can't follow you," Maren said softly.

**Good:**
"It's not safe."
"Nothing is safe."
"This is different."
"How?"
Maren didn't answer right away. When she did, her voice had dropped to something small and private. "Because this time, I can't follow you."

**Why:** Four bookism-laden tags (warned, responded, insisted, challenged) plus "said softly." The fix strips them and saves the characterization for the final, important line.

## Beat Repeats Dialogue

### 34.
**Bad:**
"I'm angry," she said, her voice full of anger.

**Good:**
"I'm angry." She said it calmly, which meant she was furious.

**Why:** The beat restates what the dialogue already communicated. The fix creates CONTRAST between words and delivery, which is more interesting.

### 35.
**Bad:**
"I'm scared," she whispered, fear evident in her eyes.

**Good:**
"I'm scared." She said it the way you state a fact — weather, directions, the price of grain. Flat and undeniable.

**Why:** The beat illustrates the dialogue's already-clear content. The fix makes the DELIVERY of the admission interesting.

### 36.
**Bad:**
"I don't trust her," she said, suspicion clear in her tone.

**Good:**
"I don't trust her." She paused. "I don't trust anyone right now, if I'm being honest."

**Why:** The beat adds nothing — of course suspicion is in the tone. The replacement widens the mistrust into something more revealing.

### 37.
**Bad:**
"This is beautiful," she said, admiring the view.

**Good:**
"This is beautiful." A pause. "I don't say that about many things."

**Why:** "Admiring the view" restates the dialogue. The follow-up line turns the observation into character information.

### 38.
**Bad:**
"I hate you," she spat, her face twisted with hatred.

**Good:**
"I hate you." She said it like she meant it. She did.

**Why:** "Spat" + "face twisted with hatred" is triple redundancy with the dialogue. The replacement's bluntness (she meant it, she did) is more chilling.

## Tagless Dialogue Done Well

### 39.
**Bad:**
"We should stop," Maren said.
"We can't," she replied.
"We're going to die out here if we don't," Maren said.
"We're going to die in there if we do," she pointed out.

**Good:**
"We should stop."
"We can't."
"We're going to die out here if we don't."
"We're going to die in there if we do."

Stalemate. They stood in the rain and weighed the two kinds of dying, and neither one blinked first.

**Why:** The exchange doesn't need any tags — the parallel structure keeps the speakers clear. The paragraph after lands harder because it follows clean, uninterrupted back-and-forth.

### 40.
**Bad:**
"Do you love her?" Tiku asked.
She hesitated before answering. "I don't know what that word means anymore," she replied honestly.
"That's not a no," Tiku observed.
"No," she agreed quietly. "It's not."

**Good:**
"Do you love her?"

A long silence. Rain on the tent. The fire popping.

"I don't know what that word means anymore."
"That's not a no."
"No. It's not."

**Why:** Tags and adverbs removed. The silence is filled with ambient sound instead of "she hesitated before answering." The final exchange is cleaner and more devastating without the "observed" and "agreed quietly."

### 41.
**Bad:**
"One more?" Maren offered, holding up the bottle.
"One more," she agreed, extending her cup.
"Last one," Maren said, pouring.
"You said that two cups ago," she teased.
"And I meant it both times," Maren replied with a grin.

**Good:**
"One more?"
"One more."
"Last one."
"You said that two cups ago."
"And I meant it both times."

Maren poured. The wine was too warm and not very good. They drank it like it was the finest thing they'd ever tasted, because it was tonight's wine, and tonight was tonight, and neither was coming back.

**Why:** Five lines, five tags, five beats — all unnecessary in a two-person exchange. The fix strips everything and puts the emotional weight in the paragraph after, where the cheap wine becomes a vehicle for the moment's fleeting quality.

### 42.
**Bad:**
"I'm leaving in the morning," she announced.
"I know," Maren said.
"You're not going to argue?" she asked, surprised.
"Would it change anything?" Maren asked.
"No," she admitted.
"Then no," Maren said simply. "I'm not going to argue."

**Good:**
"I'm leaving in the morning."
"I know."
"You're not going to argue?"
"Would it change anything?"
"No."
"Then no." Maren reached for her hand under the blanket, found it, held it. "I'm not going to argue."

**Why:** The fix uses one physical gesture at the moment of emotional significance — finding the hand under the blanket. One beat, one gesture, one moment. Everything else is clean tagless exchange.

## Mixed Attribution Problems

### 43.
**Bad:**
"I've seen worse," she chuckled darkly, running a hand through her hair. "Much worse, actually," she added, her eyes distant with memory.

**Good:**
"I've seen worse." A smile that didn't reach her eyes. "Much worse."

**Why:** Chuckled-as-tag (you can't chuckle words), adverb, action filler (running hand through hair), restated attribution, and "eyes distant with memory" — five problems in two sentences. The fix: one beat, no tags, no adverbs.

### 44.
**Bad:**
"Tell me everything," she demanded intensely, leaning forward in her chair, her hands gripping the armrests. "Don't leave anything out," she instructed firmly.

**Good:**
"Tell me everything." She leaned forward. "Don't leave anything out."

**Why:** Demanded intensely + instructed firmly + gripping armrests = the narration is working harder than the dialogue. The dialogue is already demanding. Let it.

### 45.
**Bad:**
"Oh," she breathed softly, her eyes widening with surprise as realization dawned on her face. "I see," she whispered quietly, nodding slowly in understanding.

**Good:**
"Oh." A silence. Then, quieter: "I see."

**Why:** Breathed softly + eyes widening + surprise + realization dawning + whispered quietly + nodding slowly + in understanding. Seven redundancies for a two-word discovery moment. The fix: let the silence and the drop in volume do the work.

### 46.
**Bad:**
"Stop!" she screamed loudly, her voice echoing through the corridor as she raised her hand in a commanding gesture. "Everyone just stop!" she repeated desperately.

**Good:**
"Stop."

The word filled the corridor. Everyone did.

**Why:** Screamed loudly (redundant), echoing (over-described), raised hand (unnecessary), commanding gesture (narrator label), repeated desperately (stacked attribution). The fix: one word, one effect.

### 47.
**Bad:**
"Maybe," she hedged cautiously, biting her lower lip thoughtfully. "Or maybe not," she added uncertainly, shrugging her shoulders.

**Good:**
"Maybe." She turned the idea over, looking for the catch. "Maybe not."

**Why:** Hedged cautiously + biting lip thoughtfully + added uncertainly + shrugging shoulders = four different ways of saying "she's unsure," plus the lip-bite and shrug are cliché gestures. The fix: one action (turning the idea over) that shows deliberation.

### 48.
**Bad:**
"Interesting," Maren mused thoughtfully, stroking her chin in contemplation. "Very interesting indeed," she murmured, her brow furrowed in deep thought.

**Good:**
"Interesting," Maren said.

The word sat between them. She didn't elaborate. In the silence, the fire popped, and outside, something with wings passed close to the window.

**Why:** Mused + thoughtfully + stroking chin + murmured + furrowed brow + deep thought = six thinking indicators. The fix uses silence and ambient detail to create the space where thought happens, without narrating the thinking.

### 49.
**Bad:**
"I can't believe you did that," she exclaimed in shock, her jaw dropping in disbelief.

**Good:**
"I can't believe you did that." She stared at Maren. Maren stared back. Neither of them blinked.

**Why:** Exclaimed in shock + jaw dropping in disbelief = three redundancies with dialogue that already expresses disbelief. The fix: a staring contest that creates tension.

### 50.
**Bad:**
"Thank you," she said gratefully, tears of gratitude glistening in her eyes. "I mean it," she added sincerely, her voice thick with emotion.

**Good:**
"Thank you." Her voice broke on the second word. She didn't try to fix it.

**Why:** Gratefully + tears of gratitude + glistening + sincerely + thick with emotion = the narrator drowning the moment in labels. The fix: one physical detail (voice breaking) and one choice (not fixing it). The restraint is the emotion.
