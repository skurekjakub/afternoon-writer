# Internal Monologue Overload

AI models love thinking. Given a choice between showing a character DO something and showing a character THINK about doing something, they'll choose thinking every time. The result: paragraphs of unbroken interiority where the character analyses their own feelings, explains their own motivations, and narrates their own emotional state as if writing a therapy journal. The body vanishes. The scene vanishes. What remains is a disembodied mind producing sentences about itself.

Good internal thought is brief, embedded in action, and often contradicted by what the character does. The character thinks "I should leave" and then stays. The character thinks "This doesn't bother me" while their hands shake. The tension between thought and action is where character lives. Pure thought, without that friction, is an essay.

**Hard limit:** No more than 3 consecutive sentences of pure interiority without an action beat, a sensory detail, or a dialogue interruption. Internal monologue must be INTERRUPTED by the world, not allowed to run free.

## Extended Thinking with No Action

### 1.
**Bad:**
She thought about what Maren had said. The words had carried a weight she hadn't expected, a gravity that pulled at something deep inside her. She wondered if Maren had meant it the way it sounded, or if she was reading too much into it. She had a tendency to do that — to find meaning where none was intended, to construct elaborate theories from insufficient evidence. It was a habit born of years of needing to anticipate threats, of living in a world where the space between words mattered more than the words themselves. She knew this about herself, and knowing it didn't stop her from doing it.

**Good:**
She thought about what Maren had said. Couldn't stop, actually. The words had a weight to them she hadn't expected.

She poured the tea. Watched the steam rise. Thought about it more. Poured Maren's cup too, out of habit, then remembered Maren wasn't here and set the second cup on the counter where it could cool and be useless and remind her of things she'd rather not be reminded of.

**Why:** Six consecutive sentences of pure thought. The fix breaks after three with an action beat (pouring tea) that itself becomes a vehicle for the emotion — pouring the second cup out of habit, the cup cooling uselessly. The action does more emotional work than the analysis did.

### 2.
**Bad:**
She knew she should tell the truth. It was the right thing to do, the honest thing, the thing that Maren deserved. But honesty had consequences, and she wasn't sure she was ready for those consequences. She weighed the options carefully. On one hand, the truth would free her from the burden of deception. On the other hand, the truth might destroy the fragile peace they'd built. She considered the possibility that Maren already knew, that the truth was already out there, living in the silence between them, and that her confession would merely be giving voice to something that had existed unspoken for months.

**Good:**
She should tell her. She knew she should tell her.

She picked up a stone from the path and threw it at the river. It didn't skip. Just sank. She picked up another and threw that too. Same result. She'd never been able to skip stones. Maren could — five, six bounces, casual, infuriating.

She picked up a third stone and held it. Smooth, warm from the sun. The truth was in her mouth, had been for weeks, sitting there like this stone in her palm — real, solid, ready to throw. She just couldn't find the angle.

**Why:** The bad version is eight sentences of deliberation. The fix: two sentences of thought, then stones. The stone-throwing becomes the physical expression of the dilemma (can't find the angle). The detail about Maren skipping stones adds character without the narrator explaining it.

### 3.
**Bad:**
She reflected on how much had changed in the past year. She had arrived in this city a stranger, with nothing but a name and a sword. Now she had allies, responsibilities, a place to sleep that she could call her own. She had built something here, something fragile but real. She thought about the people who had helped her — Tiku, Maren, the old woman at the market who always saved her the good bread. She felt a sudden, fierce gratitude for all of them. She realised, with a start, that this feeling — this warmth, this sense of belonging — was something she had never expected to have again.

**Good:**
A year. She'd been here a year.

The thought caught her while she was hanging laundry — sheets, heavy with water, fighting the wind. She pinned one corner and the wind took the other and she chased it, swearing, and pinned that too.

A year ago she'd arrived with a name and a sword. Now she was fighting a bedsheet.

She laughed. Short, surprised. The sound carried across the courtyard and startled a pigeon off the wall. A year. Allies, a bed, the old woman at the market who saved her the good bread. She pinned the last corner and stood back and looked at the sheet snapping in the wind and thought: this is mine. This stupid, ordinary, wind-fighting life. Mine.

**Why:** Seven sentences of reflective analysis. The fix: the reflection happens WHILE she's doing laundry. The sheet fight is comic and physical. The laugh is unplanned. The "this is mine" arrives through action, not analysis.

## Character Explaining Their Feelings to Themselves

### 4.
**Bad:**
She was afraid. She recognised the fear for what it was — not the sharp, sudden fear of physical danger, but the deeper, slower kind. The fear of vulnerability. The fear of opening herself up to someone who had the power to hurt her. She understood that this fear was rooted in past experience, in the betrayals and losses that had taught her to keep her walls high and her heart guarded. She knew, intellectually, that not everyone was a threat. But the fear didn't listen to intellect.

**Good:**
She was afraid. Not of the woman in front of her — of what she'd said yes to. Of the door she'd opened. She could feel the fear in her hands, a coldness that had nothing to do with the weather.

Maren was talking. Something about tomorrow, the plan, the route. She heard the words and filed them somewhere useful and kept most of her attention on the fear, which was doing something complicated in her chest, something that felt like a fist unclenching and clenching at the same time.

"Are you listening?"

"Yes." She wasn't. She was listening to the fist. She was listening to the part of her that had spent years building walls and was now watching someone walk through the front door as if the walls weren't there.

**Why:** The bad version is self-therapy: "She recognised... she understood... she knew, intellectually." The fix: the fear is physical (cold hands, fist in chest). The self-analysis is replaced by distraction (not listening to Maren). The wall metaphor arrives naturally, not as a diagnosis.

### 5.
**Bad:**
She loved Maren. She could admit that now, after months of denial and deflection. The feeling had crept up on her gradually, disguising itself as friendship, then as concern, then as something she couldn't name but that kept her awake at night and made her pulse quicken when Maren entered the room. She analysed the emotion carefully, examining it from every angle, and concluded that yes, this was love. Real, genuine, terrifying love.

**Good:**
She loved Maren.

She said it in her head and waited for the panic. It didn't come. That was new. Usually the thought triggered a whole cascade — denial, deflection, a mental pivot to something safer. This time: nothing. Just the statement, sitting there, calm and obvious and apparently tired of being argued with.

She laughed. Maren looked up from across the fire. "What?"

"Nothing." Everything. "Just thinking."

**Why:** The bad version narrates the emotional journey as analysis (crept up, disguised itself, examined from every angle, concluded). The fix: the character tests the thought, waits for her usual panic, and is surprised when it doesn't arrive. The laugh and the "Nothing / Everything" do the work.

## Thinking That Summarises Rather Than Dramatises

### 6.
**Bad:**
She thought about their journey together. They had been through so much — the battle at Kaelin, the crossing of the desert, the long winter in the mountains. Each challenge had brought them closer together and forged a bond that she knew would last a lifetime. Their shared experiences had created a foundation of trust and mutual respect that nothing could shake.

**Good:**
Kaelin. The desert. That winter.

Three words, three places, a whole year in them. She didn't think about the details — the details came on their own, uninvited, the way they always did. Maren's face at Kaelin, covered in someone else's blood, looking at her and grinning like a lunatic. The desert, where they'd shared the last water and Maren had lied about not being thirsty and she'd pretended to believe it.

The winter. She didn't think about the winter. Not the whole thing. Just one night of it — the coldest, the one where they'd slept back to back for warmth and she'd woken up facing the wrong way, arm over Maren's waist, and pretended to still be asleep because moving would mean acknowledging what she'd done.

**Why:** The bad version summarises the journey as a thesis about bonding. The fix: three fragments trigger specific, vivid memories. Each memory is a scene in miniature. The winter memory — the spooning, the pretended sleep — is worth more than any sentence about "trust and mutual respect."

### 7.
**Bad:**
She considered the situation carefully. The alliance was unstable, the political landscape was shifting, and their position was precarious. She needed to act decisively, but every option carried risk. She weighed the possibilities, turning them over in her mind like stones in her palm, and arrived at a conclusion: they had no good choices, only less bad ones.

**Good:**
Three options. She scratched them in the dirt with a stick, because seeing them helped, and because the stick gave her hands something to do besides shake.

Option one: the alliance. Shaky. Maren's assessment, and Maren was usually right about people. She drew a line through it.

Option two: the border. Risky, but possible. She stared at it. Drew a half-line through it. Left the other half.

Option three: the thing she didn't want to do. She didn't write it down. She didn't need to. It was the only one without a line through it, and it had been the only one without a line through it since before she'd sat down with the stick.

She snapped the stick. Threw the pieces. Stood up.

**Why:** The bad version is abstract strategy narration. The fix: she scratches options in the dirt. The physical act of drawing lines, half-lines, and NOT writing the third option dramatises the decision-making. The stick-snapping is the decision.

## Good Embedded Thought

### 8.
**Good (thinking woven into action):**
She sharpened the blade. Long strokes, steady, the whetstone singing its one-note song. Maren would be angry. The strokes didn't change — same pressure, same angle, same speed. Maren was always angry about something, and this was as good a reason as any. She tested the edge with her thumb. Sharp. Not sharp enough. She went back to the stone.

The thing was — and this was the part she kept circling — Maren wasn't wrong. That was the problem with people who were angry at you for good reason. You couldn't dismiss them. You had to sit with it.

She sat with it. She sharpened the blade. The two activities were surprisingly compatible.

**Why:** The thought about Maren is embedded between sharpening strokes. The physical task continues while the thinking happens, and the rhythm of the sharpening (same pressure, same angle) mirrors the character's controlled avoidance of the emotional issue. The thought never runs away — it's always interrupted by the blade.

### 9.
**Good (thinking woven into walking):**
The road was long and she was thinking too much, which was the usual arrangement. Her feet found the rhythm — left, right, left, right, the kind of walking that happens below consciousness and frees the mind to be stupid.

She was in love. Left, right. She was in love with someone who might not survive the week. Left, right. She was in love with someone who might not survive the week and she hadn't told her. Left, right, left, right, left, right, faster now, as if she could outpace the thought.

She couldn't. The thought kept pace easily. It had better endurance than she did.

**Why:** The walking rhythm is the pulse under the thought. "Left, right" serves as both physical action and metronome for the increasingly urgent internal loop. The thought "keeping pace" personifies it in a way that's grounded in the walking metaphor.

## Good Object-Proxy

### 10.
**Good (state shown through what hands do):**
The mug. She'd been holding it for — she looked at the tea. Cold. A film on the surface. Long enough for that.

She set it down. Picked it up. Set it down again. Adjusted its position on the table. Aligned the handle with the wood grain. Moved it slightly. Aligned it again.

Maren watched this. Said nothing. Let the mug-arranging happen. She was good at that — at letting people do the thing they needed to do with their hands while their mind caught up with their mouth.

"She's dead," she said, finally, and set the mug down for the last time.

**Why:** The character's state is shown entirely through the mug — the cold tea (lost time), the picking up and setting down (avoidance), the grain-alignment (control-seeking). The narrator never labels the emotion. The mug IS the emotion.

### 11.
**Good (object-proxy in argument):**
She folded the map. Opened it. Folded it again. Different fold this time — thirds instead of halves.

"Say something," Maren said.

She smoothed the crease. The paper was wearing thin along the fold line. Another few rounds and it would tear. She folded it again.

"Anything."

Halves again. Then thirds. The map crackled under her fingers, and the sound was the only thing in the room besides Maren's breathing and her own silence and the enormous, impossible thing that had just been said and couldn't be unsaid.

She set the map down. "I need to think."

"You've been thinking for ten minutes. You've been thinking AT that map."

True. The map was suffering for it. She smoothed it flat one more time, apology for the folds, and left her hands on it, palms down, as if holding it still could hold everything still.

**Why:** The map-folding IS the thinking. The mechanical repetition shows a mind processing something too big for words. Maren naming it ("thinking AT that map") adds humor and insight. The final image — palms holding the map flat as if that could hold everything still — carries the emotional weight without naming it.

## Good Compression

### 12.
**Bad:**
She wondered if she was doing the right thing. She thought about all the people who would be affected by her decision. She considered the consequences, both immediate and long-term. She asked herself if there was another way, a better path, a less destructive option.

**Good:**
Was she doing the right thing?

She pulled on her boots. Laced them. Tight — too tight, angry-tight. She loosened them and started over.

**Why:** Four sentences of deliberation compressed to one question and a boot-lacing. The anger in the lacing (too tight, starting over) says more than the deliberation did.

### 13.
**Bad:**
She realised she had to let go. She'd been holding on to this anger for too long, carrying it like a stone in her pocket, letting it weigh her down. She knew that the only way to move forward was to release it, to set it down, to walk away from it. She was tired of being angry. It was time.

**Good:**
She was tired of being angry.

She threw the stone into the river. It sank without ceremony.

**Why:** Five sentences of internal resolution compressed to one statement and one action. The stone (literal, not metaphorical) does the work. The "without ceremony" is the emotional note — no drama, just done.

### 14.
**Bad:**
She wondered what Maren was doing right now. She imagined her in the kitchen, cooking something that smelled like home, humming that tuneless song she always hummed. She felt a pang of longing that surprised her with its intensity. She hadn't expected to miss her this much, this quickly. She missed her with an ache that had settled into her body like something permanent.

**Good:**
She wondered what Maren was cooking tonight. The thought arrived with a pang, sharp and quick, and she let it come and go. Some doors you don't open on the road. She picked up her pace.

**Why:** Five sentences of longing compressed to one wondering, one pang, one rule (doors on the road), one action (walking faster). The compression MATCHES the character's coping strategy — she's not allowing herself to dwell.

### 15.
**Bad:**
She thought about the future. It stretched before her, uncertain and vast, full of possibilities both exciting and terrifying. She didn't know what it held, but she knew she would face it. She had survived worse. She would survive this too. She was strong, and she was resourceful, and she was not alone. The future, whatever it contained, would not find her unprepared.

**Good:**
Tomorrow, and the day after, and the one after that.

She cleaned the sword. The future could wait until morning.

**Why:** Seven sentences of affirmation compressed to a fragment and two actions. The character doesn't need to tell herself she's strong. She cleans the sword. That's the statement.

### 16.
**Bad:**
She thought about trust. What it meant, how it was built, how easily it could be broken. She considered the nature of trust between people who had been through what they had been through — the battles, the betrayals, the slow rebuilding. She realised that trust was not a wall but a bridge, and that bridges required maintenance, and that she had not been maintaining hers.

**Good:**
She hadn't been fair to Maren. The thought hit between one step and the next, blunt and undeniable.

She kept walking. The unfairness walked with her, and neither of them had much to say about it.

**Why:** Six sentences of abstract philosophising about trust compressed to one specific admission (unfair to Maren) and a personification of the unfairness. The character doesn't resolve it — she keeps walking. The unfairness keeps pace.
