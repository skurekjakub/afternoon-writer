# Mixed Metaphors

AI models stack metaphors. They reach for comparison after comparison, piling images from incompatible domains into the same sentence or paragraph. Fire becomes water becomes fabric becomes architecture, all in three sentences. The reader's mental image keeps rebuilding itself, and by the end, nothing is visualised — just a blur of competing pictures.

The rule is simple: one metaphor domain per paragraph. If grief is an ocean, it stays an ocean. If the argument is a fire, it stays a fire. When you finish with one metaphor, close it cleanly before opening another. And dead metaphors (clichés so worn they're invisible) still count — "the weight of the world" colliding with "a light at the end of the tunnel" creates physical nonsense.

**Hard limit:** One metaphor domain per paragraph. Max 2 explicit comparisons (simile or metaphor) per paragraph. No switching domains mid-sentence.

---

## Two Incompatible Domains in One Sentence

### 1.
**Bad:**
The fire of her anger drowned everything in its path.

**Good:**
Her anger burned through everything in its path.

**Why:** Fire doesn't drown. Pick one: fire or water. The fix stays with fire.

### 2.
**Bad:**
The weight of her grief sang through her bones like a cathedral bell.

**Good:**
Grief rang through her bones like a cathedral bell.

**Why:** Weight doesn't sing. The fix keeps the bell (auditory) and drops the weight (physical mass).

### 3.
**Bad:**
A wall of silence flowed between them.

**Good:**
Silence filled the space between them, heavy and still.

**Why:** Walls don't flow. The fix replaces the wall with a fluid image and gives it physical properties (heavy, still) that are consistent.

### 4.
**Bad:**
She planted the seeds of doubt, and they ignited a firestorm of suspicion.

**Good:**
Doubt took root. By morning, suspicion had spread through the camp like bindweed.

**Why:** Seeds don't ignite. The fix stays in the botanical domain: roots, spreading, bindweed.

### 5.
**Bad:**
The foundation of their relationship was unraveling at the seams.

**Good:**
Their relationship was unraveling, thread by thread.

**Why:** Foundations don't have seams. The fix picks fabric (unraveling, thread) and stays there.

### 6.
**Bad:**
His words cut deep, leaving a bitter taste in her mouth.

**Good:**
Her words cut deep. She flinched, as if the blade had been physical.

**Why:** Cutting is tactile; taste is gustatory. The fix stays with the blade metaphor.

### 7.
**Bad:**
She swallowed the sharp edge of her fear.

**Good:**
Fear sat in her throat, a tightness she couldn't swallow past.

**Why:** You can't swallow a sharp edge (or you can, but the metaphor goes somewhere the author didn't intend). The fix makes fear a physical constriction — consistent, believable.

### 8.
**Bad:**
The tapestry of lies crumbled under the weight of the truth.

**Good:**
The lies collapsed. One truth, and the whole structure came down.

**Why:** Tapestries don't crumble — they tear, unravel, fray. The fix drops the metaphor entirely and uses "structure" which CAN collapse.

### 9.
**Bad:**
A tidal wave of emotion blossomed in her chest.

**Good:**
Emotion flooded her chest, rising, filling, pressing against her ribs.

**Why:** Tidal waves don't blossom. The fix stays with water: flooded, rising, filling.

### 10.
**Bad:**
The iron grip of winter melted into spring.

**Good:**
Winter's grip loosened. Spring crept in through the cracks.

**Why:** Iron doesn't melt into spring (it melts into liquid iron). The fix separates the images: winter's grip loosens (physical), spring arrives through cracks (spatial).

## Extended Metaphor That Switches Domain

### 11.
**Bad:**
Their love was a garden — carefully tended, watered with patience, nurtured through seasons of doubt. But now the walls of that garden were crumbling, the foundation cracking, and no architect could rebuild what they'd spent years cultivating. She tried to sew the torn fabric of their bond back together, but the needle kept bending.

**Good:**
Their love was a garden — carefully tended, watered with patience, nursed through droughts of doubt. But something had gotten into the roots. Rot, or worm, or the slow creep of salt in the soil. The leaves were curling. The flowers had stopped. She kept watering, because that was all she knew how to do, and the water ran through the dead soil and came out the bottom unchanged.

**Why:** The bad version starts in garden, shifts to architecture (walls, foundation, architect), then jumps to sewing (fabric, needle). Three domains. The fix stays in garden throughout: roots, rot, salt, leaves, watering. Every detail belongs.

### 12.
**Bad:**
The argument was a storm — thunder and lightning, the air electric with tension. Words crashed like waves against the shore of her patience. She tried to build a bridge across the flooding river of their disagreement, but the stones of her resolve kept slipping.

**Good:**
The argument was a storm. It started with wind — the cold front of Maren's silence, the pressure dropping. Then the thunder, sudden and close. She stood in it. No shelter. Nothing to do but let it hit her and wait for the weather to pass, the way it always passed, leaving the ground wet and the air different and both of them a little stunned.

**Why:** Bad version: storm → waves/shore → bridge/river → stones. Four domains. The fix: storm throughout. Wind, cold front, pressure, thunder, shelter, weather passing. The metaphor develops instead of scattering.

### 13.
**Bad:**
She was navigating a labyrinth of emotions, each turn revealing a new landscape of pain. She climbed the mountains of her grief and waded through rivers of regret, searching for the key that would unlock the door to peace.

**Good:**
She was lost. Not in any place she could map — in herself, in the tangle of what had happened and what she'd done about it and what she should have done instead. Every thought led to another thought, and that thought led to three more, and none of them led anywhere useful.

She sat down. Not literally — she was walking, still walking, feet on the road — but her mind sat down. Stopped trying. Let the tangle be a tangle.

**Why:** Bad version: labyrinth → landscape → mountains → rivers → key/door. Five domains in three sentences. The fix drops all metaphor and uses direct language: lost in herself, tangle of thoughts. The "sat down" is a mental action with a literal correction, which is more interesting than any of the stacked metaphors.

## Dead Metaphor Collision

### 14.
**Bad:**
She turned over a new leaf, determined to break the chains of her past.

**Good:**
She started over. Or tried to. Starting over turned out to be less like turning a page and more like trying to write a new sentence while the old one was still wet on the paper.

**Why:** "Turn over a new leaf" + "break the chains" = botanical + metalwork. The fix uses ONE metaphor (writing/paper) and develops it.

### 15.
**Bad:**
She wore her heart on her sleeve and shouldered the weight of the world.

**Good:**
She carried everything. Other people's problems, her own problems, problems that didn't belong to anyone and had been orphaned in her direction. She was tired. She was always tired.

**Why:** Two dead metaphors colliding (heart on sleeve + weight of world). The fix drops both and goes direct.

### 16.
**Bad:**
The ball was in her court, and she was skating on thin ice.

**Good:**
The decision was hers. She wished it wasn't.

**Why:** Tennis court + ice rink. Two dead metaphors, two sports, one sentence. The fix: direct statement.

### 17.
**Bad:**
She'd opened a can of worms and now had to face the music.

**Good:**
She'd started something. The consequences were arriving.

**Why:** Fishing + performance venue. The fix is plain and more ominous for it.

### 18.
**Bad:**
She burned bridges while trying to keep all her eggs in one basket.

**Good:**
She destroyed her exits. Deliberately, methodically, as if the only way to commit to going forward was to make going back impossible.

**Why:** Fire + farming. The fix drops both and the direct language is more interesting and more specific.

## Metaphor Overload

### 19.
**Bad:**
The news hit her like a punch to the gut. Her world shattered like glass. The ground beneath her feet crumbled like sand. Her heart was a stone sinking through dark water. Hope, that fragile bird, beat its wings uselessly against the cage of her despair.

**Good:**
The news hit her. She sat down. Not a decision — her legs just stopped working, the way they do when the body gets information before the mind has finished processing it.

**Why:** Five metaphors in five sentences, each from a different domain (fighting, glass, earth, water, birds/cages). The fix uses zero metaphors. The body's response — legs stopping — is more devastating than any of them.

### 20.
**Bad:**
Her thoughts were a raging river, tumbling over rocks of confusion, pooling in eddies of doubt. The current of her anxiety pulled her under, drowning her in waves of fear, each one a hammer blow against the ship of her composure.

**Good:**
Her thoughts wouldn't settle. She couldn't hold one long enough to examine it — they came fast, each one shoving the last aside, a queue that wouldn't form a line. She gave up trying to sort them and sat with the mess.

**Why:** River → rocks → eddies → current → drowning → waves → hammer → ship. Eight images in two sentences. The fix: one simple concept (thoughts won't settle, won't queue up) and a pragmatic response (sat with the mess).

### 21.
**Bad:**
The sunrise painted the sky in shades of gold and crimson, like an artist's masterpiece draped across a canvas of infinite possibility. Light cascaded like liquid amber, jewels scattered across the velvet dawn, a symphony of colour that sang to the awakening world.

**Good:**
Gold sky. Crimson at the edges, the colour bleeding upward through the clouds. She watched it change. Fast — faster than you'd expect, the light shifting in real time, the gold deepening, the crimson fading, the whole show over in minutes.

**Why:** Painting + canvas + cascade + jewels + velvet + symphony — six metaphor domains for one sunrise. The fix: direct colour observation with one verb metaphor (bleeding) that's consistent with the visual domain.

## Good Sustained Metaphor

### 22.
**Good (one domain carried through):**
Their partnership was a rope. She'd thought about this. Two strands, wound tight, each one stronger for the twisting. The rope held weight — had held weight, for years, loads that would have snapped either strand alone.

But rope frays. She could feel it — the first threads going, the outer fibres separating, the core still holding but the surface rough now, shedding. You could ignore fraying for a long time. You could tell yourself the core was sound. You could keep loading weight onto a rope that was thinning and trust the physics until the physics stopped cooperating.

She didn't know which thread had gone first. She didn't know how many were left. She only knew the rope felt different in her hands — lighter, rougher, less certain — and that she was still hanging from it.

**Why:** One domain: rope. Three paragraphs. The metaphor develops — twisting, fraying, loading, thinning, hanging. Every detail belongs to the same physical object. The reader can SEE the rope throughout.

### 23.
**Good (one domain carried through):**
Grief, she'd discovered, was a tenant. It moved in without asking. It occupied the best room in the house — the one with the light, the one she used to sit in with a book — and it filled it with its own furniture: heavy, dark, impossible to shift.

She'd tried evicting it. Knocked on the door, polite at first, then louder. No answer. Changed the locks — it had a key she didn't remember giving. Left notes. Made threats. The grief sat in the room with the light and did not leave.

So she learned to live around it. Took the smaller room. Read by candlelight instead of by the window. Walked past the occupied door without knocking. Some days she could hear it in there, rearranging the furniture, settling in deeper. Some days it was quiet, and she'd press her ear to the wood and listen for signs of departure that never came.

**Why:** One domain: tenant/house. Three paragraphs. Every detail is consistent: room, furniture, locks, key, notes, knocking, candlelight, door, listening. The grief-as-tenant is developed, not just stated.

## Good Metaphor Exit

### 24.
**Good (ending the comparison cleanly):**
The city was a body. She'd thought that the first time she'd seen it from the wall — the river, its spine; the market, its stomach; the temple district, its heart; the docks, open arms. A body, alive, breathing, its streets like veins carrying the commerce that kept it fed.

That was before the siege. Now the body was sick. The river ran brown. The market was empty. The temple had burned.

She stopped thinking of it as a body. It was a city. Cities can be rebuilt. Bodies can't.

**Why:** The metaphor (city as body) is introduced, developed, and then EXPLICITLY EXITED: "She stopped thinking of it as a body." The exit is clean, purposeful, and the final observation (cities can be rebuilt, bodies can't) uses the metaphor's departure as its own emotional beat.

### 25.
**Good (ending one metaphor before starting another):**
The argument had been a fire. Hot, fast, consuming — she could still feel the heat of it on her face, could still smell the smoke in the words they'd exchanged.

But fires burn out. This one had. The coals were grey. The air was cooling.

What came next was different. Not fire — ice. A slow, creeping cold that filled the space where the heat had been. The cold was worse. Fire is honest. Cold pretends to be nothing, and that's its power.

**Why:** Fire metaphor: hot, consuming, coals, grey. Explicit exit: "fires burn out." Then ice metaphor begins cleanly. The transition between metaphors is itself a beat — the shift from fire to ice is the shift from anger to silence. Each metaphor is sustained within its own space.
