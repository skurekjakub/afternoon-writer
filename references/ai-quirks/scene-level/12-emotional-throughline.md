# Emotional Throughline

AI prose treats emotion like weather — each paragraph has its own local conditions with no connection to what came before or after. A character is anxious in paragraph one, contemplative in paragraph two, amused in paragraph three. Each emotional state is self-contained: introduced, explored, and resolved within its own paragraph. The effect is a series of emotional snapshots rather than a continuous experience.

Real emotional life accumulates. Anxiety doesn't reset to neutral between paragraphs — it evolves, compounds, leaks into unrelated moments. An argument three paragraphs ago still flavours how the character looks at the soup in front of them. Emotion has undertow: the surface changes, but the current underneath persists.

**Hard limit:** No emotion should be introduced and fully resolved within a single paragraph. No consecutive paragraphs should operate on completely unrelated emotional channels. If paragraph 2's mood has zero inheritance from paragraph 1's mood, something is wrong.

---

## Emotion Resets Between Paragraphs

### 1.
**Bad:**
She was furious about the lie. How could Maren have kept this from her? The betrayal burned in her chest like a hot coal, and she wanted to scream, to throw something, to make the walls shake with the force of what she felt.

The kitchen was warm. She noticed the bread was almost done, its golden crust just beginning to darken at the edges. She should probably take it out before it burned. The routine of baking had always calmed her.

Outside, the garden needed tending. The herbs had grown wild in the weeks since she'd last bothered, and the roses were starting to climb the fence. She'd have to deal with that tomorrow. For now, she was content to stand at the window and watch the light change.

**Good:**
She was furious. The lie sat in her chest like a coal — hot, radiating, impossible to ignore. She paced the kitchen and couldn't stop pacing, because standing still meant thinking and thinking meant deciding what to do, and she wasn't ready for that.

The bread. Almost done. She pulled it from the oven with hands that were shaking — from the anger, not the heat, though the heat didn't help. She set it on the counter too hard and the crust cracked. She stared at the crack and felt something in her own chest mirror it.

The garden through the window. Overgrown. Neglected. She had neglected everything this week — the herbs, the roses, the conversation she should have had three days ago instead of letting Maren's silence stand as an answer. She wasn't content. She was just tired enough to pretend.

**Why:** The bad version: furious → calm baking → content garden-watching. Three paragraphs, three moods, no connection. The fix: the fury carries through — shaking hands in the bread paragraph, neglect as metaphor in the garden paragraph. The bread crack mirrors the emotional crack. The contentment is explicitly identified as a fake.

### 2.
**Bad:**
The argument had been vicious. Words said that couldn't be unsaid, accusations that would leave marks. She sat in the aftermath and felt the damage.

The fire was dying. She should add wood. The room was getting cold, and the chill was starting to seep through her clothes. She got up, added a log, watched the flames catch. Better.

She thought about dinner. There was leftover stew from yesterday, and some bread that was still good enough. She'd eat, then sleep. Tomorrow would be easier. These things always looked different in the morning.

**Good:**
The argument had been vicious. Words said that couldn't be unsaid. She sat in the aftermath and felt the damage — a ringing silence, like the moment after a bell stops but the sound is still in your teeth.

The fire was dying. She should add wood. She didn't move. The cold crept in, and she let it, because the cold felt appropriate and warmth would have been an insult to whatever had just happened between them.

Eventually she moved. Added a log. Watched the flames catch with the distant, uninvested attention of someone watching something that doesn't concern them. The stew from yesterday was in the pot. She'd eat. She'd sleep. Tomorrow — but she couldn't finish the thought. Tomorrow was on the other side of tonight, and tonight was still happening.

**Why:** The bad version resolves the argument's emotion in three paragraphs: damage → practical task → tomorrow's optimism. The fix: the silence carries forward (ringing bell), the refusal to warm herself carries the anger, the inability to think about tomorrow shows the damage is still active.

## Self-Contained Emotional Paragraphs

### 3.
**Bad:**
Fear washed over her. It was the cold kind, starting in the stomach and spreading outward until her whole body felt frozen. Her heart hammered, her palms sweated, and every instinct screamed at her to run. She breathed through it. In, out. In, out. Slowly, the fear receded, leaving her shaky but functional.

She examined the map on the table.

**Good:**
Fear — the cold kind, starting in the stomach, spreading. Her heart hammered. Her palms went slick. Every instinct she had was screaming one word, and the word was "run."

She didn't run. She stood there and let the fear do its work, because running wouldn't change anything and at least standing still felt like a choice.

The map on the table. She forced her eyes to it. The lines blurred, resolved, blurred again. Focus. She traced the route with a finger that wouldn't quite hold steady.

**Why:** The bad version introduces fear, experiences it, resolves it ("the fear receded"), and moves on — all in one paragraph. The fix: the fear enters in paragraph one, is resisted in paragraph two, and still affects her in paragraph three (blurring vision, unsteady finger). It doesn't "recede." It persists.

### 4.
**Bad:**
Guilt gnawed at her. She shouldn't have left without saying goodbye. The weight of it pressed down on her shoulders, heavy and suffocating. But she'd had no choice. She reminded herself of that, and slowly the guilt faded, replaced by the grim resolve of someone who had done what was necessary.

"Which way?" Maren asked.

"East," she said, her voice steady and sure.

**Good:**
She shouldn't have left without saying goodbye.

The thought came and wouldn't leave. It sat behind her eyes while she saddled the horse. It followed her down the road. It rode with her, patient and persistent, a passenger she hadn't invited and couldn't throw off.

"Which way?" Maren, pulling up alongside. Looking at her. Probably seeing something on her face, because Maren always saw things on her face.

"East." The word came out rough. She cleared her throat. Didn't repeat it. Didn't look at Maren. If she looked at Maren, she'd have to explain the expression, and explaining the expression meant talking about the goodbye she hadn't said, and she wasn't ready for that.

**Why:** The bad version: guilt → reminded herself → guilt faded → grim resolve → steady voice. A complete emotional cycle in one paragraph. The fix: the guilt is never resolved. It follows her. It's visible on her face. It affects her voice. She actively avoids addressing it, which is more realistic and more interesting.

## Good Accumulation

### 5.
**Good (each paragraph inherits and builds):**
She found the letter on the table. Maren's handwriting, the sharp, angular script she'd know anywhere. She picked it up.

Three lines. An address. An apology that was too short to be adequate and too long to be nothing. She read it twice, the way you press a bruise to confirm it hurts.

The address. She knew it — a house on the eastern road, the one with the green door. She'd passed it a hundred times and never thought anything of it. Now it felt like a revelation, and the revelation felt like being stupid, and being stupid felt like something she should have gotten used to by now.

She folded the letter. Put it in her pocket. Stood in the kitchen with her hands at her sides and the whole world rearranged, and thought: of course. Of course it was the green door. Of course she hadn't seen it. You never do, until you do, and then you can't stop.

**Why:** Each paragraph picks up from where the last one left. Paragraph 2 inherits the letter and adds the bruise metaphor. Paragraph 3 inherits the address and builds through revelation → stupidity → resignation. Paragraph 4 inherits all of it and adds the philosophical "of course."

### 6.
**Good (emotion builds through action):**
She started packing. One bag, light enough to carry. Clothes, knife, the money from under the floorboard. Practical. Efficient. She was fine.

The book on the shelf. Maren's book, the one with the cracked spine and the margin notes. She picked it up. Put it in the bag. Took it out. Put it back on the shelf. Picked it up again.

The book went in the bag. She didn't look at it as she packed it. She didn't think about why she was taking it. She thought about the weight of the bag and whether she could add one more thing without slowing herself down, and the answer was yes, and the one more thing was the book, and that was that.

Except it wasn't that. She was standing in the doorway with the bag on her shoulder and the book making a rectangular weight against her hip, and she couldn't leave. Her feet had stopped. Her hand was on the doorframe. The house smelled like rosemary and smoke and someone who wasn't here anymore.

**Why:** The emotion (loss, attachment) is never named. It shows up in the action: packing the book, removing it, repacking it. Then the inability to leave. The rosemary and smoke. Each paragraph raises the emotional stakes without any paragraph stating the emotion.

## Emotional Callback

### 7.
**Good (later paragraph references earlier feeling):**
Maren laughed. Full, loud, her head thrown back, the sound filling the camp like something warm and physical. She hadn't heard Maren laugh in — weeks? Months? The sound hit her in the sternum, and she had to look away because whatever her face was doing, she didn't want it witnessed.

The fire needed tending. She knelt, fed it, watched the sparks climb. Practical. Useful. Not at all about needing a reason not to look at Maren while her face did whatever it was doing.

Later — an hour, maybe two, the fire built up and dying back again — she was almost asleep when Maren said something quiet she didn't catch.

"What?"

"I said thank you."

For what, she didn't ask. The laugh from earlier was still in her sternum. She held still and let the thank-you settle next to it, two warm things in the same spot, and closed her eyes.

**Why:** The laugh's physical impact ("hit her in the sternum") in paragraph one is called back in the final paragraph ("still in her sternum"). The emotion thread persists across paragraphs about fire-tending and near-sleep, resurfacing when triggered by the thank-you.

### 8.
**Good (anger residue hours later):**
The insult had been offhand. Casual. Maren hadn't even seemed to realise she'd said it. She'd just kept walking, kept talking, moved on to the next subject the way you step over a puddle without noticing.

She'd let it go. Or — no, that wasn't right. She'd set it down. Carefully, deliberately, the way you set down something that might break if you throw it. She'd set it down and walked away from it and gone about her day.

But that evening, sitting by the fire, watching Maren sharpen her knife with the casual competence of someone who has never doubted her own hands, she felt it. Still there. Not where she'd set it down — it had followed her, relocated, made itself comfortable in the space between her ribs where quiet anger lives.

**Why:** The insult in paragraph one isn't resolved or addressed. Paragraph two shows the deliberate suppression (set it down, not let it go). Paragraph three: hours later, it's still there, having "followed her." The throughline is unbroken across a full day.

## Undertow Technique

### 9.
**Good (surface changes, current persists):**
The market was loud. She bought apples — three, small, the kind that are more tart than sweet. Maren wanted bread. She found bread. The bread seller was chatty. She endured the chatting. The bread was warm.

The road home was quiet. The apples in the bag knocked against her hip. Maren walked slightly ahead, slightly fast, the way she walked when she was thinking about something she wasn't saying.

The kitchen. She set the bag down. Maren set the bread down. They moved around each other with the careful geometry of two people who are trying not to touch because touching might start a conversation that the morning's silence had been specifically designed to prevent.

**Why:** Three paragraphs about buying food and going home. On the surface: market, road, kitchen. Underneath: something unsaid, carried through — Maren walking fast, the careful geometry, the silence designed to prevent conversation. The mundane activity is the surface; the emotional tension is the undertow.

### 10.
**Good (landscape carries the internal state):**
The bridge was old. Stone, three arches, the middle one cracked. The river below was low — summer had been dry, and the water barely covered the stones. She could see the bottom. Every rock, every piece of debris, every leaf caught on a branch. Nothing hidden. The water too clear, too shallow, too honest.

On the other side, the road continued through fields. Open country, flat, nowhere to hide. She'd be visible for miles. Anyone watching — and someone was always watching — would see her coming long before she arrived.

The inn appeared at the crossroads like an afterthought. Low stone, thatched roof, smoke from the chimney going straight up in the windless air. She could see through the windows. Firelight, figures, the shape of someone behind the bar. Nothing hidden here either. She was getting tired of nothing being hidden.

**Why:** The surface is landscape description — bridge, fields, inn. The undertow is exposure and vulnerability: water too clear, nowhere to hide, visible for miles, seeing through windows. "Tired of nothing being hidden" lands because it's been building through three paragraphs of landscape that all carried the same emotional current.
