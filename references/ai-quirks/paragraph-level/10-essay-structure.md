# Essay Structure in Fiction Paragraphs

AI models were trained on vast amounts of expository writing — essays, articles, reports, documentation. This shows. Their fiction paragraphs follow essay structure: topic sentence → supporting evidence → concluding restatement. The paragraph opens with a claim, supports it, and then closes by restating the claim. In an essay, this is good practice. In fiction, it's a pacing killer. Fiction paragraphs should end FORWARD — on a new beat, a shift, an action, an incomplete thought — not by circling back to where they started.

Another variant: the list paragraph. "[Quality]. [Evidence of quality]. [More evidence]. [Quality restated]." This creates the sense that the narrator is writing a report about the story rather than being inside it.

**Hard limit:** No paragraph should open and close with the same idea. No paragraph should follow topic-sentence → support → conclusion structure. Every paragraph must end somewhere different from where it began.

---

## Topic Sentence → Support → Conclusion

### 1.
**Bad:**
Maren was a cautious woman. She checked the perimeter twice before entering any building. She slept with one hand on her knife. She never sat with her back to a door. Her caution was the thing that had kept her alive this long, and she wore it like a second skin.

**Good:**
Maren checked the perimeter twice before entering the building. Checked it again after. She slept with one hand on her knife — had for years, long enough that the callus on her palm had a callus of its own. She sat with her back to the wall, facing the door, and when she caught herself doing it, she didn't correct it. Why would she? It worked.

**Why:** The bad version opens with "Maren was cautious," lists evidence, then closes with "her caution." A thesis paragraph. The fix removes the thesis and conclusion — starts with a specific action, lets the evidence accumulate, and ends on a pragmatic thought ("It worked") that moves forward.

### 2.
**Bad:**
The city was dying. Shops stood empty along the main street. The fountain in the square had run dry months ago. Weeds pushed through the cobblestones. The city's decline was evident in every crumbling wall and abandoned building.

**Good:**
Shops stood empty along the main street — not boarded up, just empty, the doors standing open as if the owners had stepped out and never come back. The fountain in the square had run dry. Weeds in the cobblestones. She counted three buildings that had lost their roofs since the last time she'd been here. Four, if you counted the temple, which she didn't. The temple had been falling apart long before the city was.

**Why:** Opens with "The city was dying" (thesis), closes with "the city's decline" (restatement). The fix: starts with a specific image, adds personal history (she's been here before), and ends on a side-thought about the temple — moving AWAY from the topic, not circling back.

### 3.
**Bad:**
She was angry. The kind of anger that burns slow and lasts. She'd been angry since the morning, when the message arrived, and the anger hadn't faded with the day. If anything, it had grown. She was deeply, fundamentally angry.

**Good:**
The message had arrived that morning. She'd read it twice, folded it, put it in her pocket, and gone about her day as if nothing had changed. But the anger had followed her — through the market, through the afternoon's training, into the evening. Now it sat in her stomach like something swallowed whole, too big to digest, too deep to cough up.

**Why:** Opens with "She was angry," closes with "deeply, fundamentally angry." The fix: opens with the cause (the message), tracks the anger through the day chronologically, and ends on a physical metaphor that leaves the anger unresolved.

### 4.
**Bad:**
The forest was dangerous at night. Wolves prowled the undergrowth, and the paths were treacherous in the dark. Strange lights had been reported between the trees, and two travellers had disappeared in the past month. It was no place to be after dark, and everyone knew it.

**Good:**
Wolves. The undergrowth moved with them after sunset — she could hear them, or hear something, the kind of sound that could be wind or could be something with teeth. Paths that were clear in daylight turned to ankle-breakers in the dark. And the lights. Everyone talked about the lights — strange, cold, blue-white, moving between the trees with a purpose that no one could identify and no one wanted to investigate.

**Why:** Opens with "The forest was dangerous at night" (thesis), closes with "no place to be after dark" (restatement). The fix: opens with a one-word fragment (Wolves), gives specific sensory detail, and ends on the mystery of the lights without resolving or restating.

### 5.
**Bad:**
Trust was hard to come by in this place. People lied as easily as they breathed. Alliances shifted daily. Even friends kept secrets. In a city where trust was the rarest commodity, she had learned to rely on no one but herself.

**Good:**
People lied. Not maliciously — or not always — but fluently, the way you speak a mother tongue. Alliances shifted by the hour. She'd watched three friendships collapse this week alone, each one over a secret that should have been shared and wasn't. She trusted no one. That wasn't pessimism — it was bookkeeping.

**Why:** "Trust was hard" opens, "trust was the rarest commodity" closes. Same idea, restated with more syllables. The fix: starts with the evidence, ends on a sharp character voice ("bookkeeping") that moves the paragraph forward into personality rather than back into thesis.

## "It Was [Quality]" Structure

### 6.
**Bad:**
It was a beautiful morning. Sunlight streamed through the windows, painting the floor in gold. Birds sang in the garden. The air was warm and carried the scent of jasmine. It was the kind of morning that made you believe the world was a good place.

**Good:**
Sunlight through the windows, gold on the floor. Birds in the garden — she could hear them through the open shutters, arguing about something territorial. The air was warm, jasmine-scented, the kind of warmth that makes you want to stay in bed. She stayed in bed.

**Why:** "It was a beautiful morning" (thesis) → "the kind of morning that made you believe" (restatement with escalation). The fix: starts with the sensory evidence, gives the birds personality, and ends on an action (staying in bed) rather than a philosophical claim.

### 7.
**Bad:**
It was clear that she was in trouble. Her clothes were torn, her face was bruised, and she was breathing in the shallow, careful way of someone protecting broken ribs. It was obvious to everyone who looked at her that something terrible had happened.

**Good:**
Torn clothes. A bruise spreading across her jaw, the swelling still fresh enough to be going purple. She breathed shallow and careful — broken ribs, probably, or at least cracked. She walked in anyway, sat down, and asked for wine in a voice that dared anyone to comment.

**Why:** "It was clear she was in trouble" / "it was obvious" — opening and closing with the same observation. The fix: physical evidence listed as fragments, then the character's response (walking in, sitting, ordering wine) which tells us she's tough without stating it.

### 8.
**Bad:**
It was quiet. The kind of quiet that presses against your ears. Not the absence of sound but the presence of silence. It was the kind of quiet that made you afraid to breathe, afraid to break whatever spell kept the world so utterly, perfectly still.

**Good:**
Quiet. Not the absence of sound — the presence of it. A silence that pressed against the ears, thick, physical, the kind you could almost reach out and touch. She held her breath. Not on purpose. Her body had decided breathing was too loud.

**Why:** Opens with "It was quiet," closes with "utterly, perfectly still" (restated). The fix: fragment opener (Quiet), one strong description of the silence, then the body's response — making the character PART of the scene rather than commenting on it.

## Paragraph Opens and Closes with Same Idea

### 9.
**Bad:**
She missed home. Every morning, she woke in an unfamiliar bed and felt the absence of everything she'd known. The smells were wrong, the light was wrong, even the birds sounded different. She missed home with an ache that lived in her bones.

**Good:**
Every morning, an unfamiliar bed. The smells wrong, the light wrong, even the birds — different birds here, with different calls, and she kept listening for the ones from home and not hearing them. The ache lived in her bones. She was getting used to it, the way you get used to a limp.

**Why:** "She missed home" / "missed home with an ache" = same idea bookending. The fix: starts with the sensory experience, ends with adaptation ("getting used to it"), which moves the emotional story forward rather than circling.

### 10.
**Bad:**
Maren was not someone who forgave easily. She held grudges the way other people held possessions — tightly, carefully, with a sense of ownership. Years could pass, and still the slight would be as fresh as the day it happened. Forgiveness was not in her nature, and she made no apologies for it.

**Good:**
Grudges. Maren held them the way other people held possessions — tightly, carefully, with a sense of ownership that bordered on the religious. Years could pass. The slight would stay fresh. She maintained it, polished it, kept it in working order, the way you maintain a blade you might need someday.

**Why:** "Not someone who forgave" / "Forgiveness was not in her nature" = same idea, same words, bookend. The fix: starts with a fragment (Grudges), develops the metaphor further (polishing, maintaining), and ends INSIDE the metaphor (blade you might need) rather than stepping outside to restate the thesis.

## List Paragraphs

### 11.
**Bad:**
Maren was many things. She was strong. She was brave. She was stubborn. She was loyal to a fault. She was reckless when it mattered. She was everything she needed to be and nothing she wanted to be.

**Good:**
Strong, brave, stubborn, loyal to a fault, reckless when it mattered. She was all of those things the way a sword is all of those things — not because she'd chosen them but because someone had forged her that way and she didn't know how to be anything else.

**Why:** The bad version lists qualities with "She was" at the start of each. The fix: compresses the list to one sentence, then uses a single metaphor to reframe them as something imposed rather than chosen.

### 12.
**Bad:**
The room told a story. There were books on every surface. There was a half-eaten meal on the table. There were clothes strewn across the floor. There was a map pinned to the wall with notes scribbled in the margins. There was evidence of a life interrupted.

**Good:**
Books on every surface. A half-eaten meal going cold on the table, the fork still balanced on the plate's edge as if someone would be right back. Clothes on the floor. A map on the wall, pinned at the corners, the margins covered in handwriting she recognised.

She wasn't coming back. Whoever had lived here had left in a hurry and with no intention of returning.

**Why:** "There was" × 5, bookended by "told a story" / "evidence of a life interrupted" (same thesis). The fix: fragments for the list, one detail expanded (fork balanced, handwriting recognised), then an inference that moves the scene forward.

### 13.
**Bad:**
The market offered everything. Spices from the south, their scents mingling in the morning air. Fabrics from the east, silk and cotton and something that shimmered like liquid metal. Weapons from the north, practical and unadorned. Food from every direction, piled high and colourful. It was a place where the whole world came together.

**Good:**
Spices from the south. Fabrics from the east — silk, cotton, something that shimmered like water pretending to be cloth. Northern weapons, practical and ugly and probably reliable. She stopped at a food stall, pointed at something she didn't recognise, paid too much for it, and ate it standing in the crowd while it dripped on her boots. Good. Unidentifiable, but good.

**Why:** "Offered everything" / "whole world came together" = thesis/restatement. The fix drops the framing, lists the goods, and then ENGAGES with the market through action (buying food, eating it, getting it on her boots).

## Thesis Paragraphs in Narration

### 14.
**Bad:**
War changes people. She'd known this before the fighting started, had been told by veterans with distant eyes and careful words. But knowing it and living it were different things. War had changed her. It had taken parts of her she hadn't known were removable and replaced them with harder, stranger things. War changes people, and it doesn't ask permission.

**Good:**
Before the war, she'd slept on her back. Now she slept on her side, facing the door, one hand under the pillow where the knife was. Before the war, she'd eaten slowly. Now she ate like someone was going to take it. Before the war, she'd looked at strangers and seen people. Now she looked at strangers and saw angles of approach.

She didn't like the woman she was becoming. But she couldn't find the seam where the old one ended and this one began.

**Why:** "War changes people" opens, "War changes people" closes. Identical. The fix: never states the thesis. Instead, three concrete before/after pairs that SHOW the change, then a reflection that moves forward (looking for the seam).

### 15.
**Bad:**
Grief is a strange companion. It arrives uninvited and refuses to leave. Some days it sits quietly in the corner; other days it fills the entire room. She had learned to live with grief the way one learns to live with a chronic pain — not by defeating it but by accommodating it. Grief was her constant companion, and she had made an uneasy peace with its presence.

**Good:**
Some mornings she forgot. That was the cruelest part — the forgetting, the half-second between waking and remembering where the world was whole and intact and then wasn't. She'd learned to brace for it. Coffee, boots, remember. In that order.

**Why:** "Grief is a strange companion" / "constant companion" = thesis bookends with personification. The fix: no abstraction. Just the specific morning routine of loss — the forgetting, the remembering, the three-step coping mechanism.

## Good Fiction Paragraphs

### 16.
**Good (ending mid-thought):**
She sat at the desk and opened the ledger. Three months of entries, all in Maren's handwriting — careful, precise, the numbers adding up in columns that told a story she wasn't sure she wanted to read. The final entry was dated two weeks ago. After that, blank pages.

She turned one. Then another. As if the entries might have migrated deeper into the book, hiding from her, waiting for—

"Find anything?"

**Why:** The paragraph is interrupted by dialogue. The thought is left incomplete (waiting for—). This creates forward momentum: the reader's brain completes the thought while the scene continues.

### 17.
**Good (ending on shift):**
Maren cooked. It was a strange thing to watch — this woman who could kill without hesitation, who'd once broken a woman's arm so efficiently the bone didn't even have time to crack properly, standing at a stove stirring soup and tasting it from the spoon with a frown that said it needed more salt. She added more salt. Tasted it again. Added something green from a jar.

The soup was, against all probability, excellent.

**Why:** The paragraph ends on a shift — from the violent characterization to the domestic, from "she could kill" to "excellent soup." The contrast does the work; no thesis statement required.

### 18.
**Good (ending on action):**
She'd been thinking about it all day. All week. The conversation they needed to have, the words she needed to find, the tone she'd need to strike — firm enough to be taken seriously, gentle enough not to start a war. She'd rehearsed it in her head a dozen times. It was perfect. Clean, logical, compassionate.

She opened her mouth, and what came out was: "What the hell is wrong with you?"

**Why:** The paragraph builds toward an imagined perfect conversation. The next line demolishes it with reality. The ending is an action (speaking) that contradicts everything the paragraph set up.

### 19.
**Good (ending on a new question):**
The letter answered three of her questions. The guild was behind it. Maren's sister was alive. The money trail led to the capital.

It raised four more.

**Why:** The paragraph ends on forward momentum — new questions, not answered ones. The reader wants to know what the four questions are, which pulls them into the next paragraph.

### 20.
**Good (ending on contradiction):**
She should have been furious. She had every right to be furious. Maren had lied, had hidden the truth for months, had made decisions that affected both of them without so much as a conversation. Fury was warranted. Fury was appropriate.

She wasn't furious. She was just tired.

**Why:** The paragraph builds the case for anger with escalating evidence. The final line inverts it completely. The paragraph ends somewhere entirely different from where it began — not restating the thesis, not supporting it, but collapsing it.
