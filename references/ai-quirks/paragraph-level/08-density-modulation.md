# Density Modulation

AI prose suffers from a specific rhythm problem: every sentence tries to be remarkable. Every image is vivid. Every verb is muscular. Every observation carries weight. The result is exhausting — like a song played at fortissimo for four minutes straight. Real prose breathes. It has workman sentences that carry the reader forward without demanding attention. It has pauses — ambient detail, physical movement, mundane observation — between the sentences that actually matter.

The inverse problem also exists: AI sometimes writes in constant reportage where every sentence delivers new plot information, new emotion, new revelation. No breathing room, no rest beats. The paragraph reads like a case file.

**Hard limit:** No more than 2 consecutive high-intensity sentences (loaded with metaphor, dense imagery, or high-emotion content) without a workman sentence or ambient beat between them.

---

## Every Sentence Beautiful

### 1.
**Bad:**
The sunset bled crimson across the sky like a wound refusing to close. Each cloud was a bruise painted by an artist's angry hand. The horizon shimmered with the last defiant light of a dying day. Even the shadows seemed to weep with the weight of the fading beauty. The trees stood as dark sentinels against nature's final, glorious display.

**Good:**
The sunset went red. Crimson, really — the kind that stains the clouds bruise-purple at the edges. She watched it for a while. The trees were black against it, sharp-edged, and somewhere behind her a bird was making a noise that sounded like a question nobody was going to answer.

**Why:** Five consecutive "beautiful" sentences, each with a metaphor or personification. The fix keeps one strong image (crimson staining clouds purple), adds a flat workman sentence ("She watched it for a while"), and closes with an ambient detail (bird) that doesn't try to be impressive.

### 2.
**Bad:**
Maren's eyes held the depth of ancient oceans and the warmth of summer storms. Her smile was a crack in armour she'd spent years forging. When she laughed, the sound was silver and wild, untamed by the weight of everything she carried. Her hands — scarred, capable — told stories that her mouth refused to share.

**Good:**
Maren's eyes were brown. Dark, almost black in this light, and warm in a way she hadn't expected. The smile came easy. She'd expected that to be harder — Maren had the look of someone who'd stopped smiling years ago and was only just remembering how.

**Why:** Four sentences of stacked metaphor (ancient oceans, crack in armour, silver and wild, telling stories). The fix: a plain observation (brown eyes), one specific detail (dark in this light), one workman transition ("The smile came easy"), then a single insight that does the character work.

### 3.
**Bad:**
Rain hammered the cobblestones like a thousand tiny fists of fury. Each drop was a silver bullet tearing through the veil of dusk. The gutters ran like rivers of forgotten tears, carrying the city's grief toward the sea. Lightning split the darkness with electric veins of divine judgment.

**Good:**
Rain. Hard, straight down, the kind that stings exposed skin. The gutters were already running. She pulled her hood tighter and walked faster, boots splashing through water that came up to her ankles on the low spots. Lightning, somewhere to the east. She counted — one, two, three — before the thunder.

**Why:** Four metaphor-laden sentences. The fix has one fragment (Rain.), one physical detail (stings skin), one workman observation (gutters running), one action sentence, and one specific detail (counting to thunder) that shows character.

### 4.
**Bad:**
The library was a cathedral of knowledge, its shelves rising like pillars to an unseen heaven. Dust motes danced in the slanting light like tiny celestial beings. The scent of aged parchment wrapped around her like a lover's embrace. Each book was a universe waiting to be explored, a door that opened onto infinite possibility.

**Good:**
The library was enormous. Shelves floor to ceiling, three floors of them, the upper levels lost in gloom. It smelled like old paper and dust and something faintly chemical — preservation spells, maybe. She ran a finger along the nearest shelf and it came back grey.

**Why:** Four poetic sentences in a row. The fix starts with a flat statement (enormous), gives concrete sensory detail (old paper, chemical smell), and ends with a small, grounding action (finger, dust, grey).

### 5.
**Bad:**
The wound pulsed with a pain that spoke of deeper injuries, wounds of the soul that no healer could touch. Blood welled like dark rubies from the torn flesh. Her body trembled with the effort of remaining conscious, each breath a battle won against the darkness that beckoned. The cold crept in like death's patient fingers.

**Good:**
The wound pulsed. She pressed her hand against it — too much blood, she could feel it soaking through the bandage. Her teeth chattered. Cold from blood loss, not the air. She needed pressure, she needed thread, she needed someone who knew what they were doing. She had none of those things.

**Why:** Four poetic sentences aestheticizing an injury. The fix stays in the body: the pulse, the wet bandage, the chattering teeth. Then a pragmatic list of needs followed by the flat statement of what she doesn't have.

## Every Sentence Carries Plot Information

### 6.
**Bad:**
Maren had betrayed them. The gate guard was dead. The supplies were gone. The horses had been scattered. They had six hours until the enemy reached the valley. There was no one left to send for help. The last healer had fled with the refugees.

**Good:**
Maren had betrayed them.

She sat with that for a moment. Let it settle. The gate guard was dead — she'd seen the body on her way in. The supplies were gone, the horses scattered, and the last healer had fled with the refugees.

Six hours. That's what they had. Six hours until the enemy arrived, and she was sitting in the mud cataloguing disasters like a merchant taking stock.

**Why:** Seven consecutive plot facts with no breathing room. The fix: the betrayal gets its own line and a moment of processing. The middle section groups facts. The time limit gets its own paragraph with character voice (cataloguing disasters).

### 7.
**Bad:**
She drew her sword. The blade was enchanted with fire magic that she'd learned from the monks of Kaelin. The monks had taught her in exchange for protecting their temple from the northern raiders. The raiders were led by a woman named Syl who had once been Maren's lover. Maren still carried the scar from their last encounter.

**Good:**
She drew her sword. The blade caught the light — faintly orange, the fire enchantment still holding. Good. She'd need it.

**Why:** Five sentences of escalating backstory dumped into a sword-draw moment. The fix: one action, one sensory detail about the blade, one reaction. The backstory about monks and raiders and Syl and Maren can go elsewhere (or nowhere).

### 8.
**Bad:**
The letter confirmed her suspicions. The guild had been funding the rebellion. The rebellion was organized through the merchant network. The merchant network answered to Maren's sister. Her sister had been presumed dead for three years. The entire plot connected back to the incident at the northern border.

**Good:**
The letter confirmed it. The guild, the rebellion, the merchants — all connected, all leading back to one person. Maren's sister. Who had been dead for three years, except apparently not.

She set the letter down. Picked it up again. Read the name one more time.

**Why:** Six facts in sequence with no character reaction between them. The fix groups the discoveries, delivers the surprise (not dead), and then shows the character processing the information through physical action.

## Poetic Overload

### 9.
**Bad:**
The forest was a living tapestry of emerald and gold. Sunlight wove through the canopy like threads of liquid amber. The breeze whispered secrets through the leaves, composing a symphony of nature's voice. Each step on the mossy carpet released the perfume of earth's oldest memories.

**Good:**
Green everywhere. The canopy thick enough to turn sunlight liquid, amber-coloured where it came through. Moss underfoot, soft, giving off a smell like wet stone and something sweeter underneath. She could hear the wind in the upper branches but couldn't feel it. Not down here. Down here was still.

**Why:** Four metaphors in four sentences (tapestry, wove, whispered secrets, oldest memories). The fix: one metaphor (liquid sunlight), then sensory detail without metaphor — smell, sound, temperature, stillness.

### 10.
**Bad:**
Her grief was an ocean, vast and fathomless, pulling her under with invisible currents. Tears carved rivers down her face, tributaries of a sorrow that had no shore. Her heart was a stone sinking through dark waters where no light could reach. Each breath was a battle against the tide of loss that threatened to swallow her whole.

**Good:**
She cried. Not gracefully, not quietly. Ugly crying, the kind where your face crumbles and you can't stop it. She sat on the floor because her legs had stopped working, and she cried until her chest hurt and her eyes burned and there was nothing left but hiccups and the dull certainty that tomorrow she'd have to do everything again.

**Why:** Four metaphors all drawn from the same vehicle (ocean, water). The fix: no metaphor. Just the body. The ugly, ungraceful, physical reality of hard crying, ending with the bleak thought about tomorrow.

### 11.
**Bad:**
Love bloomed between them like a rare flower in a forgotten garden. Each shared glance was a petal unfurling toward the sun of mutual understanding. Their hearts beat in a rhythm that the universe itself had composed, a melody written in the stars before either of them drew breath.

**Good:**
She didn't know when it had started. That was the unsettling thing — she couldn't point to a moment and say THERE, that's when. One day Maren was someone she worked with. Then someone she looked forward to seeing. Then someone whose absence felt like a room with the furniture rearranged. She'd never decided to love her. The decision had been made somewhere she couldn't access.

**Why:** Three sentences of stacked floral/celestial metaphor. The fix uses no metaphor until the furniture image, which is domestic and specific. The progression is shown through stages of attachment, and the final insight (she never decided) is more interesting than "love bloomed."

## Description Overload

### 12.
**Bad:**
The tavern was built of dark oak beams that had aged to the colour of old honey. The floor was flagstone, worn smooth by generations of boots. The bar was carved from a single piece of heartwood, its surface glossy with decades of spilled ale and elbow polish. Iron lanterns hung from chains at irregular intervals, casting pools of amber light that overlapped at the edges. A fireplace large enough to roast an ox dominated the far wall, its stones blackened by centuries of use.

**Good:**
The tavern was warm and dark and smelled like ale and woodsmoke. She found a table near the fire — the only one left, sticky with something she decided not to investigate — and sat down.

**Why:** Five sentences of equal-weight description, each with the same structure (noun + was/were + detailed modifier). The fix: one sentence of atmosphere, then the character's experience of arriving and sitting.

### 13.
**Bad:**
Maren wore a leather jerkin dyed deep burgundy, stitched with reinforced seams at the shoulders. Her trousers were dark wool, tucked into boots of supple black leather that rose to mid-calf. A belt of braided silver cord held a knife in a tooled sheath and a pouch of dark velvet. Her cloak was grey, hooded, fastened with a bronze pin shaped like a hawk in flight. Her hair was pulled back in a tight braid, revealing earrings of hammered copper.

**Good:**
Maren had dressed for the road — leather jerkin, dark trousers, boots that had seen better decades. The knife at her belt was new. That was the thing that caught her eye: everything else was worn, familiar, practical, but the knife was sharp and bright and expensive.

**Why:** Five sentences cataloguing an outfit like a character sheet. The fix: one summary, then one specific detail (the new knife) that raises a question.

## Good Density: High-Info Followed by Pause

### 14.
**Bad:**
"She's dead." Maren's voice was flat and her hands were shaking and the implication of what she'd said changed everything they'd planned because the alliance depended on her and without her there was no leverage and no leverage meant—

**Good:**
"She's dead."

Silence. Maren's hands shook. Outside, rain on the roof, steady and indifferent.

She's dead. The words sat in the room like something spilled.

**Why:** The bad version packs the emotional reaction and the political consequence into one breathless sentence. The fix: the news gets its own line. A beat of silence. One physical detail (hands). One ambient detail (rain). Then the words returned to, given weight.

### 15.
**Bad:**
The arrow hit Tiku in the shoulder. She staggered. Blood immediately. The attacker was on the wall, eastern parapet, recurve bow, range about forty yards. Tiku was going down. They needed cover. The wall to the left was close enough.

**Good:**
The arrow hit Tiku in the shoulder.

She staggered. Blood, immediately, a bright shock of it against her coat. For a half-second, everything stopped — Tiku looking down at the shaft like she was insulted by it, Maren already moving, the world gone quiet the way it does when something terrible has just happened and hasn't fully arrived yet.

Then noise. Everything at once.

**Why:** The bad version mixes emotional beat (arrow hit) with tactical assessment (eastern parapet, range forty yards) at the same density. The fix: the arrow hit gets its own paragraph. The freeze-frame slows time. The return to action ("Then noise") gets its own line.

### 16.
**Bad:**
She kissed Maren. Maren kissed back. They pressed together against the wall. Her hand found Maren's hip. Maren's breath caught. It was the first real thing that had happened all day.

**Good:**
She kissed Maren.

It wasn't planned. Wasn't graceful. She just — turned, and Maren was right there, closer than expected, and the distance between them closed before either of them decided to close it.

Maren kissed back. Slow, careful, like testing the temperature of water.

It was the first real thing that had happened all day.

**Why:** Six sentences at the same density, all action beats, creating a mechanical feel for what should be an intimate moment. The fix: the kiss gets its own line. A paragraph of context. The return kiss gets its own paragraph with a simile. The reflection gets space.

## Full Paragraph Rewrites

### 17.
**Bad:**
The battlefield stretched before her like a canvas painted in blood and shadow. Warriors clashed with desperate fury, their swords singing songs of death in the crimson twilight. The ground trembled with the weight of their desperate struggle. Banners fell like wounded birds. Screams rose like smoke into the uncaring sky. She stood at the edge and watched as history wrote itself in iron and agony.

**Good:**
The battlefield. She'd seen them before, but the size of this one — a mile across, maybe more, the fighting stretching from the river to the treeline. Banners down. Hard to tell who was winning. Hard to tell who was who, honestly, at this distance and in this light. The screaming reached her as a single sound, like wind, so constant it almost passed for silence.

She stood at the edge and didn't know which way to run.

**Why:** Six poetic sentences, every one with a simile or metaphor. The fix: practical observation (size, distance, visibility), one turned simile (screaming like wind), and a closing line that's more powerful because it's personal and uncertain.

### 18.
**Bad:**
Maren was beautiful. She was beautiful the way mountains are beautiful — dangerous, immovable, commanding respect through sheer presence. Her eyes held storms. Her hands had held worlds. Her voice could gentle a frightened horse or shatter a man's resolve with equal ease. She was, in a word, magnificent.

**Good:**
Maren was — something. She couldn't have said what, not then, not with Maren standing in the doorway backlit by the hall fire, her hair still wet, one sleeve pushed up to the elbow, looking at her with an expression she couldn't parse.

Something. That was the only word that fit.

**Why:** Six sentences of escalating praise, all telling. The fix: the POV character TRYING to find the word and failing. "Something" repeated is more honest and more effective than "magnificent" because it captures the experience of being struck by someone and not having the language.

### 19.
**Bad:**
She was exhausted. The kind of exhaustion that seeps into your bones. The kind that makes your eyes burn. The kind that turns every thought to sludge. The kind that makes you wonder if you'll ever feel rested again. She was tired beyond tired, drained beyond drained, empty beyond empty.

**Good:**
She was exhausted. Her eyes burned. Her thoughts had the consistency of wet sand — slow, heavy, refusing to hold a shape.

She sat on the edge of the bed and pulled off one boot. Then sat there, holding it, too tired to pull off the other one.

**Why:** Six sentences that all describe the same thing with escalating emphasis. The fix: the statement, two physical details, one metaphor, and then a specific action (sitting with one boot) that SHOWS exhaustion better than six sentences of telling.

### 20.
**Bad:**
Fear gripped her like a cold hand around her heart. Every shadow concealed a potential threat, every sound was a harbinger of doom. Her pulse raced with primal urgency, her blood singing with adrenaline's ancient song. She could taste the copper of fight-or-flight on her tongue. Her body screamed at her to run, to flee, to escape this place before it consumed her entirely.

**Good:**
Fear. The cold kind, the kind that starts in the stomach and spreads.

Every shadow looked wrong. Every sound was too loud, too close. She couldn't think past the pulse in her ears.

Move. She had to move. Her body was already moving — backing toward the door, one hand behind her finding the wall, the other on her knife. She didn't remember drawing it.

**Why:** Five sentences of metaphor-heavy fear description. The fix: the physical experience (cold stomach, loud sounds, pulse), then the body taking over (moving without deciding, drawing the knife without remembering). The split between thought and action shows fear better than any metaphor.
