# MOST IMPORTANT

## Dialogue Register Contamination (zero uses in spoken lines)

Institutional, clinical, and bureaucratic vocabulary in character dialogue. A character can be intelligent, analytical, or a trained specialist — she still does not speak the way a scientific paper or government briefing reads. The AI defaults to formal-technical language for "smart character speaks" and the result is prose that sounds like a report being read aloud.

**The test:** Would this person say this word out loud, to another person, in the middle of a scene? Intelligence is not the same as vocabulary formalism. Smart characters speak plainly about hard things; formal vocabulary in dialogue signals AI, not intelligence.

**Fix rule:** Replace with the grounded equivalent. If the sentence needs a formal term to mean anything at all, the sentence is wrong — cut and rephrase from the character's actual perspective.

**Known offenders (zero tolerance, cut on sight):**

- *actionable intelligence* — bureaucratic/military jargon. → "something worth acting on," "a threat worth responding to," "a reason to move."
- *diagnostic network* — institutional-technical. → "detection grid," "the ward-stones," "the only thing with enough range to map this."
- *vector* (when meaning source or path of spread) — epidemiology jargon. → "the source," "how it's spreading," "what's carrying it." ("Vector" is fine in a magical/physical direction sense only.)
- *optimal [noun]* — lab-report language ("optimal injection site," "optimal position"). → "the best place," "where you'd put it," "the deepest point."
- *tissue saturation / tissue exposure* — clinical pathology. → "until the body was full of it," "until everything it drank had soaked in," "until the flesh was through with it."
- *low-concentration exposure* — lab report. → "weeks of drinking tainted water," "slow accumulation," "too little to kill quickly, too much to leave clean."
- *primary [distribution/supply/route/network]* — logistics bureaucratese. → "the main [road/supply line/route]," or just name it.
- *actionable* in any construction — → plain verbs: "worth doing," "enough to move on."
- *propagation* (in spoken lines) — engineering term. → "spreading," "moving north," "following the ley junctions." (Acceptable in highly technical briefing context if the character is demonstrably using specialist register deliberately.)
- *at full complexity / simplified / dialed back* — difficulty-slider language for speech acts. → "the real version," "without softening it," "the whole picture." (This also applies in narration describing speech.)
- *substrate* (when not talking to a specialist about physical soil layers) — → "the ground beneath," "what the ley network runs through," "deep down." Note: the carve-out is narrow. A character alone sensing contamination through her palm is not talking to a specialist — "substrate" is still document voice there.
- *concentration* (in "contamination concentration") — clinical. → "how strong it is," "the density," "how much is in the water."
- *ingested* — clinical ingestion verb. → "drank," "ate," "swallowed."

**The broader signal:** If a line of dialogue could appear verbatim in a toxicology report, a government memo, or a military intelligence briefing — it belongs in a document, not a character's mouth. Rewrite from the character's lived perspective, not from the official's summary of it.

## Document Voice vs. Living Voice (zero uses in narration or dialogue)

The deepest register failure. Documents summarize events from outside them, after they happened. Characters live events from inside them, while they're happening. The AI defaults to document voice whenever it codes a character as intelligent, expert, or authoritative — because the training data for "smart person" is full of reports, papers, briefings, and memos, not people.

**The test:** Is this sentence written *from inside the moment*, or *about the moment from a distance*? A character in a burning building does not think "suboptimal egress conditions." She thinks the door is hot and there are two windows. A ranger reading a map does not think "the optimal injection site." She thinks: *there, the deepest point, that's where you'd put it.* Document voice at any remove — whether it appears in narration, internal monologue, or spoken dialogue — is the same failure.

**The four document registers to hunt:**

1. **Institutional/bureaucratic** — vocabulary of government memos, military intelligence reports, official communications. `actionable`, `actionable intelligence`, `geographic intelligence`, `primary [distribution/supply/network]`, `optimal [noun]`, `formal disposition`. These words belong to officials summarizing events, not people inside them.

2. **Clinical/medical/lab** — vocabulary of scientific papers, toxicology reports, diagnostic submissions. `ingested`, `tissue saturation`, `low-concentration exposure`, `diagnostic network`, `propagation rate`, `concentration` (of a substance), `vector` (as source/spread path). These words belong to someone writing up findings, not someone smelling the contamination on her hands.

3. **Academic/theoretical** — vocabulary of university courses and theoretical frameworks (see also the Academic-Discipline section below). The failure here isn't just mathematics and physics — it's any word that belongs in a description of a phenomenon rather than an experience of one.

4. **Meta-language about the prose itself** — language that describes the chapter's own register, difficulty, or craft: `at full complexity`, `simplified`, `dialed back`, `full version`. These words belong in editorial notes, not in narrated scenes or spoken lines.

**Fix rule:** Ask what the character would actually say or think if she *were inside the moment* rather than summarizing it afterward. The replacement is almost always shorter and more concrete. If the sentence collapses when the document word is removed, the construction was wrong — the character was never actually inside the moment to begin with. Rewrite from there.

**Academic-Discipline Register Contamination (zero uses in narration)**

Academic vocabulary applied to bodies, sensation, or social dynamics — the AI reaching into mathematics, engineering, physics, architecture, or computer science to describe lived experience. These words import a framework for the experience instead of rendering it. A character inside sensation does not think in academic disciplines. She thinks in what she feels, sees, hears, or can't stop noticing.

**The test:** Could a person standing inside this experience, while it was happening, use this word? If no — cut it.

**Fix rule:** Replace the academic word with the concrete physical observation or sensation. If the sentence collapses without the academic word, the construction was wrong, not just the word.

**Known offenders (zero tolerance, cut on sight):**

- *geometry* — "the geometry of her hips," "the specific geometry of an erect cock pushing against wool blend," "the geometry of what they were doing." → The shape, the curve, the line, the way it pushes against the fabric. Or just describe what the eye lands on.
- *mechanics* — "an accident of crowd mechanics," "the mechanics of desire," "the mechanics of the kiss," "the mechanics of the thing." → Describe what happened: a shoulder, a body, the brief press of someone else's cock through two layers of cloth. Or describe what desire does to this body, in this moment.
- *physics / forces / vectors* — "the physics of the situation," "the force of it," "a vector of." → The pull, the weight, the pressure, what the body does without asking permission.
- *entropic / entropy* — "entropic decay," "the entropic signature," "entropic frequency." → "the rot," "the wrongness in the ground," "the decay." A character feeling contamination through her hand doesn't reach for thermodynamics. She reaches for what it feels like.
- *engineered* (when meaning deliberate/intentional) — "it's engineered," "an engineered contamination." → "deliberate," "built," "someone made this work this way." Reserve "engineered" for literal machinery.
- *topology / configuration* — "the topology of the room," "the configuration of their bodies." → Where they were. Who was pressed against what.
- *trajectory* — "a trajectory of desire," "the trajectory of her gaze." → Where she was heading. Where her eye went. Or cut.
- *dynamics* — "the power dynamics," "the dynamics between them." → What was actually happening between them. Who moved first. Who didn't move at all.
- *architecture (of)* — "the architecture of shame," "the architecture of the moment." → Cut. Describe the shame. Describe the moment.
- *choreography* — "the choreography of bodies," "an unconscious choreography." → The way they moved. Who stepped back. Who stepped in. Describe the movement.
- *shape (of)* — abstract use: "the shape of it," "the wrong shape of the problem," "it had taken a shape she could use," "the true shape of what he meant." → Name the specific thing: the mechanism, the plan, the truth, the horror, the lie. "Shape" is a placeholder that avoids saying what the thing actually is. Physical shape of a physical object is fine ("the shape of the doorway," "the shape of the blade").
- *algorithm / pattern / system* — when used to describe a person's behavior as if they run on rules rather than feeling. "Her usual algorithm," "the system she'd built." → Her habit, the way she always did it, the thing she'd learned to do when.
- *data* — "incomplete data," "let data accumulate." → "something she hadn't figured out yet," "let the pieces collect." Characters turn facts over, not process data.
- *catalogued / cataloguing* (verb of perception) — "she catalogued his tells," "her gaze catalogued the room." → "she knew his tells," "her gaze swept the room." Turns a person into a database. (Noun "catalogue" — "the usual catalogue of complaints" — is fine.)
- *recalibrate / recalibration* — "a small recalibration," "she recalibrated her assessment." → Cut, or show through a physical tell. People blink, shift, go still. They don't recalibrate.
- *register* (speech-style noun) — "she shifted register," "the register that used four words." → "her voice dropped," "the tone she saved for—." (Literal register — cash register, parish register — is fine.)
- The pause was surgical.
- *registered* (verb of perception) — "she registered his discomfort," "he registered the change." → "she saw it," "he caught the change." A character who "registers" input is a sensor array.
- *absorbed* — "she absorbed the implication." → Cut. Let the next paragraph carry it. Characters sit with things, turn them over, go quiet.
- *filed* — "she filed the observation." → Cut. If the observation is shown, the reader knows.
- *operating systems / interfacing / processing / downloading / rebooting / bandwidth / buffering* — tech jargon in narration. Replace with world-appropriate metaphors.
- *assessment* — "her assessment shifted." → Show through what the character notices: the boots, the posture, the calluses. The word belongs in HR evaluations.
- *thermal* — "a thermal boundary," "thermal window." → "the break where the warmth stopped." Characters feel temperature, not thermal properties.
- *at full complexity / simplified / dialed back* — difficulty-slider language for speech. → "the real words, not the careful ones." Don't treat speech as having a settings menu.

The broader signal: any word that belongs in a university course description is wrong in the narration of a person experiencing something.

## Negation Addiction (zero uses)

AI defines things by what they're not instead of committing to what they are. All forms of this are the same tic: the writer is afraid to land the image, so she backs into it through exclusion.

- *Triple negation:* "not on X, not on Y, but on Z." → Cut the negations, start at Z.
- *Parallel negation pairs:* "not X but Y. Not A but B." → Same. Start at Y, start at B.
- *Telescoping negation with dramatic reveal:* serial "Not [noun]" clauses → em dash → the real thing. "Not the orgasm itself — not the direct, clenching, whiteout pleasure — but the shockwave." → Write "the shockwave" and trust it.
- *Negation-to-metaphor cascade:* "Not loudly. Not the way X had broken..." → Cut the negations, start at the image. Or cut the image too and write the concrete action.
- *Contrastive character cataloging:* "not Tiku's practical confidence or Yanna's breathless exploration, but a soldier's hand." → Just describe the hand. The reader knows whose it is.
- *Vague-negate-commit:* state something vague → negate a more dramatic interpretation → commit to the simple thing. "She made a small noise through her nose. Not a gasp. A snort." → "She snorted." If you know what it was, write what it was. The negation step is vestigial scaffolding the writer forgot to remove. Three sentences to vaguely claim, deny, then restate. Fix: delete sentences one and two, start at the commit.
- *Mundane-reveal negation chain:* Not-X / Not-Y structure where the final reveal is the pedestrian noun, not a dramatic image. "Not radiator warmth. Not school-building heat. Closer than that. Body warmth." → "Body warmth." Every word in the chain earns nothing the final noun couldn't have opened with.
- *Latent triple negation:* three negated adjectives where the third implies a positive via double-negative without ever naming it. "a smile that wasn't kind, wasn't friendly, and wasn't uninterested either" → name what the expression actually was. The implied inference is always weaker than the committed word.
- *Preamble negation:* noting the stronger event that didn't happen before landing the weaker one that did. "She did not laugh out loud. She did, however, smile." → "She smiled." The preamble primes for the dramatic then makes the real reaction feel like a letdown. Cut it; start at the thing that happened.
- *Negated anthropomorphic redefinition:* "That isn't sleep. That's cavalry lying to itself." → This is a double failure: negation scaffolding plus fake-clever personification. If the judgment is "barely sleep," write that. If the people matter, name the people: "That barely counts as sleep. Only cavalry would call it that." Do not make abstractions, professions, institutions, or broad categories perform self-aware human acts to dress up a simple thought.

## Expression Decomposition (zero uses)

Narrating a facial expression or body movement as a mechanical sequence with named phases — "Not a smile. The structural precursor to one." "Something shifted in her expression — the groundwork for surprise, not the surprise itself." "The corners of her mouth moved in the direction of amusement without arriving." The AI decomposes a human expression into its engineering stages because it's afraid to commit to the expression itself. Write what the POV character sees: "Her mouth twitched." "Almost a smile." "The corner of her mouth moved, and stopped." Do not name the phase. Do not use "precursor," "predecessor," "preliminary," "structural," or any word that belongs in a process diagram. The fix is always shorter than the violation.

## Narrator Over-Explanation (zero uses)

The narrator explaining what the reader already received. Two forms:

- *Attribution over-explanation:* "[Character] said. She sounded surprised. She rarely sounded surprised." The third sentence holds the reader's hand through the second. If "she sounded surprised" doesn't land alone, the observation isn't strong enough — rewrite the observation, don't footnote it.
- *Statement-then-restatement:* "[Comparison]. She looked at X the way she might look at a new variable in an equation — with interest, curiosity, and no particular emotion." The comparison already communicated clinical detachment. The list is a three-item glossary unpacking what the image said. Trust the comparison. If it needs a glossary, it's the wrong comparison. Cut the list. Keep the image. Or cut both and show through action.

## Sentence Opener Monotony

Two default AI sentence patterns that become tics at density:

- *"The [noun] [verb]ed":* "The fire cracked. The drops welled. The cock pushed. The tongue paused." Each instance is fine; 25 in a chapter is a tic. Fix: lead with the noun directly ("Fire cracked"), with a character name, with a gerund, or with the sensation itself.
- *"Her [noun] [verb]ed":* "Her nipples beaded. Her cock thickened. Her hands closed." Same tic wearing a possessive. Fix: drop the possessive ("Fingers twisted in the linen"), or restructure the sentence entirely.

## Repetition and Monotony

- *Contact verb dominance:* when one verb (pressed, found, settled, caught) exceeds 6 uses per chapter, it has become the default. Rotate: pressed, settled, closed around, landed on, rested against, caught, met, grazed, brushed, traced, cupped. Each touch deserves the verb that says how THIS one differs from the last.
- *Adverb monotony:* if everything defaults to "slowly," rotate: deliberate, unhurried, measured, languid, gradual.
- *Image repetition:* identical multi-word image reuse within 500 words. Once an image is established ("the flushed lips settling against the head"), the second instance needs a different camera angle, different verb, or different sensory channel ("the slick folds pressing to the crown").
- *Contrast repetition:* when a specific comparison phrase appears 3+ times in a chapter, it's a refrain. Use it once powerfully. If it recurs, vary the framing or compress to a fragment.

## POV Distance (zero uses)

Three ways the narrator inserts a layer between reader and experience:

- *Filter words:* "she felt the," "she could feel the," "she noticed," "she saw," "she heard." In limited third, everything described IS what she feels/sees/hears. The filter adds distance. Cut it, describe the sensation directly. Reserve "felt" only when the surprise of feeling something is the point.
- *POV sanitization:* "pure function," "purely functional" — laundering a physical action through clinical neutrality so nobody has to be present for it. "A grip that was pure function." Cut. Describe what the hands did. A POV character watching another woman doesn't reach for "functional" as a frame.
- *Inference dodging:* "left nothing to inference," "left nothing to the imagination" — signaling that anatomy is visible without describing it. The construction is an excuse not to write the moment. Write the look instead.

## Clinical Anatomy (zero uses in narration)

- *Medical terminology:* corona, glans, pelvic floor, abdominal wall. Describe what the character FEELS, not the structure. "The underside of the head, where every nerve lived" instead of "the corona." "The clench deep in her belly" instead of "pelvic floor contraction."
- *Compound-noun clinical inventions:* nerve-cascade, nerve-dense, nerve endings firing. The body doesn't name its own nervous system. Write the sensation: "a sharpness that bloomed outward," "a jolt that made her hips buck."
- *"Involuntary" as clinical label:* "involuntary contraction," "involuntary response." In limited third, the character doesn't label her own reflexes. She notices her body doing something without permission: "her hips moved before she told them to."

## Planning Vocabulary Firewall (zero uses in prose)

Story-structure and craft-framework terms belong in the plan JSON, never in narrated prose. Two families:

- *Story structure:* The Lie, The Truth, value shift, turning point, try-fail, yes-but, no-and. "The Lie cracked" → "The certainty she'd carried for twenty years buckled."
- *Romance taxonomy:* romance phase, adhesion beat, midpoint of love, grand gesture, sequel beat, scene beat. The character doesn't think in craft vocabulary. She thinks in emotions and decisions.

## Stacked Descriptor Clusters (max 1 per noun)

Comma-separated descriptors piled onto one noun — "her eyes — blue, steady, the colour of deep ice —", "grey-skinned, hollow-eyed, upright, waiting." AI defaults to stacking three or more. One strong descriptor does more than three. Pick the one that earns its place; cut the rest. If multiple qualities matter, spread them across the paragraph. The test: if you can delete two items and the sentence still works, you had too many.

## Melodramatic Intensifiers (zero uses)

"Devastating" / "devastatingly" — AI's favorite adjective for anything that should hit hard. If the moment is devastating, the reader should feel it through concrete detail, not the label. "A devastating kiss" → describe the kiss. Cut on sight, no exceptions.

## Character Label Bans (zero uses)

"Fighter" as Tifa synonym — never use "the fighter" as an identifying noun. She is Tifa, or she is described by what she's doing. The word reduces a character to a class label from a game menu.

## Participial Phrase Attachment (max 3 per chapter)

The comma-plus-ing construction: "She crossed the room, pulling her coat tighter." "He leaned back, studying the ceiling." "Tifa reached for the glass, her fingers trembling." Research shows AI uses these at 2–5x human rates. The construction implies simultaneity when the actions are usually sequential, and it becomes a rhythmic tic — every sentence ending on a dangling present participle. One or two per scene is natural; more than that and the prose develops a lilting, rocking cadence where every action trails a participial tail.

**Fix:** Make the actions sequential with separate sentences or coordinating conjunctions. "She crossed the room and pulled her coat tighter." Or restructure: "Her fingers trembled on the glass."

## Elegant Variation (zero uses)

AI avoids repeating a character's name by cycling through synonyms: "the woman," "the warrior," "the taller figure," "the dark-haired woman," "the futa," "the older woman." This is a prose anti-pattern called elegant variation — the reader has to solve a little riddle each time to figure out who is being referred to. In limited third POV, the character's name or a pronoun is almost always correct.

**Fix:** Use the character's name or "she." The POV character thinks in names, not in rotating descriptors. If two characters share pronouns, use names more frequently rather than inventing epithets.

## "Something" as Vague Intensifier (max 2 per chapter)

"Something in her voice changed." "Something shifted in the air between them." "Something about the way she stood." "There was something almost predatory in her smile." AI reaches for "something" when it can't name the specific sensation, producing a gesture toward meaning without delivering it. Occasional deliberate vagueness is a legitimate tool — when the POV character genuinely can't identify what she's perceiving. But AI uses it as a default, dozens of times per chapter, to avoid the harder work of specifying.

**Fix:** Name the specific thing. "Her voice dropped half a register." "The air between them went taut." "The way she stood — weight forward, shoulders loose." If you can't name it, the scene needs more sensory detail, not more vagueness.

## Breath as Universal Tell (max 3 breath-reactions per chapter)

"She let out a breath she didn't know she was holding." "Her breath caught." "A sharp intake of breath." "She released a slow breath." "He exhaled through his nose." AI defaults to breath as the physical channel for every emotion — surprise, relief, desire, grief, tension, release. Real bodies use a wider palette: jaw tension, stomach-drop, skin prickling, swallowing, stillness, the way hands move or stop moving. When breath carries every emotional beat, the prose develops a respiratory monotony.

**Fix:** Rotate through the full body. Throat, stomach, hands, spine, skin, posture. Reserve breath-reactions for moments where breathing is genuinely disrupted — a kiss, a gut-punch, surfacing from water.

## Inanimate-Subject Agency (max 2 per scene)

"The silence pressed against her." "The darkness seemed to breathe." "The room held its breath." "Something in the air shifted." "The tension coiled between them." AI stacks sentences where abstractions and objects perform emotional labor, creating a world where rooms have feelings and silences have intentions. One or two per scene is atmosphere; three or more and the prose becomes a haunted house where every noun is sentient.

**Fix:** Anchor atmosphere in what the POV character's body does in that space. "She couldn't hear anything but her own pulse" instead of "The silence pressed." "The dark was thick enough to feel on her skin" instead of "The darkness breathed."

## Uniform Paragraph Cadence

Every paragraph clusters at 3–5 sentences, producing identical visual bricks on the page. No one-sentence paragraphs for impact. No nine-sentence immersive passages. AI's statistical averaging produces metronomic paragraph lengths that kill the pacing variation prose needs. Human writers vary paragraph length the way musicians vary dynamics — a one-line paragraph after a dense block hits harder than any word in it.

**Fix:** Vary deliberately. After a dense descriptive paragraph, drop to a single line. Let a tense moment breathe in a long, run-on paragraph that doesn't let the reader up for air. The shape of the text on the page is a pacing instrument.

## Simile Overload — `like` and `as if` (max 3 each per chapter)

"The silence fell like a blade." "She looked at him as if he'd grown a second head." "It moved like water." "She smiled as if she'd won something." AI reaches for `like` and `as if` comparisons constantly — they're easy image-generators that feel literary without doing much work. Stack enough of them and the prose starts to float above the story, comparison-fogging every concrete moment. Three `like` similes per chapter. Three `as if` constructions per chapter. Count them. Cut the weakest ones first.

**Fix:** Replace with direct sensory detail. "The silence fell like a blade" → "The silence had an edge." "As if she'd grown a second head" → cut the comparison, write the actual reaction: what did the POV character's face do? Trust the concrete over the comparison. One precise image lands harder than five soft ones.

## Abstract-Noun Locomotion (zero uses)

"Memory surfaced in her face, slow and ugly." "Certainty moved so fast it left no room." "Recognition settled into his jaw." "Grief threaded through her chest." "Fear crawled up her spine." "Realization flooded through her." "Understanding crept across his expression." "Dread pooled in her stomach."

AI gives internal states velocity and a destination — the abstract noun travels, surfaces, floods, settles, crawls, threads, pools, or moves through or across a body part. It sounds literary. It delivers nothing. The reader gets the label of the emotion and a kinetic verb and a body location, and zero sensory specificity about what the face or body actually did.

This is emotional labeling dressed in movement clothes. "Memory surfaced in her face" tells you the cause (memory), the effect location (face), and the direction (up), and leaves out the only thing that matters: what did the face do? Eyes going unfocused? A stillness spreading from the jaw? The mouth pulling down on one side?
